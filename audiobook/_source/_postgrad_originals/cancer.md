## SEGMENT 1 — What cancer actually is

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. Today and for the next two hours, we're going to talk about cancer. And I want to begin by stripping the word down, because "cancer" is one of those words that has accumulated so much cultural weight — fear, metaphor, military rhetoric — that the underlying biology gets buried. The biology is, in some ways, simpler than the word suggests. In other ways, much stranger.

Here is the definition I want you to carry for the next two hours. Cancer is a disease in which a single cell, somewhere in your body, acquires a series of genetic and epigenetic changes that allow it and its descendants to grow when they shouldn't, refuse to die when they should, and eventually leave the tissue where they belong and colonize tissues where they don't. Cancer is, in other words, the evolution of a renegade cell lineage inside a multicellular organism. It is Darwinian. It is clonal. It starts as one cell.

\[Pause.\]

Let me unpack that, because every word in it is doing work.

"A single cell." Cancer is monoclonal in origin. When a pathologist looks at a tumor of a hundred billion cells, every one of those cells is, almost without exception, the descendant of one ancestor — one cell that, at some moment, took the first wrong step. We can prove this. We can sequence the genomes of cells across a tumor and find shared mutations that trace back to a single trunk on the evolutionary tree. We can use X-inactivation patterns in women to show that all the cells in a tumor came from the same originating cell. Cancer is not a tissue going bad all at once. It is the lineage of one cell winning.

"A series of genetic and epigenetic changes." Cancer is not one mutation. It is many. The number varies enormously by tumor type — a pediatric leukemia might carry only a handful of driver mutations, while a melanoma soaked in ultraviolet light or a lung tumor soaked in tobacco smoke might carry thousands of mutations, of which perhaps five to ten are actually doing the driving. We make a distinction you should learn now: **driver mutations** are the ones that confer a selective advantage on the cell, that drive the cancer forward. **Passenger mutations** are the ones that happened to be along for the ride, acquired during the chaotic life of a tumor cell but not contributing to its malignancy. Most mutations in a cancer genome are passengers. The drivers are the targets we care about.

"Grow when they shouldn't, refuse to die when they should, and leave the tissue where they belong." That's three things. Sustained proliferation. Evasion of apoptosis. Invasion and metastasis. Hold those three; we'll see them dressed up as more elaborate "hallmarks" in a few minutes.

"The evolution of a renegade cell lineage inside a multicellular organism." This is the deepest framing in modern cancer biology, and I want you to take it seriously. A cancer is a tiny evolutionary experiment running inside you. The unit of selection is the cell. The environment is your tissue. The fitness landscape is shaped by oxygen availability, by immune surveillance, by nutrient supply, by drugs we throw in. Mutations generate variation, selection picks the variants that grow fastest in that environment, and the population evolves. Every clinical phenomenon you will encounter in oncology — relapse, resistance, metastatic divergence — flows from this one fact. Cancer is evolution in fast-forward, with you as the ecosystem.

\[Pause.\]

If you walk out of this lecture in two hours and you can do three things — one, sketch the hallmarks of cancer and explain why each one is a logically necessary capability for a tumor to acquire; two, look at any cancer drug and tell me which hallmark it attacks and which molecular target it engages; three, explain to a confused friend why we cure some cancers and lose patients to others, in terms of clonal evolution and therapeutic windows — then we've succeeded. Everything else, the dosing details, the trial data, the staging systems, hangs off those three.

Now let me set up the single most important strategic concept in cancer therapy, because it will frame the whole back half of this lecture. It's called the **therapeutic window**. Here's the problem we're trying to solve. A cancer cell, unlike a bacterium, is not foreign. It is _you_. Its biochemistry is your biochemistry; its genome is, to a first approximation, your genome with a handful of mutations. If you build a poison that kills cancer cells, it tends to kill normal cells too — particularly the dividing ones in your bone marrow, your gut lining, your hair follicles. That's why classical chemotherapy makes people lose their hair, develop mouth sores, and become neutropenic. The drug doesn't know which dividing cell is the enemy.

So the entire art of cancer therapy is finding the _gap_ between a cancer cell and a normal cell, and exploiting it. A drug that hits something the tumor has and absolutely needs, but that normal cells either don't have, don't need as urgently, or can survive without. The therapeutic window is the dose range between "kills the cancer" and "kills the patient." For classical cytotoxic chemotherapy, that window is uncomfortably narrow, which is why oncologists spend so much energy on supportive care. For modern targeted therapies, the window is much wider, because the target is something specific to the cancer — a mutant kinase, a fusion protein, a tumor-specific neoantigen. When you hear in later segments that a particular drug like imatinib has dramatically less toxicity than older chemotherapies, the underlying reason is almost always that its target is something the cancer has and normal cells don't, or have but don't critically depend on. Keep that frame in your head the whole time: every cancer drug we discuss is an answer to the question, "what does this cancer have or need that the patient's healthy tissue doesn't, and how do I attack it?"

That's the central problem. Now let's build up the biology that lets us answer it.

## SEGMENT 2 — The hallmarks of cancer

In the year 2000, two cancer biologists — Doug Hanahan and Bob Weinberg — published a paper in the journal _Cell_ called "The Hallmarks of Cancer." It is, by some metrics, the most-cited paper in the entire field. What they did was deceptively simple. They looked at the bewildering diversity of human tumors — hundreds of cancer types, thousands of mutated genes, dozens of tissues — and they asked: is there a small, finite list of capabilities that a normal cell must acquire to become a cancer cell? Are there a few logical requirements that every tumor, regardless of its tissue of origin, must satisfy?

They argued there were six. In 2011 they updated the list to ten. In 2022 Hanahan added two more, bringing the total to fourteen. We're going to walk through the framework, because it organizes the rest of this lecture. Each hallmark is a capability the cell must gain; each capability is, in principle, a therapeutic target.

The original six, from 2000. **Sustained proliferative signaling** — the cell drives its own growth, no longer waiting for growth signals from the environment. **Evasion of growth suppressors** — the brakes that normally stop a cell from dividing are removed. **Resistance to cell death** — the cell ignores the programmed-death signals that should be eliminating it. **Replicative immortality** — the cell escapes the normal limit on how many times it can divide. **Induction of angiogenesis** — the tumor recruits a blood supply. **Activation of invasion and metastasis** — the cell breaks out of its tissue of origin.

Look at that list as an evolutionary biologist would. Each one is a problem a multicellular organism solved over a billion years to prevent any single cell from going its own way. Growth factors say "divide only when we need you." Tumor suppressors say "if anything looks wrong, stop." Apoptosis says "if you can't be repaired, die." Telomeres say "you only get to divide so many times." Vascular cues say "you only get a blood supply if the tissue licenses it." Tissue architecture says "stay where you belong." A cancer cell, to become a cancer cell, must defeat each of these constraints in turn. The hallmarks are the list of safeguards the cancer has broken.

In 2011 Hanahan and Weinberg added two more **emerging hallmarks**: **deregulating cellular energetics** — the Warburg effect, the cancer cell's preference for aerobic glycolysis even when oxygen is plentiful — and **avoiding immune destruction**, the recognition that the immune system normally surveys for and removes nascent cancers, and that established tumors must evade that surveillance. They also added two **enabling characteristics** — features that aren't hallmarks themselves but that allow the hallmarks to be acquired: **genome instability and mutation**, the engine that generates the variation cancer evolves on, and **tumor-promoting inflammation**, the recognition that chronic inflammation in a tissue creates the fertile ground in which cancers arise.

In 2022 Hanahan added two more emerging hallmarks: **unlocking phenotypic plasticity** — the ability of cancer cells to switch between developmental states, dedifferentiate, transdifferentiate, escape lineage commitment — and **senescent cells as tumor promoters**. And two more enabling characteristics: **non-mutational epigenetic reprogramming** — the recognition that not all the changes are at the DNA-sequence level; methylation and chromatin states matter too — and **polymorphic microbiomes**, the influence of the microorganisms living in and on us, particularly the gut microbiome, on cancer risk and treatment response.

That's the framework. Fourteen capabilities. You do not need to memorize the list in order — but you should hold the logic in your head, because every drug we discuss in the back half of this lecture targets one of these capabilities. Anti-angiogenics like bevacizumab attack hallmark five. Apoptosis-restorers like venetoclax attack hallmark three. Checkpoint inhibitors like pembrolizumab attack the immune-evasion hallmark. CDK4/6 inhibitors attack hallmark two. The hallmarks aren't just an organizing trick; they're a target list.

\[Pause.\]

And I want you to notice one more thing about the framework, which is that not every cancer has acquired every hallmark to the same degree. Some tumors are driven hard by proliferation; some are driven by death-evasion; some by metastatic spread. Different cancers have different hallmark profiles, and that's a hint about which therapeutic strategy will work best against any given tumor. The hallmarks aren't a uniform checklist; they're a portrait, and each cancer has its own portrait.

OK. With the framework in place, let's go down to the molecular level — to the genes that, when broken, allow these capabilities to emerge.

## SEGMENT 3 — The genetic basis: oncogenes and tumor suppressors

At the molecular heart of cancer is a simple binary distinction that you must internalize, because it governs everything that follows. The genes that drive cancer come in two flavors. **Oncogenes** and **tumor suppressors**. They work in opposite directions, they get activated in opposite ways, they require different therapeutic strategies, and they have completely different inheritance patterns. Get this distinction solid and a lot of cancer genetics becomes obvious.

An **oncogene** is a gene that, when _activated_ or _overactive_, drives cancer. The normal version of the gene — what we call the **proto-oncogene** — has a legitimate job. It might be a growth factor receptor, or a kinase in a signaling pathway, or a transcription factor that licenses cell division. It does this job in a regulated way, turning on when appropriate, turning off when its work is done. The cancer-causing version, the oncogene, is one in which that regulation is broken — the gene is on when it shouldn't be, or makes too much of its product, or makes a hyperactive mutant version of its product. The key concept: oncogenes are **gain-of-function**, and at the cellular level they are **dominant**. You only need one copy of an activated oncogene to push a cell toward cancer. The other copy of the gene is fine, but the cell is still being told to divide.

A **tumor suppressor**, by contrast, is a gene whose _normal job_ is to restrain cell growth or maintain genomic integrity. When it is doing its job, it suppresses tumors. The cancer-causing event is **loss of function** — the gene is broken, deleted, silenced, or otherwise removed from action. The cell loses its brakes. The key concept: tumor suppressors are loss-of-function, and at the cellular level they are typically **recessive**. You generally need to lose _both_ copies of the gene — both alleles — to see a phenotype. One working copy is usually enough to keep the cell restrained.

Hold those two patterns in your head, because they map onto two completely different therapeutic strategies. Oncogenes you can drug. You can build a small molecule that fits into the active site of a hyperactive kinase and turns it off. The cancer is addicted to the oncogene's signal, and removing the signal stops the cancer. This is the entire premise of targeted therapy. Tumor suppressors, on the other hand, are hard to drug — because the protein isn't there anymore. You can't inhibit a gene that's been deleted. To restore a lost tumor suppressor you'd have to put it back, which is gene therapy, which is hard. So tumor suppressors mostly tell us about cancer risk and mechanism; oncogenes give us most of our drug targets. We'll see exceptions — synthetic lethality with BRCA being the great example — but the asymmetry is fundamental.

\[Pause.\]

Now, how did we figure out tumor suppressors are recessive at the cellular level? This is one of the most beautiful pieces of reasoning in twentieth-century medicine, and it came from a quiet study of a rare childhood eye cancer. The story is **retinoblastoma**, and the man is **Alfred Knudson**, working in the early 1970s.

Retinoblastoma is a tumor of the developing retina. It affects young children, sometimes both eyes, sometimes one. Knudson noticed a striking pattern in the epidemiology. There were two forms of the disease. A **hereditary form**, in which the cancer ran in families, tended to appear in both eyes — **bilateral** disease — and appeared at very young ages, often before age one. And a **sporadic form**, in which there was no family history, the tumor was almost always in one eye only, and the age of onset was later, typically two to three years old. Same disease, two presentations, depending on whether you'd inherited a predisposition.

Knudson did something brilliant. He plotted the age of diagnosis against the cumulative probability of disease for the two groups, and he asked what mathematical model would fit. He found that the hereditary cases fit a curve consistent with a **single rate-limiting event** happening during childhood. The sporadic cases fit a curve consistent with **two** such events. From this, in 1971, he proposed what we now call the **two-hit hypothesis**: retinoblastoma requires two mutational events in the same cell, knocking out both copies of a single tumor-suppressor gene. In hereditary cases, the child inherits one defective copy in every cell of their body — the first hit is already there at birth — so only a single somatic event is needed to lose the second copy and produce a tumor. That second event happens at high enough frequency that it occurs in multiple retinal cells during development, producing tumors in both eyes. In sporadic cases, the child is born with two working copies, and both must be lost in the same cell during development for cancer to arise. That requires two independent rare events in the same cell — orders of magnitude less likely, hence later, hence usually unilateral.

This was inferred entirely from epidemiology, before the gene was cloned. The gene — **RB1**, retinoblastoma 1 — was identified in 1986. It encodes the **RB protein**, a tumor suppressor that controls the cell cycle by binding and inhibiting the E2F transcription factors that drive cells into S phase. We'll meet RB again when we talk about the cell cycle. Knudson's two-hit hypothesis was confirmed in molecular detail, and it has been extended to essentially every tumor suppressor we know — TP53, BRCA1, BRCA2, APC, PTEN, NF1, NF2, VHL, and on and on. The pattern is universal: hereditary cancer syndromes are explained by inheriting a defective copy of a tumor suppressor, which makes every cell in the body one mutational event away from cancer.

\[Pause.\]

That two-hit framework also tells you something important about hereditary cancer counseling. A child who inherits a defective BRCA1 allele isn't born with cancer. She's born with a much higher _probability_ of getting cancer over her lifetime, because each one of her breast and ovarian cells is only one event away from losing the function entirely. The risk is dramatically elevated, but it's still probabilistic, and it's still constrained by the second-hit rate. That nuance — predisposition versus inevitability — runs through all of clinical cancer genetics.

OK. With the oncogene-versus-tumor-suppressor distinction in hand, let's spend the next segment looking at the proto-oncogenes in detail, and then turn to the tumor suppressors.

## SEGMENT 4 — Proto-oncogenes in depth

Let's get specific. I'm going to walk you through the most important proto-oncogenes in human cancer, because these gene names will follow you for the rest of your clinical career. Each one teaches a slightly different lesson about how a normal gene becomes an oncogene.

We start with **RAS**, because RAS is, depending on how you count, the most frequently mutated oncogene in human cancer. There are three RAS genes in humans — **KRAS**, **HRAS**, and **NRAS**. They encode small GTPases — proteins that bind GTP and act as molecular switches in growth-factor signaling pathways. The picture is: a growth-factor receptor at the plasma membrane gets activated by its ligand, recruits some adaptors, activates a guanine-nucleotide exchange factor that loads GTP onto RAS, and RAS-GTP then activates downstream effectors, most importantly the RAF-MEK-ERK kinase cascade that drives proliferation. RAS, when its job is done, has its own intrinsic GTPase activity — it hydrolyzes its bound GTP to GDP, switching itself off. That GTP hydrolysis is helped along by GTPase-activating proteins, GAPs. So RAS cycles between an active GTP-bound state and an inactive GDP-bound state, and that cycle is normally tightly regulated.

Cancer mutations in RAS — overwhelmingly at codons 12, 13, or 61 — cripple the intrinsic GTPase activity and make RAS resistant to GAPs. The result is a RAS protein locked in the GTP-bound, active state. The growth signal is permanently on. Downstream pathways fire continuously, and the cell is driven to divide. **KRAS** mutations are signature drivers in pancreatic adenocarcinoma — nearly 90% of pancreatic cancers carry mutant KRAS — and they're frequent in colorectal cancer and lung adenocarcinoma. **NRAS** is prominent in melanoma. **HRAS** in some bladder and head-and-neck cancers.

For decades, RAS was considered **undruggable**. The protein has a smooth surface with no good drug-binding pocket; the GTP-binding site has picomolar affinity, so you can't compete with GTP. Generations of medicinal chemists tried and failed. Then in the 2010s, a clever insight: the **KRAS-G12C** mutation, common in lung adenocarcinoma, leaves a reactive cysteine at codon 12 that sits next to an allosteric pocket. You can design a small molecule that covalently attacks that cysteine — irreversibly tethering itself to the mutant protein only — and locks KRAS in the inactive GDP-bound state. That insight produced **sotorasib** (Lumakras) and **adagrasib**, the first KRAS-specific drugs ever approved, both targeting the G12C mutant. We'll come back to these in the targeted-therapy segment. The lesson: "undruggable" usually means "not yet drugged."

Next, **MYC**. MYC is a transcription factor — strictly, a basic helix-loop-helix leucine-zipper transcription factor — that dimerizes with a partner called MAX and binds DNA at sequences called E-boxes. MYC drives the expression of an enormous program of genes involved in cell growth, ribosome biogenesis, metabolism, and proliferation. When MYC is on, the cell is being told to grow. Normally MYC is exquisitely tightly regulated, induced briefly by growth signals and then rapidly degraded.

MYC becomes oncogenic when it is **overexpressed** — when its levels are pushed too high or it is no longer turned off. The most spectacular example is **Burkitt lymphoma**, a B-cell lymphoma in which a chromosomal translocation moves the **MYC** gene on chromosome 8 next to one of the **immunoglobulin** loci — typically the heavy-chain locus on chromosome 14, written t(8;14). B cells transcribe their immunoglobulin genes at enormous rates as part of antibody production. When MYC is dragged under the control of the immunoglobulin regulatory elements, MYC gets transcribed at the same enormous rates. The cell becomes a runaway proliferator. MYC is also amplified in many other cancers — neuroblastoma carries **MYCN** amplification as its dominant driver — and is broadly upregulated downstream of many other oncogenic signals.

MYC is also, until very recently, considered undruggable. It's a transcription factor with a flat dimerization interface; there's no enzyme to inhibit. The field has tried indirect approaches — BET bromodomain inhibitors that disrupt MYC transcription, for instance — with partial success. New approaches using protein degraders are in early trials. But the central lesson: many of the most important oncogenes are not enzymes, and our chemical-biology toolkit was built for enzymes. The next generation of cancer drugs has to break that ceiling.

\[Pause.\]

**EGFR**, epidermal growth factor receptor. A receptor tyrosine kinase that sits in the plasma membrane, binds extracellular EGF or related ligands, dimerizes, autophosphorylates on its cytoplasmic tail, and triggers downstream signaling — RAS-RAF-MEK-ERK, PI3K-AKT, and others. In normal epithelium it controls proliferation in response to wound signals. In cancer, particularly **non-small-cell lung cancer**, EGFR can be activated by point mutations in its kinase domain — most famously exon 19 deletions and the L858R substitution in exon 21 — that lock the kinase in an active state without needing ligand. In **glioblastoma**, EGFR is often **amplified** to enormous copy numbers and sometimes carries a truncating deletion called EGFRvIII. In some head-and-neck cancers, the wild-type receptor is overexpressed.

EGFR was one of the first really successful targets of small-molecule inhibition in solid tumors. **Gefitinib** and **erlotinib** were developed as ATP-competitive kinase inhibitors of EGFR, and they had striking activity in patients whose tumors carried activating EGFR mutations. We'll see in segment 15 how resistance to those drugs evolves through a specific gatekeeper mutation — **T790M** — and how the third-generation drug **osimertinib** was designed to overcome it. EGFR is the textbook case of how genotyping a tumor changes treatment.

**HER2**, also called ERBB2, is a related receptor tyrosine kinase. It doesn't bind any known ligand directly; instead it acts as a preferred dimerization partner for other ERBB-family receptors. In about 15-20% of breast cancers, HER2 is **amplified**, sitting in dozens to hundreds of copies per cell. The cell makes enormous amounts of HER2, the receptors crowd the membrane and dimerize spontaneously, and proliferative signaling fires continuously. HER2 amplification used to be a marker of particularly aggressive, poor-prognosis breast cancer. Then **trastuzumab** (Herceptin), a monoclonal antibody that binds the extracellular domain of HER2, changed the natural history of the disease. We'll come back to HER2-directed therapy — antibodies, kinase inhibitors, antibody-drug conjugates — in segment 11.

\[Pause.\]

And finally, the founding example of a translocation-generated oncogene: **BCR-ABL**. This is the lesion that defines **chronic myeloid leukemia**, CML, and its story is so important that we'll come back to it as the founding success of targeted therapy.

Here's the genetics. **ABL** is a tyrosine kinase that lives on chromosome 9, normally tightly regulated. **BCR** is a gene of less-clear function on chromosome 22. In CML, there is a reciprocal translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22 — pieces of each chromosome swap. The visible result is a shortened chromosome 22 called the **Philadelphia chromosome**, named after the city where it was first described by Peter Nowell and David Hungerford in 1960. The molecular consequence is that the front half of BCR gets fused to the back half of ABL, producing a **BCR-ABL fusion gene** that encodes a fusion protein. The BCR portion drives the protein to dimerize constitutively; the ABL kinase, no longer subject to its normal regulatory domains, is permanently active. The result is a constitutively active tyrosine kinase that drives leukemic proliferation.

The lesson, and we'll see it repeated: chromosomal translocations are a major mechanism of oncogene activation. They can fuse two genes to create a hybrid protein, as in BCR-ABL or EML4-ALK in lung cancer, or they can move a proto-oncogene under the control of a strong promoter, as in MYC in Burkitt lymphoma. Translocations are particularly common in hematological malignancies — leukemias and lymphomas — where the immune-cell recombination machinery is occasionally hijacked into making the wrong joins.

So that's the proto-oncogene catalog: **point mutations** that activate a kinase or lock a GTPase on (EGFR, RAS), **amplifications** that crank up the dose of a normal gene (HER2, MYCN, EGFR in glioma), and **translocations** that either fuse two proteins (BCR-ABL) or drive transcriptional dysregulation (MYC in Burkitt). Three mechanisms. Memorize them; they cover most of clinical oncogene activation.

## SEGMENT 5 — Tumor suppressors

Now the other side of the ledger. Tumor suppressors. These are the genes whose normal job is to keep cells in line, and whose _loss_ is what permits cancer. We've already done the conceptual setup with Knudson and RB; let me walk you through the most important ones.

**TP53**, encoding the **p53 protein**, is the single most frequently mutated gene in all of human cancer. About half of all human tumors carry a TP53 mutation. It has been called "the guardian of the genome," and the moniker has stuck because it captures the function well. p53 is a transcription factor that sits, in healthy cells, at a low steady-state level — its protein is constantly being marked for degradation by an E3 ubiquitin ligase called MDM2. When the cell experiences certain stresses — DNA damage, oncogenic signaling, hypoxia, nucleotide depletion — signaling pathways modify p53 and stabilize it, allowing it to accumulate. Stabilized p53 binds DNA at specific response elements and turns on a transcriptional program that does one of three things, depending on the severity and context of the stress: it pauses the cell cycle to allow repair, it triggers senescence to permanently remove the cell from the dividing pool, or it triggers apoptosis to kill the damaged cell outright.

p53 is the integrator of cellular stress responses. When p53 is working, a cell that has experienced significant DNA damage either fixes itself, stops dividing forever, or dies. When p53 is broken, that cell carries its damage forward, divides again, accumulates more damage, and continues to do so. Loss of p53 doesn't just remove a brake; it permits the accumulation of additional mutations, accelerating the path to malignancy. That is why TP53 mutation is associated with worse prognosis across so many cancer types — not because p53 mutation alone is enough to cause cancer, but because losing p53 lets the rest of the cancer-causing process happen faster and more chaotically.

Inherited TP53 mutation causes **Li-Fraumeni syndrome**, in which affected individuals develop multiple cancers — sarcomas, breast cancers, brain tumors, adrenocortical carcinomas — often beginning in childhood. The two-hit hypothesis applies: people with one defective TP53 allele are one event away from p53 loss in every cell of their body. Li-Fraumeni patients require lifelong intensive cancer surveillance.

**RB1**, the retinoblastoma gene, we've already met. Its product, the RB protein, is the gatekeeper of the **restriction point** in the cell cycle. RB, in its unphosphorylated active state, binds E2F transcription factors and prevents them from turning on the genes needed to enter S phase. When the cell receives sustained growth signals, cyclin-CDK complexes phosphorylate RB, releasing E2F, allowing S-phase entry. Loss of RB removes the requirement for growth signaling — E2F is constitutively free, and the cell drives itself into S phase. RB loss is the defining lesion of retinoblastoma but is also common in small-cell lung cancer, osteosarcoma, and various other tumors, and is functionally inactivated in many more cancers via dysregulation of the cyclin-CDK-RB axis we'll discuss next segment.

**BRCA1** and **BRCA2** are the most clinically famous tumor suppressors after p53. They are involved in **homologous recombination repair** of DNA double-strand breaks — the high-fidelity repair pathway that uses the sister chromatid as a template. Loss of either gene cripples homologous recombination, leaving the cell dependent on lower-fidelity repair pathways and prone to genomic instability. Inherited BRCA1 mutations confer dramatically elevated lifetime risk of breast cancer (roughly 60-70%) and ovarian cancer (40-50%); BRCA2 mutations confer similar elevations in breast and ovarian cancer plus increased risks of pancreatic, prostate, and male breast cancer.

The BRCA story is a beautiful illustration of why tumor suppressors are usually hard to drug — and how clever biology can sometimes find a way. You can't restore lost BRCA1. But you can exploit the dependency that BRCA-loss creates. Cells without BRCA-mediated homologous recombination rely heavily on a backup repair pathway involving **PARP**, poly-ADP-ribose polymerase, which helps repair single-strand breaks before they convert to double-strand breaks. Block PARP with a small molecule — **olaparib**, **niraparib**, **rucaparib**, **talazoparib** — and BRCA-deficient cells can no longer cope with the resulting double-strand breaks. They die. BRCA-competent normal cells, with intact homologous recombination, survive. This is **synthetic lethality** — two mutations that are each individually tolerable, but lethal in combination. Each PARP inhibitor essentially turns the tumor's BRCA mutation into a vulnerability that the rest of the body doesn't share. It is one of the most elegant ideas in modern oncology and a template for how to drug the undruggable.

\[Pause.\]

**PTEN** is a phosphatase that opposes the **PI3K-AKT** signaling pathway. PI3K, activated by receptor tyrosine kinases, phosphorylates a membrane lipid called PIP2 to PIP3, which recruits AKT and triggers a survival- and growth-promoting cascade. PTEN dephosphorylates PIP3 back to PIP2, turning the pathway off. Loss of PTEN therefore permits unrestrained PI3K-AKT signaling, even without upstream activation. PTEN is among the most frequently lost tumor suppressors in human cancer — prominent in glioblastoma, prostate cancer, endometrial cancer, and many others. Inherited PTEN mutations cause **Cowden syndrome**, with elevated risk of breast, thyroid, and endometrial cancers.

**APC**, adenomatous polyposis coli, is the gatekeeper tumor suppressor for colorectal cancer. APC is a scaffolding protein in the **Wnt signaling pathway** that normally participates in a destruction complex targeting beta-catenin for degradation. Loss of APC causes beta-catenin to accumulate, translocate to the nucleus, and drive proliferative transcription. Inherited APC mutation causes **familial adenomatous polyposis**, FAP, in which affected individuals develop hundreds or thousands of colonic polyps starting in adolescence, with essentially 100% lifetime risk of colorectal cancer if untreated. Sporadic colorectal cancers also frequently begin with APC loss; the **adenoma-carcinoma sequence** described by Bert Vogelstein in the late 1980s — APC loss, then KRAS activation, then loss of additional tumor suppressors including TP53 — became one of the most influential models of multistep carcinogenesis.

Other tumor suppressors you'll encounter: **VHL** in clear-cell renal cell carcinoma and von Hippel-Lindau syndrome; **NF1** and **NF2** in the neurofibromatoses; **MLH1**, **MSH2**, **MSH6**, **PMS2** — the mismatch repair genes whose loss causes Lynch syndrome and **microsatellite-unstable** colorectal and endometrial cancers; **CDKN2A**, which encodes both p16/INK4a and p14/ARF and is lost in many cancers; **SMAD4** in pancreatic cancer; **STK11** in Peutz-Jeghers syndrome.

The collective lesson: tumor suppressors are the body's safeguards. Each one stands for a different cellular constraint — DNA-damage response, cell cycle gate, repair pathway, signaling brake, tissue-architecture rule. When we lose them, those constraints disappear, and the cell is one step closer to malignancy.

## SEGMENT 6 — Cell cycle dysregulation and CDK inhibitors

Now I want to zoom in on one specific machinery that ties the previous two segments together: the **cell cycle**. Cancer is, at one level, a disease of inappropriate cell division. So the molecular machinery that controls cell division is, at one level, the central machinery of cancer.

You learned the cell cycle in undergraduate biology, but let me refresh the key pieces because they'll be load-bearing for the rest of this lecture. A cell that's going to divide passes through four phases: **G1** (gap one — growth and preparation), **S phase** (DNA synthesis — the genome is replicated), **G2** (gap two — more growth and preparation for division), and **M phase** (mitosis — actual division into two daughter cells). Cells that are not actively dividing sit in a quiescent state outside the cycle called **G0**.

The cycle is driven by a family of regulatory proteins called **cyclins**, paired with enzymes called **cyclin-dependent kinases**, or **CDKs**. The cyclins are produced and destroyed in waves, rising and falling at specific points in the cycle, and each one activates its partner CDK. Cyclin-CDK complexes phosphorylate substrate proteins that push the cell into the next phase. Different cyclin-CDK pairs control different transitions. **Cyclin D-CDK4 and CDK6** drive the cell through early-to-mid G1. **Cyclin E-CDK2** drives the G1-to-S transition. **Cyclin A-CDK2** runs through S phase. **Cyclin B-CDK1** drives entry into mitosis.

Layered on top of this engine is a system of checkpoints — places where the cell pauses to verify that conditions are right to proceed. The most clinically important checkpoint sits in late G1 and is called the **restriction point**. The restriction point is the moment of commitment: once a cell passes it, it is committed to completing the cycle and dividing, regardless of subsequent growth-factor signals. Before the restriction point, the cell can decide not to divide. After it, the decision is made.

The molecular gatekeeper of the restriction point is **RB**, the same RB protein we just discussed as a tumor suppressor. In G0 and early G1, RB is in its unphosphorylated, active state, bound to **E2F** transcription factors and holding them in an inactive complex. E2F's job, when free, is to turn on the genes needed for S phase — cyclin E, DNA polymerase, dihydrofolate reductase, thymidylate synthase, and the rest of the replication machinery. As long as RB is sitting on E2F, the cell cannot enter S phase.

Growth signals coming into the cell — through receptor tyrosine kinases like EGFR, through RAS-RAF-MEK-ERK, through PI3K-AKT — converge on the induction of **cyclin D**. Cyclin D pairs with CDK4 or CDK6 and the complex begins to phosphorylate RB. Initial phosphorylation by cyclin D-CDK4/6 partially inactivates RB. Cyclin E-CDK2, induced as E2F begins to be released, then hyperphosphorylates RB, fully releasing E2F, which drives the cell into S phase. So the restriction point is, at the molecular level, the cumulative phosphorylation of RB by cyclin-CDK complexes responding to sustained growth signaling.

\[Pause.\]

Now look at this pathway through the cancer lens. To bypass the restriction point — to drive a cell into S phase without legitimate growth signaling — you can break any of several components. You can activate RAS upstream and drive cyclin D induction. You can overexpress cyclin D itself, which happens in mantle cell lymphoma via a t(11;14) translocation. You can amplify CDK4, common in liposarcoma and some gliomas. You can delete **p16/INK4a** — encoded by CDKN2A — which is the natural inhibitor of CDK4/6. Or you can delete RB itself. Every one of these lesions feeds into the same node: RB is hyperphosphorylated, E2F is released, the cell divides. The **cyclin D-CDK4/6-INK4a-RB-E2F axis** is dysregulated in essentially every human cancer.

That makes the axis an obvious therapeutic target. And it has become one of the most successful drug-development stories of the past decade. **Palbociclib**, **ribociclib**, and **abemaciclib** are small-molecule **CDK4/6 inhibitors** that block the phosphorylation of RB and arrest cells in G1. They were developed and approved in **hormone-receptor-positive, HER2-negative metastatic breast cancer**, where they are combined with endocrine therapy — aromatase inhibitors or fulvestrant — and have substantially extended progression-free survival. They work because hormone-receptor-positive breast cancers are heavily dependent on cyclin D-CDK4/6 to drive proliferation; block that step, you arrest the tumor.

There's a subtlety worth knowing. CDK4/6 inhibitors work only if RB is intact in the tumor. If RB is deleted — as it is in many small-cell lung cancers and some other tumors — the drug has nothing to do, because there's nothing downstream for it to constrain. So you select patients for CDK4/6 inhibitor therapy by, among other things, ensuring their tumor still has functional RB. This is the targeted-therapy logic in action: the drug's efficacy depends on the molecular state of the tumor, not just on the tissue of origin.

There are toxicities, of course. CDK4/6 is also needed in normal proliferating cells, particularly in the bone marrow, so neutropenia is the dose-limiting toxicity. But because CDK4/6 inhibition is reversible and the dependency of normal cells on the kinase is less acute than the cancer's, the therapeutic window is wide enough to make these drugs broadly useful. CDK4/6 inhibitors are now being explored across many tumor types, including in earlier disease settings as adjuvant therapy after surgery.

So that's the cell-cycle story: a beautifully ordered molecular clock with the restriction point as its commitment gate, dysregulated in essentially every cancer through one of several converging lesions, and now drugged at the central CDK4/6 node. Hold onto this picture — we'll need it when we talk about resistance, because cancers eventually find ways around CDK4/6 inhibition by activating alternative cyclin-CDK pairs or losing RB altogether.

## SEGMENT 7 — Evasion of apoptosis: BCL2 and the venetoclax revolution

Cancer is, as we've said, a disease of inappropriate proliferation. But it is equally a disease of inappropriate survival. A cell that should die — because it has damaged DNA, because it has wandered out of its niche, because its growth signaling looks abnormal — should be killed by a built-in program of self-destruction called **apoptosis**. When apoptosis works, damaged or dangerous cells are removed quietly and the tissue moves on. When apoptosis fails, those cells live, divide, and accumulate further damage. Evasion of apoptosis is hallmark number three, and it's a hallmark every cancer must, in some way, achieve.

Apoptosis runs through two converging pathways. The **extrinsic** pathway is triggered by external death signals — TNF, FAS ligand, TRAIL — binding to death receptors on the cell surface. The **intrinsic** pathway, also called the **mitochondrial** pathway, is triggered from inside the cell, by signals like DNA damage, oncogenic stress, nutrient deprivation, or loss of survival signaling. Both pathways converge on the activation of a family of cysteine proteases called **caspases**, which then dismantle the cell from within — cleaving structural proteins, fragmenting the nucleus, triggering the orderly packaging of the cellular contents into membrane-bound apoptotic bodies that are phagocytosed by neighbors without inflammation.

The intrinsic pathway is the one that matters most for cancer, and it is governed by the **BCL2 family** of proteins. Here is the logic. The BCL2 family comes in three subgroups. **Pro-survival** members — BCL2, BCL-XL, MCL1, BCL-W, A1 — sit on the outer mitochondrial membrane and keep the cell alive. **Effector** members — BAX and BAK — are the apoptosis-executing proteins that, when activated, oligomerize in the mitochondrial outer membrane and punch holes in it, releasing cytochrome c and other apoptogenic factors into the cytoplasm. And **BH3-only** proteins — BIM, BID, BAD, PUMA, NOXA, and others — are the sensors of cellular stress that tip the balance, either by directly activating BAX and BAK or by inhibiting the pro-survival proteins. The cell's life-or-death decision is the moment-to-moment balance between pro-survival and pro-death members of this family.

A cancer cell tilts that balance toward survival. It can do this by overexpressing a pro-survival protein, most commonly **BCL2** itself, or it can do it by deleting or silencing a pro-death protein. The defining example of the first strategy is **follicular lymphoma**, which arises from a translocation called **t(14;18)** that moves the BCL2 gene next to the immunoglobulin heavy-chain enhancer. BCL2 is transcribed at enormous levels in B cells carrying the translocation. Those cells, which would normally undergo apoptosis during the rigorous selection of the germinal center reaction, instead survive and accumulate. Follicular lymphoma is, at its origin, a disease of failed apoptosis.

The therapeutic implication is direct. If a cancer cell is being kept alive by BCL2 — if BCL2 is sitting in the membrane sequestering pro-death signals — then inhibiting BCL2 should release those signals and allow the cell to die. For a long time this seemed impractical: BCL2 is a protein-protein interaction surface, with a long shallow groove, and protein-protein interactions are notoriously hard to drug with small molecules. But in the 2000s, AbbVie chemists working with **fragment-based drug discovery** built up a small molecule that fit into the BH3-binding groove of BCL2. After years of optimization, the result was **venetoclax** (Venclexta), a **BH3 mimetic** that competes for the same groove that BH3-only proteins use and displaces the pro-death signals BCL2 had been sequestering.

Venetoclax was approved first in **chronic lymphocytic leukemia**, CLL, where the response rates in patients with disease that had failed standard therapy were striking. It is now also approved in **acute myeloid leukemia** in combination with hypomethylating agents like azacitidine, a regimen that has substantially extended survival in older patients who can't tolerate intensive chemotherapy. Venetoclax has a characteristic toxicity to be aware of: in CLL patients with bulky disease, the first dose can cause **tumor lysis syndrome**, an explosive collapse of huge numbers of leukemic cells releasing potassium, phosphate, uric acid, and other intracellular contents into the bloodstream fast enough to overwhelm renal clearance and cause electrolyte derangements, arrhythmias, even death. This is why venetoclax is started with a careful ramp-up and aggressive hydration and uric-acid management.

\[Pause.\]

The deeper lesson of venetoclax is conceptual. It validated the idea that you can drug protein-protein interactions in the cell-death machinery, and it opened the way for related drugs targeting other pro-survival BCL2 family members. **MCL1 inhibitors** are in trials. **BCL-XL inhibitors** are being developed, with the challenge that BCL-XL is required for platelet survival, so dose-limiting thrombocytopenia is a problem. The general principle — exploit a tumor's specific survival dependency by inhibiting the pro-survival protein it relies on most — is called targeting **apoptotic priming** or **dependence on a single survival protein**, and it's a major theme of modern hematology drug development.

## SEGMENT 8 — Angiogenesis, invasion, and metastasis

A solid tumor faces a logistical problem: once it grows beyond about a millimeter or two in any dimension, the cells in the center are too far from the nearest capillary to get oxygen and nutrients by simple diffusion. Without a blood supply, those cells become hypoxic, starve, and die — or, more accurately, the tumor stalls. To grow larger, a tumor must recruit a blood supply. This is **angiogenesis**, hallmark five, and it was identified as a critical limit on tumor growth by Judah Folkman starting in the 1970s, in work that was initially controversial and is now textbook.

The molecular orchestrator of tumor angiogenesis is **VEGF**, vascular endothelial growth factor. VEGF is produced by tumor cells, especially those in hypoxic regions of the tumor, and binds VEGF receptors on endothelial cells of nearby blood vessels. Activation of VEGF signaling drives endothelial cells to proliferate, migrate toward the tumor, and form new capillary sprouts that grow into and through the tumor mass. The new vessels are typically abnormal — leaky, tortuous, with poor pericyte coverage and chaotic flow — but they suffice to bring in enough oxygen and nutrients to support continued growth.

The therapeutic logic is direct. Block VEGF signaling, you starve the tumor of its blood supply. **Bevacizumab** (Avastin) is a humanized monoclonal antibody that binds VEGF-A and prevents it from engaging its receptors. It was the first anti-angiogenic drug approved, initially in metastatic colorectal cancer in combination with chemotherapy, and has since been approved across multiple tumor types — non-small-cell lung cancer, ovarian cancer, cervical cancer, glioblastoma, hepatocellular carcinoma. A parallel approach uses small-molecule kinase inhibitors of the VEGF receptors — **sunitinib**, **sorafenib**, **pazopanib**, **axitinib**, **lenvatinib**, **regorafenib** — which block VEGFR signaling on the inside of the endothelial cell.

A subtlety worth knowing: anti-angiogenic therapy turns out not to be a magic bullet. It extends survival modestly in many indications, dramatically in a few, but it doesn't eradicate tumors on its own. Tumors compensate by switching to alternative angiogenic factors — FGF, angiopoietin, PDGF — or by co-opting existing vessels rather than building new ones (a process called **vascular co-option**). The toxicities are notable: hypertension, proteinuria, bleeding, impaired wound healing, gastrointestinal perforation. Still, anti-angiogenics are a permanent fixture of modern oncology, and they validated Folkman's once-controversial premise.

\[Pause.\]

Now, **invasion and metastasis**. This is hallmark six, and it is the property that turns a tumor from a local problem into a lethal disease. The vast majority of cancer deaths — somewhere around 90% — are caused not by the primary tumor but by its **metastases**, the secondary tumors that arise when cancer cells leave the primary site, travel through the body, and establish new colonies in distant organs.

The metastatic cascade is a brutal multi-step gauntlet, and most cells that attempt it fail. Roughly: a cancer cell at the primary site must first acquire **invasive** properties — the ability to break through the basement membrane and migrate through the stroma. It must then **intravasate** — push its way into a blood vessel or lymphatic. Once in circulation, it must survive — the bloodstream is a hostile environment, with shear forces and immune cells, and most circulating tumor cells die within hours. It must then **arrest** somewhere in a capillary bed, **extravasate** out of the vessel, and finally establish itself in the foreign tissue — a process called **colonization** that is often the rate-limiting step. The metastatic efficiency — the fraction of disseminated cells that successfully establish macrometastases — is well under one percent.

The molecular program underlying invasiveness is most famously the **epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition**, **EMT**. Epithelial cells normally sit in organized sheets, attached to one another by tight junctions and to the basement membrane below by integrins, with apicobasal polarity. They don't migrate. **Mesenchymal cells**, by contrast, are individual, motile, lack polarity, and freely traverse extracellular matrix. EMT is a coordinated transcriptional program — driven by transcription factors like **SNAIL**, **SLUG**, **TWIST**, and the **ZEB** family — that converts an epithelial cell into a mesenchymal-like state. The cell loses **E-cadherin**, the central adhesion molecule of epithelial junctions, gains markers like **vimentin** and **N-cadherin**, and becomes capable of migration and invasion. EMT during normal development drives gastrulation and neural crest migration; in cancer, the same program is co-opted to enable invasion. There's a corresponding reverse program, **mesenchymal-to-epithelial transition** or **MET**, that operates at the site of metastasis to allow the disseminated cells to re-acquire epithelial features and form a colony.

There's a long-standing observation in cancer that different primary tumors have different patterns of metastatic spread. Breast cancers preferentially metastasize to bone, lung, liver, and brain. Prostate cancer almost always goes to bone. Colon cancer goes to liver first, then lung. Uveal melanoma goes to liver, almost exclusively. This is not random. In 1889, an English surgeon named Stephen Paget proposed the **seed-and-soil hypothesis**: the metastatic distribution is determined not just by where the blood flow takes the circulating tumor cells (the seeds), but also by the receptiveness of the destination tissue (the soil). Some organs are simply more hospitable to certain tumor types than others, because the microenvironment provides — or fails to provide — the growth factors, adhesion molecules, and stromal partners that the disseminated cells need.

The modern molecular updating of the seed-and-soil idea includes the concept of the **pre-metastatic niche**: primary tumors secrete factors and release exosomes that prime distant tissues to receive metastatic cells, before any cells have actually arrived. Bone marrow-derived cells are mobilized to those sites, fibronectin is deposited, the local microenvironment is modified — all to make the destination tissue more hospitable to incoming cancer cells. Cancer reaches out into the body and prepares its own landing strips.

The therapeutic implications of all this are sobering. Metastasis, once established, is the principal driver of cancer mortality, and we have very few therapies that specifically target the metastatic process. Most of what we call cancer therapy targets proliferation, not dissemination. There's active work on inhibiting EMT, on disrupting the pre-metastatic niche, on targeting **circulating tumor cells** before they colonize — but the field is younger and less developed than the proliferation-targeted side. If you wanted to know where the next great wave of cancer therapy is likely to come from, the metastatic process is one good place to look.

## SEGMENT 9 — The tumor microenvironment

For most of the twentieth century, cancer biologists looked at a tumor and saw cancer cells. The other cells in the tumor — the fibroblasts, the immune cells, the endothelial cells — were considered passive bystanders, scaffolding for the malignant population. That view has been wrong for at least two decades, and the contemporary picture is dramatically different. A tumor is not just cancer cells. It is an **ecosystem** — a complex community of malignant and non-malignant cells, embedded in a remodeled extracellular matrix, perfused by abnormal vasculature, often hypoxic, often acidic, and intimately shaped by — and shaping — the host's immune system. This is the **tumor microenvironment**, the TME, and it is now understood to be a central determinant of tumor behavior and therapy response.

Let's enumerate the major cellular components.

**Cancer-associated fibroblasts**, **CAFs**. The fibroblasts in a tumor's stroma are not normal resting fibroblasts. They are activated, contractile, and metabolically reprogrammed — sometimes called myofibroblasts. They secrete an enormous quantity of extracellular matrix proteins, especially collagen, contributing to the **desmoplastic** reaction that gives pancreatic cancers, for instance, their characteristic stony, fibrous texture. CAFs also secrete growth factors and cytokines that support tumor growth, and they remodel the matrix in ways that facilitate invasion. In some tumor types, CAFs are a substantial fraction of the tumor mass and a major driver of treatment resistance — drugs have to penetrate through the fibrotic stroma to reach the cancer cells, and in pancreatic adenocarcinoma the stroma is so dense that drug delivery is a fundamental challenge.

**Tumor-associated macrophages**, **TAMs**. Macrophages infiltrate most solid tumors in large numbers. Naive thinking says: good, the immune system is showing up. But the macrophages that arrive at a tumor are often **polarized** by tumor-derived signals into a phenotype called **M2** or **alternatively activated** — anti-inflammatory, pro-angiogenic, pro-invasive, immunosuppressive. M2 macrophages support, rather than oppose, the tumor. They suppress T cell responses, secrete growth factors, contribute to angiogenesis, and facilitate metastatic spread. High TAM infiltration correlates with worse prognosis in many cancer types. There's active work on reprogramming TAMs from the M2 to the **M1** (classically activated, pro-inflammatory, tumoricidal) phenotype, or simply on depleting them — for instance, with small-molecule inhibitors of **CSF1R**, the receptor that TAMs depend on for survival.

**Myeloid-derived suppressor cells**, **MDSCs**, and **regulatory T cells**, **Tregs**, are two additional immunosuppressive populations that accumulate in tumors. Both actively suppress the cytotoxic T cells that would otherwise attack the tumor. The tumor, in other words, recruits its own internal security force to fend off the immune response.

**Hypoxia**. Because tumor vasculature is abnormal and flow is chaotic, large regions of solid tumors are chronically or intermittently hypoxic. Hypoxia drives a transcriptional response mediated by **HIF-1-alpha**, hypoxia-inducible factor, which is normally degraded under normoxia but stabilizes when oxygen falls. HIF-1-alpha turns on genes for angiogenesis (VEGF), glycolysis (the Warburg adaptation), and a host of survival programs. Hypoxic tumor cells are more aggressive, more invasive, and frustratingly, more resistant to radiation and many chemotherapies — because radiation kills via reactive oxygen species, and you need oxygen to make those.

**Acidity**. Hypoxic and glycolytic tumors export lactic acid, lowering the extracellular pH of the tumor to as low as 6.5. The acidic microenvironment suppresses immune cell function and selects for cancer cells adapted to acid stress.

**The extracellular matrix** itself is remodeled — stiffened, cross-linked, reorganized by enzymes like lysyl oxidase and matrix metalloproteinases. The mechanical properties of the matrix feed back into cancer cell behavior via mechanotransduction pathways like YAP/TAZ.

\[Pause.\]

The take-home is that a tumor is an ecosystem, and the ecosystem matters. It matters because tumor behavior is shaped by it; it matters because therapy response is shaped by it; and it matters because the microenvironment itself is a therapeutic target. Anti-angiogenics target the vasculature. Hypoxia-activated prodrugs are being developed to selectively kill hypoxic cells. CSF1R inhibitors target TAMs. CAF-targeting agents are in trials. And most importantly — looking ahead to segment 12 — checkpoint inhibitors target the immunosuppressive features of the TME, freeing T cells from the suppression that the tumor has imposed.

That brings us to a natural pause point. We've built up the biology — what cancer is, the hallmarks, the genes, the cell cycle, the apoptosis machinery, the microenvironment. Let's take a break here. When we come back, we'll move into the therapeutic and clinical half of the course: genomics, targeted therapy, immunotherapy, screening and prevention, classical pillars, resistance, liquid biopsy, AI, and the frontier. Stretch, get water, and I'll see you in a few minutes.

## SEGMENT 10 — Cancer genomics

Welcome back. We're going to spend this segment on **cancer genomics**, because the way oncology is practiced today has been fundamentally reshaped by our ability to sequence tumors. The old paradigm was: classify a cancer by its tissue of origin — breast, lung, colon — and treat based on that classification. The new paradigm is: classify a cancer by its molecular drivers, which may cut across tissues of origin, and treat based on what's actually broken. Genomics made that pivot possible.

The watershed project was **The Cancer Genome Atlas**, **TCGA**, a US-led international consortium launched in 2006 that systematically sequenced and characterized over 11,000 primary tumors across 33 cancer types. TCGA produced an enormous public catalog of mutations, copy-number changes, gene-expression patterns, methylation profiles, and clinical outcomes. The international counterpart, the **International Cancer Genome Consortium**, **ICGC**, extended the catalog with tumors collected across many additional countries. Together they constitute the empirical foundation of modern cancer genomics.

A few generalizations that fell out of these large-scale sequencing efforts. First, **mutation burden varies enormously across cancer types**. At the high end, melanomas and lung cancers in smokers carry tens to hundreds of mutations per megabase of DNA — driven by ultraviolet light and tobacco carcinogens respectively. At the low end, pediatric cancers — leukemias, neuroblastomas, medulloblastomas — often carry only a handful of mutations. Adult solid tumors of internal organs that are not chronically exposed to mutagens sit in the middle. This range matters therapeutically, because high-mutation-burden tumors generate many neoantigens and tend to respond better to immunotherapy.

Second, **most mutations are passengers**. Even in a heavily mutated melanoma, only a small fraction of mutations are doing the driving. Distinguishing drivers from passengers is a continuing challenge in cancer genomics, and statistical approaches that look for genes mutated more often than expected by chance — **MutSig**, **OncoDriveCLUST**, and others — have generated curated catalogs of likely driver genes, of which there are perhaps 500 to 700 in human cancer.

Third, **the same gene can be a driver in many cancer types, but the spectrum of drivers per tumor type is distinct**. TP53 is mutated in almost everything. KRAS is heavily mutated in pancreas, colon, and lung. APC dominates colorectal cancer. PTEN dominates endometrial. Some tumor types have one or two clear dominant drivers; others have long-tailed distributions of many low-frequency drivers.

\[Pause.\]

Now I want to introduce a deeper concept from cancer genomics: **mutational signatures**. The idea, pioneered by Mike Stratton and his colleagues at the Sanger Institute and now catalogued in the **COSMIC** database of somatic mutations, is this. Different mutational processes — different carcinogens, different DNA repair defects — leave characteristically different fingerprints on the genome. Tobacco smoke causes mostly C-to-A transversions in particular sequence contexts. Ultraviolet light causes mostly C-to-T transitions at dipyrimidines. Defects in mismatch repair generate microsatellite instability and a specific spectrum of mutations. Defects in homologous recombination — BRCA1/2 loss — produce a signature dominated by certain insertion/deletion patterns and large structural rearrangements. APOBEC editing, an antiviral enzyme that goes off-target in cancer, leaves yet another fingerprint.

By decomposing a tumor's mutation spectrum, you can read out the carcinogenic exposures and DNA repair defects that contributed to its formation — sometimes literally pointing to which mutagens caused the cancer. This is forensics on the genome. The current COSMIC catalog contains dozens of signatures, most with assigned etiologies. Clinically, it has meant we can sometimes identify treatment-relevant repair defects — for instance, a "BRCAness" signature in a tumor without an obvious BRCA mutation, suggesting the tumor may still benefit from PARP inhibition.

Two genomic features have become routine clinical biomarkers, both because they predict response to immune checkpoint inhibitors. The first is **tumor mutational burden**, **TMB** — simply the count of mutations per megabase. High TMB tumors generate many altered peptides that can be presented as neoantigens on MHC and recognized by T cells; they tend to respond better to immune checkpoint blockade. The FDA has approved pembrolizumab for any solid tumor with TMB above 10 mutations per megabase — a **tissue-agnostic** indication, granted regardless of where the cancer started, based purely on the molecular feature.

The second is **microsatellite instability**, **MSI**. Microsatellites are short, repetitive DNA sequences scattered throughout the genome — runs of As, repeats of CA dinucleotides, that sort of thing. They are intrinsically prone to slippage during DNA replication, and the mismatch repair system normally fixes the slips. When mismatch repair is broken — through inherited mutations in MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 (Lynch syndrome) or through epigenetic silencing (typically by MLH1 promoter methylation) — microsatellites accumulate length changes throughout the genome, and the tumor is said to be **MSI-high**. MSI-high tumors carry an enormous mutation burden, generate many neoantigens, and respond exceptionally well to checkpoint inhibitors. Pembrolizumab was first approved tissue-agnostically for MSI-high solid tumors in 2017 — the first such approval in oncology history.

The general lesson of cancer genomics is that molecular features can supersede tissue of origin as the determinant of treatment. We are still in the early years of this transition, but tissue-agnostic approvals are accumulating, and the future of cancer drug labeling is likely to look much more like "treat any cancer with this molecular feature" than "treat this specific organ's cancer."

## SEGMENT 11 — Targeted therapy

So now let's talk about how all this genomic knowledge becomes drugs. **Targeted therapy** is the umbrella term for cancer drugs that engage a specific molecular driver of a specific tumor's biology, as opposed to classical cytotoxic chemotherapy, which attacks proliferating cells indiscriminately. The story of targeted therapy is one of the most dramatic in modern medicine, and it begins with a single drug.

**Imatinib** (Gleevec), approved in 2001 for chronic myeloid leukemia. This is the founding success of the field, and the story is worth telling. We met BCR-ABL in segment 4 — the fusion tyrosine kinase produced by the Philadelphia chromosome translocation that drives CML. By the 1990s, the biology was clear: every CML cell carried BCR-ABL, every CML cell depended on its kinase activity, and the kinase looked drug-able. A small group at Ciba-Geigy (later Novartis), led by Brian Druker and Nick Lydon, set out to find a small molecule that would specifically inhibit the ABL kinase. They screened compounds, optimized hits, and identified a molecule originally called STI571, later named imatinib.

What happened in the early clinical trials of imatinib was unlike anything oncology had seen. Patients with chronic-phase CML — who had been facing a slow, inexorable disease that progressed to fatal blast crisis within 3-5 years — went into deep, lasting hematological remission on a single oral pill a day, with side effects that were modest by comparison with anything else in oncology. The five-year survival rate for chronic-phase CML on imatinib is now over 90%, and patients on continuous imatinib have a life expectancy approaching the general population. CML went, in a few years, from a fatal hematological cancer to a chronic, manageable disease controlled with a once-daily pill.

The conceptual lesson was enormous. It validated the entire targeted-therapy hypothesis: identify the molecular driver, design a drug against it, and you can achieve transformative responses with limited toxicity. Imatinib also revealed the **principle of oncogene addiction** — that cancer cells driven by a particular oncogene become so dependent on it that inhibiting it collapses them. And of course imatinib also revealed the **inevitability of resistance**: a small fraction of patients develop new BCR-ABL mutations, particularly the **T315I gatekeeper mutation**, that block imatinib binding, requiring second-generation TKIs like **dasatinib** and **nilotinib**, and ultimately **ponatinib** for the T315I-resistant cases.

\[Pause.\]

After imatinib, the floodgates opened. Let me run through the major categories of targeted therapy you should know.

**EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors** in non-small-cell lung cancer. Gefitinib and erlotinib, the first generation. **Afatinib**, an irreversible second-generation drug. **Osimertinib**, the third-generation drug designed to overcome the T790M resistance mutation while sparing wild-type EGFR — a far better-tolerated and more effective drug than its predecessors, now used as first-line therapy in EGFR-mutant lung cancer. The decision to use osimertinib is driven entirely by molecular testing: if the tumor has an activating EGFR mutation, osimertinib transforms the natural history of the disease; if it doesn't, the drug is essentially useless.

**HER2-directed therapy** in breast cancer and other HER2-amplified tumors. Trastuzumab, the monoclonal antibody, was the first. **Pertuzumab** binds a different HER2 epitope and is used in combination with trastuzumab. **Lapatinib**, **neratinib**, and **tucatinib** are small-molecule kinase inhibitors of HER2. And the field has been transformed by **antibody-drug conjugates**, **ADCs** — most notably **trastuzumab emtansine** (T-DM1) and **trastuzumab deruxtecan** (T-DXd, Enhertu). These are trastuzumab molecules with a cytotoxic payload covalently attached via a cleavable linker. The antibody delivers the drug specifically to HER2-expressing cancer cells, where the payload is released intracellularly, killing the cell while sparing HER2-negative tissue. T-DXd has been particularly transformative — so potent that it has activity even in tumors with low-level HER2 expression, leading to the new category of "HER2-low" breast cancer. ADCs are a major growth area in oncology.

**BRAF inhibitors** in melanoma. About half of cutaneous melanomas carry an activating mutation in BRAF, most commonly **V600E**, that locks the BRAF kinase in an active state and drives constitutive MAPK signaling. **Vemurafenib**, the first BRAF inhibitor, produced rapid and dramatic responses in patients with metastatic BRAF-mutant melanoma — patients with bulky disease would have visible regression within days. Resistance emerged quickly, often through reactivation of MAPK signaling downstream of BRAF. The solution was combination therapy with MEK inhibitors — **dabrafenib + trametinib**, **vemurafenib + cobimetinib**, **encorafenib + binimetinib** — which delay resistance and improve responses substantially. The BRAF story is now extending into other BRAF-mutant cancers, including colorectal cancer (where BRAF-mutant disease has historically had very poor prognosis but responds to BRAF + MEK + EGFR triplet therapy) and the rare condition of erdheim-Chester disease.

**ALK inhibitors** in non-small-cell lung cancer. A small fraction (4-7%) of lung adenocarcinomas carry rearrangements that fuse ALK (anaplastic lymphoma kinase) with EML4 or other partners, producing a constitutively active ALK fusion kinase. **Crizotinib** was the first ALK inhibitor; **alectinib**, **brigatinib**, **lorlatinib** are later-generation drugs with better CNS penetration (important because ALK-rearranged lung cancers have a particular propensity to brain metastasize) and improved resistance profiles. ALK-rearranged lung cancer is another paradigm of how molecular subtyping transformed treatment.

**KRAS-G12C inhibitors**, the ones we mentioned in segment 4. Sotorasib and adagrasib were the first drugs to ever specifically target KRAS, opening up therapeutic options in a substantial subset of lung adenocarcinomas. They are a triumph but also a chastening lesson: response rates and response durations are modest by comparison with EGFR or ALK inhibitors, partly because KRAS-driven tumors are more heterogeneous and resistance pathways are diverse.

Several other notable targeted classes you'll want to know: **FLT3 inhibitors** (midostaurin, gilteritinib) in FLT3-mutant AML; **IDH1/IDH2 inhibitors** (ivosidenib, enasidenib) in IDH-mutant AML, cholangiocarcinoma, and glioma; **BTK inhibitors** (ibrutinib, acalabrutinib, zanubrutinib) in CLL, mantle cell lymphoma, and Waldenström macroglobulinemia; **FGFR inhibitors** in FGFR-altered cancers; **NTRK inhibitors** (larotrectinib, entrectinib) in NTRK-fusion-positive tumors of any tissue origin — another tissue-agnostic approval, by virtue of the fusion being a robust driver wherever it occurs.

The unifying lesson: targeted therapy works when the cancer is driven by a specific, druggable molecular lesion and is genuinely **addicted** to that lesion. When those conditions are met, the responses can be transformative. When they aren't — when the cancer has many drivers, when the target is undruggable, or when alternative survival pathways are quickly engaged — the wins are more modest. Modern oncology trials almost always include molecular profiling at study entry to identify the patients most likely to benefit from a given targeted drug. This is the **precision oncology** paradigm.

## SEGMENT 12 — Cancer immunotherapy

Now we turn to what is, in my judgment, the single most important development in cancer therapeutics of the past fifteen years. **Cancer immunotherapy**. The idea is old — the late-nineteenth-century surgeon William Coley used to inject bacterial toxins into tumors hoping to provoke an immune response, and he got occasional dramatic remissions — but the modern era of immunotherapy that has reshaped clinical oncology began only in the 2010s, with two technologies that emerged in parallel: **checkpoint inhibitors** and **CAR-T cells**.

Let's do the science first. Your immune system is constantly surveying for abnormal cells. T cells, in particular, can recognize and kill cells displaying abnormal peptides on their MHC class I molecules — for instance, peptides derived from mutated cancer proteins, called **neoantigens**. This surveillance is real; it removes early transformed cells routinely, and individuals with impaired T cell immunity have elevated cancer rates. So why do cancers ever escape? Because they evolve. The hallmark of avoiding immune destruction is acquired through a number of mechanisms — loss of MHC class I expression, secretion of immunosuppressive cytokines, recruitment of Tregs and MDSCs into the TME — but one mechanism turned out to be particularly common and particularly drug-able. Tumors exploit the natural **immune checkpoints** that normally prevent T cells from over-reacting to self-antigens.

Two checkpoints have dominated the field. **CTLA-4** is a receptor expressed on T cells that competes with the co-stimulatory receptor **CD28** for the same ligands (CD80 and CD86) on antigen-presenting cells. CTLA-4 acts as a brake during T cell priming in the lymph node. When CTLA-4 wins the competition, T cell activation is dampened. **Anti-CTLA-4 antibodies** — **ipilimumab** is the canonical example — block CTLA-4 from engaging its ligands, releasing the brake on T cell priming.

The second checkpoint is **PD-1** (programmed death-1), expressed on activated T cells, and its ligands **PD-L1** and **PD-L2**, often expressed on cancer cells (and on antigen-presenting cells). When PD-1 engages PD-L1, the T cell receives a strong inhibitory signal that suppresses its effector function. Tumors that upregulate PD-L1 are essentially turning on a "don't kill me" signal that disarms the infiltrating T cells. Blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 axis — with **anti-PD-1 antibodies** like **pembrolizumab** (Keytruda) and **nivolumab** (Opdivo), or with **anti-PD-L1 antibodies** like **atezolizumab**, **durvalumab**, and **avelumab** — releases T cells from this suppression and allows them to attack the tumor.

The clinical impact of checkpoint blockade has been the single most dramatic transformation in modern oncology. Metastatic melanoma — a disease with a median survival of about nine months in the early 2000s — now has long-term durable remissions in a substantial fraction of patients on ipilimumab plus nivolumab. Patients are alive five, eight, ten years out, off therapy, with no detectable disease. Pembrolizumab is now approved in over 30 cancer indications. Checkpoint inhibitors are first-line therapy for advanced non-small-cell lung cancer, renal cell carcinoma, urothelial carcinoma, head-and-neck cancer, Hodgkin lymphoma, MSI-high tumors of any origin, and on and on. James Allison and Tasuku Honjo shared the 2018 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for the discovery of CTLA-4 and PD-1, respectively, as therapeutic targets.

The toxicities are characteristic — and instructive. Because checkpoint blockade releases the brakes on T cells generally, patients can develop **immune-related adverse events**, **irAEs**: autoimmune-like inflammation in any organ. Colitis. Hepatitis. Pneumonitis. Hypophysitis and other endocrinopathies. Dermatitis. Severe irAEs require interruption of the immunotherapy and treatment with high-dose steroids or other immunosuppression. The therapeutic window is the gap between unleashing useful anti-tumor immunity and unleashing useless autoimmunity, and patient selection and management are an art.

\[Pause.\]

The second big immunotherapy story is **CAR-T cells** — **chimeric antigen receptor T cells**. The concept: take a patient's own T cells out of their body, genetically engineer them to express a synthetic receptor that recognizes a specific antigen on cancer cells, expand the engineered cells to enormous numbers in vitro, and infuse them back. The synthetic receptor — the chimeric antigen receptor — is built by fusing the antigen-binding domain of an antibody (giving specificity for any extracellular target you choose) to the intracellular signaling domains of the T cell receptor complex plus a co-stimulatory domain. When the CAR-T cell encounters its target antigen, it is triggered to kill — without needing MHC presentation.

The first home-run target was **CD19**, a B-cell-lineage marker expressed on essentially all B-cell malignancies and on normal B cells. CD19-directed CAR-T cells — **tisagenlecleucel** (Kymriah), **axicabtagene ciloleucel** (Yescarta), **brexucabtagene autoleucel** (Tecartus), **lisocabtagene maraleucel** (Breyanzi) — have produced remarkable responses in relapsed/refractory B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia, large B-cell lymphoma, and mantle cell lymphoma. Children with B-ALL who had failed multiple lines of therapy and had no other options have achieved durable, sometimes curative remissions on CD19 CAR-T. The toxicity profile is distinctive: **cytokine release syndrome**, **CRS**, an inflammatory storm driven by massive cytokine release from activated T cells, can be life-threatening but is now managed with the IL-6-receptor blocker **tocilizumab** and supportive care; and **immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome**, **ICANS**, a poorly understood neurologic toxicity that ranges from mild confusion to severe encephalopathy.

A second successful target has been **BCMA**, B-cell maturation antigen, expressed on plasma cells. BCMA-directed CAR-Ts — **idecabtagene vicleucel** (Abecma) and **ciltacabtagene autoleucel** (Carvykti) — have transformed treatment of relapsed multiple myeloma.

The pattern from CAR-T so far is that the technology has been spectacularly successful in **liquid tumors** — leukemias, lymphomas, myeloma — and considerably less successful in solid tumors, where access to the tumor, the immunosuppressive microenvironment, target heterogeneity, and on-target/off-tumor toxicity have all been obstacles. A great deal of current effort is going into solid-tumor CAR-T design — armored CARs, switchable CARs, CARs targeting tumor-specific neoantigens — but the home runs so far have been in B-cell and plasma-cell malignancies.

A third immunotherapy modality worth knowing is **bispecific T-cell engagers**, **BiTEs** and related formats. These are antibody-derived molecules with two binding domains: one that grabs a tumor antigen, and one that grabs CD3 on a T cell, physically forcing the T cell into contact with the tumor cell and triggering T cell activation. **Blinatumomab** is a CD19 x CD3 BiTE used in B-ALL. **Teclistamab** and **elranatamab** are BCMA-directed bispecifics in multiple myeloma. **Tarlatamab** is a DLL3 x CD3 bispecific in small-cell lung cancer. Bispecifics achieve some of the same goals as CAR-T but as off-the-shelf drugs, without requiring patient-specific cell manufacturing.

The overarching message of segment 12: immunotherapy has shifted oncology's center of gravity. We are no longer simply trying to poison cancer cells faster than we poison patients. We are now harnessing the patient's own immune system as the therapeutic agent. For tumors that respond, the responses can be durable in a way that classical cytotoxic therapy almost never achieves — true long-term remissions, sometimes apparent cures. The challenge now is extending the fraction of patients who respond, and bringing immunotherapy to the cancer types that have so far been refractory.

## SEGMENT 13 — Epidemiology, screening, and prevention

Step back from the molecules for a segment. Cancer is also a public health problem, and the public-health levers — prevention and screening — have arguably done more to reduce cancer mortality in the past fifty years than any chemotherapeutic advance. Let me lay out the broad picture.

Globally, cancer is the second-leading cause of death, behind cardiovascular disease, and it accounts for roughly ten million deaths per year. In high-income countries, the absolute incidence is high because the population is aging — cancer is overwhelmingly a disease of accumulated mutations, and the longer you live, the more time you have to accumulate them. Age is by a large margin the dominant cancer risk factor.

But beyond age, a relatively small list of modifiable factors accounts for a very large share of cancer mortality. Tobacco use is at the top of the list. Smoking causes lung cancer, of course — the link was established by Doll and Hill in the 1950s — but it also causes cancers of the larynx, oral cavity, pharynx, esophagus, bladder, kidney, pancreas, cervix, and acute myeloid leukemia. Smoking is responsible, depending on the population, for somewhere between a quarter and a third of all cancer deaths. The single most cost-effective cancer intervention in the world is helping people not smoke. **Tobacco control** — taxation, advertising restrictions, indoor-air policies, smoking cessation programs — has done more for cancer mortality in the developed world than any oncology drug.

Other major modifiable risk factors: alcohol (oral, pharyngeal, esophageal, liver, colorectal, breast). Obesity and physical inactivity (colorectal, postmenopausal breast, endometrial, kidney, esophageal adenocarcinoma, and others). Diet (red and processed meat, low fiber — colorectal). Sun exposure (skin cancers, especially melanoma). Occupational and environmental carcinogens — asbestos, radon, certain industrial chemicals. And specific infections, which are now understood to contribute meaningfully to global cancer mortality — perhaps 15-20% globally, much higher in low-income countries. Hepatitis B and C cause hepatocellular carcinoma. Helicobacter pylori causes gastric adenocarcinoma and gastric MALT lymphoma. HPV causes cervical cancer and a growing share of head-and-neck and anal cancers. EBV is linked to nasopharyngeal carcinoma and some lymphomas. HIV — directly and through the immunosuppression it causes — is linked to Kaposi sarcoma and several lymphomas. HTLV-1, as we mentioned in the retrovirus lecture, causes adult T-cell leukemia.

That infectious-cancer list creates the second great preventive opportunity: **vaccination**. The **HPV vaccine**, introduced in the mid-2000s, targets the high-risk HPV types responsible for cervical and other cancers. Population-level data from countries that introduced universal HPV vaccination programs early — Australia is the classic example — now show dramatic reductions in HPV infection prevalence, in precancerous cervical lesions, and increasingly in invasive cervical cancer. Cervical cancer, one of the leading causes of female cancer death globally, may genuinely be on track to be eliminated as a public health problem in vaccinated populations. The **hepatitis B vaccine**, introduced earlier, has similarly reduced hepatocellular carcinoma incidence in vaccinated cohorts. Cancer vaccines, in this preventive sense, may be the most underappreciated cancer triumph of the past generation.

\[Pause.\]

The other public-health lever is **screening** — detecting cancers early, at a stage where they can be cured. The logic of screening is straightforward: a cancer that is small, localized, and not yet metastatic can often be cured by local therapy (surgery, sometimes radiation); a cancer that has spread is much harder to cure. Screening tests aim to catch cancers during the period before they become symptomatic, in the early curable window.

Screening only makes sense for cancers that meet certain criteria: the cancer must be common enough that screening a population is worthwhile; there must exist a meaningful curative treatment for early disease; the screening test must be reasonably sensitive and specific; and the lead time from screen-detectable disease to clinically apparent disease must be long enough that early detection actually changes outcomes. Not all cancers meet those criteria. For those that do, screening can be transformative.

The major screening programs in high-income countries:

**Colorectal cancer screening** with colonoscopy, sigmoidoscopy, or stool-based tests (fecal occult blood, FIT, multitarget stool DNA). Colonoscopy is the gold standard because it both detects cancers and allows the removal of premalignant polyps — adenomas — at the same time, interrupting the adenoma-to-carcinoma sequence we discussed in segment 5. The data are robust: colonoscopy-based screening substantially reduces both colorectal cancer incidence and mortality.

**Mammography for breast cancer**. Mammography in women aged 50-74 (the exact age range varies by country) reduces breast cancer mortality, though by a more modest amount than was once claimed, and the benefit-to-harm balance — including the harms of false positives, overdiagnosis, and overtreatment of slow-growing tumors that would never have caused harm — is the subject of continuing debate. MRI screening is added for high-risk women, particularly BRCA mutation carriers.

**Cervical cancer screening** with Pap smear, increasingly supplemented or replaced by HPV testing. Cervical cancer is a paradigmatic screening success — the introduction of Pap screening in the mid-twentieth century reduced cervical cancer mortality in screened populations by 70-80%, by detecting and treating precancerous lesions. The combination of HPV vaccination plus screening is on track to eliminate cervical cancer in many countries.

**Lung cancer screening** with low-dose CT in heavy smokers (and former heavy smokers). A randomized trial called the National Lung Screening Trial demonstrated a reduction in lung cancer mortality, and low-dose CT screening is now recommended in eligible high-risk populations. Uptake has been slow but is increasing.

Several other cancers have screening programs in some populations: **prostate cancer** with PSA (controversial due to overdiagnosis), **hepatocellular carcinoma** in high-risk patients with cirrhosis, **gastric cancer** in high-incidence countries like Japan and Korea, **skin cancer** with periodic skin examinations.

The general lesson: prevention through risk-factor modification (tobacco, vaccination, weight) and early detection through screening have collectively had an enormous impact on cancer mortality. They are unglamorous compared to the latest checkpoint inhibitor or CAR-T, but they save more lives. As you go into clinical practice, never let the molecular sophistication of oncology distract you from the boring, powerful interventions: ask about smoking, vaccinate the kids, schedule the colonoscopy.

## SEGMENT 14 — The classical pillars: surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy

Before targeted therapy and immunotherapy, there were three pillars of cancer treatment. And despite all the molecular sophistication of the modern era, those three pillars — surgery, radiotherapy, and cytotoxic chemotherapy — remain the backbone of clinical oncology. Most cancers cured today are cured with one or more of these classical modalities, often in combination, sometimes augmented by targeted or immune therapy but not replaced by them. Let me walk you through each.

**Surgery** is the oldest cancer treatment and remains the single most effective curative modality for solid tumors that are detected before they have metastasized. If a cancer is local — confined to its tissue of origin and to nearby lymph nodes — physically removing it can be curative. Early-stage breast cancer, colorectal cancer, lung cancer, prostate cancer, kidney cancer, melanoma, and many others are routinely cured by surgical resection. The development of surgical oncology has been driven by two related themes: figuring out how much tissue actually needs to be removed (the long arc from radical mastectomy to breast-conserving surgery, for instance), and figuring out how to remove tumors with less morbidity (laparoscopic and robotic approaches, minimally invasive techniques). The principle: get the cancer out, with negative margins, with attention to draining lymph nodes, and you may be done. Surgery cannot reach micrometastatic disease that has already disseminated, which is why surgery is so often combined with adjuvant systemic therapy.

**Radiotherapy** uses ionizing radiation — high-energy photons (x-rays, gamma rays), increasingly also protons and other charged particles — to damage DNA in cancer cells and trigger their death. Radiation works by ionizing molecules in the cell, generating reactive oxygen species, and causing double-strand DNA breaks. Cancer cells, which are typically proliferating and often have compromised DNA repair, are more sensitive to radiation than most normal tissues — though some normal tissues (bone marrow, mucosa, the small bowel) are also exquisitely radiosensitive. The therapeutic window in radiation oncology is achieved largely through **fractionation** (dividing the total dose into many small daily fractions, allowing normal tissue to repair between fractions while gradually accumulating damage in the tumor) and **conformal delivery** (targeting the beam to the tumor while sparing surrounding tissue, increasingly using technologies like intensity-modulated radiation therapy, stereotactic body radiation, and proton therapy).

Radiation is used as definitive curative therapy for some cancers — head and neck cancers, cervical cancer, prostate cancer, some lymphomas. It is used as adjuvant therapy after surgery for many others — breast cancer after lumpectomy, rectal cancer, some sarcomas. It is used as palliative therapy to shrink tumors that are causing symptoms — relieving pain from bone metastases, decompressing the spinal cord, controlling bleeding. Radiation also has a place in combination with immunotherapy in the modern era — there is provocative evidence that radiation can sometimes generate an immune response against the tumor that synergizes with checkpoint blockade, the so-called **abscopal effect**.

**Cytotoxic chemotherapy** is the third pillar, and the one most associated in the public mind with the unpleasant aspects of cancer treatment. Cytotoxic drugs attack proliferating cells, with limited selectivity for cancer cells over normal proliferating cells, which is why they cause myelosuppression, mucositis, hair loss, and nausea. Despite their lack of molecular specificity, cytotoxic drugs cure cancers — they cured testicular cancer in young men starting in the 1970s with cisplatin-based regimens, they cure most childhood acute lymphoblastic leukemia, they cure Hodgkin lymphoma and aggressive B-cell lymphomas, they substantially reduce relapse risk and improve survival in many adjuvant settings.

You should know the major classes by mechanism. **Alkylating agents** — cyclophosphamide, ifosfamide, melphalan, busulfan, temozolomide, the platinum drugs cisplatin, carboplatin, and oxaliplatin — covalently modify DNA, generating adducts and crosslinks that block replication. **Antimetabolites** — methotrexate (a folate analog), 5-fluorouracil and capecitabine (pyrimidine analogs), gemcitabine, cytarabine, the purine analogs 6-mercaptopurine, fludarabine, and clofarabine — mimic normal metabolites and disrupt nucleic acid synthesis. **Topoisomerase inhibitors** — doxorubicin and the other anthracyclines (topo II), etoposide (topo II), irinotecan and topotecan (topo I) — trap the topoisomerase enzymes mid-cleavage, generating lethal DNA strand breaks. **Microtubule poisons** — vincristine, vinblastine, paclitaxel, docetaxel — disrupt the mitotic spindle and arrest cells in mitosis, ultimately triggering apoptosis. **Bleomycin** causes oxidative DNA damage.

These drugs are typically given in combination, on schedules carefully optimized to maximize tumor kill while allowing normal tissue recovery. Names like **R-CHOP** (rituximab, cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, vincristine, prednisone — for diffuse large B-cell lymphoma), **FOLFIRINOX** (5-FU, leucovorin, irinotecan, oxaliplatin — for pancreatic and colorectal cancer), **BEP** (bleomycin, etoposide, cisplatin — for testicular cancer), and **ABVD** (doxorubicin, bleomycin, vinblastine, dacarbazine — for Hodgkin lymphoma) are the workhorse regimens of modern oncology.

The take-home for this segment: cytotoxic chemotherapy is not going away. It is unglamorous, often unpleasant, and limited in its ability to discriminate cancer from normal. But for many cancers — especially the hematological cancers and pediatric malignancies — it cures patients, and we have nothing better. The newer modalities augment chemotherapy in most settings; they do not yet replace it. A modern oncologist must be expert in all four — surgery, radiation, chemo, and the new biologics — and must know how to combine them.

## SEGMENT 15 — Therapy resistance

If targeted therapies are so effective, why do patients eventually relapse? The answer, in a word, is **resistance**. And resistance, in turn, is just clonal evolution playing out under the selection pressure of drug. This segment is about how cancers acquire resistance and what we do about it.

Let's set up the conceptual picture. A treatment-naive tumor of a hundred billion cells is not genetically homogeneous. Over its lifetime it has accumulated genetic and epigenetic diversity — subclones with distinct mutational profiles, some of which carry, by chance, variants that are partially or fully resistant to the drug you're about to give. Most of those resistant subclones are rare in the population; the sensitive bulk dominates. You give the drug. The sensitive cells die. The resistant subclones, formerly outcompeted, now have the field to themselves and expand. Within months to years, the tumor is composed largely of the previously rare resistant clone, and the drug no longer works. That is **clonal selection**, and it is the engine of acquired resistance.

We distinguish **acquired resistance** — emerging during treatment as just described — from **intrinsic resistance**, which is present from the outset, and which is why some patients never respond at all to a drug they ought to have responded to.

The canonical molecular example of acquired resistance is the **T790M** mutation in EGFR. Patients with EGFR-mutant lung cancer treated with first-generation EGFR TKIs like gefitinib and erlotinib initially respond dramatically. But over a median of about 9-14 months, most progress. When you re-biopsy the tumor and sequence it, about 50-60% of those resistant tumors have acquired the T790M mutation — a substitution at the "gatekeeper" residue of the EGFR kinase domain that sterically hinders the binding of first-generation drugs while preserving the kinase's catalytic activity. T790M is a beautiful example of how a single point mutation can produce drug resistance.

The therapeutic response to T790M is **osimertinib**, the third-generation EGFR TKI we mentioned in segment 11. Osimertinib was designed specifically to bind EGFR irreversibly (via a covalent attachment to a particular cysteine) and to retain affinity for the T790M mutant. It works in T790M-resistant disease. Of course, osimertinib resistance then emerges — through C797S mutations that disrupt the cysteine osimertinib attaches to, or through bypass mechanisms like MET amplification, or through histologic transformation to small-cell lung cancer. The arms race continues.

Resistance mechanisms fall into several categories. **On-target** resistance modifies the drug target itself — T790M in EGFR, T315I in BCR-ABL, gatekeeper mutations across many kinase inhibitors. **Bypass** resistance activates a parallel signaling pathway that compensates for the inhibited one — MET amplification bypassing EGFR inhibition, NRAS activation bypassing BRAF inhibition. **Lineage plasticity** resistance occurs when the cancer cell changes its phenotype to one that doesn't depend on the drug target — prostate cancer transitioning to a neuroendocrine phenotype that no longer needs the androgen receptor, EGFR-mutant lung adenocarcinoma transitioning to small-cell lung cancer. **Pharmacological** resistance involves drug efflux pumps or metabolic inactivation. **Microenvironmental** resistance involves the tumor adopting a state — quiescence, sequestration in a sanctuary site like the brain or the bone marrow niche — in which the drug doesn't reach it effectively.

\[Pause.\]

A more recent and clinically relevant idea is **drug holidays** — and the related strategy of **adaptive therapy**. The reasoning is from evolutionary biology. If you treat a tumor at maximum tolerated dose continuously, you create the maximum selection pressure for resistance, and the resistant clone eventually takes over. If you treat intermittently, allowing periods of drug-free recovery, the sensitive bulk of the tumor can re-expand, outcompeting the resistant clones and keeping them suppressed. Adaptive therapy, pioneered by Robert Gatenby's group, attempts to modulate drug dosing based on tumor response — backing off when the tumor shrinks, intensifying when it grows — to maintain the tumor at a stable size for longer, even if not to eradicate it. This is being studied in prostate cancer with some encouraging early data. It's a fundamental reframing of the goal: not "cure," but stable, durable control via evolutionary management.

The deeper lesson: in oncology, you cannot defeat evolution by ignoring it. Every drug we give applies a selection pressure, and the cancer evolves under that pressure. The strategies that work in the long term either pick targets where resistance is hard to evolve (this is rare), combine multiple drugs to make resistance require multiple simultaneous mutations (the success of combination antiretroviral therapy translated into oncology), or actively manage the evolutionary dynamics. Understanding resistance is not optional; it is the central problem of modern targeted-therapy oncology.

## SEGMENT 16 — Liquid biopsy

For most of the history of oncology, to know what was happening in a tumor you had to biopsy it — physically remove a piece of tissue, fix it, stain it, sequence it. Tissue biopsies are invasive, sometimes risky, often impossible in a metastatic patient with widespread disease, and they sample only a single point in space at a single point in time. In a tumor that is genetically heterogeneous and constantly evolving, a single biopsy is a snapshot of one corner of the building.

**Liquid biopsy** is the technology of detecting cancer-derived molecules in a blood sample. It is one of the most consequential developments in the past decade of oncology, and its potential is still being mapped. The key molecule is **circulating tumor DNA**, **ctDNA** — short fragments of DNA released into the bloodstream by tumor cells, mostly through apoptosis and necrosis. ctDNA is a tiny fraction of the total cell-free DNA in plasma (most of which comes from normal cells), but it carries the genetic signatures of the tumor — mutations, copy-number changes, methylation patterns. By sequencing plasma DNA with sufficient depth and bioinformatic sophistication, you can read out the tumor's genetics from a blood draw.

The clinical applications fall into several buckets.

**Genotyping for treatment selection**. In a patient with advanced cancer whose tissue biopsy is old, inadequate, or impossible, ctDNA can identify actionable mutations and direct targeted therapy. ctDNA-based EGFR testing is now standard in advanced lung cancer when tissue is unavailable. Similar plasma-based assays are used for KRAS, BRAF, HER2, and many other genes.

**Monitoring response to therapy**. ctDNA levels rise and fall with tumor burden. A dropping ctDNA level early in therapy correlates with response; a rising level signals progression, often before it becomes radiographically apparent. This is being incorporated into clinical trials and is moving into practice.

**Detection of resistance mutations**. As resistance emerges, the resistant clones release ctDNA carrying the resistance variants. ctDNA monitoring can detect the emergence of, say, T790M in EGFR-mutant lung cancer or C797S after osimertinib, sometimes weeks to months before clinical progression — potentially in time to switch therapy.

**Minimal residual disease**, **MRD**, **detection**. After surgery for early-stage cancer, the question of whether any tumor cells remain — too few to be visible on imaging but enough to seed eventual relapse — has historically been answerable only by waiting to see if the patient relapsed. ctDNA assays for MRD detect tumor-derived DNA at very low levels after curative-intent treatment, identifying patients with residual disease who are at high risk of relapse and might benefit from additional therapy. This is a particularly hot area in colorectal cancer, breast cancer, and others, with trials underway to test whether MRD-guided adjuvant therapy improves outcomes.

**Early detection in asymptomatic individuals**. This is the most ambitious application, and the one with the largest potential to change cancer outcomes at a population level. Multi-cancer early detection — **MCED** — tests aim to detect not-yet-symptomatic cancers by their ctDNA signatures, often using methylation patterns that distinguish tumor-derived DNA from normal background. The most prominent example is **Galleri**, a methylation-based test that screens for over 50 cancer types and predicts the tissue of origin. The clinical utility — whether MCED-detected cancers can actually be cured at rates that justify the cost and harms of screening — is being evaluated in large prospective trials. If the technology delivers, it could shift cancer screening from organ-by-organ programs to a single annual blood test that surveys for many cancers at once. If it doesn't, we'll have learned an important lesson about the limits of liquid biopsy. Stay tuned; this is one of the most consequential ongoing clinical research questions in the field.

## SEGMENT 17 — Computational pathology and AI in oncology

Cancer diagnosis, for over a century, has depended on the human pathologist looking down a microscope at a stained tissue section and recognizing patterns. That's still how most cancer diagnoses are made. But the past decade has seen the emergence of **computational pathology** and broader **artificial intelligence** applications across oncology, and the practice is changing.

Start with **image analysis**. Digitized whole-slide images of stained pathology sections can now be fed into deep learning models — convolutional neural networks, transformers, foundation models — trained on thousands or millions of annotated examples. These models can: detect tumors in lymph nodes (early successes in metastatic breast cancer); grade tumors with reproducibility comparable to or exceeding human experts (Gleason grading in prostate); predict molecular features like microsatellite instability or specific mutations directly from H&E sections; predict patient outcomes from morphology alone, sometimes discovering prognostic features that pathologists were not consciously using. Foundation models trained on hundreds of thousands of pathology slides — for example, the various pan-cancer pathology models that have emerged in the past two or three years — can be fine-tuned for specific tasks and are starting to enter clinical workflows.

**Radiology** has been transformed by similar deep-learning approaches. AI-assisted detection of lung nodules on CT, of breast cancers on mammography, of polyps during colonoscopy is moving into routine practice. The promise is twofold: improved sensitivity (catching things humans miss) and improved efficiency (reading more studies more quickly). The pitfalls are also real — overconfidence in automated readings, brittleness when image quality drifts, the difficulty of integrating AI outputs into clinical workflows in a way that genuinely helps rather than overwhelms.

**Integrated multi-omics**. The frontier of cancer characterization integrates many data layers — genome, transcriptome, methylome, proteome, sometimes single-cell, sometimes spatial — into unified models that predict tumor behavior and treatment response. Machine learning approaches that can integrate these heterogeneous data types are critical to making sense of the resulting datasets. Foundation models trained jointly on imaging, sequencing, and clinical data are an active research area, with the long-term goal of producing a single integrated representation of a patient's cancer that can be queried for any clinical question.

**Drug discovery**. AI is being deployed across the cancer drug-discovery pipeline — predicting protein structures (the AlphaFold revolution), identifying new targets, designing molecules that bind specific targets, predicting toxicity. Several AI-discovered drugs are in clinical trials. Whether AI will substantially shorten the timeline from target to approved drug is, at this point, an open empirical question — but the investment is enormous and the field is moving fast.

The honest summary: AI is transforming oncology, but unevenly. Some applications — image analysis, certain prediction tasks — are already at clinical-grade and entering practice. Others remain in research. As with any technology, the gap between demonstrated capability and routine clinical impact is filled by validation, integration, regulation, and trust-building. Expect the next decade of clinical oncology to look meaningfully different from this one because of AI.

## SEGMENT 18 — The frontier

In our final segment, I want to look forward. Where is cancer biology and oncology going in the next decade or two? Several frontiers are visibly active, and any one of them might be the next CAR-T-level transformation.

**Personalized neoantigen vaccines**. The idea: a tumor's unique mutations generate peptides that no other cell in the body produces. Those tumor-specific peptides, presented on MHC, are visible to T cells as foreign — they are **neoantigens**. If we can identify the neoantigens of a given patient's tumor, we can design a vaccine that trains the patient's immune system to attack them specifically. This requires sequencing the tumor, computationally predicting which mutations generate good neoantigens (peptides that bind well to the patient's MHC alleles and are likely to be immunogenic), and rapidly manufacturing a custom vaccine — typically as an mRNA construct encoding the predicted neoantigens.

The technology has become feasible because of mRNA platform maturation during the COVID-19 vaccine era. **BioNTech** and **Moderna**, the two major mRNA vaccine companies, both have substantial cancer vaccine programs. Early-phase trials in melanoma combining personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccines with PD-1 blockade have shown promising signals of reduced recurrence. Trials are underway in pancreatic cancer (a notoriously immunotherapy-resistant tumor), colorectal cancer, and others. If the results hold up in larger phase III trials, personalized cancer vaccines could become a standard adjuvant strategy across many tumor types — making each patient's cancer the target of its own custom therapy. This would represent the most extreme version of precision oncology imaginable.

**Base editing and other gene editing for cancer**. CRISPR and its derivatives — base editors, prime editors — have transformed the toolkit for making precise genetic modifications. In cancer, gene editing is being deployed in several ways: engineering CAR-T cells to be more potent (knocking out PD-1, knocking out TCR to make off-the-shelf allogeneic CAR-T), correcting genetic predispositions to cancer, and potentially directly editing tumor cells in vivo. The first allogeneic edited CAR-T products are in clinical trials. Base editing in particular, because it can make precise single-base changes without double-strand breaks, is well-suited to therapeutic applications.

**Organoid drug screening**. Cancer **organoids** — three-dimensional tissue cultures grown from patient tumor samples — can be propagated in vitro, screened against panels of drugs, and used to predict which drugs will work for a given patient's tumor. The early data from organoid-based drug prediction have been promising, and several centers are running prospective studies to test whether organoid-guided therapy improves outcomes. If it works, every patient with advanced cancer might in principle have a personalized drug-screening assay run on their own tumor cells to select therapy.

**AI-discovered targets and drugs**. We touched on this in the previous segment, but it's worth emphasizing as a frontier in its own right. Machine learning approaches that can identify previously unrecognized cancer dependencies — for instance, by integrating CRISPR screens, expression data, and patient outcomes across thousands of cancers — are generating new target hypotheses at unprecedented rates. The challenge is translating computational hits into validated, drug-able targets.

**Targeted protein degraders**. Conventional small-molecule inhibitors block the activity of an enzyme; **PROTACs** (proteolysis targeting chimeras) and related molecular glues actively recruit the cell's ubiquitin-proteasome system to destroy the target protein outright. This approach can drug previously undruggable proteins — including some transcription factors, scaffolding proteins, and other non-enzyme targets that have resisted small-molecule inhibition. Several PROTACs are in clinical trials, including against the androgen receptor in prostate cancer.

**Synthetic lethality at scale**. The PARP-BRCA paradigm we discussed earlier is one example of synthetic lethality. With genome-scale CRISPR screens, we can now systematically identify synthetic lethal partners for almost any cancer dependency. This is generating a rapidly expanding catalog of drug targets that are specifically lethal in cancers with particular genetic features, allowing therapy to be tailored to molecular subgroups in ways that were inconceivable a generation ago.

**The microbiome**. The gut microbiome influences responses to checkpoint inhibitors — patients with certain microbiome compositions respond better. Whether we can modulate the microbiome therapeutically to improve immunotherapy responses (through diet, probiotics, fecal microbiota transplant) is an active question with early trial data emerging.

\[Pause.\]

Let me close with a wider thought. Cancer is, at one level, a problem of cellular evolution running inside a multicellular organism. As long as we are multicellular and our cells divide, we will have cancer. It is, in that sense, intrinsic to our biology and not a problem we will ever "solve" once and for all.

But the relationship between people and cancer has changed enormously, and is still changing. Cancers that were death sentences a generation ago — CML, certain leukemias and lymphomas, HER2-positive breast cancer, metastatic melanoma, ALK-positive lung cancer — are now controlled, often for many years, sometimes apparently cured. Children with B-ALL who once would have died can now be saved. Childhood cancer overall has gone from a category of nearly uniformly fatal diseases to one in which survival rates exceed 80%. Cervical cancer is on track for elimination through HPV vaccination. Smoking rates are declining in much of the world. ctDNA-based early detection may pull more cancers into the curable window. Immunotherapy is durably curing patients who would have died of metastatic disease a decade ago.

And all of this is built on the conceptual foundation we walked through today. Clonal evolution. The hallmarks. Oncogenes and tumor suppressors. The cell cycle. Apoptosis. The microenvironment. The genomic catalog. Each generation of cancer therapy has emerged from a deeper understanding of these foundations, and there is no reason to believe we have hit a limit. The next generation will be built on what we don't yet know — about plasticity, about evolution, about the immune system, about the interactions between cancer and the rest of the body.

If you go into oncology — clinical or research — you are joining a field at a moment of genuine transformation. The pace of change is fast. The toolkit is expanding. The patients are benefiting in ways that would have been unimaginable a generation ago, and the rate of improvement is, if anything, accelerating. It is not always an easy field — patients still die, and they still die hard — but it is one of the most consequential and intellectually rewarding fields in all of medicine.

Thank you. We'll take questions.

## APPENDIX — Quick-reference summary (not for reading aloud)

**Core idea**: cancer is the clonal evolution of a renegade cell lineage inside a multicellular organism — driver mutations + selection. Therapeutic windows come from differences (genetic, dependency, immune) between cancer and normal cells.

**Hallmarks (Hanahan & Weinberg 2000, 2011, 2022)**: sustaining proliferative signaling; evading growth suppressors; resisting cell death; replicative immortality; angiogenesis; invasion/metastasis; deregulating energetics (Warburg); avoiding immune destruction; phenotypic plasticity; senescent cells as promoters. Enabling: genome instability, tumor-promoting inflammation, non-mutational epigenetic reprogramming, polymorphic microbiomes.

**Two gene classes**:
- **Oncogenes** (gain-of-function, dominant): activated by point mutation (KRAS G12/13/61, BRAF V600E, EGFR L858R/exon 19), amplification (HER2, MYCN, EGFR in glioma), translocation (BCR-ABL t(9;22), MYC-IgH t(8;14), EML4-ALK). Drug-able.
- **Tumor suppressors** (loss-of-function, recessive, Knudson two-hit): TP53 (~50% of cancers, "guardian of the genome"); RB1 (restriction-point gate); BRCA1/2 (HR repair → PARP synthetic lethality); PTEN (PI3K-AKT brake); APC (Wnt brake, FAP); VHL; NF1/2; mismatch-repair genes (Lynch, MSI). Generally not directly drug-able.

**Cell cycle**: cyclin D–CDK4/6 → RB phosphorylation → E2F release → S-phase entry. CDK4/6 inhibitors (palbociclib, ribociclib, abemaciclib) in HR+/HER2− breast cancer; require intact RB.

**Apoptosis**: BCL2 family — pro-survival (BCL2, BCL-XL, MCL1) vs effector (BAX, BAK) vs BH3-only sensors. BCL2 overexpression by t(14;18) in follicular lymphoma. Venetoclax = BH3 mimetic, used in CLL and AML+azacitidine; watch for tumor lysis syndrome.

**Angiogenesis & metastasis**: VEGF/VEGFR drives tumor vasculature → bevacizumab, VEGFR-TKIs (sunitinib, sorafenib, etc.). EMT (Snail/Twist/ZEB, loss of E-cadherin) enables invasion. Seed-and-soil (Paget 1889) + pre-metastatic niche.

**Tumor microenvironment**: CAFs (desmoplasia), TAMs (M2 polarization, CSF1R), MDSCs, Tregs, hypoxia (HIF-1α), acidity (lactate), remodeled ECM. Major immunotherapy target.

**Cancer genomics**: TCGA/ICGC. Drivers vs passengers. Mutational signatures (COSMIC) — tobacco, UV, BRCA, MMR, APOBEC. TMB-high + MSI-high → checkpoint inhibitor responsiveness; tissue-agnostic FDA approvals.

**Targeted therapy**:
- Imatinib (Gleevec) → BCR-ABL in CML (founding success); resistance via T315I → ponatinib.
- EGFR-mutant NSCLC: gefitinib/erlotinib → T790M resistance → osimertinib → C797S/MET amp.
- HER2-amplified breast: trastuzumab, pertuzumab, lapatinib, T-DM1, T-DXd (ADCs).
- BRAF V600E melanoma: vemurafenib/dabrafenib + MEK inhibitor (trametinib/cobimetinib).
- ALK-rearranged NSCLC: crizotinib, alectinib, lorlatinib (CNS penetration).
- KRAS G12C: sotorasib, adagrasib (covalent allosteric).
- Others: FLT3 (midostaurin, gilteritinib), IDH1/2, BTK (ibrutinib), NTRK (larotrectinib, tissue-agnostic).

**Immunotherapy**:
- Checkpoint inhibitors: anti-CTLA-4 (ipilimumab), anti-PD-1 (pembrolizumab, nivolumab), anti-PD-L1 (atezolizumab, durvalumab). Allison & Honjo Nobel 2018. irAEs from de-restrained autoimmunity.
- CAR-T: CD19 (tisagenlecleucel, axi-cel, brexu-cel, liso-cel) in B-ALL/DLBCL/MCL; BCMA (ide-cel, cilta-cel) in myeloma. CRS managed with tocilizumab; ICANS neurotoxicity.
- Bispecifics: blinatumomab (CD19xCD3), teclistamab/elranatamab (BCMAxCD3), tarlatamab (DLL3xCD3).

**Epidemiology/prevention**: age = dominant risk. Modifiable: tobacco (#1), alcohol, obesity, UV, infections (HPV, HBV/HCV, H. pylori, EBV, HIV, HTLV-1). Vaccines: HPV, HBV. Screening: colonoscopy, mammography, Pap/HPV, low-dose CT (smokers), PSA (controversial).

**Classical pillars**:
- Surgery: best curative modality for localized disease.
- Radiotherapy: ionizing radiation → DSBs; fractionation; conformal delivery; abscopal effect with immunotherapy.
- Chemotherapy classes: alkylators (cyclophosphamide, platinums, temozolomide), antimetabolites (methotrexate, 5-FU, gemcitabine), topo I/II inhibitors (irinotecan, anthracyclines, etoposide), microtubule poisons (vincas, taxanes). Regimens: R-CHOP, FOLFIRINOX, BEP, ABVD.

**Resistance**: clonal selection under drug pressure. On-target (T790M, T315I, C797S), bypass (MET amp), lineage plasticity (SCLC transformation, neuroendocrine prostate), pharmacological, microenvironmental. Adaptive therapy modulates dosing to preserve drug-sensitive bulk.

**Liquid biopsy**: ctDNA in plasma for genotyping, response monitoring, resistance detection, MRD assessment, MCED (Galleri).

**AI/computational pathology**: deep-learning whole-slide image analysis, radiology AI, multi-omics integration, AI drug discovery (AlphaFold etc.).

**Frontier**: personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccines (BioNTech, Moderna) + checkpoint blockade; base/prime editing for engineered cell therapies and germline-correction; organoid drug screening; PROTACs/molecular glues to drug undruggable targets; genome-scale synthetic-lethality screens; microbiome modulation of immunotherapy response.

**Two organizing principles**: (1) every hallmark is a logically necessary capability and thus a therapeutic target — anatomy of the cancer cell predicts the pharmacology; (2) every therapy applies a selection pressure and the cancer evolves under it — durable benefit requires understanding and managing the evolutionary dynamics, not just hitting the target.
