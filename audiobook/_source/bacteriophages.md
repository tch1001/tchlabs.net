## SEGMENT 1 — Opening: the most abundant biological entities on Earth

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. Today and for the next two hours, we're going to talk about **bacteriophages** — viruses that infect bacteria — and we're going to talk about them in a slightly unusual way. We're not going to spend the whole two hours on basic phage biology, although we will get to that. We're going to spend most of the time on phages as **tools** — as the substrate on which the entire field of molecular biology was built, as therapeutic agents making an unlikely comeback against antibiotic-resistant infections, as delivery vehicles, as research instruments, as the ancestral source material for CRISPR. The phage is the most useful virus you have probably never had a personal encounter with — except, of course, that you've had trillions of them inside you continuously since you were a few days old.

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do four things — first, tell me what a bacteriophage is and explain in plain English the difference between lytic and lysogenic lifestyles; second, sketch how the original molecular biology was done on T-even phages and lambda, and why phage was the right substrate for that work; third, articulate why phage therapy was abandoned in the West, why it survived in the East, and why it's coming back now; and fourth, list at least three modern biotech applications of phages beyond therapy — display, food safety, delivery, CRISPR origins, vaccine vehicles, take your pick — then we have succeeded. Everything else, the historical color, the molecular detail, the regulatory weeds, hangs off those four.

So let me start with the single most arresting fact in this entire field, because once you internalize it, you'll never think about microbiology quite the same way. **Bacteriophages are the most abundant biological entities on Earth.** Not the most abundant viruses — the most abundant entities, period. The current best estimate for the global phage count is about **ten to the thirty-first** — that's a one followed by thirty-one zeros. To put that in some kind of perspective, the number of stars in the observable universe is somewhere between ten to the twenty-second and ten to the twenty-fourth. So there are, conservatively, more phages on Earth than there are stars in the visible universe — by something like seven orders of magnitude. \[Pause.\] More phages than stars. Let that sit for a second.

Where are they all? They are everywhere there are bacteria, and there are bacteria essentially everywhere. There are phages in the ocean — a milliliter of seawater contains roughly ten million phage particles, and the global ocean is, by particle count, more virus than fish. There are phages in soil. There are phages in extreme environments — hot springs, salt flats, the deep subsurface. And there are phages in you. Your gut contains somewhere in the neighborhood of **ten to the twelfth** phage particles — a trillion phages, give or take, in your intestines right now, locked in a continuous, ancient predator-prey dance with your gut bacteria. Your skin has them. Your mouth has them. Your lungs have them. There is no phage-free part of you, unless you count the inside of healthy organs, and even there it's debatable.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy I want you to hold for the next two hours. **Phages are not broad-spectrum poisons. They are precision predators.** When we talk later about phage therapy, I want you to feel the contrast with antibiotics. An antibiotic is a chemical that, once you swallow it, distributes through your body and kills any bacterium of the sort it can kill — your pathogen, yes, but also your harmless commensals, your gut symbionts, the bacteria on your skin and in your mouth that you depend on. It's a chemical carpet bomb, calibrated for selective toxicity against bacteria as a category but not for selectivity between species. A phage, by contrast, is a hunter. It infects one species — sometimes only one strain within a species. It replicates only where its prey is. It dies out when its prey is gone. It does not touch your commensals. It does not linger in tissues. It is, in a real and biological sense, the opposite kind of antimicrobial — precise where antibiotics are sweeping, targeted where antibiotics are broad.

That contrast — broad-spectrum chemical poisons versus precision biological predators — is going to be the central frame for the therapy half of this lecture. And the contrast between phages as the original molecular biology subject and phages as a forgotten tool that's now coming back is going to be the central frame for the history. Keep both of those frames in your head and we'll be fine.

One more orienting fact before we get into the biology. Phages are old. Older than us, older than mammals, older than the trees. They have been infecting bacteria for somewhere between three and four billion years. Every piece of bacterial biochemistry you can name has been shaped by the selective pressure of phages. Bacterial restriction enzymes — the molecular scissors that became the foundation of recombinant DNA — exist because bacteria evolved them to chop up incoming phage DNA. CRISPR-Cas — the genome editing system you've heard about in the CRISPR lecture — is a bacterial immune system against phages. The reason these biotech tools exist at all is because bacteria and phages have been at war since deep time, and we're picking up the weapons left on the battlefield. We've done this before, by the way. The antibiotic story — soil microbes evolving chemical weapons over millions of years, us showing up and harvesting them — is the same shape of story. The biotech revolution is, to a remarkable degree, the consequence of evolutionarily ancient microbial conflicts.

Alright. With that in mind, let's build a phage.

## SEGMENT 2 — What a phage actually is

So what is a bacteriophage, structurally and functionally? At the simplest level: a phage is a virus that infects bacteria. The word itself comes from the Greek — "phagein," to eat — so "bacteriophage" literally means "bacteria eater," and the name was coined by Félix d'Hérelle in 1917 because that's exactly what they looked like they were doing on his plates. We'll get to him in a moment.

A phage is, like any virus, basically two things: a genome, and a protein shell that protects and delivers that genome. The genome can be DNA or RNA, single-stranded or double-stranded, linear or circular — different phages use all combinations. The shell, called the **capsid**, is built from one or a handful of repeating protein subunits, packed into a precise geometry. For many of the phages we'll discuss today, the capsid is an icosahedron — a twenty-faced polyhedron, the most efficient shape for enclosing a given volume with identical subunits — sitting on top of a tail structure that does the work of attaching to and injecting through the bacterial cell wall.

Picture the classic textbook phage — say, **T4**, which infects _E. coli_. T4 looks like a lunar lander. It has a roughly icosahedral head, the capsid, containing the DNA genome — about 170 kilobases of double-stranded DNA, which is a lot for a virus, encoding around 300 proteins. Attached to the head is a long, helical tail, like a syringe. At the bottom of the tail is a baseplate with tail fibers sticking out — six long protein fibers that splay out and dangle. Those fibers are the recognition apparatus. When T4 bumps into an _E. coli_ cell, the tail fibers bind specific molecules on the bacterial outer membrane — lipopolysaccharide, in T4's case — and once enough fibers have engaged, the baseplate reorients and the tail contracts like a syringe plunger, driving a hollow tube through the bacterial envelope and injecting the DNA. The empty protein shell is left outside, stuck to the cell surface like a discarded delivery package. Inside, the DNA takes over.

\[Pause.\]

Not all phages look like T4. **M13**, which we discussed at length in the phage display lecture, is a filamentous phage — a long, thin, floppy noodle of a particle, with single-stranded DNA inside a protein tube. **Lambda**, the workhorse of molecular biology, looks more like T4 — icosahedral head with a tail — but its tail is shorter and not contractile. **MS2**, an RNA phage, is just a small icosahedral shell with no tail at all. **P22**, which infects _Salmonella_, has a short stubby tail. The morphological diversity of phages is enormous, and it reflects the diversity of strategies they've evolved for getting into different bacterial hosts.

The other crucial feature of phages — and this is what makes them precision predators rather than broad poisons — is **host range**. Each phage species recognizes a specific set of bacterial surface molecules. T4 needs the lipopolysaccharide of certain _E. coli_ strains. M13 needs the F pilus. Lambda needs a maltose transporter called LamB. P22 needs a specific O-antigen on _Salmonella_. If your bacterium doesn't display the right surface molecule, the phage can't bind, can't inject, can't infect. That host specificity is exquisite. A phage that beautifully kills one strain of _E. coli_ may completely fail against a closely related strain that differs only in a single sugar on its surface lipopolysaccharide. \[Pause.\] This is the source of both the magic and the headache of phage therapy. The magic: phages don't touch off-target bacteria. The headache: matching the right phage to the right patient's bug is non-trivial.

Phages are also, by virus standards, structurally elaborate. A T4 head is built from over a thousand protein subunits. The tail tube alone is dozens of distinct proteins. The assembly process is a sequence of choreographed protein-protein interactions that has to happen in a specific order — first the head shell, then the DNA is packaged into it under enormous pressure, then the tail is attached, then the tail fibers. The whole thing is a tiny, autonomous molecular machine of extraordinary complexity. And you can grow a milliliter of culture and harvest ten to the twelfth of them. The plumbing of life at industrial scale.

One more thing about size, because it'll matter later. Phage particles are small — typically 50 to 200 nanometers in their largest dimension. That's smaller than a single ribosome by a noticeable margin, and well below the resolution of light microscopy. You can't see phages with an ordinary microscope. You can see what they _do_ — the clear holes they punch in a bacterial lawn, the plaques, which we'll get to in the discovery section. But the particles themselves are visible only by electron microscopy. The fact that for the first thirty years of phage research nobody had ever actually seen a phage, only its effects, is part of why early phage work feels a little like astronomy — you're inferring an invisible thing from its consequences.

Alright. We have a particle. Now let's build the lifestyle.

## SEGMENT 3 — Lytic versus lysogenic: two lifestyles, one switch

Phages come in two basic flavors of behavior, and the distinction between them turns out to be central — both to phage biology and to the experiments that founded molecular biology. The two lifestyles are **lytic** and **lysogenic**.

A **lytic** phage is a hit-and-run killer. It infects a bacterium, hijacks the cellular machinery, makes hundreds or thousands of copies of itself inside the host, and then bursts the host cell open — lyses it — releasing the new progeny phages to find new hosts. The cell dies. The phage population grows. From infection to lysis can be as fast as twenty or thirty minutes for a phage like T4 — call it a doubling time of half an hour with a burst size of a hundred or two hundred, and you can imagine how fast a lytic infection rips through a bacterial population. T4, T7, MS2 — these are obligate lytic phages. They have no other gear. Infect, replicate, lyse, repeat.

A **lysogenic** phage, by contrast, has two gears. It can do the same lytic thing — infect, replicate, lyse — but it has the option of doing something else: integrating its DNA into the host's chromosome and going quiet. The phage genome inserts itself, sometimes at a specific site, and the host bacterium goes back to its normal life, dividing and replicating its own DNA, faithfully copying the integrated phage DNA along with everything else. This integrated state is called a **prophage**, and a bacterium carrying a prophage is called a **lysogen**. The phage is essentially a passenger. It rides along through generations of bacterial division, doing nothing, contributing nothing, costing very little, until something triggers it — typically DNA damage, stress, UV light — at which point the prophage excises itself from the chromosome, switches into lytic mode, and now it's the same hit-and-run killer it could have been from the start. The cell that was its quiet host suddenly becomes its slaughterhouse.

\[Pause.\]

So the analogy I want you to hold: **lytic equals kill the host immediately. Lysogenic equals integrate quietly and wait.** A lytic phage is a wolf. A lysogenic phage is a sleeper agent. And critically, lysogenic phages — the ones that can integrate and wait — possess a molecular **switch** that decides, on infection, which behavior to follow. We'll spend Segment 5 entirely on that switch, because it's one of the most important pieces of biology ever worked out.

The classic lysogenic phage, the one that taught us almost everything we know about gene regulation, is **lambda** — bacteriophage lambda — which infects _E. coli_. Lambda has a beautifully balanced switch: when it infects, it can go lytic (with a probability that depends on conditions) or it can integrate as a prophage and wait. The same genome. Two completely different outcomes. The reason it has been so studied is that lambda forced biologists to confront, in a small and tractable system, the basic question of how cells decide: how does one piece of DNA produce two different developmental outcomes, depending on signals? Lambda was the test case where we learned how to think about that question. And the answer turned out to involve transcription factors binding DNA in mutually exclusive states, which is the foundation of all developmental biology.

There's a third twist on the lifestyle question that's worth a footnote. M13 and its filamentous relatives are neither classically lytic nor classically lysogenic. They establish a chronic, non-lytic infection — they don't integrate, but they also don't lyse. The infected cell keeps living, continuously extruding new phages into the medium. We discussed this in the phage display lecture because it's exactly the property that makes M13 so convenient as a display vehicle: you can grow infected cultures indefinitely and harvest phage from the supernatant. So the world of phage lifestyles isn't strictly binary. Lytic, lysogenic, chronic — there's a small zoo of strategies. But lytic-versus-lysogenic is the central axis, and it's the one to anchor your mental model around.

One more practical implication of the lytic-lysogenic distinction, because it'll be important when we get to therapy. **For phage therapy you generally want lytic phages.** Why? Because the whole point of therapy is to kill the pathogen. A lysogenic phage that integrates into the pathogen's chromosome and goes quiet hasn't done anything useful to the patient. Worse, lysogenic phages have a habit of carrying additional genes that aren't part of their own replication machinery — what we call **morons**, just to make the literature confusing — and some of those passenger genes are toxins or virulence factors. The classic, terrible example is the diphtheria toxin, which is encoded not on the _Corynebacterium diphtheriae_ chromosome but on a lysogenic phage that infected an ancestor of the bacterium and has been carried along ever since. The bacterium is dangerous because the phage is there. So lysogenic phages can _arm_ bacteria, can _enhance_ pathogenicity. For therapy, you screen lytic phages, you confirm they're obligately lytic, and you reject the lysogenic ones. Keep that in mind for later.

\[Pause.\]

Alright. Lytic and lysogenic. The two basic lifestyles. The switch between them is one of the foundational stories in molecular biology, and we'll get to it. But first, let's talk about how we discovered any of this — because the discovery story is genuinely strange.

## SEGMENT 4 — Discovery: Twort, d'Hérelle, and the dysentery patients of 1917

The discovery of bacteriophages is one of those stories where two people arrive at roughly the same observation, independently, within a year or two of each other, and then spend decades arguing about who got there first. It's a recurring pattern in science — penicillin, the structure of DNA, evolution, calculus — and phage discovery fits the pattern.

The first observation, in print, is from **Frederick Twort**, a British bacteriologist, in 1915. Twort was trying to grow vaccinia virus on agar plates, and he kept running into a strange problem: bacterial colonies that contaminated his plates would sometimes develop little glassy patches in them — patches where the bacteria seemed to have dissolved. He took those patches, filtered the contents through a porcelain filter that should have removed any bacteria — the filter's pores were too small for bacterial cells to pass — and the filtrate, applied to fresh bacterial colonies, would produce more glassy dissolution. Whatever was doing the dissolving was smaller than a bacterium. It could be propagated. It killed bacteria. Twort wrote a careful, hedged paper proposing that he might have discovered an "ultramicroscopic virus" or perhaps an enzyme — he wasn't sure — and then the First World War interrupted his work. He didn't follow up. The paper sat largely unnoticed for years.

Two years later, in 1917, **Félix d'Hérelle** — a self-taught French-Canadian microbiologist working at the Pasteur Institute in Paris — independently rediscovered the phenomenon, and he had no doubts at all about what he was seeing. D'Hérelle was studying dysentery patients during a French Army outbreak. He was culturing _Shigella_ from their stools, and he noticed the same kind of clear patches that Twort had seen — what we now call **plaques** — appearing on his bacterial lawns. Crucially, he noticed something else. When he tracked the plaques day by day in patients, the appearance of the plaques in the stool culture _coincided with the patient's recovery_. The patients who developed the most plaque-forming activity in their stools were the patients getting better. D'Hérelle put the pieces together with confidence. There was an invisible agent, smaller than a bacterium, that ate _Shigella_, and it was helping the patients recover. He named it the bacteriophage — the bacteria-eater.

\[Pause.\]

D'Hérelle then did something Twort had not done — he developed the technique that turned plaques into a quantitative assay. You take molten agar, you mix in your bacteria so it'll grow into a confluent lawn when it sets, you spread it on a plate, and you spot dilutions of the phage suspension on top. Where each individual phage particle lands and successfully infects a bacterium, it triggers a local lytic cascade — the infected cell bursts, releasing dozens of progeny, which infect adjacent cells, which burst, and so on outward — and after overnight incubation, that one initial infection has produced a clear circular hole, a **plaque**, visible to the naked eye in the otherwise uniform bacterial lawn. Count the plaques, multiply by the dilution factor, and you have a precise count of how many infectious phage particles were in your starting suspension. This is the **plaque assay**, and it is still, in 2026, the gold standard method for counting phages. It's also a beautiful example of how a single biological event — one phage particle infecting one cell — can be amplified into a macroscopically visible signal. One particle equals one hole. \[Pause.\] That kind of single-molecule sensitivity, achieved with no fancy equipment, on a petri dish, in 1917, was a hundred years ahead of its time.

D'Hérelle was a remarkable, somewhat eccentric figure — never finished a formal degree, taught himself microbiology, traveled the world chasing infectious disease outbreaks — and he had absolutely no doubt that his discovery was going to revolutionize medicine. He believed, from the start, that bacteriophages were therapeutic agents. He started doing what we'd now call compassionate-use experiments — applying phage preparations to dysentery patients, plague patients, cholera patients — and in many cases reporting dramatic recoveries. He set up phage therapy clinics in France, in India, in Egypt. He founded a phage research institute in Tbilisi, Georgia — which we'll come back to — alongside the Soviet microbiologist Giorgi Eliava. He was decades ahead of the field, and he was also, like many decades-ahead-of-the-field people, somewhat hard to take seriously, because he overstated his results, ignored confounders, and was bad at the controlled-trial methodology that the field was developing. The phage therapy he pioneered worked sometimes, didn't work other times, and nobody could quite figure out the rules.

That ambiguity — does this stuff actually work? — set the stage for the next chapter of the story, in which phage therapy got eclipsed by antibiotics. But for the moment, hold onto the picture: 1917, a French-Canadian autodidact at the Pasteur Institute, looking at clear plaques in _Shigella_ lawns, correctly identifying invisible particles smaller than bacteria as the predator, inventing the plaque assay, and going off to treat dysentery patients with phage preparations. That's the founding act. Everything that follows — molecular biology, phage therapy, phage display, CRISPR — rests on that first observation.

## SEGMENT 5 — Phages as the original substrate of molecular biology

Now we leave the therapy thread for a while — we'll come back to it — and we follow the other thread, the one where phages became the experimental substrate on which essentially every fundamental concept of molecular biology was first established. If you want to understand why the 1940s and 1950s biologists were obsessed with phage, you have to understand what they were trying to do and why phage was the perfect tool for it.

The question, in the 1930s and 1940s, was: what is a gene, made of? What carries genetic information? Cells, they reasoned, were too complicated. A bacterial cell, even a stripped-down one like _E. coli_, has thousands of genes, dozens of metabolic pathways, an internal architecture you could barely characterize. If you wanted to ask the most basic question — what molecule carries heredity — you needed the simplest possible genetic system. And phages, it turned out, were the simplest genetic systems available. A phage is essentially a tiny package of nucleic acid wrapped in protein. Infect a bacterium, the phage's hereditary material directs the production of hundreds of new phages, and those progeny carry the same heritable traits. It's heredity reduced to its absolute minimum.

In 1940, a group of physicists-turned-biologists — most notably **Max Delbrück**, a German physicist who'd emigrated to the United States, and **Salvador Luria** — formed what came to be called the **phage group**. They met every summer at Cold Spring Harbor on Long Island, ran courses, taught each other the techniques, agreed on which phages they would standardize on — the so-called T-even phages, T2, T4, and T6, infecting _E. coli_ — and built a community around the idea that if you wanted to understand life at the molecular level, you should work on phage. Delbrück was famous for insisting on rigor — he came from physics, he was used to clean quantitative experiments — and his influence shaped the field's culture. The phage group included almost everyone who would go on to win Nobel Prizes for foundational molecular biology in the 1950s and 60s.

\[Pause.\]

The single most famous experiment to come out of this tradition is the **Hershey-Chase experiment** of 1952, and it's worth dwelling on because it's the experiment that proved, beyond reasonable doubt, that DNA is the genetic material. **Alfred Hershey** and **Martha Chase**, working at Cold Spring Harbor, designed the experiment around T2 phage and the fact that, when T2 infects an _E. coli_ cell, only the genetic material enters the cell — the protein shell stays outside, stuck to the surface like a discarded delivery package, as we mentioned in Segment 2. So if you could label the DNA and the protein with distinguishable tags, you could ask: which one goes _inside_ the cell, and therefore which one carries the heritable instructions for making new phages?

They did this with radioactive isotopes. Phage protein contains sulfur — sulfur in cysteine and methionine — but no phosphorus. Phage DNA contains phosphorus — phosphorus in the sugar-phosphate backbone — but essentially no sulfur. So Hershey and Chase grew T2 phages in two batches: one labeled with radioactive sulfur-35, which got incorporated only into the protein, and one labeled with radioactive phosphorus-32, which got incorporated only into the DNA. Two batches of phages, identical except for which part was labeled. They infected fresh _E. coli_ with one batch or the other, waited briefly, and then put the cultures in a kitchen blender — yes, an ordinary kitchen blender — to shear off whatever was stuck to the outside of the cells without damaging the cells themselves. Then they spun the mixtures in a centrifuge to separate the cells, which pellet, from the loose stuff, which stays in the supernatant. Then they asked: where did the radioactivity end up?

The result was clean. In the sulfur-labeled batch — where the label was on the protein — the radioactivity ended up in the supernatant. The protein had been on the outside, was sheared off by the blender, and stayed in solution. In the phosphorus-labeled batch — where the label was on the DNA — the radioactivity ended up in the cell pellet. The DNA had gone _inside_. And critically, the cells that had DNA inside them went on to produce normal progeny phage. **The DNA had entered. The protein had not. And the DNA, alone, contained enough information to direct the production of complete new phages.** Therefore, the genetic material was DNA, not protein. This was the experimental settlement of a question that had been open for decades. Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty had pointed in the same direction with their 1944 transformation experiments in _Streptococcus_, but their finding had been resisted; many biochemists still believed protein, with its twenty amino acids and seemingly endless variability, was the more plausible carrier. Hershey and Chase, with T2 phage and a kitchen blender, made the answer impossible to dodge.

\[Pause.\]

That's the analogy I want you to hold for this segment. **Hershey-Chase is the experiment that proved DNA, not protein, carries the genetic information — and it was done in phage T2, in 1952, with a blender.** The whole shape of the next seventy years of biology — Watson and Crick the next year, the genetic code in the 1960s, recombinant DNA, sequencing, CRISPR — all of it follows from the moment when phage T2 made it clear that nucleic acid, not protein, was the heritable substance.

And it wasn't just the Hershey-Chase result. The phage group worked out, in T-even phages and lambda, essentially the entire grammar of molecular biology. The Luria-Delbrück fluctuation experiment of 1943, which showed that bacterial mutations are random rather than induced by the selective pressure — done with phage as the selector. The first map of a viral genome — done in T4. The discovery of mRNA — done by infecting _E. coli_ with T2 and showing that a new, rapidly-turning-over RNA species appeared, encoding phage proteins. The genetic code itself — cracked using mutations in T4. The first synthetic gene — for a phage tRNA. The first complete sequencing of a DNA genome — phage phi-X174 by Fred Sanger in 1977. The first restriction enzymes — discovered as bacterial defenses against phage. The list runs to dozens of foundational discoveries, all done on phage. For a generation of biologists between 1940 and 1975, "doing molecular biology" largely meant "doing phage genetics."

Why was phage so productive a substrate? A few reasons. First, **simplicity**: a phage genome is small and tractable. T4 has 300 genes, but lambda has only about 70, and phi-X174 has 11. You can hope to understand the whole thing. Second, **fast generation times**: a phage life cycle is minutes to hours, so you can run many experiments per day. Third, **large populations**: you can easily grow ten to the eleventh phages in a flask, so rare mutants are not so rare. Fourth, **clean genetic selections**: plaques on a lawn give you a single-particle readout, as we discussed. Fifth, **the host is also tractable**: _E. coli_ has its own deep genetic toolkit, and you can use host mutants to dissect what the phage needs from the host.

So phage was the right tool, at the right time, for the right question. The molecular biology you know — the central dogma, the operon model, the genetic code — was largely worked out by people staring at plaques on petri dishes and asking what the phages were doing inside those clear holes.

## SEGMENT 6 — The lambda switch: how cells decide

I want to spend a whole segment on one specific phage discovery, because it's beautiful and because it's a case study in how a deep biological principle can come out of a small, specific system. I'm talking about the **lambda switch** — the molecular mechanism by which phage lambda decides, on infection, whether to go lytic or go lysogenic.

The setup: lambda infects an _E. coli_ cell. Its DNA is injected. Now lambda has a choice. It can launch into a lytic program — express its replication genes, copy itself a hundred times, package the copies into new capsids, lyse the cell — or it can launch a lysogenic program — integrate its DNA into the host chromosome, shut off all the lytic genes, and wait. Same genome, two completely different outcomes. **How does it decide?**

The decision, it turns out, is governed by a remarkable little genetic circuit involving two regulatory proteins that bind to overlapping DNA sites, and that mutually exclude each other. The two proteins are called **CI** (the "lambda repressor") and **Cro**. Both are encoded by the lambda genome itself. Both can bind to the same set of DNA operator sites near the center of the lambda genome. But they bind in mutually opposing ways — and the outcome of the binding contest determines lifestyle.

When **CI** wins the contest — when it occupies the operators — it represses the lytic genes and activates its own production in a positive feedback loop. The cell becomes a lysogen. CI stays bound. The prophage stays integrated, silent, stable, across thousands of bacterial generations. The cell barely notices it's carrying a phage.

When **Cro** wins the contest — when it occupies the operators — it represses CI and lets the lytic genes fire. The replication program turns on. The lytic cascade runs. The cell bursts. New phages emerge. Two outcomes from one decision.

\[Pause.\]

The molecular elegance is in the details, and this is where **Mark Ptashne** and his collaborators at Harvard, over the 1970s and 80s, worked out essentially every piece of the mechanism. Each operator is not a single site but a set of three closely spaced binding sites — OR1, OR2, OR3 — and the two repressors prefer the sites in opposite orders. CI binds OR1 first, then OR2 cooperatively, and that arrangement happens to activate CI's own promoter while repressing the lytic genes. Cro binds OR3 first, and that arrangement happens to shut down CI's promoter while leaving the lytic genes open. The two proteins compete for the same DNA real estate, in different patterns, with mutually exclusive outcomes. Once one wins, positive feedback locks the decision in: CI makes more CI, or Cro shuts CI off and the lytic cascade ratchets forward. It is a **bistable switch** at the molecular level — two stable states, no intermediate, locked in by feedback. \[Pause.\]

Why does this matter beyond lambda? Because **this is the molecular basis of decision-making in biology**, full stop. Every developmental decision you can think of — should this cell become a neuron or a glial cell? Should this T cell become a helper or a cytotoxic killer? Should this stem cell stay quiescent or divide? — every one of those decisions is, mechanistically, some version of the lambda switch: a small number of regulatory factors, binding DNA in mutually exclusive patterns, with feedback loops that lock the decision in once made. Ptashne's book, _A Genetic Switch_, makes the case in detail, and it changed how a generation of biologists thought about gene regulation. Lambda was the model. Lambda was where we learned that "deciding" is something a piece of DNA and a few binding proteins can do, by themselves, with no central executive.

So when you hear, in any modern developmental biology context, about a "fate decision" or a "cell-fate switch" or a "bistable network," what you're hearing is the great-grandchild of the lambda CI-Cro circuit. The vocabulary and the framework came from sixty years of staring at this one phage. \[Pause.\]

Alright. We've done phage biology, phage discovery, phage as molecular biology substrate, and the lambda switch. Now let's pivot — because phages are not just laboratory curiosities. From the very beginning, d'Hérelle thought they were drugs. And in one corner of the world, they actually became drugs, and stayed drugs, for a hundred years. Time to talk about phage therapy.

## SEGMENT 7 — Phage therapy in the East: Eliava, Tbilisi, and the Soviet tradition

We left d'Hérelle in Segment 4 traveling the world, treating dysentery and cholera and plague patients with phage preparations, with mixed results he was not very careful about quantifying. By the late 1920s, d'Hérelle had concluded that the most promising place to develop phage therapy systematically was the Soviet Union, in collaboration with a Georgian microbiologist named **Giorgi Eliava**. Eliava had spent time at the Pasteur Institute in Paris in the early 1920s, had become a friend and admirer of d'Hérelle's, and had returned to Tbilisi, the capital of Soviet Georgia, with the conviction that phage therapy could become the cornerstone of public health in the Soviet bloc.

In 1923, Eliava founded what is now called the **Eliava Institute of Bacteriophage, Microbiology and Virology** in Tbilisi, and d'Hérelle joined him there for stretches of time in the 1930s. The Eliava Institute became the world's largest phage research and production center. They isolated phages against essentially every clinically relevant bacterium they could get their hands on. They built up huge libraries — collections of thousands of characterized phage strains, matched against thousands of bacterial strains. They developed methods for producing phage preparations at industrial scale, in liquid form, in tablets, in topical ointments. They distributed phage products through the Soviet pharmaceutical system. By the 1940s, you could walk into a Soviet pharmacy and buy phage preparations against _Staphylococcus_, _Streptococcus_, _Pseudomonas_, _E. coli_, _Salmonella_, _Shigella_, _Klebsiella_, and several others, sometimes as single-phage products and sometimes as standardized cocktails.

\[Pause.\]

The story would be more uniformly triumphant except that Eliava himself was arrested by Stalin's NKVD in 1937, accused of being a "people's enemy," and executed. D'Hérelle, who had been in Tbilisi just two years before, lost his closest collaborator and most of his political cover in the Soviet system. He went back to France. The Eliava Institute survived — somewhat miraculously — under new leadership, and it kept producing phage products through the Soviet era, all the way to the present day. Today it's a tourist attraction as well as a working clinic. You can fly to Tbilisi, present your antibiotic-resistant infection, have it cultured, have phages matched against it from the institute's library, and receive a customized phage cocktail prepared specifically for your bug. Patients have been doing this for decades — Eastern European patients routinely, Western "phage tourists" increasingly over the last twenty years.

The Soviet and Polish phage tradition, which also produced a major center at the **Hirszfeld Institute** in Wrocław, Poland, developed methods and accumulated clinical experience that the rest of the world simply ignored for most of the twentieth century. They published in Russian and Polish, in journals the West didn't read. They used clinical methodologies that were not designed to satisfy the randomized-controlled-trial standards developing in the United States. So when American and Western European clinicians considered phage therapy, they tended to dismiss it as folk medicine — interesting, possibly real, certainly not evidence-based. That dismissal lasted until roughly the 2010s, when the antibiotic resistance crisis forced a re-examination. We'll get there.

But for now, hold this picture. **From the 1920s through the 1990s, phage therapy was a routine part of medicine in the Soviet Union and in Poland. It never died in the East.** Patients walked into pharmacies and bought phages. Doctors prescribed phage cocktails for skin infections, urinary tract infections, surgical infections. The expertise accumulated continuously. The phage libraries were maintained. When the West eventually decided, in the 2010s, that phage therapy might be worth another look, the Eliava Institute and its Polish counterpart had been doing it without interruption for almost a hundred years. The center of gravity for clinical phage knowledge was, and to a significant degree still is, Tbilisi.

## SEGMENT 8 — The Western abandonment: antibiotics, and a hundred-year detour

Why did phage therapy die in the West? The straightforward answer is that **antibiotics happened**. But the more interesting answer involves a combination of antibiotics happening, methodological inadequacy of the early phage work, and a series of high-profile scientific embarrassments that turned the Western medical establishment against the whole field.

The methodological problem first. D'Hérelle and his early followers were enthusiasts, not clean experimentalists. They reported successes without adequate controls. They didn't always know what was in their phage preparations — sometimes the "phage" preparation contained dead bacteria, host debris, endotoxin, who knew what else. The host range of their phages was often imperfectly characterized, so a preparation might work against one strain and fail against a related strain for reasons nobody understood. Storage and shelf life were inconsistent — phages are biological entities, and a preparation that worked when fresh might be inert after sitting on a shelf for a year. Clinical trials, where they were done at all, were often single-arm and uncontrolled. Reading the early phage therapy literature, you find a lot of "we treated 200 patients and 180 got better" without any control group, without rigorous case definition, without standard dosing. It's not that the results were wrong — many were probably real. It's that they were not _convincing_ by the methodological standards developing in mid-twentieth-century American medicine.

In 1934, the Council on Pharmacy and Chemistry of the American Medical Association commissioned a critical review of phage therapy by two researchers, **Krueger** and **Scribner**. They evaluated the evidence and concluded, more or less, that phage therapy might have something to it in certain narrow applications but that the bulk of the published work was scientifically inadequate, that the results were inconsistent, and that the mechanism by which phages were supposed to work in tissues remained obscure. The review was not a categorical dismissal, but it was a strong statement of doubt, and it landed on American physicians' desks just as a much shinier alternative was emerging.

That alternative, of course, was **sulfonamides** in the late 1930s and then **penicillin** in the 1940s. Antibiotics had everything phages didn't. They were small molecules, chemically defined, stable on the shelf, predictable in dose-response, broad-spectrum, and produced industrially with consistent quality. You didn't have to match a particular antibiotic to a particular patient's bug — penicillin worked on essentially anything gram-positive, sulfonamides worked on a broad swath of bacteria, and you could prescribe empirically without waiting for culture results. The contrast with phage therapy was brutal. Phage required matching, required culture, required stocks of dozens of specific phages, and worked only against the exact bug it was matched to. Antibiotics required none of that.

\[Pause.\]

By 1950, in the United States and Western Europe, phage therapy was essentially dead. The companies that had been producing phage products in the 1920s and 30s — Eli Lilly had a small phage product line, as did several European pharmaceutical houses — discontinued them. Medical schools stopped teaching phage therapy. The Soviet tradition kept going behind the Iron Curtain, but Western doctors didn't read Russian and weren't inclined to take seriously a therapeutic tradition associated with what they regarded as a backward state medical system. **Phages went into hibernation in the West.** For sixty years, they survived in molecular biology labs as research tools — that's the line we picked up in Segment 5 — but as therapeutics they were essentially extinct in the Western pharmacopeia.

And then antibiotic resistance happened, and the calculation flipped.

## SEGMENT 9 — The resurgence: antibiotic resistance and the return of phage

The hinge of the modern phage therapy story is **antibiotic resistance**. We covered the resistance problem at length in the antibiotics lecture, so I'll just summarize the relevant piece. Over the seventy years since penicillin came into widespread use, bacteria have evolved resistance to essentially every antibiotic we've ever deployed. The pace of resistance evolution has outstripped the pace of new antibiotic discovery by a wide margin. The pipeline of new antibacterial drugs has been thin for decades. Some bacteria — notably the so-called **ESKAPE pathogens**: _Enterococcus faecium_, _Staphylococcus aureus_, _Klebsiella pneumoniae_, _Acinetobacter baumannii_, _Pseudomonas aeruginosa_, _Enterobacter_ — have evolved strains that are resistant to essentially every clinically available antibiotic, including the last-resort drugs. **Pan-resistant** is the term of art. There are patients dying in hospitals today because there is no antibiotic left to give them.

In that context, doctors and biologists started, in the late 1990s and accelerating through the 2010s, to look back at phage therapy with fresh eyes. Suddenly the Soviet tradition looked less like backward folk medicine and more like an unjustly ignored alternative. Suddenly the precision of phages — their species-specific killing, evolution-aware narrow spectrum — looked less like a limitation and more like an advantage. Suddenly the fact that phages co-evolve with their bacterial targets, that they can in principle keep up with resistance as it emerges, looked like an evolutionary feature rather than an unpredictable bug. The phage therapy resurgence, in short, was driven by desperation — and the desperation forced a reconsideration of what the precision of phages actually meant.

\[Pause.\]

Companies started forming. **Intralytix**, founded in 1998 in Baltimore, became one of the first Western phage therapy companies and got FDA approval in 2006 for an anti-_Listeria_ phage cocktail used in food safety — we'll come back to that. **Locust Biosciences**, **Adaptive Phage Therapeutics**, **Armata Pharmaceuticals**, **Pherecydes Pharma** in France, **BiomX** in Israel, **Felix Biotechnology** — by the late 2010s and early 2020s, a small but growing biotech industry was building phage products, mostly for compassionate-use cases initially and increasingly toward formal regulatory approval. The Eliava Institute became a partner and a consultant to many of these companies, providing phage libraries, methodologies, and clinical experience accumulated over decades.

The clinical successes that captured the public imagination came in the late 2010s. The most famous, and the one that genuinely shifted the field, was the **Tom Patterson case** at UCSD in 2016 and 2017. Patterson — a psychiatry researcher at UCSD — contracted a multi-drug-resistant _Acinetobacter baumannii_ infection during a vacation in Egypt. He was airlifted home, slowly deteriorated despite every available antibiotic, and was in a coma in a hospital bed in San Diego, dying. His wife, **Steffanie Strathdee** — an epidemiologist at UCSD — refused to accept the prognosis and started researching alternatives. She found the phage therapy tradition. She started cold-calling phage researchers around the world, asking if anyone had phages against _A. baumannii_. A network mobilized — phage labs at Texas A&M, the U.S. Navy Medical Research Center, and other institutions screened their libraries, found phages active against Patterson's specific bacterial isolate, and on an emergency compassionate-use basis, the FDA approved infusing those phages into Patterson intravenously. Within days of starting the phage treatment, Patterson woke up. Within weeks, the _Acinetobacter_ infection cleared. He survived. \[Pause.\]

The Patterson case became, justifiably, a media phenomenon. Strathdee wrote a book about it — _The Perfect Predator_ — and the case became the rallying example for the resurgence. UCSD founded the **Center for Innovative Phage Applications and Therapeutics**, **IPATH**, in 2018 — the first dedicated phage therapy center at a major American academic medical center. Compassionate-use cases started accumulating. The 2019 case of **Spencer**, the cystic fibrosis patient at Great Ormond Street in London who received engineered phages for a _Mycobacterium abscessus_ infection — that case used phages from Graham Hatfull's lab at Pittsburgh, and the engineering involved knocking out a lysogeny gene to convert a lysogenic phage into an obligately lytic one. The CF patient survived. Other compassionate-use cases — for endocarditis, prosthetic joint infections, deep wound infections — accumulated, each one published as a case report, each one adding to the slowly-rebuilding clinical evidence base.

By 2026, where we sit now, formal phase 2 and phase 3 clinical trials are underway for several phage products. The FDA has not yet approved a phage therapy for human systemic infection — that's still pending — but compassionate-use access has been streamlined, and the regulatory pathway is starting to crystallize. Phage therapy is no longer a fringe alternative. It's an active, growing area of pharmaceutical development, with billions of dollars of investment, dozens of companies, and a small but real clinical pipeline. The hibernation is over.

## SEGMENT 10 — Phage versus antibiotic: pros, cons, and the trade-offs

Welcome back. Settled in? Good. Let's get into the practical comparison, because if you're going to think clearly about phage therapy, you need to hold both the advantages and the disadvantages in mind simultaneously. Phages are not magic. They are a different kind of antimicrobial with a different set of properties, and the trade-offs are real.

Let's start with the advantages.

**Advantage one: precision.** This is the headline. Phages target specific bacterial species, often specific strains within a species. A phage matched to a patient's _Pseudomonas aeruginosa_ infection will kill that _Pseudomonas_ and leave the rest of the patient's microbiome alone. The gut commensals — your _Bacteroides_, your _Lactobacillus_, your _Faecalibacterium_ — will not be touched, because they don't display the surface receptors the phage recognizes. Compare this to an antibiotic like ciprofloxacin, which will hit the pathogen and also mow down vast swaths of your gut flora, predisposing you to _C. difficile_ overgrowth, to fungal overgrowth, to long-term microbiome disruption. \[Pause.\] The precision is genuinely transformational, if you can achieve it.

**Advantage two: self-amplification.** Phages replicate. An antibiotic dose is a finite chemical quantity that distributes, is metabolized, and excreted — once it's gone, it's gone. A phage dose, in principle, replicates at the site of infection: each phage that successfully infects a bacterium produces a hundred progeny, which infect more bacteria, and so on. The phage population grows where the bacteria are, then dies back when the bacteria are gone. You can, in principle, achieve effective therapy with much smaller doses than you'd need for a comparable antibiotic, because the phage does its own amplification at the target. This property is sometimes called the **auto-dosing** feature of phage therapy.

**Advantage three: evolution-aware.** Bacteria evolve resistance to phages — of course they do; this is a billion-year-old predator-prey relationship — but phages also evolve to overcome that resistance, and unlike a static antibiotic molecule, a phage is a self-replicating biological entity that can evolve along with its prey. In practice this means resistance to phage therapy, when it arises, often comes with a fitness cost — the bacterium has to alter or lose its surface receptor, which is usually a receptor for some legitimate biological purpose, so the resistant bacterium is often less fit than its sensitive ancestor. There are also documented cases where resistance to phage produces a re-sensitization to antibiotics, because the mutations that evade the phage knock out efflux pumps or other resistance mechanisms. So phage and antibiotic can be combined in ways that synergize.

**Advantage four: deep diversity.** There are estimated ten to the thirty-first phages on Earth. The library of available antibacterial weapons is, in practical terms, inexhaustible. If a resistant bug emerges, you can in principle find a new phage active against it, somewhere — in the sewage, in the soil, in the ocean. Phage discovery is a matter of looking. Antibiotic discovery is much harder, because the small-molecule diversity is bounded by what soil microbes happen to have evolved and what chemists happen to have synthesized.

\[Pause.\]

Now the disadvantages.

**Disadvantage one: narrow host range.** The precision that's an advantage is also a logistical headache. You can't prescribe a phage empirically the way you prescribe a broad-spectrum antibiotic. You have to know the species of the pathogen, often the strain, sometimes more, and you have to match a specific phage from a specific library to that specific isolate. This requires turnaround time — culture the bug, identify it, test phage susceptibility, dispense — that can run days. For a patient in septic shock who needs antibiotics within the hour, that timeline is too slow. Phage therapy, in its current form, is not a frontline empirical therapy. It's a precision therapy that requires diagnosis.

**Disadvantage two: immune response.** Phages are biological entities. They are protein. Your immune system will see them, develop antibodies against them, and — over repeated exposures — neutralize them before they can do their work. For a single course of phage therapy this is usually not a problem, because the phages do their job before the antibody response peaks. But for chronic infections requiring repeated dosing, anti-phage antibody development is a real concern, and it limits some applications. There's also the question of innate immune activation — phage preparations contain endotoxin and other immunogenic components from the bacterial hosts they're grown on, and purification has to be aggressive to get safe systemic doses.

**Disadvantage three: regulatory pathway.** Antibiotics fit cleanly into the small-molecule drug regulatory framework. Phages don't. Each phage preparation is a biological product. If you're customizing the phage cocktail to the patient's specific bug — which is the natural way to do phage therapy — then technically every patient is getting a different drug, which doesn't fit the standard one-drug-one-trial regulatory model. Regulators have been figuring out how to handle this, with frameworks for "personalized phage therapy" and for fixed cocktails, but the regulatory pathway remains less well-defined than for traditional drugs.

**Disadvantage four: phage neutralization in vivo.** When phages are injected intravenously, they get rapidly cleared by the reticuloendothelial system — the liver and spleen filter them out within hours. Their effective half-life in circulation is short. Achieving therapeutic concentrations in tissues, especially in difficult-to-reach sites like bone or biofilm, is non-trivial. Some phages have been engineered for longer circulation half-life, but it's a real pharmacokinetic challenge.

\[Pause.\]

So that's the balance sheet. Precision, self-amplification, evolution-awareness, and deep diversity on the plus side. Narrow host range, immune response, regulatory novelty, and pharmacokinetics on the minus side. The plus side is what's driving the resurgence. The minus side is why phage therapy is not going to replace antibiotics any time soon — it's going to be a complement, particularly for resistant infections and biofilm infections and chronic infections where antibiotics have failed.

## SEGMENT 11 — Phage cocktails: the strategy for broadening coverage

One of the practical answers to the narrow host-range problem is the **phage cocktail** — combining multiple phages into a single therapeutic preparation, so that the cocktail covers a broader range of bacterial strains and is less likely to be defeated by any single resistance mutation.

The logic is straightforward. Suppose you have a phage A that kills 60% of clinical _Pseudomonas aeruginosa_ isolates. That's not great coverage on its own. But suppose you can pair it with phage B that kills a different, partially overlapping 60%, and phage C that kills yet another partially overlapping 60%. The union of the three — the fraction of clinical isolates susceptible to at least one of A, B, or C — might be 90% or 95%. By stacking phages with overlapping but not identical host ranges, you broaden coverage. And as long as the cocktail contains at least one phage active against any given patient's isolate, the cocktail works.

The cocktail also addresses **resistance evolution**. Bacteria evolving resistance to phage A might require one mutation. Evolving resistance to A and B and C simultaneously requires three mutations — and if the three phages bind to different receptors, those mutations are independent. The compound probability of simultaneous resistance is the product of the individual probabilities, which is vanishingly small. So a well-designed cocktail isn't just broader-spectrum, it's also harder for the bacteria to evade. This is the same logic as combination therapy in HIV and tuberculosis — combine multiple drugs with different mechanisms, and evolution can't escape all of them at once.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to hold for this segment is: **cocktails are the way to broaden coverage. One phage targets one bacterium. Mix five phages, cover a broader range — and make resistance much harder.**

In practice, designing a cocktail involves a screening process. You take your phage library — say, the Eliava Institute's collection, or a curated library from a phage company — and you test each phage against a panel of clinical isolates of the target pathogen. You score each phage for its host range. You pick a set of three to ten phages that together cover the broadest fraction of isolates, that use distinct surface receptors (so resistance to one doesn't confer resistance to others), and that have compatible production characteristics. The cocktail is then manufactured as a single product, with each phage component characterized by titer.

The two major approaches to cocktails differ in how customized the cocktail is. The **fixed-cocktail** approach, which is what most Western phage companies are pursuing, uses a single standardized product — three or five phages mixed in defined ratios — that's been tested against a large panel of clinical isolates and shown to cover a high fraction. This is easier to manufacture, easier to regulate, and works for empirical use without per-patient matching. The downside is that it doesn't cover the resistant fraction.

The **personalized-cocktail** approach, which is closer to the Eliava tradition, custom-builds a cocktail for each patient. You isolate the patient's pathogen, test it against the phage library, pick the phages that work best against this specific isolate, and prepare a cocktail just for this patient. This gives much higher hit rates but is logistically much harder and regulatorily much messier. Some hybrid approaches — pre-build a few standard cocktails for the most common pathogens, customize when the standard cocktails fail — are emerging.

There's also a useful concept called **phage-antibiotic synergy**, which is when a phage and an antibiotic together kill better than either alone. In some documented cases, the phage forces the bacterium to alter its surface to escape phage killing, and that alteration sensitizes the bacterium to an antibiotic it had previously been resistant to. Or the antibiotic stresses the bacterium in a way that enhances phage replication. The interactions are complex and case-by-case, but the practical implication is that phage therapy doesn't have to replace antibiotics — it can be combined with them, sometimes synergistically.

## SEGMENT 12 — Engineered phages: when natural phages aren't enough

The next frontier, which is where a lot of current academic and biotech work is happening, is **engineering phages** — modifying their genomes to give them properties their natural counterparts don't have. The natural phage library is enormous, but it has gaps. Sometimes no natural phage exists with the right host range for a particular pathogen. Sometimes the available phages are lysogenic and you need them to be lytic. Sometimes you want to add a payload — a CRISPR system, an antibiotic-resistance-reversal cassette, an immune-evasion modification. Engineering closes the gaps.

The first and most important kind of engineering is **lysogenic-to-lytic conversion**. As we discussed, lysogenic phages aren't useful for therapy — they integrate quietly and don't kill the host. But sometimes the best phage you have against a particular pathogen is naturally lysogenic, and you'd like to convert it. The conversion is conceptually simple: knock out the lysogeny gene — the one encoding the repressor, the CI equivalent — and the phage loses its option to integrate. It can only do lytic. The Spencer cystic fibrosis case at Great Ormond Street in 2019 used exactly this — a phage that was naturally lysogenic against _Mycobacterium abscessus_, knocked out for its lysogeny gene by Graham Hatfull's lab at Pittsburgh, so that the engineered phage was obligately lytic and clinically useful.

The second kind of engineering is **host range modification**. The tail fibers of a phage — the proteins that recognize the bacterial surface — determine which species and strains the phage can infect. If you swap the tail fiber genes between phages, you can in principle swap host ranges. This has been done experimentally with several phages — the Lu lab at MIT, the Marraffini lab at Rockefeller, the Voigt lab — and the dream is to be able to "program" a phage's host range by choosing tail fibers from a parts library. **Edit the phage's host range — make it kill the bacteria you want, not its natural prey.** That's the analogy. We're not there yet for arbitrary host ranges, but the field is moving fast.

\[Pause.\]

The third kind of engineering is **CRISPR-armed phages**. This is a particularly clever idea pioneered by Tim Lu's lab at MIT and others. You take a phage and load its genome with a CRISPR-Cas system targeted at the bacterial chromosome — for instance, targeted at antibiotic resistance genes. When the phage infects the bacterium, the CRISPR machinery activates and cleaves the bacterial chromosome at the target sites. The bacterium dies not from the phage's normal lytic program but from CRISPR-mediated chromosomal destruction. And critically, the CRISPR can be programmed to specifically target resistance genes or virulence genes, so even if the phage doesn't kill the bacterium outright, it strips it of its resistance, re-sensitizing it to antibiotics. The phage becomes a delivery vehicle for an antibacterial CRISPR weapon. **Locus Biosciences** is one company commercializing this concept.

The fourth kind of engineering is **payload addition for therapeutic purposes other than killing**. Phages can be loaded with genes that produce antibiotics on entry — the phage delivers an antibiotic gene to the host, which expresses it and kills itself. Or genes that disrupt biofilm matrix — the phage isn't just killing the bacterium, it's degrading the protective biofilm that surrounds chronic infections. Or genes that quench quorum sensing, preventing the bacterial population from coordinating virulence behaviors. The phage becomes a chassis for various antibacterial payloads, not just a self-replicating virus.

There's also work on engineering phages as **gene-delivery vehicles** for therapeutic purposes _other_ than antibacterial therapy. We touched on this briefly in the transposons lecture, and it overlaps with the AAV and adenovirus delivery discussions. Phages can be engineered to deliver genes to mammalian cells, although this is much more experimental than their use against bacteria. Filamentous phages, in particular — M13, fd, f1 — have been explored for delivering DNA payloads to tumors and to other mammalian targets. The advantages are that phages don't naturally replicate in mammalian cells, so there's no risk of unintended infection, and they can carry larger payloads than some viral vectors. The disadvantages are that they're not naturally good at entering mammalian cells, so they need surface modifications — display of cell-targeting peptides, fusion to nanoparticles — to get efficient uptake. The application is still niche, but it exists.

And finally, you can engineer phages to **display antigens** as vaccines. We'll come back to this in Segment 13.

The general theme is that phages have, by now, become an engineering substrate as much as a natural resource. You don't have to take the phage as you find it. You can edit it, retool it, weaponize it differently. The toolkit for phage engineering — CRISPR-based phage genome editing, recombineering, in vitro assembly of phage genomes from synthetic DNA — has matured to the point where building a custom phage with desired properties is a months-long lab project, not a years-long heroic effort.

## SEGMENT 13 — Phages as vaccine vehicles: display revisited

We spent the entire phage display lecture on the use of phages to display peptides and proteins on their capsids for the purpose of finding binders. Here I want to make a related but distinct point: the same display capability can be used to **build vaccines**.

The basic idea: take a phage — M13 or T7 are the most common, although T4 and lambda also work — and engineer it to display, on its surface, a piece of an antigen from a pathogen you want to vaccinate against. Could be a fragment of HIV gp120. Could be a piece of malaria circumsporozoite protein. Could be a SARS-CoV-2 spike fragment. The phage particle, with the antigen sticking out from its surface, is then injected as a vaccine. The immune system sees the antigen displayed on a highly immunogenic, particulate, repetitive viral surface — exactly the kind of presentation immune systems respond strongly to — and develops a robust antibody and T cell response against the antigen.

Phage-displayed vaccines have several appealing properties. **Particulate immunogenicity**: a viral particle covered in hundreds or thousands of identical copies of an antigen is a much stronger immunogen than the antigen alone in solution. Immune systems are tuned to recognize repetitive, ordered arrays of antigen as a danger signal, because that's what viruses look like. **Self-adjuvanting**: phages contain their own pathogen-associated molecular patterns — unmethylated CpG DNA, lipopolysaccharide from their host bacteria — that activate innate immune sensors. You don't always need a separate adjuvant. **Cheap and scalable production**: phages grow in _E. coli_, which is the cheapest expression system on Earth. You can produce phage vaccines at industrial scale for very low cost per dose. **Stable**: phage particles are remarkably hardy, surviving room temperature for long periods, surviving freeze-drying. This makes them attractive for vaccines in low-resource settings, where cold-chain logistics are a major barrier.

\[Pause.\]

The major phage display platforms for vaccines are **M13** and **T7**. M13 displays peptides on its surface coat proteins, typically pIII or pVIII, the same proteins we discussed in the phage display lecture. T7, an icosahedral phage, displays peptides on its capsid protein gp10. T7 has the advantage of higher copy number per particle — hundreds of antigen copies on a single phage — which makes for stronger immune responses. T4 has even higher capacity. Several clinical-stage phage vaccine candidates are in development, against malaria, against opioid addiction (yes, vaccines against drugs of abuse are a real research area), against various pathogens for veterinary use. None are FDA-approved for human use yet, but the platform is maturing.

There's also a hybrid concept where phages serve as **DNA vaccine carriers** — the phage capsid carries a DNA payload that, when delivered to mammalian cells, expresses an antigen from inside. This combines the immunogenicity of phage particles with the antigen-expression flexibility of DNA vaccines. Still early-stage, but interesting.

## SEGMENT 14 — Phage as the ancestral substrate of CRISPR

I want to make one last historical connection before we get to the final practical segments, because it's important and because students often miss it. **CRISPR — the genome editing system — was discovered because of phage biology.** The link is direct, and it's worth pulling out.

Recall from the CRISPR lecture (or if you haven't taken it, here's the gist): CRISPR is a bacterial adaptive immune system that lets bacteria remember and resist phage infections. When a phage infects a bacterium and the bacterium survives, CRISPR machinery captures a small piece of the phage DNA — a **spacer** — and incorporates it into a special locus in the bacterial chromosome, between repeating sequences called CRISPR arrays. The next time that phage shows up, the bacterium expresses RNA from the array, the RNA guides a Cas nuclease to the matching phage DNA, and the nuclease chops it up. The bacterium has, in effect, vaccinated itself against the phage.

The system was first noticed as a strange pattern in bacterial genomes in the late 1980s and 90s. **Francisco Mojica**, a Spanish microbiologist working on archaea in salt ponds, was sequencing his organisms' genomes and kept seeing these strange repetitive sequences with unique spacers in between. He started cataloging them. In 2005 he made the crucial observation: the unique spacer sequences matched, when he searched databases, to **phage and plasmid sequences**. He realized — and this was the insight — that the spacers were a record of past phage infections. The CRISPR arrays were a bacterial memory of phages the cell had encountered. The system was an immune system. **Mojica's spacers were phage-derived**, and the function of CRISPR was anti-phage defense.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to hold for this segment: **CRISPR is bacteria's anti-phage immune system. The entire genome-editing revolution traces back to studying how bacteria defend themselves against phages.** Without phage biology, no CRISPR. Without phage as the selective pressure shaping bacterial genomes, no CRISPR arrays. Without Mojica looking at his archaea and asking "why are these spacers similar to phages?", no insight into function. The whole edifice — Charpentier and Doudna's 2012 reprogramming of the system as an editing tool, Zhang's mammalian application, the prime editors, the base editors, all of it — sits on top of the bacteria-versus-phage war.

This is one of those moments where the value of fundamental research becomes inarguable. People studying salt-pond archaea, on the margins of microbiology, found CRISPR. People studying yogurt cultures — _Streptococcus thermophilus_ being attacked by phages during cheese-making — confirmed the function. People studying the molecular machinery, mostly funded as basic science, worked out the mechanism. And then it became the most important biotech tool of the twenty-first century. The pattern — fundamental study of microbial biology leading to transformative tools — is one we've seen repeatedly. Phage biology is the substrate. CRISPR is what grew out of it.

So when you think about phages, don't think about them as a niche specialty. Think about them as the underlying terrain on which much of modern molecular biology has been built. Restriction enzymes — anti-phage defense, became recombinant DNA. CRISPR — anti-phage defense, became genome editing. Phage display — repurposing phage capsids, became antibody discovery. The pattern is consistent. Phages keep paying off.

## SEGMENT 15 — Phages in food safety: already in your supermarket

Now let me bring this down from the theoretical to the very practical, because phages are already in commercial use in a way that most people don't know about, and the example is worth knowing because it shows the regulatory path can work.

In 2006, the FDA approved a phage product called **ListShield** — manufactured by Intralytix — for use as a food spray against _Listeria monocytogenes_ on **ready-to-eat meat and poultry products**. Deli meats. Cold cuts. Hot dogs. The product is a cocktail of six phages, all lytic, all specific to _Listeria_, sprayed on the surface of processed meats before packaging. The phages kill any _Listeria_ that might be on the surface, reducing the risk of listeriosis — a foodborne illness that's particularly dangerous for pregnant women, the elderly, and the immunocompromised. The product is GRAS — Generally Recognized As Safe — and is approved for use on organic foods, on kosher foods, on a wide range of products. **It is already in your supermarket.** If you've eaten any commercial deli meat in the last fifteen years, there's a reasonable chance you've consumed bacteriophages with it.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to hold for this segment: **FDA-approved Listeria-killing phage spray on deli meats. Already in your supermarket. Already in you, in trace quantities, if you eat processed meat.** The regulatory acceptance came through the food safety pathway, which is different from the drug pathway — phages applied to food are regulated as food additives, not as drugs, and the bar is somewhat lower. But the precedent matters. The FDA has formally accepted that phage applications to surfaces in contact with humans are safe, that they work, that they reduce pathogen loads. That precedent has greased the path for broader phage approvals.

Other food safety phage products have followed. **EcoShield** for _E. coli_ O157:H7 on beef. **SalmoFresh** for _Salmonella_ on poultry. **ShigaShield** for _Shigella_ on produce. The food safety market for phages is small but growing, and it's been a quiet proof-of-concept for the regulatory and manufacturing challenges of getting phage products into the marketplace.

There are also agricultural applications — phages applied to crops to prevent bacterial plant diseases. **AgriPhage** for _Xanthomonas_ on tomatoes and peppers. Phage products for fire blight on apples. Aquaculture applications — phages for fish farm pathogens like _Aeromonas_ and _Vibrio_. The list is small but growing.

The broader point: phage technology has been quietly entering commercial agriculture and food production for two decades, building a track record of safety and efficacy that may eventually grease the path for the therapeutic applications. The regulatory and manufacturing infrastructure is partly already in place, just for different applications. That existing infrastructure is one of the reasons the therapy resurgence is plausible — we're not starting from zero. We've been making and selling phage products for a long time.

## SEGMENT 16 — Phage as research tools: still the workhorse

Let me bring us back, for a moment, to the laboratory use of phages — because while phage therapy and food safety get the headlines, phages remain a foundational research tool, and a sense of the breadth of that use is part of the field's portrait.

We covered phage display extensively in its own lecture, so I'll just recap: M13 phage is the dominant display platform, used in essentially every antibody discovery campaign, every peptide screening, every protein-protein interaction mapping. The 2018 Nobel Prize. Humira and a dozen other antibody drugs.

Beyond display, phages serve as **cloning vectors**. Lambda was, for decades, the workhorse cloning vector for inserts too large for plasmids — you could clone forty kilobases of foreign DNA into a lambda vector and propagate it. Cosmids and phagemids — hybrids combining phage and plasmid features — extended the range further. The first generation of complete genome libraries — including the Human Genome Project's early libraries — used phage-derived vectors. Modern cloning has largely moved on to BAC and YAC vectors for big inserts and standard plasmids for small inserts, but the conceptual lineage runs through phage.

Phages are **delivery vehicles for genome engineering experiments**. The most common technique for moving DNA between _E. coli_ strains, called **transduction**, uses phages — typically lambda or P1 — to package and transfer bacterial DNA. P1 transduction has been a standard tool of bacterial genetics for sixty years and is still used routinely. The technique simply exploits the fact that phages occasionally make packaging errors and put bacterial DNA into a phage capsid instead of phage DNA — and when that mispackaged particle infects a new cell, it injects the bacterial DNA, which can recombine into the recipient's chromosome.

Phages are **selection systems**. The CRISPR screening field uses phage challenge as one method of selecting bacteria with desired genetic properties — you infect a library with phage and recover the survivors. **Phage-assisted continuous evolution** — PACE, developed by David Liu's lab — uses phage-host coupling as a directed evolution tool, where the phage's reproduction depends on a function you want to evolve, so phages with that function out-compete others. PACE has been used to evolve novel protein binders, T7 RNA polymerases with new promoter specificities, base editors with optimized properties. It's a powerful framework, and it sits squarely on phage biology.

Phages are **single-virus genetic dissection systems** — meaning, because of the plaque assay, you can do genetics on individual viral particles in a way you can't easily do with most other organisms. You can mutate, plate, pick single plaques, characterize them, recombine them in defined crosses. The genetic toolkit developed for lambda — temperature-sensitive mutants, amber suppressors, mutant complementation — is unrivaled in any other virus. For a graduate student learning rigorous genetics, doing a few experiments on lambda is still considered a useful rite of passage in some labs.

\[Pause.\]

So phages are still, eighty years after Delbrück and Luria, the workhorses of a remarkable range of research. The reason is simple: they're small, they're tractable, they're fast, they're cheap, and the genetic and molecular toolkits around them are extraordinarily mature. New applications keep emerging. The phage will likely remain a fundamental research tool for as long as we keep doing molecular biology.

## SEGMENT 17 — Practical challenges: manufacturing, matching, regulation

Before we end, I want to spend one segment on the practical hurdles, because phage therapy has a number of unglamorous logistical problems that have to be solved if the field is going to scale, and these problems are what most of the operational work in phage companies currently focuses on.

**Manufacturing.** Producing phage at clinical scale is harder than it sounds. You're growing the phage on a bacterial host — typically the very pathogen you're trying to treat — and you have to purify the phage away from the host. That means lysing the bacteria, separating phage particles from bacterial debris, removing endotoxin (which is highly inflammatory and can cause sepsis if injected at meaningful doses), removing host DNA, removing host proteins. Endotoxin removal is particularly hard, because phage particles tend to associate with LPS, and aggressive purification can damage the phage. Achieving GMP-grade phage product — pharmaceutical-quality, characterized, sterile, low-endotoxin, stable — is a non-trivial manufacturing challenge. The companies working on this are figuring it out, but it's slow and expensive.

**Host range matching.** As we discussed, phages are narrow. To use them therapeutically, you need to confirm that your candidate phage actually kills the patient's bacterial isolate. This requires a culture-and-susceptibility-test step that adds days to the treatment timeline. Some companies are developing rapid phage-matching assays — droplet microfluidics, automated plate-based screens, even sequencing-based prediction — to get the matching time down from days to hours. The faster the matching, the more broadly phage therapy can be deployed.

**Regulation.** We touched on this in Segment 10. The FDA and other regulators are still figuring out how to handle phage products, particularly personalized ones. The general direction is toward frameworks that allow for "phage drug products" with defined components and ratios, while also providing pathways for personalized cocktails under expanded access or compassionate use. The European regulators have moved somewhat ahead of the FDA, with several phage products approved for compassionate use. The regulatory framework is evolving, but slowly.

**Phage banking.** To do personalized therapy at scale, you need a large, characterized phage library — thousands of phages, with their host ranges mapped against thousands of bacterial isolates. Building and maintaining such a library is a significant institutional commitment. The Eliava Institute has one. Some Western institutions are building them. But there's no global, shared, well-characterized phage bank yet, which is a real bottleneck.

**Pharmacokinetics.** As we mentioned, phages are cleared rapidly from circulation, and reaching tissues — especially difficult sites like bone, lung, biofilm-encased chronic wounds — is non-trivial. Optimizing routes of delivery (intravenous, inhaled, topical, intra-tissue) is an active area. Engineering phages for longer circulation half-life is another.

**Immune response.** Repeated dosing leads to anti-phage antibody development, which limits chronic use. Strategies include rotating phages, masking phage surfaces with polymers, using phage cocktails that share less antigenic surface with each other. Solving the immune problem cleanly is still ahead of the field.

\[Pause.\]

So the field is real, the resurgence is real, the science is real, but the operational and regulatory work needed to scale phage therapy into a routine clinical option is substantial and ongoing. The next decade is going to be largely about solving these practical problems.

## SEGMENT 18 — The frontier: in-pharmacy matching, AI-driven discovery, and the precision-medicine vision

Let me end where the field is going. The frontier — five to ten years out — is starting to look like a precision-medicine framework specifically for antibacterial therapy, and it's worth painting the picture.

Imagine a patient walks into a clinic with a stubborn urinary tract infection that hasn't responded to two rounds of antibiotics. The clinician swabs the urine, the sample goes to the lab. Within hours, the lab has identified the species, sequenced the bacterial genome, and characterized the resistance profile. The bacterial sequence is then matched, computationally, against a curated phage library — say, a database of 10,000 well-characterized phages, with their host ranges experimentally mapped against thousands of historical isolates, and with their genomes sequenced so that genomic predictors can be applied. A machine learning model — trained on the historical matching data — predicts which phages in the library are most likely to kill this particular bacterial strain. The top candidates are pulled from storage, tested in a quick plaque assay to confirm, and a personalized cocktail is dispensed.

End-to-end timeline: 48 hours, perhaps less. The patient walks out with a phage cocktail specifically matched to their bug. The cost is comparable to a course of antibiotics. The bacteria, having been precisely targeted with a phage they can't escape without a fitness cost, fail to evolve significant resistance. The microbiome is undisturbed.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to leave here with: **in-pharmacy phage matching. Sequence the patient's bacterial infection, match against a phage library, dispense a personalized phage cocktail in 48 hours. Precision antimicrobial medicine.** That's the vision. We're not there yet — the libraries aren't big enough, the matching models aren't accurate enough, the regulatory framework isn't ready, the manufacturing turnaround for personalized cocktails isn't fast enough — but the pieces are coming together.

The enabling technologies are several. **Sequencing**: cheap, fast bacterial whole-genome sequencing makes accurate species and strain identification routine. **Phage genomics**: thousands of phage genomes are now in public databases, and the rate of new sequencing is accelerating. **Machine learning**: models that predict phage-host compatibility from genome sequences are improving, and AI-driven phage discovery — using generative models to design new phages with desired host ranges — is starting to appear at the frontier. **Synthetic phage**: the ability to assemble phage genomes from synthetic DNA means you don't have to find the right natural phage; you can in principle design and synthesize one. **Standardization**: emerging frameworks for characterizing phage products in standardized ways are starting to allow comparison across labs and across companies.

The broader vision is that antimicrobial medicine — which has been dominated for eighty years by broad-spectrum small-molecule drugs — gradually shifts toward a precision framework, where the antibacterial therapy is selected based on the specific pathogen, with phages as the customizable component. Antibiotics don't disappear — they remain the backbone for emergency empirical therapy and for situations where rapid matching isn't feasible. But for resistant infections, for chronic infections, for biofilm infections, for situations where preserving the microbiome matters, phage cocktails become a routine, prescribable option. Maybe one of three antimicrobial courses by 2040 is phage-based. Maybe more.

And underlying all of this is the deep biological fact we started with: there are ten to the thirty-first phages on Earth. The library is essentially infinite. The challenge is not finding phages — they're everywhere. The challenge is characterizing them, matching them, delivering them, regulating them, manufacturing them at scale. Engineering and informatics problems. Solvable problems. The biology — the deep ancient war between phages and bacteria, ongoing for four billion years — has already done most of the work. We just need to learn to read the battlefield and pick up the weapons.

\[Pause.\]

That's where we end. Let me leave you with the four learning objectives we started with, and check that we've hit them.

**One**: a phage is a virus that infects bacteria, the most abundant biological entity on Earth at roughly ten to the thirty-first, with lytic and lysogenic lifestyles — lytic meaning hit-and-run killing, lysogenic meaning quiet integration with the option to switch later.

**Two**: phages were the original substrate of molecular biology. Hershey-Chase proved DNA was the genetic material in T2 phage in 1952. Lambda gave us the molecular basis of cell-fate decisions through the CI-Cro switch. The genetic code, restriction enzymes, mRNA — all worked out in phage systems.

**Three**: phage therapy was a Soviet tradition from 1923, was abandoned in the West after antibiotics arrived, and is now coming back because of antibiotic resistance. Patterson 2017, Spencer 2019, IPATH, the new biotech companies. Phages are precision predators, complementary to antibiotics, deployable as cocktails to broaden coverage and slow resistance.

**Four**: phages do many things beyond therapy. They're vaccine display platforms. They're food safety tools — ListShield is in your supermarket. They're the substrate from which CRISPR was discovered, because CRISPR is bacterial anti-phage immunity. They're cloning vectors, transduction tools, directed evolution platforms. They're an engineering substrate now — you can rewrite their host range, you can arm them with CRISPR, you can make them carry payloads.

If you've held all four of those in your head, we've done what we set out to do. Phages are precision predators, ancient research tools, returning therapeutics, and the substrate from which much of modern molecular biotech grew. They've been here for four billion years. They are coming back, with engineering and informatics on their side this time, and they may well be one of the most important antimicrobial categories of the twenty-first century.

Thanks for two hours of your attention. See you next time.

- 1917: d'Hérelle independently discovers phages in dysentery patients; coins "bacteriophage"

- 1937: Eliava executed under Stalin
- 1940: Phage group forms around Delbrück and Luria at Cold Spring Harbor
- 1943: Luria-Delbrück fluctuation experiment
- 1952: Hershey-Chase experiment establishes DNA as genetic material

**Key model phages**

- **T4**: lytic, _E. coli_, classic lunar lander morphology, large genome, the phage group's workhorse
- **Lambda (λ)**: lysogenic/lytic switch, _E. coli_, used for cloning vectors and for the CI-Cro switch story
- **M13**: chronic, non-lytic, filamentous, _E. coli_ (F+), the phage display chassis
- **P22**: _Salmonella_, transduction tool, lysogenic
- **T7**: lytic, _E. coli_, fast lifecycle, used as expression vector and vaccine display platform
- **Phi-X174**: lytic, _E. coli_, tiny genome, first sequenced

**Key concepts**

- **Lytic**: phage kills host immediately; analogy: wolf
- **Lysogenic**: phage integrates as prophage, can switch to lytic later; analogy: sleeper agent
- **Plaque assay**: one phage particle, one visible plaque in a bacterial lawn — gold-standard count method
- **Host range**: which bacterial strains a phage can infect, determined by surface receptor specificity
- **CI / Cro switch**: lambda's bistable lysogeny/lytic switch, the foundational model for cell-fate decisions
- **Cocktail**: multiple phages combined for broader coverage and resistance prevention
- **Phage-antibiotic synergy**: combined therapy can outperform either alone
- **Endotoxin removal**: a major manufacturing challenge for systemic phage products

**Key clinical cases to remember**

- **Tom Patterson, UCSD, 2016-17**: multi-drug-resistant _Acinetobacter baumannii_, rescued by emergency phage therapy, became the rallying example for the resurgence; wife Steffanie Strathdee's book _The Perfect Predator_
- **Spencer, Great Ormond Street, 2019**: cystic fibrosis patient, _Mycobacterium abscessus_, engineered lytic phage from Graham Hatfull's Pittsburgh lab
