## SEGMENT 1 — The central pharmacology — focused delivery

Alright, let's get started. Today we're going to spend two hours on one of the strangest, most beautiful, and most commercially explosive drug classes ever invented — antibody-drug conjugates, or ADCs. And I want to begin not with the molecule, but with the problem they were invented to solve, because that problem is older than oncology itself. It's older than chemistry as we know it. It's a problem that has haunted every doctor who has ever tried to kill something inside a patient without killing the patient.

The problem is this. Cancer cells are us. They are our own cells, with our own DNA, our own membranes, our own metabolism, gone rogue. The differences between a cancer cell and a healthy cell are real, but they are subtle — a few mutated genes, a few overexpressed proteins, some altered surface markers. From a chemical standpoint, a breast cancer cell and a normal breast cell are about as similar as two leaves from the same tree. So when you try to poison the cancer, you almost inevitably poison the rest of the body too. The patient loses their hair, their immune system collapses, they vomit for weeks, their gut lining sloughs off. Sometimes the chemotherapy kills them faster than the tumor would have. We accept this because we have no choice. Or — until recently — we had no choice.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy I want you to hold in your head for the next two hours. Imagine you are a general trying to bomb an enemy command bunker that happens to be located in the middle of a friendly city. You have two options. Option one: you carpet-bomb the entire city. You kill the enemy, but you also kill thousands of civilians, you flatten hospitals, you destroy infrastructure. This is traditional chemotherapy. You spray cytotoxic poison through the entire bloodstream, hoping the rapidly dividing cancer cells will die a little faster than the rapidly dividing cells in your hair follicles, your bone marrow, and your gut lining. Sometimes they do. Often they don't, fast enough.

Option two: you build a guided missile. A missile with a targeting computer that recognizes the enemy bunker — by its shape, by its radio signature, by something unique about it — and flies through the city without exploding until it reaches the exact target. Then, and only then, it detonates. The civilians live. The bunker dies. That is an antibody-drug conjugate. It is a guided missile for cancer.

The targeting computer is an antibody — a Y-shaped protein, exactly like the antibodies your immune system makes, engineered to recognize a protein that sits on the surface of cancer cells. The warhead is a chemotherapy drug so toxic that you could never inject it directly into a patient — it would kill them at doses ten thousand times below what you'd need to treat a tumor. The thing that holds the warhead onto the missile until it arrives — that's the linker, a stretch of chemistry that releases the payload only at the right moment, in the right place.

That's it. That's the whole concept. Antibody plus linker plus payload. A guided missile carrying a poison too dangerous to use any other way, delivered selectively to cells that bear the right surface marker. Everything we'll talk about today — the chemistry of the linkers, the classes of payloads, the math of how many warheads you can hang off one antibody, the bystander effect, the lung toxicity, the manufacturing nightmares — all of it hangs off that one central idea: focused delivery of a poison too toxic to use without focusing it.

\[Pause.\]

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do four things — one, sketch an ADC and tell me what each of its three components does and why; two, explain in plain English why T-DXd is rewriting the textbook on HER2-low breast cancer when T-DM1 couldn't; three, look at a new ADC in clinical trials and tell me what its target is, what payload class it carries, and roughly where it will fail; four, explain what the bystander effect is and why it's both a superpower and a source of toxicity — then we've succeeded. Everything else hangs off those four.

A quick scale-setting before we go further. Trastuzumab deruxtecan — the drug we'll call T-DXd or by its brand name Enhertu — was approved in 2019 and by 2024 it was generating over five billion dollars a year in revenue. Five billion. From one drug. It is rewriting the entire approach to HER2-low breast cancer, which is a patient population we previously thought was beyond the reach of HER2-targeted therapy at all. There are now over a dozen approved ADCs, hundreds in clinical development, and the field is producing more startup activity, more acquisitions, and more frontier chemistry than almost any other area of drug development. We are living through the ADC moment. By the end of this lecture, you'll understand why.

## SEGMENT 2 — The three components — antibody, linker, payload

Let's break the missile down into its three parts. If you take nothing else from this segment, take the three-component picture and the reason each component exists.

Picture an antibody — a Y-shape, with two arms reaching up and a stem hanging down. You've seen this molecule a thousand times. Its job in nature is to grab a target — a virus, a bacterium, an infected cell — using the variable tips of its arms, while the stem of the Y signals to the rest of the immune system that something needs to be destroyed. In an ADC, we hijack the grabbing function and add something brand new: we hang a poison off the molecule. The antibody still grabs, but now the antibody is also carrying a hidden payload, and when the whole package gets pulled inside the target cell, the payload is released and the cell dies.

So component number one is the **antibody**. This is the guidance system. It is typically a fully humanized monoclonal antibody — meaning the protein sequence has been engineered to look as much like a human-made antibody as possible, so the patient's immune system doesn't reject it. The antibody is chosen for one reason above all others: it has to bind, with high affinity, to a protein that sits on the surface of cancer cells, and ideally a protein that is much more abundant on cancer cells than on healthy cells. HER2 is the famous example — it's overexpressed on certain breast and gastric cancers at levels twenty to a hundred times higher than on normal tissue. CD30 sits on Hodgkin lymphoma cells but is essentially absent from healthy tissues. TROP2 is overexpressed on a wide variety of solid tumors. CD33 sits on acute myeloid leukemia cells. Find a surface protein that screams cancer, and you have a target.

But the antibody alone, in most cases, doesn't kill the cancer. Trastuzumab — the bare antibody against HER2 — works in some patients, but in many others it just sits on the cell surface and the cancer grows anyway. We need something more lethal hanging off it. That's where the other two components come in.

\[Pause.\]

Component number two is the **payload**. This is the warhead — the cytotoxic drug that does the actual killing. And here is the crucial point: ADC payloads are not normal chemotherapy drugs. They are not drugs you could ever give to a patient as a standalone treatment. They are, almost without exception, two to four orders of magnitude more potent than conventional chemotherapy. We're talking about molecules that kill cells at picomolar concentrations — that's billionths of millionths of a molar concentration. If you injected one of these payloads directly into someone's bloodstream at a therapeutic dose, you would almost certainly kill them.

So why do we use such ferociously toxic molecules? Because of the math. An antibody can only carry a small number of payload molecules — typically two to eight, sometimes a few more — and once you've targeted that antibody to a tumor cell, only a fraction of the injected dose actually reaches the tumor. Most ADC drugs deliver only about one percent of their injected dose to the actual tumor cells; the rest gets cleared through normal antibody recycling. So the payload that does arrive has to be potent enough that those handful of molecules per cell are sufficient to kill the cell. The whole strategy only works if the warhead is monstrously powerful. Hold that thought — we'll come back to it when we talk about drug-antibody ratio.

Common payload classes include microtubule poisons — the auristatins like MMAE and MMAF, and the maytansinoids like DM1 and DM4 — which jam up the cell's mitotic spindle and stop division. There are DNA-damaging agents like the calicheamicins, which create double-strand DNA breaks. And there are topoisomerase I inhibitors like exatecan and its derivatives, which is what T-DXd carries. We'll spend a whole segment later on the chemistry of these classes, but for now, just know: the payload is the warhead, and the warhead has to be terrifyingly potent.

\[Pause.\]

Component number three is the **linker**. The rope that holds the warhead to the missile. And this is where the engineering gets subtle and beautiful, because the linker has to do two things that are in tension with each other.

In the bloodstream, the linker has to be stable. Rock solid. The whole point of the ADC is to deliver the payload only to the cancer cell — if the linker breaks loose in circulation, the warhead floats free in the patient's blood, and you've just given them naked chemotherapy with all the systemic toxicity you were trying to avoid. So the linker needs to survive hours to days of circulation, exposure to plasma enzymes, pH changes, you name it.

But once the ADC binds its target on a cancer cell and the whole complex gets pulled inside the cell, the linker has to break. It has to release the payload. Inside the right compartment — typically the lysosome, which is the cell's acidic recycling bin — the linker needs to come apart so the warhead is freed to do its job. If the linker doesn't release, the payload stays stuck to the antibody and doesn't kill anything.

Stable outside, releasable inside. That's the linker's job. Think of it as **a rope with a controlled-release mechanism** — strong in the open air, but designed to dissolve when it hits acid, or get snipped by a specific enzyme, or break under reducing conditions. There are essentially two big design philosophies for how to do this: cleavable linkers, which break in response to some specific intracellular trigger, and non-cleavable linkers, which require the entire antibody to be digested by the lysosome before the payload is released. We'll do a whole segment on linker chemistry. For now: linker stable in blood, releasable in cell.

Three components. Antibody for targeting. Payload for killing. Linker holding them together with controlled release. That's the architecture. Every approved ADC, every ADC in clinical trials, every ADC in academic preprints — they're all variations on this same three-part theme. The art is in the choices. Which antibody, which payload, which linker, and how do you stick them together. Let's look at how this art has evolved over twenty-five years of clinical experience, starting with the drug that proved the concept could work — and then proved it could fail spectacularly.

## SEGMENT 3 — Mylotarg — proof of concept, withdrawal, and resurrection

The first ADC ever approved was a drug called gemtuzumab ozogamicin, marketed as Mylotarg. It was approved by the FDA in May of 2000, which makes it older than YouTube, older than the iPod, older than most of you in this room. And its story is the foundational drama of the entire field — concept proven, drug withdrawn, drug brought back. The Mylotarg arc is, in microcosm, the whole intellectual history of how this field learned what an ADC needs to be.

The target was CD33, a surface protein that sits on most acute myeloid leukemia cells. The antibody was a humanized anti-CD33 monoclonal. The payload was calicheamicin gamma-1, which is one of the most toxic small molecules ever isolated — it's a natural product from a soil bacterium, and what it does is bind to DNA in the minor groove and cleave both strands of the double helix simultaneously, generating breaks that are essentially unrepairable. It is a molecular guillotine for DNA. The linker was a hydrazone — a chemical bond designed to be stable at neutral pH and break under the acidic conditions of the lysosome.

You can see the logic. CD33 was a beautiful target, exquisitely AML-specific. Calicheamicin was monstrously potent. The acid-labile linker would release the payload inside the lysosome after internalization. It was supposed to be a triumph. And in 2000, it received accelerated approval for relapsed AML in older patients, based on a single-arm trial showing that about thirty percent of patients had some kind of response.

\[Pause.\]

Then it started to go wrong. The first problem was that the linker wasn't stable enough. Hydrazones break not just in lysosomes but also, slowly, in the bloodstream. So a meaningful fraction of patients had calicheamicin leaking off the antibody while the drug was still in circulation, leading to systemic toxicity that the design was supposed to prevent. The second problem was the drug-antibody ratio — the number of calicheamicin molecules conjugated to each antibody. Mylotarg was made by a random conjugation chemistry that produced a mixture: some antibodies had no payload, some had one or two, some had four or five, and a substantial fraction were unconjugated. The unconjugated antibody competed for CD33 binding without delivering any payload — essentially diluting out the active drug. We didn't fully understand this at the time.

The third problem, the one that actually killed it, was a confirmatory trial called SWOG-S0106. This was supposed to confirm the benefit of Mylotarg in newly diagnosed AML patients, added on top of standard chemotherapy. The trial showed no survival benefit, and worse — patients on the Mylotarg arm had higher rates of treatment-related deaths, primarily from a condition called veno-occlusive disease of the liver, which is a severe and sometimes fatal complication. In 2010, Pfizer pulled Mylotarg from the market voluntarily. The first ADC, the proof of concept, had been withdrawn.

For a few years, ADCs were in the doghouse. The field had its early big success — its proof that you could in principle conjugate a poison to an antibody and get tumor selectivity — and then it had its first big failure, a withdrawal that suggested maybe the concept was fatally flawed.

\[Pause.\]

But here's the twist. Investigators didn't give up on Mylotarg. They went back and looked carefully at the data, and they realized something important: at lower doses, given on different schedules, the drug worked. The issue had been dosing — the original approved dose was nine milligrams per square meter, given once. That was too much, too fast. When researchers in Europe and Japan tested fractionated dosing — three milligrams per square meter given on three separate days — the toxicity dropped dramatically and the efficacy held. Several trials in newly diagnosed AML, particularly in patients with favorable cytogenetics, showed real survival benefits when Mylotarg was added to chemotherapy at this lower fractionated dose.

So in 2017, after seven years off the market, the FDA brought Mylotarg back. Reapproved for AML, with a revised dosing schedule. The drug that had been the first ADC to be approved, and the first to be withdrawn, became also the first to be reapproved.

The lessons from Mylotarg shape every ADC developed since. Lesson one: linker stability matters enormously. A linker that leaks in circulation makes the whole strategy fail. Lesson two: the drug-antibody ratio matters, and heterogeneous DAR mixtures with lots of unconjugated antibody are a problem. Lesson three: dose and schedule are not afterthoughts. The therapeutic window of an ADC is narrow, and finding the right dose is critical. Lesson four: payload toxicity profiles need to be understood. Calicheamicin's tendency to cause hepatic veno-occlusive disease was a feature of that particular payload, and every subsequent payload class has been profiled for its own characteristic toxicities.

Mylotarg was the canary in the coal mine. It told us the concept worked. It told us the execution mattered. And every ADC that came after — every drug we're about to talk about — was designed with the Mylotarg lessons in mind.

## SEGMENT 4 — Brentuximab vedotin — Adcetris and the CD30 story

The next major ADC, the one that really vindicated the concept after the Mylotarg mess, was brentuximab vedotin, marketed as Adcetris. Approved in 2011 by the FDA, it was the first ADC approval in over a decade — Mylotarg had been withdrawn just the year before — and it landed in a clinical space where the medical need was screaming. Hodgkin lymphoma and a rare related cancer called anaplastic large-cell lymphoma. Patients who had failed chemotherapy and stem cell transplant. People with no good options.

The target was CD30, a surface protein that sits very specifically on Hodgkin lymphoma cells — particularly the Reed-Sternberg cells that are pathognomonic for classical Hodgkin disease. CD30 is essentially absent on most healthy tissues, except for a small population of activated lymphocytes. So as targets go, this was about as clean as it gets. The antibody was a chimeric anti-CD30 monoclonal called brentuximab. So far, so unsurprising.

The interesting move was the payload. Brentuximab vedotin uses **monomethyl auristatin E**, abbreviated **MMAE**. Auristatins are a synthetic class derived from a natural product called dolastatin, which was originally isolated from a sea slug in the Indian Ocean — yes, really, a sea slug. The structure was simplified and modified to make it more druggable, and MMAE was the result. MMAE works by binding to tubulin — the protein that polymerizes to form microtubules — and preventing the assembly of the mitotic spindle. Cells that try to divide can't separate their chromosomes; they arrest in mitosis and die.

\[Pause.\]

MMAE is, in the language of pharmacology, somewhere in the picomolar to low nanomolar range of cytotoxicity. You cannot give it to a patient as a free drug. The therapeutic index is too narrow; the patient would die. But hung off an antibody, delivered selectively to CD30-expressing cells, it becomes therapeutically useful. This is the ADC promise in action.

The linker was another innovation that distinguished Adcetris from Mylotarg. Instead of a hydrazone — the acid-labile chemistry that had caused so many problems with Mylotarg — the Adcetris team used a peptide linker called valine-citrulline, abbreviated **vc**. Valine-citrulline is a two-amino-acid sequence that is recognized by a specific lysosomal enzyme called cathepsin B. The linker is essentially invisible to enzymes in the bloodstream — the plasma proteases can't cut it efficiently — but inside the lysosome, where cathepsin B is abundant and active, the linker is cleaved cleanly, releasing the MMAE payload. This is much more controlled than acid-labile chemistry. The drug stays intact in circulation, and the payload is released only after internalization.

So Adcetris combined a clean target, a potent payload, and a well-designed enzymatically cleavable linker. The clinical results reflected that. In relapsed Hodgkin lymphoma after stem cell transplant, the overall response rate was about seventy-five percent, with about a third of patients achieving a complete response. These were patients with essentially no other treatment options. The drug was a clear win, and the FDA approved it on the strength of single-arm data.

In the years since, Adcetris has expanded into additional indications — frontline Hodgkin lymphoma in combination with chemotherapy, peripheral T-cell lymphoma, and others. It now generates over a billion dollars a year in revenue and has become a backbone therapy for CD30-positive lymphomas.

\[Pause.\]

But the bigger contribution of Adcetris was not just the drug itself. It was the validation of an entire technology stack. The MMAE payload, the valine-citrulline linker, the conjugation chemistry — all of this work was done by a company called Seattle Genetics, later renamed Seagen, and that platform became the template for an entire generation of ADCs. Polatuzumab vedotin, enfortumab vedotin, tisotumab vedotin — you can hear the family resemblance in the names. They all use MMAE, they all use valine-citrulline linkers, they all use the same conjugation chemistry. Adcetris proved the technology stack worked, and then dozens of follow-on drugs used the same stack with different antibodies.

Seagen was eventually acquired by Pfizer in 2023 for forty-three billion dollars, which is a useful number to bookmark when you think about how the industry values ADC platforms. Forty-three billion for the company that figured out how to make MMAE-vc ADCs reliably. The chemistry of the linker and the chemistry of the payload, both pioneered for Adcetris, are now responsible for a substantial fraction of the global ADC market.

So Adcetris is the drug that did two things. It rescued the ADC field from the post-Mylotarg slump, and it established a platform that other drugs would build on for the next fifteen years. The next major ADC, T-DM1, would take a different approach — different payload, different linker, different conjugation — and prove that there was more than one way to skin this particular cat.

## SEGMENT 5 — T-DM1 — Kadcyla and the maytansine approach

In 2013, the FDA approved a drug called ado-trastuzumab emtansine, marketed as Kadcyla. Among insiders it's almost always called **T-DM1**, which is a useful name to learn because it tells you exactly what the molecule is. The T is trastuzumab — the famous anti-HER2 antibody. The DM1 is the payload, a maytansine derivative. And the dash in between is the linker. T-DM1: trastuzumab plus DM1 plus a specific linker chemistry. Read the name, get the molecule.

The target was HER2. HER2 is a growth factor receptor that sits on the surface of many cells, but it is dramatically overexpressed on about fifteen to twenty percent of breast cancers — the so-called HER2-positive subtype. Trastuzumab, the bare antibody, had been approved back in 1998 under the brand name Herceptin, and it had transformed HER2-positive breast cancer from one of the most aggressive forms of the disease to one of the most treatable. Trastuzumab works through several mechanisms — it blocks HER2 signaling, it recruits immune cells to kill HER2-positive cells through a process called antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity. But trastuzumab alone is not curative in metastatic disease. Many patients respond initially, then progress. The question was: could we make trastuzumab more powerful by hanging a payload on it?

\[Pause.\]

The payload was **DM1**, which stands for maytansinoid 1. Maytansine is a natural product originally isolated from an Ethiopian shrub called Maytenus serrata. It was studied as a potential chemotherapy in the 1970s and 1980s and abandoned because it was too toxic to use as a free drug. Like MMAE, maytansine works on microtubules — it binds tubulin and prevents spindle formation, causing mitotic arrest. But maytansine binds at a different site on tubulin than the auristatins, and it has somewhat different cellular pharmacology. DM1 is a modified version of maytansine that has been chemically engineered to allow attachment to a linker.

The linker chemistry for T-DM1 was a deliberate departure from the Adcetris approach. Instead of a cleavable peptide linker, T-DM1 uses a **non-cleavable thioether linker** called SMCC. The name doesn't matter — what matters is the concept. The linker between the antibody and DM1 is a stable chemical bond that cannot be cleaved by any enzyme in the cell. The only way to release the payload is to digest the entire antibody itself.

How does that work? When T-DM1 binds to HER2 on a cancer cell, the whole complex gets internalized into the cell through receptor-mediated endocytosis. The vesicle containing the complex fuses with a lysosome, which is the cellular trash compactor — a low-pH compartment packed with proteases and other digestive enzymes. The lysosomal proteases chew up the antibody protein piece by piece, but they can't break the thioether linker. What's released at the end of this process is not free DM1 — it's DM1 still attached to the single amino acid residue, lysine, that it had been conjugated to on the antibody. So the active molecule that ends up free inside the cell is **lysine-MCC-DM1**, a small molecule consisting of DM1 plus the linker plus a single lysine.

\[Pause.\]

This non-cleavable design has consequences. First, it's even more stable in circulation than a cleavable linker — there's essentially no way for the payload to leak free in the bloodstream. Second, the released payload, lysine-MCC-DM1, is a charged molecule, which is important because it has trouble crossing cell membranes. Once it's released inside a target cell, it tends to stay inside that target cell. It does its job locally — killing the HER2-positive cell it was delivered to — but it doesn't easily diffuse out to kill neighboring cells.

That's a feature in some ways. It means T-DM1 has a relatively clean toxicity profile in tissues that don't express HER2. But it's also a limitation, and the limitation matters. In a tumor, HER2 expression is heterogeneous. Some cells express HER2 at very high levels. Some express it at moderate levels. Some express it at low or undetectable levels. With T-DM1, only the cells that express enough HER2 to be efficiently targeted will internalize the drug and be killed. The neighboring cells with low or no HER2 expression get a free pass. This is what oncologists call **antigen-low escape**, and it's one of the major mechanisms by which T-DM1 fails over time.

T-DM1 was approved in 2013 for HER2-positive metastatic breast cancer that had progressed on Herceptin and a taxane. It worked — it extended progression-free survival meaningfully compared to other available options. It became a standard second-line therapy for HER2-positive metastatic disease, and it has been used for years in that setting, generating about two billion dollars a year at peak. It is a real drug, with real benefit, for real patients.

But it also has real limits. The non-cleavable design and the charged payload mean that T-DM1 only kills the cells it directly targets. It can't reach into a tumor and kill cells the antibody never bound. And for patients with HER2-low disease — the much larger population whose tumors have some HER2 expression but not enough to be classified as HER2-positive — T-DM1 doesn't work at all. There's just not enough antigen for the drug to do its job.

T-DM1 was the high-water mark of the first-generation ADC design philosophy. Stable, clean, internalize-and-kill, no leakage. It did its job for HER2-positive patients. But it set the stage for the next drug, the one that would break the rules of first-generation ADC design and, in doing so, rewrite an entire chapter of breast cancer treatment. That drug is T-DXd.

## SEGMENT 6 — T-DXd — Enhertu and the second-generation revolution

In December 2019, the FDA approved trastuzumab deruxtecan, marketed as Enhertu, abbreviated **T-DXd**. The T is the same trastuzumab antibody as in T-DM1. The DXd is the payload — a derivative of exatecan, which is a topoisomerase I inhibitor. The dash in between is a different linker. Same target, same antibody, different warhead, different rope. And it turns out, when you change those last two things, you get a fundamentally different drug — one that has rewritten the textbook on breast cancer in five years.

Let me give you the headline numbers, because they're staggering. In HER2-positive metastatic breast cancer, T-DXd was compared head-to-head against T-DM1 in a trial called DESTINY-Breast03. The progression-free survival on T-DXd was about twenty-eight months. On T-DM1, it was about seven months. Four times longer. That kind of effect size is rare in oncology. We usually celebrate two-month survival improvements.

But the truly disruptive result came from a trial called DESTINY-Breast04, published in 2022. That trial enrolled patients with HER2-_low_ breast cancer — meaning their tumors had some HER2 staining, but not enough to qualify as HER2-positive. This is a patient population that was previously thought to be outside the reach of HER2-targeted therapy entirely. T-DM1 had been tried in HER2-low disease and failed. The consensus was that you needed HER2-positive expression for HER2-directed treatment to work. Period.

T-DXd ignored this consensus. In DESTINY-Breast04, T-DXd extended progression-free survival from about five months to about ten months in HER2-low metastatic breast cancer. The hazard ratio for death was around 0.64 — a thirty-six percent reduction in mortality. Overnight, the field had a new patient population to treat. Sixty percent of breast cancers, give or take, have some HER2 expression. T-DXd extended the addressable population from twenty percent of breast cancers to potentially eighty percent.

\[Pause.\]

So what changed? How did a drug that looks superficially similar to T-DM1 produce such dramatically different clinical results? The answer is mostly in two design choices that we'll explore in much more depth in the next few segments — but I'll give you the executive summary now.

Choice one: the payload. Instead of DM1, T-DXd carries deruxtecan, abbreviated DXd. DXd is a topoisomerase I inhibitor — a class of drug that includes irinotecan and topotecan, which you may have heard of as standard chemotherapy agents. Topo I inhibitors stabilize a transient DNA-protein complex that forms during DNA replication, generating DNA damage that triggers apoptosis. DXd is the engineered, ADC-optimized version of exatecan, an experimental topo I inhibitor that was abandoned as a standalone drug because it was too toxic. So once again — we're using a payload that's too toxic to give as a free drug, delivered selectively by an antibody.

Choice two: the linker. T-DXd uses a cleavable tetrapeptide linker — specifically a glycine-glycine-phenylalanine-glycine peptide that's cut by lysosomal enzymes, similar in concept to the valine-citrulline linker used in Adcetris but with different specifics. So unlike T-DM1's non-cleavable design, T-DXd's linker is cut inside the cell, releasing free DXd — not a charged DXd-amino-acid conjugate, but the actual small molecule.

That second choice is where the magic happens, and it's worth dwelling on for a moment. The free DXd molecule, once released inside a target cell, is small and uncharged enough that it can diffuse across cell membranes. It doesn't stay trapped inside the cell that internalized the ADC. It leaks out — and leaks into neighboring cells. Including, crucially, neighboring cells that don't express HER2. And in those neighboring cells, the DXd does its job — it inhibits topoisomerase I, generates DNA damage, kills the cell.

This is the **bystander effect**, and it is so important that I'm going to dedicate the entire next segment to it. But you can already see the shape of the implication. With T-DM1, only HER2-positive cells die. With T-DXd, HER2-positive cells die _and so do the neighboring cells_, regardless of their HER2 expression. In a tumor where HER2 expression is heterogeneous — high in some areas, low in others — T-DXd can reach the low-HER2 cells through bystander killing. T-DM1 cannot. That, in a sentence, is why T-DXd works in HER2-low disease where T-DM1 doesn't.

\[Pause.\]

The other design choice worth mentioning is the drug-antibody ratio. T-DM1 has an average DAR of about 3.5 — meaning each antibody carries, on average, about three and a half DM1 molecules. T-DXd has a DAR of 8 — each antibody carries eight DXd molecules. More than double the warheads per missile. And this was a deliberate choice by the Daiichi Sankyo team that designed T-DXd, but it was also risky. Historically, high-DAR ADCs had been shown to have worse pharmacokinetic properties — they got cleared from circulation faster, they aggregated in solution, they were more toxic. The conventional wisdom was that DAR should be kept in the 2 to 4 range. T-DXd doubled that, and made it work, by engineering the linker and payload to be more hydrophilic — more water-loving — so the high-DAR molecule still behaved well in plasma.

We'll do a whole segment on DAR. But for now: T-DXd carries twice the payload of T-DM1, and it carries a payload that, once released, can leak to kill neighboring cells. Different drug, different physics, different clinical reality.

By 2024, T-DXd was generating over five billion dollars in annual revenue. It's been approved in HER2-positive breast cancer, HER2-low breast cancer, HER2-mutant lung cancer, HER2-positive gastric cancer, and several other indications. It is one of the most successful drug launches in oncology history. And it is, in retrospect, the drug that proved second-generation ADC design — with cleavable linkers, bystander-capable payloads, and high DAR — could outperform first-generation ADCs by a margin that surprised even the developers.

Daiichi Sankyo, the Japanese company that developed T-DXd in partnership with AstraZeneca, became overnight one of the most valuable ADC companies in the world. We'll come back to their pipeline. But first, let's understand the bystander effect — because it's the central pharmacological superpower that makes T-DXd what it is.

## SEGMENT 7 — The bystander effect — T-DXd's leak-and-kill superpower

I keep using this phrase, the bystander effect, and now it's time to slow down and explain exactly what it is, because it's the single most important concept that distinguishes the second generation of ADCs from the first, and it's the reason T-DXd is rewriting clinical practice.

Here's the analogy. Imagine you're back to bombing that enemy command bunker in the middle of a friendly city. Your guided missile works — it flies through the city, finds the bunker, detonates inside. But the bunker is part of a complex. There are tunnels underneath, ammo dumps next door, a fuel depot down the street. None of those secondary targets are emitting the radio signal that your missile's targeting computer is locked onto. From the missile's perspective, they're invisible. They get a free pass.

Now imagine you upgrade the warhead. Instead of a contained explosion, the warhead is a chemical agent — a vapor — that, when released inside the bunker, leaks out through the air vents, drifts through the tunnels, seeps into the ammo dump, fills the fuel depot. It doesn't need to be specifically targeted at those secondary structures, because it diffuses there on its own. One missile, one detonation, but the killing radius is now much larger than just the bunker. That's the bystander effect.

\[Pause.\]

Mechanically, here's what's happening. A T-DXd molecule binds to HER2 on a cancer cell. The HER2-positive cell internalizes the ADC. Inside the lysosome, the cleavable linker is cut, and free DXd — the topoisomerase I inhibitor payload — is released inside the cell. DXd is a small molecule, only about five hundred daltons, with no permanent charges. That combination of small size and neutral charge means DXd can pass through cellular membranes by simple diffusion. It does not need a transporter. It does not need an active uptake mechanism. It just leaks.

So some of the DXd does its job in the cell that internalized it — it inhibits topoisomerase I, generates DNA damage, kills the cell. But some of the DXd leaks out across the plasma membrane and into the extracellular space, where it encounters neighboring cells. Those neighboring cells take it up — again by diffusion, no special machinery required. And in those neighbors, DXd does the same thing it did in the original cell. It inhibits topo I. It generates DNA damage. It kills them. Regardless of whether they express HER2.

This is the bystander effect. The killing radius of the drug is no longer limited to the cells the antibody can directly bind. It extends to the neighbors, the bystanders, the cells in the tumor microenvironment that happen to be sitting next to a HER2-positive cell. And in a heterogeneous tumor, where some cells are HER2-high and others are HER2-low or HER2-negative, the bystander effect dramatically expands the population of cells you can kill.

\[Pause.\]

T-DM1, by contrast, has essentially no bystander effect. Remember from the last segment that T-DM1 uses a non-cleavable linker, so the released payload is lysine-MCC-DM1 — a charged molecule that doesn't cross membranes well. Once it's released inside a cell, it tends to stay there. It kills the cell that internalized the drug, and only that cell. Neighbors are safe.

That's why T-DM1 doesn't work in HER2-low disease and T-DXd does. In HER2-low tumors, only a fraction of the cells express enough HER2 to internalize meaningful amounts of drug. With T-DM1, the rest of the tumor is essentially untouched. With T-DXd, the HER2-positive cells act as delivery beacons — they pull in the ADC, release the payload, and the payload then diffuses out to kill the surrounding HER2-low cells. The whole tumor gets hit, even though only some of it expresses the target.

You can see why this is both a superpower and a vulnerability. The superpower is obvious — expanded killing radius, applicability to heterogeneous tumors, efficacy in low-antigen patient populations. The vulnerability is that the bystander effect doesn't stop at the tumor edge. Once payload starts leaking, it can leak from circulating drug too, or from drug that's been taken up by non-tumor tissues. Healthy cells in the vicinity of any HER2-expressing tissue can be exposed to the released DXd. That's part of why T-DXd has a different toxicity profile than T-DM1 — particularly the interstitial lung disease that we'll talk about in Segment 15.

\[Pause.\]

Here's another way to think about it. **A non-bystander ADC like T-DM1 is a sniper rifle.** Every shot hits exactly the cell that bears the target. Clean. Precise. But limited to what the sniper can see. **A bystander-capable ADC like T-DXd is a sniper rifle with a small cloud of nerve agent attached to each bullet.** The bullet hits the cell, and then a cloud of poison spreads out from the impact site, killing everything nearby. More effective against dispersed enemies hiding among the visible ones. More collateral damage when the cloud drifts where it shouldn't.

For an ADC designer, the bystander effect is the central trade-off. If you design your payload to be highly membrane-permeable, you get strong bystander killing but you also get more systemic toxicity from any payload that leaks during circulation. If you design your payload to be membrane-impermeable, you get clean targeting but you lose the ability to hit antigen-low cells. Most modern ADC programs now deliberately design for bystander activity, on the bet that the clinical benefit outweighs the toxicity. T-DXd has been the proof point for that bet.

One more thing worth saying. The bystander effect is not unique to T-DXd. Several other ADCs are designed for it — sacituzumab govitecan (Trodelvy), which we'll discuss in Segment 13, is also bystander-capable. Brentuximab vedotin (Adcetris) is partially bystander-capable, because MMAE is somewhat membrane-permeable. The bystander effect is now a feature you can choose to design in or out. T-DXd just happens to be the most successful example of designing it in.

## SEGMENT 8 — Linker chemistry — cleavable versus non-cleavable

We've been touching on linker chemistry throughout the lecture. Now let's slow down and look at it properly. The linker is the most chemically interesting part of an ADC, because it has to do something that looks contradictory — be totally stable in one environment and totally unstable in another, and switch from one to the other on the timescale of cellular trafficking.

Linkers fall into two big categories. Cleavable linkers, which break in response to some specific intracellular trigger. And non-cleavable linkers, which require the entire antibody to be enzymatically digested before the payload comes off. Let me walk through each.

Cleavable linkers come in three sub-flavors, organized by the chemical trigger that releases the payload.

The first sub-flavor is **acid-labile linkers**. These are designed to break under low pH conditions — specifically, the acidic environment of the late endosome and lysosome, which can drop to a pH of about 4.5 to 5. The classic example is the hydrazone linker used in Mylotarg. Hydrazones are stable at the neutral pH of plasma, about 7.4, but they hydrolyze slowly under acidic conditions. So when the ADC gets internalized and trafficked to the lysosome, the linker breaks, releasing the payload. The problem with this design, as Mylotarg taught us, is that the stability in plasma is only relative. Hydrazones do slowly break in circulation too, especially over long half-lives. So you get some payload leakage during the days that the ADC is circulating, and that leakage drives systemic toxicity.

The second sub-flavor is **reducible linkers**, typically built around disulfide bonds. Disulfide bonds are stable in the oxidizing environment of the bloodstream but break under reducing conditions. The cytoplasm of a cell is a more reducing environment than plasma, with lots of glutathione and other reductants, so a disulfide linker breaks once the ADC payload reaches the cytoplasm. The maytansinoid family of ADCs has used disulfide linkers in some designs. The challenge with disulfides is that the stability gradient between plasma and cytoplasm is not as steep as you'd like. Some leakage happens.

The third sub-flavor is **enzymatically cleavable linkers** — the design used by Adcetris, T-DXd, and most modern ADCs. These linkers are short peptide sequences designed to be substrates for specific enzymes that are abundant inside cells but scarce outside cells. The classic example is the valine-citrulline linker, which is cleaved by cathepsin B, a lysosomal cysteine protease. Valine-citrulline is essentially invisible to extracellular proteases — citrulline is a non-standard amino acid that most enzymes don't recognize as a substrate, so plasma proteases don't cut it. But cathepsin B inside the lysosome cuts it cleanly. The result is a linker that is exceptionally stable in plasma but cleaves promptly once inside the lysosome. T-DXd uses a similar concept with a different peptide sequence — a glycine-rich tetrapeptide.

\[Pause.\]

The enzymatic strategy is, broadly, the winning approach today. The peptide chemistry is well understood. The enzymes you're targeting are well characterized. The stability in plasma can be measured directly. And the engineering window — the difference between plasma stability and lysosomal cleavage rate — is much wider than for acid-labile or disulfide chemistry.

Non-cleavable linkers, the other big category, are exemplified by the SMCC thioether linker used in T-DM1. The chemistry here is straightforward: there is no designed trigger for cleavage. The linker is just a stable chemical bond. The way the payload gets released is that the entire antibody portion of the ADC gets digested by lysosomal proteases. The proteins are chewed up amino acid by amino acid, and at the end of this process, the only thing left is the payload still attached to one of those amino acids — typically a lysine, in the case of SMCC-DM1. That amino-acid-payload conjugate is the active species.

The advantage of non-cleavable linkers is stability — the linker simply cannot break in circulation, because there's no chemistry that breaks it short of proteolysis of the whole antibody. The disadvantages are two. First, the released payload is a charged amino acid conjugate, which is membrane-impermeable, which means no bystander effect. Second, the kinetics of release are slow — protein digestion in the lysosome takes time, so the active payload appears more gradually than with a cleavable linker.

\[Pause.\]

Which approach is better? It depends on what you want. If your target is highly homogeneous on the tumor — every cancer cell expresses lots of the target — then a non-cleavable linker can work very well. The bystander effect isn't necessary because every cell is directly targeted. The added stability of the linker reduces systemic toxicity, which improves the therapeutic window.

If your target is heterogeneous, or if you want the drug to work in low-antigen patients, then you almost certainly want a cleavable linker with bystander activity. The killing radius extension is worth the slightly worse stability.

The history of the field has trended toward cleavable, bystander-capable linkers, largely because of T-DXd's success. Most new ADC programs are now designed with this approach as the default. But the non-cleavable design has not gone away entirely — T-DM1 is still on the market, still useful for HER2-positive disease, and the chemistry of non-cleavable linkers has its place.

One more thing to know about linkers — they're not just chemistry, they're also pharmacology. The linker affects the solubility, the aggregation behavior, the plasma half-life, the off-target distribution of the entire ADC. A linker that is too hydrophobic — too water-fearing — makes the high-DAR ADC aggregate in solution and get cleared faster from circulation. A linker that is too hydrophilic — too water-loving — may not pack well against the antibody and may expose the payload to inappropriate enzymes. The right linker chemistry is a balance of stability, cleavability, solubility, and steric accessibility. T-DXd's linker has a hydrophilic spacer specifically to allow the high DAR-8 design without causing aggregation. The chemistry is finely tuned. There are no free lunches in linker design.

## SEGMENT 9 — Payload classes — tubulin, DNA damage, topoisomerase

Now let's take a tour of the actual warheads. The payload is the cytotoxic molecule that does the killing once it's released. ADC payloads fall into three big functional classes today, plus a few emerging classes that we'll touch on in the final segment. The three big classes are tubulin inhibitors, DNA-damaging agents, and topoisomerase I inhibitors. Let me walk through each one with examples.

**Tubulin inhibitors** are the most common ADC payload class historically. They work by binding to the protein tubulin, which is the building block of microtubules — the filaments that form the mitotic spindle when a cell divides. When a cell tries to divide, it needs to assemble microtubules to pull its duplicated chromosomes apart into the two daughter cells. If tubulin is jammed up by an inhibitor, the spindle can't form, the chromosomes can't separate, the cell arrests in mitosis, and eventually triggers apoptosis.

There are two famous sub-families. The **auristatins** — MMAE and MMAF — derived from a sea slug natural product called dolastatin. MMAE is the cleavable-linker-friendly version; MMAF has a charge that makes it membrane-impermeable and is therefore typically paired with non-cleavable linkers. Adcetris uses MMAE. So do enfortumab vedotin (Padcev, for urothelial cancer), polatuzumab vedotin (Polivy, for diffuse large B-cell lymphoma), and several others.

The **maytansinoids** — DM1 and DM4 — derived from an Ethiopian shrub. They bind tubulin at a different site than the auristatins. T-DM1 uses DM1. Mirvetuximab soravtansine, an ADC against folate receptor alpha for ovarian cancer, uses DM4.

Tubulin inhibitor payloads are mature technology. We've been using them for over a decade. We know their toxicities, their pharmacokinetics, their interactions. But they have limitations. They only kill dividing cells — cells in interphase, not actively dividing, are relatively resistant. And tumors can develop resistance through several well-characterized mechanisms.

\[Pause.\]

**DNA-damaging agents** are the second class. These are payloads that bind to DNA itself, or to DNA-associated proteins, and generate damage that the cell can't repair. The most famous example is **calicheamicin**, the payload used in Mylotarg. Calicheamicin binds in the minor groove of DNA and, through a unique chemistry involving an enediyne functional group, generates two carbon-centered radicals that simultaneously abstract hydrogen atoms from each strand of the DNA double helix, creating a double-strand break. The chemistry is genuinely beautiful and genuinely terrifying. Calicheamicin is one of the most potent cytotoxins ever isolated, with picomolar potency.

Other DNA-damaging payloads include the **pyrrolobenzodiazepines**, or PBDs, which form covalent cross-links in DNA. Loncastuximab tesirine, an anti-CD19 ADC for diffuse large B-cell lymphoma, uses a PBD dimer. These molecules are even more potent than calicheamicin in some cellular assays — sub-picomolar activity — and the resulting ADCs have shown impressive single-agent activity in lymphomas.

The downside of DNA-damaging payloads is that they tend to have characteristic toxicities — particularly to bone marrow, where cells are constantly dividing and the DNA repair machinery is heavily engaged. They also tend to be mutagenic in their own right, which raises long-term safety concerns. The use of these payloads has therefore been more limited than tubulin inhibitors, despite their potency.

\[Pause.\]

**Topoisomerase I inhibitors** are the third class, and they're the rising stars. Topoisomerase I is an enzyme that relaxes DNA supercoiling during replication and transcription by transiently nicking one strand of the DNA, allowing rotation, and then religating the nick. Topo I inhibitors — like camptothecin and its derivatives irinotecan, topotecan, and exatecan — bind to the transient enzyme-DNA complex and prevent religation, leaving a single-strand break behind. When a replication fork hits one of these stuck complexes, it converts to a double-strand break, which kills the cell.

The ADC version of this class is **DXd**, the deruxtecan payload in T-DXd, derived from exatecan. The ADC version in **SN-38** is the active metabolite of irinotecan, which is the payload in sacituzumab govitecan (Trodelvy). We'll discuss Trodelvy in detail in Segment 13.

Topo I inhibitors have several attractive properties for ADC payloads. They're potent — picomolar to low nanomolar. They kill non-dividing cells better than tubulin inhibitors do, because topo I is engaged not just in replication but also in transcription. They have relatively manageable toxicity profiles — the main dose-limiting toxicity tends to be neutropenia and diarrhea, both of which are reversible. And they appear to be effective against tumors that have become resistant to tubulin inhibitor ADCs, because the mechanism of cell killing is completely different.

The rise of topo I payloads is one of the major stories of the last five years of ADC development. Almost every new ADC program in the clinic now involves either a topo I payload or, less commonly, a novel payload class we'll discuss in Segment 18.

So that's the warhead taxonomy. Tubulin inhibitors for mature applications. DNA-damaging agents for high-potency specialty uses. Topo I inhibitors for the new wave of broadly applicable, bystander-capable ADCs. Three classes, dozens of specific molecules, and the toolkit keeps growing.

## SEGMENT 10 — DAR — the drug-antibody ratio

Welcome back. Let's pick up with a number — a single number that, more than almost any other parameter, controls the behavior of an ADC. It's called the **drug-antibody ratio**, or **DAR**, and it's exactly what it sounds like: the average number of payload molecules conjugated to each antibody. DAR is the warhead count per missile, and choosing the right DAR is one of the central engineering trade-offs in ADC design.

The intuition is straightforward. More warheads per missile means more killing power delivered per molecule of antibody. If you can hang ten payloads off an antibody instead of two, in principle you're delivering five times the cell-killing dose per antibody-cell binding event. That sounds great. Why not just keep cranking up the DAR — twenty, thirty, fifty payloads per antibody?

The reason is that the antibody is not an infinitely modifiable scaffold. When you attach payload molecules to an antibody, you're typically attaching them to specific amino acid residues — most commonly lysines, which are scattered all over the surface of the protein, or cysteines, which are typically generated by partially reducing the antibody's internal disulfide bonds. Each conjugation site changes the local chemistry. Each payload molecule is typically pretty hydrophobic. As you add more and more hydrophobic payloads to the protein surface, the antibody starts to look less like a soluble protein and more like a greasy aggregation-prone mess.

\[Pause.\]

Here's what actually happens, empirically. At low DARs — two to four — the antibody behaves more or less normally in plasma. Long half-life, good tumor penetration, manageable toxicity. At intermediate DARs — four to six — you start to see some effects on pharmacokinetics, but the drug is still well-behaved. At high DARs — eight and above — historically, you saw rapid clearance from circulation, aggregation in solution, and a narrower therapeutic window. The high-DAR molecules were getting cleared by the liver — particularly by Kupffer cells, the resident macrophages of the liver, which recognize the hydrophobic patches on the protein surface as something to eat.

So for the first generation of ADCs — including Mylotarg and T-DM1 and even Adcetris — the consensus DAR was in the 3-to-4 range. Anything higher seemed to compromise the drug.

T-DXd broke this rule. T-DXd has an average DAR of 8 — twice the conventional limit — and it works. How? Because the Daiichi Sankyo team engineered the linker and payload to be much more hydrophilic than previous designs. The DXd payload itself has hydrophilic functional groups, and the tetrapeptide linker carries a polyethylene glycol-like spacer that adds more water-loving character. The net result is that even with eight payloads per antibody, the molecule stays soluble, doesn't aggregate, and isn't cleared faster than normal by the liver. The therapeutic window is preserved.

This was a significant engineering achievement, and it has changed how the field thinks about DAR. The old rule — keep DAR low — has been replaced by a new rule: optimize linker and payload hydrophilicity to enable higher DARs without pharmacokinetic compromise. Several new ADCs in development are targeting DARs of 8 or even higher, betting that with the right chemistry, the increased potency per antibody outweighs the risks.

\[Pause.\]

There's one more wrinkle. When you do conjugation chemistry to attach payloads to an antibody, you don't get a clean molecule with exactly the same number of payloads on every antibody. You get a distribution. Some antibodies will have zero payloads. Some will have two. Some will have eight. The DAR you report is the average across the population. The actual distribution might look like a curve — a bell curve, or sometimes a bimodal distribution with peaks at zero and four.

This heterogeneity matters clinically. The antibodies with zero payloads are basically dead weight — they bind to the target, occupy receptors, but deliver no drug. They compete with the loaded antibodies for tumor binding, diluting out the effective dose. The antibodies with very high payload loading are more toxic and get cleared faster. The intermediate species — somewhere around the average DAR — are the workhorses.

For Mylotarg, this was a particular problem. The original conjugation chemistry produced a population with a substantial fraction of unconjugated antibody, and that unconjugated antibody was eating up CD33 binding sites without delivering payload. When researchers analyzed why Mylotarg sometimes worked and sometimes didn't, the heterogeneity of the DAR distribution was a significant contributor.

This is one of the reasons site-specific conjugation has become a major area of ADC research — because if you can put payloads at exactly defined positions on the antibody, you get a much more uniform DAR distribution, often referred to as a "homogeneous" ADC. Same number of payloads on every antibody. No dead-weight unconjugated species. No over-loaded toxic species. Just one defined molecule. That's the next segment.

For now, take away: DAR is the warhead count. Choose it carefully, balance it against pharmacokinetics, and remember that the average hides a distribution. The field has moved from a default of DAR 3-4 to DAR 8 for the most modern ADCs, enabled by better linker chemistry. And the next frontier is making sure every antibody in the batch has exactly the same DAR.

## SEGMENT 11 — Site-specific conjugation — engineered docking sites

Let's spend a segment on conjugation chemistry, which is the chemistry of physically attaching the payload to the antibody. This is the step that, more than any other, determines the quality and reproducibility of the ADC. The historical approach to conjugation is what we call **stochastic conjugation** — meaning the chemistry happens at multiple possible sites on the antibody, more or less at random, producing the heterogeneous mixture I described in the last segment. The newer approach, called **site-specific conjugation**, engineers the antibody so that payloads can only attach at defined positions.

Let me describe the two main stochastic methods first, because they're what most current ADCs use.

The first method is **lysine conjugation**. Antibodies have a lot of lysine residues — typically eighty to ninety per molecule, scattered across the surface of the protein. Lysines have a primary amine in their side chain, and primary amines react with certain electrophilic chemistry — typically activated esters like NHS esters, or related electrophiles. So if you mix an antibody with a payload-linker construct that has an activated ester at one end, the payload will attach itself to lysines on the antibody. But it'll attach to many different lysines, in many different combinations, producing a mixture. Lysine conjugation is what Mylotarg used. It's chemically simple but messy.

The second method is **cysteine conjugation** through interchain disulfide reduction. An IgG antibody has four interchain disulfide bonds — two between the heavy chains in the hinge region, and one connecting each heavy chain to a light chain. If you partially reduce the antibody with a mild reductant, you can break some of these disulfides and expose free cysteine residues. Those cysteines have a thiol group that reacts with maleimide chemistry — typically the payload-linker has a maleimide group at one end, and it reacts with the free cysteine to form a stable thioether bond. There are eight possible cysteine sites once you fully reduce the interchain bonds, so this gives you a DAR of up to 8, with payloads at relatively defined positions. This is what Adcetris and T-DXd use, more or less.

Cysteine conjugation through hinge reduction is more controlled than lysine conjugation, but it's not fully site-specific. The partial reduction gives you a distribution of zero, two, four, six, or eight payloads, depending on how many disulfides got reduced and how many cysteines got loaded.

\[Pause.\]

Site-specific conjugation tries to get away from the mixture entirely. The goal is to put payloads at exactly defined positions, on every antibody, in defined numbers.

There are several approaches. One is **engineered cysteine** — you take your antibody and you mutate one or two specific positions to introduce free cysteines that are accessible to conjugation chemistry. Genentech developed a platform called THIOMAB for this, where they identified specific positions on the antibody where a cysteine could be introduced without disrupting structure or function but would still be reactive enough for clean conjugation. You then conjugate to just those engineered cysteines, leaving the natural disulfide bonds intact. The result is a DAR-2 ADC with payload at exactly defined positions.

A second approach is **unnatural amino acids**, abbreviated **ncAA** or **uAA**. These are amino acids that don't exist in nature — synthesized by chemists, then introduced into the protein at specific positions using an engineered translation system. The most common ones for ADC conjugation include para-acetylphenylalanine, which has a ketone group that can be selectively conjugated through oxime chemistry, and various azide-containing amino acids that participate in click chemistry. By incorporating an ncAA at a specific position, you create what is essentially a custom docking site — a chemical handle that nothing else in the antibody can react with. Conjugation is then exquisitely selective.

A third approach is **enzymatic conjugation** — using an enzyme like transglutaminase or sortase to attach payloads at sequence-defined positions. Transglutaminase, for example, recognizes specific glutamine residues and can be used to attach payloads via lysine-containing linkers in a one-step reaction.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy I want you to hold. **Stochastic conjugation is like dropping payloads onto a sticky surface — they stick wherever they land. Site-specific conjugation is like building a docking port at a specific position on the antibody — payloads can only attach there, nowhere else.** With the dock approach, every antibody in your batch has the dock in the same place, and after conjugation, every antibody has exactly one payload at exactly that position. Or if you build two docks, every antibody has exactly two payloads. Defined product. Reproducible chemistry. Predictable behavior.

The clinical benefit of site-specific conjugation is still being established. Several site-specific ADCs are in clinical trials. The theoretical benefit is clear — more uniform pharmacokinetics, less risk of toxic high-DAR species, less waste from low-DAR species, easier manufacturing analytics. The question is whether the practical benefit, in patients, is worth the extra engineering complexity.

There's also a click chemistry angle here. **Click chemistry** is the name for a family of reactions — most famously the copper-catalyzed azide-alkyne cycloaddition, or CuAAC, and more recently the strain-promoted azide-alkyne cycloaddition, or SPAAC — that form covalent bonds with exquisite selectivity, in water, at room temperature, without side reactions. Carolyn Bertozzi won the Nobel Prize in 2022 for the development of bioorthogonal click chemistry, and one of the major applications of that work is in site-specific ADC conjugation. You install an azide on the antibody at a defined position, install an alkyne on the payload-linker, and then click them together. Boom. Defined product, every time.

Site-specific conjugation is, at this point, more of a frontier topic than a dominant production technology. But it's almost certainly the future. The improvement in product uniformity is so attractive, and the technologies are advancing so quickly, that within ten years most new ADCs will probably be made site-specifically. The current generation of stochastically conjugated ADCs — including T-DXd — work well enough that the field hasn't been forced to switch. But the next generation will get there.

## SEGMENT 12 — The pipeline — Daiichi Sankyo, ADC Therapeutics, Pfizer

Let's step back from chemistry and look at the industry. Who's making these drugs, who's making money, and what does the pipeline look like? Because the ADC space, in 2024 and 2025, is one of the most active areas of pharma — both in clinical development and in M&A activity.

The dominant player is **Daiichi Sankyo**, a Japanese company that historically wasn't even a major oncology player. Daiichi developed the DXd payload and the tetrapeptide linker that powers T-DXd, and they have built an entire pipeline of ADCs around that platform. Beyond T-DXd, they have datopotamab deruxtecan — an anti-TROP2 ADC with the same DXd payload, currently in late-stage trials for lung cancer and breast cancer. They have patritumab deruxtecan — an anti-HER3 ADC, also with DXd, in development for lung cancer. They have ifinatamab deruxtecan, ireltrutamab deruxtecan, and others — basically the same payload-linker platform attached to different antibodies against different targets. Each of these is potentially a multi-billion-dollar drug if it works.

In 2023, Daiichi signed a partnership with Merck — the U.S. one, the one that makes Keytruda — that included up to twenty-two billion dollars in payments for the rights to co-develop three of these ADCs. Twenty-two billion. Some of that is upfront, much of it is contingent on regulatory and commercial milestones. But the headline number tells you how the industry values the Daiichi platform.

\[Pause.\]

**Pfizer** entered the ADC space aggressively through acquisition. In 2023 they bought Seagen — the company we mentioned earlier, the one that developed Adcetris and the MMAE-vc platform — for forty-three billion dollars. Seagen brought to Pfizer not just Adcetris, but also enfortumab vedotin (Padcev, for urothelial cancer), tisotumab vedotin (Tivdak, for cervical cancer), and a pipeline of additional MMAE-based ADCs. **AstraZeneca** is the other major Western player, primarily through its partnership with Daiichi Sankyo. AstraZeneca co-promotes T-DXd, and they have invested heavily in building out the ADC franchise. They also have internal programs.

**Roche** has T-DM1 (Kadcyla) and polatuzumab vedotin (Polivy) and a pipeline of follow-on ADCs. They're a major player, though their pipeline has been slower to advance than Daiichi's.

**Gilead** acquired Immunomedics in 2020 for twenty-one billion dollars, primarily to get sacituzumab govitecan (Trodelvy), which we'll discuss in detail in Segment 13.

\[Pause.\]

**ADC Therapeutics** is a Swiss-American company focused specifically on ADCs, currently best known for loncastuximab tesirine (Zynlonta), an anti-CD19 ADC with a PBD payload, approved for diffuse large B-cell lymphoma. They have a pipeline of other PBD-based ADCs and a more focused, single-technology platform than the big pharma players. The Chinese ADC industry is producing molecules at a rapid clip and licensing them out to Western companies for global development.

In the last few years, we've seen Pfizer-Seagen at $43 billion, Gilead-Immunomedics at $21 billion, Merck-Daiichi at $22 billion in committed payments, AbbVie-ImmunoGen at $10 billion. Hundreds of millions in upfront payments for early-stage assets. The collective investment in ADCs over the last five years is somewhere between fifty and a hundred billion dollars, depending on how you count.

Why all this activity? Two reasons. First, the clinical results are real — T-DXd is genuinely transforming breast cancer outcomes, and the technology has expanded to other indications. Second, the patent cliffs facing big pharma in the next few years are enormous. Keytruda's patent expires in 2028. Revlimid's already gone. Many other multi-billion-dollar drugs are coming off patent. Pharma companies need new growth engines, and ADCs look like one of the most promising.

The pipeline is broad. Antibodies against TROP2, HER3, B7-H3, CEACAM5, NaPi2b, Nectin-4, MUC16, CD19, CD22, CD37, CD79b, and a dozen other targets are in various stages of clinical development as ADC components. The combinatorial space is enormous — any of these antibodies times any of several payload classes times any of several linker designs times any of several conjugation chemistries. We are at the beginning of a massive expansion of the ADC repertoire, not the end of it.

That's the industrial landscape. Now let's go back to specific drugs and look at one I haven't yet discussed in detail — Trodelvy, the TROP2 ADC.

## SEGMENT 13 — Trodelvy and the TROP2 story

Sacituzumab govitecan, marketed as Trodelvy, was approved by the FDA in 2020 for metastatic triple-negative breast cancer. It is the leading example of an ADC that targets TROP2 — and TROP2 is one of the most important new targets in oncology.

Let me set up the clinical problem. Triple-negative breast cancer is the subtype of breast cancer that lacks expression of all three of the major druggable receptors — estrogen receptor, progesterone receptor, and HER2. About fifteen percent of breast cancers are triple-negative, and they tend to behave aggressively. Because they lack the targets that the other breast cancer subtypes are treated against, these patients have historically had limited options beyond cytotoxic chemotherapy. Triple-negative disease was, until recently, the breast cancer subtype that had benefited least from precision medicine.

Trodelvy targets **TROP2**, which stands for trophoblast cell surface antigen 2. TROP2 is a transmembrane glycoprotein expressed at low levels on many normal epithelial tissues and at much higher levels on a wide variety of carcinomas — breast, lung, urothelial, gastric, ovarian, you name it. It's overexpressed on essentially all triple-negative breast cancers. As an ADC target, TROP2 is attractive because it's broad — many tumor types express it — but it has the wrinkle that it's also expressed at low levels on many normal tissues, so the therapeutic window can be tight.

\[Pause.\]

The antibody in Trodelvy is sacituzumab, a humanized anti-TROP2 IgG. The payload is **SN-38**, which is the active metabolite of irinotecan — a topoisomerase I inhibitor in the camptothecin family. The linker is an interesting choice — it's a hydrolyzable carbonate linker designed to be moderately stable in plasma but to slowly release SN-38 even in circulation. This is unusual. Most modern ADCs aim for very tight plasma stability. Trodelvy's linker is deliberately leakier than, say, T-DXd's.

Why? Because it gives Trodelvy a different kind of bystander effect. The free SN-38 generated by slow linker hydrolysis is small, membrane-permeable, and cytotoxic — so even before the ADC reaches the tumor, some payload is being released and circulating. This sounds like a bug, but it's part of the design. Trodelvy has high DAR — about 7.6 — and the combination of high DAR plus a moderately leaky linker delivers a lot of free SN-38 to the tumor environment, where it can kill TROP2-low cells that the antibody itself wouldn't efficiently target.

Of course, this design choice also drives toxicity. The dose-limiting side effects of Trodelvy include severe diarrhea — characteristic of free SN-38 — and neutropenia. Both of these are reversible with supportive care, but they require careful clinical management.

\[Pause.\]

Clinically, Trodelvy has been a real win. In metastatic triple-negative breast cancer that had failed prior therapy, Trodelvy extended overall survival by about five months compared to chemotherapy of physician's choice. That's a meaningful benefit in a population that previously had nothing. It's now approved for triple-negative breast cancer, HR-positive HER2-negative breast cancer, and metastatic urothelial cancer.

The bigger story is what Trodelvy proved about TROP2 as a target. After Trodelvy validated TROP2, a whole wave of follow-on TROP2-targeted ADCs entered development. Daiichi's datopotamab deruxtecan, which we mentioned in the last segment, is anti-TROP2 with a DXd payload — a different chemistry from Trodelvy. Several other companies have TROP2 ADCs in trials with various payloads and linkers. The space is crowded and competitive, and the eventual winners will be determined by which combination of antibody, payload, linker, and DAR produces the best therapeutic window in each indication.

Trodelvy is also a study in how a single drug can validate an entire target class. Before Trodelvy, TROP2 was a relatively obscure target that some pharmacologists thought might be tractable. After Trodelvy, every major oncology company has a TROP2 program. That's the leverage that a successful ADC can have on the broader research agenda.

## SEGMENT 14 — Bispecific ADCs and multi-payload designs

Let's spend a segment on the design frontier — the next generation of ADCs that go beyond the simple antibody-linker-payload architecture. Two ideas dominate this frontier: bispecific antibodies as the targeting component, and multi-payload designs that carry more than one type of warhead.

**Bispecific ADCs** use an antibody that recognizes two different targets simultaneously. The conventional ADC antibody binds one antigen — HER2, or CD30, or TROP2. A bispecific antibody is engineered to have one arm that binds one target and another arm that binds a different target. As an ADC, this can do several things that monospecific ADCs cannot.

One application is to require dual antigen expression for ADC uptake. If your antibody only binds cells that express both target A and target B, you can be much more selective for tumor cells, because the combination of two markers may be uniquely cancer-associated even when each marker individually is also found on normal tissues. The two arms have to grab simultaneously to internalize the drug. Healthy cells with only one of the two markers don't pull in enough ADC to be killed.

Another application is to use one arm for tumor targeting and the other arm to drive internalization. Some good cancer targets — proteins that are overexpressed on tumors — don't internalize efficiently when an antibody binds them. They just sit on the cell surface. By pairing such a target with a second, more endocytically active receptor on the same cell, you can get the tumor selectivity of the first target plus the internalization kinetics of the second.

A third application is to deliver payload to one cell while simultaneously engaging an immune cell. We're going to come back to immunostimulatory payloads in Segment 18, but you can imagine an ADC where one arm targets a tumor antigen, the other arm engages a T cell receptor, and the payload is a checkpoint inhibitor or a cytokine. These designs blur the line between ADC and bispecific T-cell engager.

\[Pause.\]

**Multi-payload ADCs** carry more than one type of warhead. The simplest version is an ADC that carries two different payloads — say, a tubulin inhibitor and a topoisomerase inhibitor — so that the killing mechanism is combinatorial. A tumor cell that has become resistant to one mechanism may still be vulnerable to the other. This is conceptually analogous to combination chemotherapy, but in a single molecule.

The chemistry of multi-payload conjugation is tricky. You need to install two different conjugation sites on the antibody, each chemically orthogonal to the other, so that you can attach payload A to site one and payload B to site two without cross-reactions. Site-specific conjugation chemistry — which we covered in Segment 11 — is essentially required for this. You can't reliably make multi-payload ADCs with stochastic conjugation; the mixtures get hopelessly complex.

Several multi-payload ADCs are in early clinical development. The data is still preliminary, but the concept is promising. If you can get clean combinatorial killing in a single targeted molecule, you may be able to outpace resistance mechanisms that affect any single payload.

Beyond these two specific frontiers, the broader design philosophy is becoming modular. Think of the antibody, the linker, and the payload as three independent dimensions, each with multiple options. The art of next-generation ADC design is choosing the right combination for each specific clinical problem. We'll come back to that toolkit framing in the final segment.

## SEGMENT 15 — Clinical management — ILD, ocular toxicity, neutropenia

Now let's get clinical for a segment. Because however elegant the chemistry, the drugs have to be tolerable for patients to actually benefit from them. And ADCs have characteristic toxicity profiles — not generic chemotherapy toxicities, but specific patterns driven by each drug's design. The oncologist using these drugs has to understand the toxicity signatures and manage them proactively.

The toxicity that has gotten the most attention in recent years is **interstitial lung disease**, or **ILD**, associated with T-DXd. Roughly ten to fifteen percent of patients treated with T-DXd develop some form of ILD — meaning inflammation and scarring in the lung tissue. In most cases, it's mild — graded one or two on the standard five-point scale. But a small percentage of patients develop severe ILD, which can be life-threatening or fatal. In the original T-DXd trials, there were deaths from ILD, and the FDA labeling carries a black-box warning.

Here's the analogy I want you to hold. **ILD is the lung-leak toxicity** — the price T-DXd pays for its bystander effect. Remember, the bystander effect works because the DXd payload, once released, can diffuse to neighboring cells regardless of antigen status. That's great in a tumor, where the neighbors are also cancer. But the lung is full of rapidly dividing cells that may pick up trace amounts of circulating ADC or free payload, and over time, the cumulative damage manifests as inflammation. The same property that gives T-DXd its clinical superpower against HER2-low disease is the same property that drives lung toxicity.

\[Pause.\]

Clinical management of ILD involves vigilance. Patients on T-DXd are monitored with chest imaging on a regular schedule, and any new respiratory symptoms — cough, shortness of breath, fatigue — trigger a workup. If ILD is detected, the response depends on grade. Grade 1 — asymptomatic, found only on imaging — often allows continuation with closer monitoring. Grade 2 and above — symptomatic — typically requires permanent drug discontinuation and treatment with corticosteroids. The decision to stop is consequential, because for many patients T-DXd is the most effective therapy they have access to. But the risk of progression to fatal ILD is real, and the threshold for stopping is appropriately low.

A second important toxicity is **ocular toxicity**, particularly seen with the MMAF-containing ADCs like belantamab mafodotin. MMAF accumulates in the corneal epithelium and causes microcystic changes that can blur vision and, in some patients, cause significant visual loss. Belantamab mafodotin was actually withdrawn from the U.S. market in 2022, then reapproved with modifications, partly because of the ocular toxicity profile. Patients on MMAF ADCs require ophthalmologic monitoring, and dose modifications are often required when corneal changes are detected.

A third category is **neutropenia and diarrhea**, particularly with topo I-payload ADCs like Trodelvy. Severe neutropenia — low neutrophil counts — increases infection risk and may require growth factor support with G-CSF. Severe diarrhea from SN-38 leakage may require aggressive antidiarrheal therapy and dose reduction.

A fourth category is **hepatic toxicity**, seen with multiple ADC classes but particularly with calicheamicin-based drugs like Mylotarg. Veno-occlusive disease of the liver is a serious complication, and Mylotarg's labeling carries warnings about it.

\[Pause.\]

The general principle is that ADCs are not free lunches. They are dramatically more selective than free chemotherapy in some respects, but they have their own characteristic toxicities driven by the payload class, the linker stability, and the specific tissues where antigen happens to be expressed at low levels. The oncologist using an ADC has to know its specific toxicity profile and monitor for the relevant complications. ILD for T-DXd. Ocular toxicity for MMAF. Diarrhea and neutropenia for topo I ADCs. Hepatic disease for calicheamicin ADCs.

And as the field expands, new payload classes will introduce new toxicities. This is, in some sense, unavoidable. Every payload has off-target effects, and every linker chemistry has imperfect stability, and every antigen has at least some normal-tissue expression. The art is in matching the toxicity profile to the clinical situation and managing the risks appropriately.

## SEGMENT 16 — Resistance — antigen loss and efflux

ADCs, like every other class of cancer drug, eventually fail. Some patients respond initially and then progress. Some patients never respond at all. Understanding how cancer cells become resistant to ADCs is an active area of research, and several mechanisms have been characterized.

The simplest mechanism is **antigen loss**. The cancer cells stop expressing the target protein. If the antigen isn't there, the antibody has nothing to bind, no internalization happens, and no payload is delivered. This is especially common with heterogeneous tumors where the original tumor had a mix of antigen-high and antigen-low cells. The antigen-high cells get killed by the ADC, but the antigen-low cells survive and eventually become the dominant population. Selection in action.

For T-DM1, antigen loss is a major mechanism of resistance, because the drug has no bystander effect — only the antigen-high cells get killed in the first place. For T-DXd, the bystander effect mitigates this somewhat, because even antigen-low cells can be killed through diffusion of released payload. But over time, even tumors treated with bystander-capable ADCs can lose enough antigen expression to become resistant.

\[Pause.\]

A second mechanism is **drug efflux**. Cancer cells often upregulate transporters — particularly the ATP-binding cassette transporters like P-glycoprotein (also called MDR1 or ABCB1), MRP1, and BCRP — that actively pump small molecules out of the cell. Once the payload is released inside a target cell, an efflux pump can grab it and pump it back out before it does damage. This is a classic chemotherapy resistance mechanism, and it applies to ADC payloads too, because the payloads are typically substrates for the same transporters that handle conventional chemotherapy.

Different payloads have different susceptibility to efflux. MMAE, the auristatin in Adcetris, is a known P-glycoprotein substrate, and P-gp upregulation is a known resistance mechanism. DM1 is also a P-gp substrate. The DXd payload appears to be a weaker P-gp substrate, which may contribute to T-DXd's broader efficacy. Choosing payloads that are poor efflux substrates is one of the design considerations going into newer ADCs.

A third mechanism is **defective trafficking**. The cancer cell may internalize the ADC normally but then fail to deliver it to the lysosome efficiently. Endocytic vesicles may get recycled back to the cell surface without ever fusing with the lysosome. The ADC gets re-secreted instead of digested. No payload is released. This is harder to detect clinically but has been documented in resistant cell lines.

A fourth mechanism is **payload-specific resistance**. Tumors may upregulate the molecular target of the payload — for example, downregulating topoisomerase I to reduce sensitivity to DXd, or mutating tubulin to reduce sensitivity to MMAE. These are slow-developing resistance mechanisms but they have been observed.

\[Pause.\]

What do you do about resistance? Several strategies are being pursued.

One is **switching payload classes**. If a patient progresses on a tubulin-targeting ADC, switching to a topo I-targeting ADC with the same antibody may restore activity. The antibody-target axis still works; only the payload-target axis has failed. T-DM1-failure patients often respond to T-DXd, even though both drugs target HER2, because they have different payloads.

Another is **combination therapy**. Combining an ADC with a checkpoint inhibitor, or with another ADC targeting a different antigen, or with a small-molecule kinase inhibitor, may overcome resistance through complementary mechanisms. Many such combinations are in trials.

A third is **rotating ADCs**. Some emerging strategies involve cycling between two or more ADCs against different targets, exploiting the diversity of antigen expression in a tumor to prevent selection of any single resistant population.

Resistance is the central long-term challenge in ADC therapy. The drugs work — beautifully — in the short term. The question for the field is how to extend the durability of response from months to years. That requires understanding resistance mechanisms and designing therapy strategies that anticipate them.

## SEGMENT 17 — Manufacturing complexity

Let's talk about manufacturing, because ADCs are some of the most difficult biologics to make at scale, and the manufacturing complexity is a real driver of cost and supply.

To make an ADC, you need to first manufacture the antibody. That alone is one of the more complex bioprocesses in pharma — you culture mammalian cells, usually Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells, in large bioreactors, harvest the secreted antibody, purify it through a series of chromatography steps, and characterize it for sequence, structure, and post-translational modifications. This is a months-long process and requires specialized facilities. A modern monoclonal antibody plant costs hundreds of millions of dollars to build.

You also need to manufacture the payload-linker construct as a small molecule. This is done through traditional pharmaceutical chemistry — typically a multi-step organic synthesis, often involving complex stereochemistry and sensitive functional groups. The payloads are highly potent, so the manufacturing facilities have to handle them with the strictest containment — operators wear protective gear, the equipment is closed, the air handling is specialized. A facility that can handle ADC payloads is called a high-potency API facility and is significantly more expensive to build and operate than a standard small-molecule plant.

\[Pause.\]

Then you have to combine them. The conjugation step is performed in solution — typically in a specialized bioreactor — and involves mixing the antibody with the payload-linker construct under defined conditions of pH, temperature, time, and stoichiometry. The chemistry has to be quenched at exactly the right point to achieve the target DAR. Then the conjugate has to be purified to remove unconjugated antibody, free payload, and any aggregates that formed during the reaction.

The analytics required to characterize a finished ADC are extensive. You have to measure the average DAR. You have to measure the DAR distribution — how many antibodies have zero payloads, how many have one, how many have two, and so on. You have to measure the conjugation site occupancy — which positions on the antibody are loaded and which aren't. You have to characterize aggregates. You have to verify the antibody hasn't been damaged by the conjugation chemistry. You have to check for free payload contamination. Each of these is a separate analytical method, often involving mass spectrometry, liquid chromatography, and various biological assays. The release specifications for a finished ADC are some of the most complex in all of biologics.

Then there's the supply chain. You need a reliable antibody supplier and a reliable payload-linker supplier, and they have to coordinate so that you have inventory of both at the right time to run the conjugation. Many ADC programs have struggled with supply constraints, particularly during the COVID era when biologics manufacturing globally was strained. T-DXd had supply issues for several years after launch, and Daiichi has been investing heavily in expanded manufacturing capacity.

\[Pause.\]

The economics of ADC manufacturing are correspondingly high. A typical ADC dose costs the manufacturer thousands of dollars to produce. The retail price to patients and insurers can be tens of thousands of dollars per dose. T-DXd costs in the neighborhood of fifteen thousand dollars per infusion, and patients typically receive treatment for many months. The total cost per patient can easily exceed two hundred thousand dollars over the course of therapy.

Some of that cost is the manufacturing complexity. Some of it is recovering the development investment — these drugs cost billions of dollars to develop. Some of it is the price the market will bear for life-extending cancer therapy. The cost is driving real conversations about access and value, and it's likely to remain a flashpoint as ADCs expand into more indications.

The manufacturing complexity is also one of the reasons new entrants in the ADC space struggle. It's relatively easy to design an ADC on paper. It's much harder to actually make one reliably at scale, characterize it, demonstrate batch-to-batch consistency, and pass FDA inspection. Companies that have invested in ADC manufacturing infrastructure — Seagen (now Pfizer), Daiichi, Roche, ImmunoGen (now AbbVie) — have a significant moat. New companies often partner with contract manufacturers who have the necessary capabilities, and capacity at those CMOs is a constraint.

This is why the field's industrial structure looks the way it does. ADCs are not just smart molecules; they're capital-intensive industrial products. The companies that win are the ones that combine smart molecular design with manufacturing scale.

## SEGMENT 18 — The frontier — radioligands, immunostimulatory payloads, AI design

Let me close with a tour of the frontier. Where is ADC technology heading? What are the next ten years going to look like? I'll give you four directions that I think are most likely to define the next era.

The first is **radioligand therapy**, which is not technically an ADC but is conceptually adjacent. Radioligands are targeted molecules — sometimes antibodies, sometimes small peptides — conjugated to a radioactive isotope instead of a cytotoxic small molecule. The radioactive atom decays inside or near the tumor cell and emits ionizing radiation that damages DNA at short range. The most famous example is lutetium-177-PSMA-617, marketed as Pluvicto, approved in 2022 for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer. Pluvicto is not an ADC — it uses a small peptide rather than an antibody — but the principle is the same: targeted delivery of a toxic payload to a defined cellular marker.

Radioligand therapy is exploding right now. Novartis has built a major franchise around Pluvicto and a similar drug, Lutathera, for neuroendocrine tumors. Several companies are developing radioligands against new targets. The next frontier is alpha-emitters — radioisotopes like actinium-225 that emit higher-energy radiation over shorter distances, which may be more effective and have less off-target toxicity. Alpha-emitter radioligands are in early clinical development now and may represent the next major wave.

\[Pause.\]

The second direction is **immunostimulatory payloads**. Instead of using a cytotoxic small molecule as the payload, you use a molecule that activates the immune system at the tumor site. The classic example is a TLR7 or TLR8 agonist — small molecules that bind Toll-like receptors on immune cells and trigger an inflammatory response. As an ADC payload, a TLR agonist gets delivered to the tumor environment, activates local immune cells, and turns a "cold" tumor (one the immune system is ignoring) into a "hot" tumor (one the immune system attacks).

Several immunostimulatory ADCs are in early clinical trials. Bolt Biotherapeutics, Mersana, and others have programs in this space. The clinical data is still early, but the concept is compelling — and it represents a fundamentally different mode of action from traditional cytotoxic ADCs. Instead of just killing the cancer cells the antibody binds, you're recruiting the patient's own immune system to attack the tumor more broadly.

The third direction is **AI-driven design**. The combinatorial space of ADCs — antibody times target times payload times linker times conjugation site — is enormous. Hundreds of antibody targets. Dozens of payload classes. Multiple linker chemistries. Multiple conjugation strategies. Multiple DARs. The number of possible ADC designs is far larger than any human team could explore systematically. Machine learning models are beginning to be applied to predict which combinations will have the best therapeutic windows.

Several startups — Absci, Generate Biomedicines, Lila Sciences, Xaira Therapeutics, and others — are working on computational approaches to antibody design and ADC design. The promise is that you can specify a target and a desired profile and then use AI to generate candidate molecules that hit the target with the right affinity, the right specificity, and the right physical properties for conjugation. We're not there yet. But progress over the next five years could meaningfully accelerate the design cycle.

\[Pause.\]

The fourth direction is **modular toolkit thinking**. This is less a specific technology than a way of approaching the field. The mature ADC paradigm treats the antibody, the linker, the payload, and the conjugation chemistry as four independent modules that can be mixed and matched. Pick an antibody for the target. Pick a payload for the cancer biology. Pick a linker for the desired bystander effect and stability. Pick a conjugation strategy for the desired DAR profile and homogeneity.

Here's the analogy. **The future of ADCs looks like a modular toolkit — antibody by payload by linker by conjugation — where the designer assembles the right combination for each clinical situation.** The first generation of ADCs was characterized by one-off designs — Mylotarg, Adcetris, T-DM1 each built somewhat from scratch. The current generation is dominated by platforms — the Seagen MMAE-vc platform, the Daiichi DXd platform, the various PBD platforms. The next generation will likely be characterized by even more modular composability, where new ADCs can be designed and tested with much less custom chemistry per program.

If this modular vision plays out, the rate at which new ADCs reach clinical trials will accelerate substantially. And the antibody-target landscape will broaden — antibodies that were not previously good targets, because they had poor internalization or low expression, may become tractable with the right payload and linker choices. ADCs may become applicable to indications well beyond oncology — autoimmune diseases, fibrotic diseases, even infectious diseases.

\[Pause.\]

Let me close this segment, and this lecture, with a reflection on where we are. Twenty-five years after Mylotarg's first approval, ADCs have gone from a fringe idea to a central pillar of oncology. T-DXd alone is reshaping breast cancer treatment for hundreds of thousands of patients a year. A dozen ADCs are approved. Hundreds are in clinical trials. The industrial activity is enormous, the molecular toolkit is expanding, and the clinical results in many indications are genuinely transformative.

The concept — antibody plus linker plus payload — is simple enough to describe on a napkin. The execution requires mastery of antibody engineering, organic chemistry, cellular pharmacology, manufacturing science, and clinical management. The field is the culmination of decades of biological understanding and chemical engineering, and the best chapters are probably still ahead.

If you walked in here two hours ago with no idea what an ADC was, and you now have an intuition for what they are, why they work, why they sometimes fail, and what the frontier looks like — you have a framework you can use for the next twenty years of oncology news. Because ADCs are not a passing trend. They are a permanent addition to the toolkit, and the developments coming out of the labs at Daiichi, Seagen, ADC Therapeutics, AstraZeneca, and dozens of smaller companies will be reshaping cancer care for the foreseeable future.

Thank you. Take a breath. Questions?
