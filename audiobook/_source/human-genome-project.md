# The Human Genome Project — History, Science, and Aftermath

_How to use this script: This is a long-form spoken lecture, designed to be read aloud at a conversational pace of roughly 140 words per minute. The full transcript runs about ninety minutes. Treat the bracketed pause directions as real pauses — count two beats for a short pause, five beats for a longer one. The mid-point break after Segment 9 is a natural place to stop if you are splitting the recording into two episodes. The voice is second-person undergraduate lecture: you are speaking to a room of curious students who know some biology but have never thought about how the modern genomics era actually got built. Lean into the analogies. Slow down for the numbers. The story is dramatic — let it be dramatic._

---

## SEGMENT 1 — Why this story matters, and what you should walk away with

[Pause.]

I want to start by telling you what this lecture is, and what it is not.

It is not a recitation of dates and names, although there will be plenty of both. It is not a victory lap for Big Science, although there is a victory in here somewhere. And it is not a takedown of the hype that surrounded the Human Genome Project, although we will have to talk honestly about what the project promised and what it actually delivered.

What this lecture is, is a story about how a single, audacious, thirteen-year, three-billion-dollar international scientific project rewrote what biology is, what biology can do, and what biology costs.

[Pause.]

So here is the framing. If you walk out of here today and you remember nothing else, I want you to remember this. In 1990, a coalition of governments and research institutes around the world committed to reading the entire human genome — all three billion chemical letters of it — using a technology, Sanger sequencing, that could read maybe five hundred letters at a time, slowly, expensively, by hand or by the earliest automated machines. The estimated cost was three billion dollars. The estimated timeline was fifteen years. Almost nobody, including many of the senior biologists in the room when the project was proposed, thought it could be done on time or on budget.

It came in early. It came in roughly on budget. And by the time it was finished, in April 2003, it had not just produced a reference human genome — it had built the entire technical, computational, ethical, and cultural infrastructure that modern genomics now stands on.

[Pause.]

Today, in 2026, you can walk into a clinical sequencing lab and get your whole genome sequenced for under two hundred dollars in a single day. Two hundred dollars. One day. Three billion dollars in 1990 has become two hundred dollars in 2026 — that is a fifteen-million-fold cost reduction, which is roughly four orders of magnitude faster than Moore's Law for semiconductors over the same period.

The Human Genome Project is why.

Without it, there is no next-generation sequencing industry. There is no cancer genomics. There is no GWAS, no UK Biobank, no 23andMe, no rare disease diagnostic odyssey ending in a single causal variant found in a child's exome. None of it exists. The whole modern omics edifice — and we will define omics carefully in a later segment — rests on the foundation that was poured between 1990 and 2003.

[Pause.]

That is the headline. Now let me give you the analogy I am going to use throughout this entire lecture, because I think it is the single most useful frame.

The Human Genome Project was biology's moon landing.

[Pause.]

I do not mean that flippantly. I mean it structurally. Think about what the Apollo program actually was. It was an audacious goal — put a human on the moon by the end of the decade — set by a government, funded by taxpayers, executed by an international web of contractors and academic labs, with a hard deadline, a clear endpoint, enormous public visibility, and spinoff technologies — integrated circuits, freeze-dried food, materials science, computational methods — that ended up mattering more in the long run than the moon rocks themselves.

The Human Genome Project has every one of those properties. Audacious goal: sequence three billion bases when your best tool reads five hundred at a time. Government-funded, internationally executed, with a hard deadline. Enormously visible — President Clinton and Prime Minister Blair stood in the East Room of the White House together to announce the draft. And the spinoff technologies — automated sequencing platforms, bioinformatics pipelines, the entire culture of open data sharing, the ELSI framework for thinking about genetic ethics — those spinoffs ended up reshaping biology far more than the reference genome itself did.

That is the frame. Moon landing. Hold onto it.

[Pause.]

Eighteen segments ahead of us. We will cover the audacity of the original proposal, the players who said yes when nobody else would, the bitter and productive rivalry between the public consortium and Craig Venter's Celera, the two competing strategies — clone-by-clone hierarchical assembly versus whole-genome shotgun — that defined the technical fight, the Bermuda Principles that made open data the default for modern biology, the June 2000 White House truce, the 2003 finished sequence, the surprising and humbling discoveries — only twenty thousand genes, half the genome is repetitive junk-that-is-not-quite-junk — and then the long aftermath: ENCODE, HapMap, 1000 Genomes, the Telomere-to-Telomere completion in 2022, the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium in 2023, cancer genomics, the ethical framework, and finally what HGP did not solve — because the polygenic, the regulatory, the missing heritability, those are the problems we are still wrestling with right now.

That is the menu. Let's eat.

## SEGMENT 2 — 1990: the audacity of proposing to sequence three billion bases

[Pause.]

I want you to time-travel with me to 1985. Reagan is president, the personal computer revolution is just starting to penetrate biology labs, and the standard way you sequenced a piece of DNA was this. You would take your DNA fragment, you would set up a Sanger sequencing reaction in four tubes — one for each base, A, C, G, T — you would run the products on a polyacrylamide gel that was the size of a small window, you would expose the gel to X-ray film overnight, and the next morning you would stand at a light box with a pencil and read off the bases by eye. A good day, a really good day, was five hundred bases. A great year for a graduate student was maybe fifty thousand bases — about the size of a single bacterial gene cluster.

Now hold that picture in your head — graduate student, light box, pencil, five hundred bases per gel — and let me give you the number we are trying to reach.

Three billion bases.

[Pause.]

Let me make that number physical for you, because three billion is the kind of number that washes over your ear without sticking. Here is the analogy. If you printed out the human genome at ten letters per line — A T G C C T A G C T, ten letters, line break, ten more letters — and you used standard book typography, that printout would be a stack of phone books taller than this room. Floor to ceiling. Three hundred million lines. If you started reading at one letter per second, eight hours a day, it would take you about three hundred years to finish.

That is what they proposed to sequence. And the sequencing method available to them was the graduate-student-and-light-box method, with a few prototype automated machines that were just starting to come online.

[Pause.]

The original proposal — and there were several converging proposals in 1985, 1986, 1987 — came from a few different camps. Robert Sinsheimer at UC Santa Cruz hosted a meeting in 1985 to discuss whether sequencing the human genome was even possible. Renato Dulbecco, the Nobel laureate, wrote an influential 1986 editorial in Science arguing that to make progress on cancer we needed the complete reference sequence of the human genome. And Charles DeLisi at the Department of Energy started organizing a federal effort because the DOE had a longstanding interest in the genetic effects of radiation — they had been studying atomic bomb survivors for decades — and they had supercomputers.

The Department of Energy and the National Institutes of Health eventually agreed to jointly lead the U.S. effort. The project officially launched on October 1, 1990. The first director of what became the National Human Genome Research Institute, NHGRI, was James Watson — yes, that Watson, of Watson and Crick — who lasted in the role only until 1992 when he resigned, partly over his opposition to the patenting of gene sequences, an issue that would become central to the Venter rivalry we will get to.

Francis Collins took over from Watson in 1993 and led NHGRI through the entire rest of the project. Collins is a careful, methodical, deeply religious physician-scientist who had previously co-discovered the cystic fibrosis gene, and he turned out to be exactly the right kind of leader for the kind of slow, grinding, international, politically delicate work that the HGP required.

[Pause.]

But — and this is critical — this was never just an American project. The Wellcome Trust in the UK funded what became the Sanger Centre, now the Wellcome Sanger Institute, under John Sulston. The French government funded Genoscope under Jean Weissenbach. Germany, Japan — RIKEN especially — and eventually China through what became BGI all contributed major sequencing centers. The project was structured as the International Human Genome Sequencing Consortium, with roughly twenty institutions across six countries doing the work in coordinated chunks.

The original timeline was fifteen years, 1990 to 2005. The original budget was three billion dollars over those fifteen years — roughly one dollar per base, which sounds quaint now but was an aggressive cost target in 1990.

How were they going to do it? That is the next two segments — the players, then the technology. But before we leave the audacity of the proposal, I want you to sit with one fact.

In 1990, no organism larger than a small bacterium had been fully sequenced. The first complete genome of any free-living organism, _Haemophilus influenzae_, was not sequenced until 1995 — five years into the HGP. They committed to sequencing three billion bases of human before anybody had ever sequenced two million bases of anything.

That is what audacity looks like.

## SEGMENT 3 — The players: NHGRI, Sanger, Genoscope, RIKEN, BGI, and the consortium structure

[Pause.]

Let me walk you around the world and introduce you to the labs that actually did the work, because the Human Genome Project was not one lab or one country. It was a network, and the network mattered.

[Pause.]

Start in Bethesda, Maryland. The National Human Genome Research Institute, NHGRI, is one of the twenty-seven institutes of the National Institutes of Health. NHGRI did not do most of the sequencing itself; it funded and coordinated. The big U.S. sequencing centers — the ones that actually generated the bases — were the Whitehead Institute Center for Genome Research at MIT under Eric Lander, the Washington University Genome Sequencing Center in St. Louis under Robert Waterston, and Baylor College of Medicine in Houston under Richard Gibbs. These three U.S. centers together produced about half of the public consortium's data.

Cross the Atlantic. The Wellcome Trust Sanger Centre, founded in 1992 on a converted manor estate in Hinxton, just outside Cambridge, England, was directed by John Sulston — who had previously sequenced the genome of the nematode worm _C. elegans_. The Sanger Centre under Sulston was the single largest contributor to the public draft, producing about a third of all the sequence. Sulston was a fierce defender of the open-data principle — we will come back to him in the Bermuda segment — and the culture of the Sanger Centre, with its insistence on public deposit of every read within twenty-four hours, set the tone for the entire consortium.

[Pause.]

Cross the Channel. Genoscope, the French National Sequencing Center, was established in 1996 in Evry, south of Paris, under Jean Weissenbach. The French contribution was smaller in volume than the U.S. or U.K. centers, but Genoscope took responsibility for chromosome 14 in its entirety and contributed to several others.

Germany contributed through a consortium led by the Max Planck Institute and the German Cancer Research Center, focusing on chromosome 21 and parts of others. The German chromosome 21 group, led by Marie-Laure Yaspo and André Rosenthal, finished the second-ever complete human chromosome sequence in 2000.

Japan's RIKEN Genomic Sciences Center under Yoshiyuki Sakaki took on parts of chromosomes 21 and 22 and several other regions. And in 1999, near the end of the draft phase, China joined the consortium through the Beijing Genomics Institute, BGI, which contributed about one percent of the sequence — a small fraction, but a critical political and scientific entry that established BGI as a player, and BGI would go on to become one of the largest sequencing operations on earth.

[Pause.]

Here is the analogy I want you to hold for the structure. The Human Genome Project was organized like a continental railroad — different national companies, working on assigned segments, but all needing to lay down the same gauge of track so that the final assembly would meet up. The chromosomes were divided as assignments — Sanger took chromosomes 1, 6, 9, 10, 13, 20, 22, and X; Washington University took 7, 16, Y; Whitehead took 17; Baylor took 3, 12; Genoscope took 14; the Japanese took parts of 21 and 22; and so on. Each center mapped, sequenced, and finished their assigned chromosomes, and the data flowed into a central deposit at GenBank, the public sequence database run by the National Center for Biotechnology Information at NIH.

The coordination meetings — there were dozens of them over the years — were where the gauge of the track got agreed upon. What clone library? What sequencing protocol? What quality threshold counts as finished? What goes into GenBank when?

And then, in May 1998, into the middle of this carefully coordinated public effort, walked Craig Venter, and the entire project nearly came apart.

## SEGMENT 4 — Craig Venter, Celera, and the shotgun challenge

[Pause.]

Craig Venter is one of the most consequential and most polarizing figures in modern biology, and to understand the Human Genome Project you have to understand Venter.

Venter started his career at NIH, where in the early 1990s he developed a technique called Expressed Sequence Tags, ESTs — short sequence reads from the active genes in a cell, generated quickly and cheaply by sequencing the ends of cDNA clones. The EST approach was a faster, lower-resolution way to identify genes than full genomic sequencing, and Venter built it into a production pipeline at NIH that was generating tens of thousands of ESTs while the official genome project was still mapping clones.

The trouble started when NIH, under pressure from its tech transfer office, tried to patent thousands of these EST sequences. Watson, then still the head of NHGRI, was vehemently opposed to patenting partial gene sequences — he thought it would poison the well of open science — and the resulting fight contributed to Watson's resignation in 1992. Venter, frustrated with NIH bureaucracy, left NIH in 1992 to found a nonprofit called The Institute for Genomic Research, TIGR, with private funding from a venture capitalist named Wallace Steinberg.

At TIGR, Venter teamed up with Hamilton Smith, a Nobel laureate, and in 1995 they did something nobody had ever done before. They sequenced the complete genome of _Haemophilus influenzae_ — 1.8 million bases — using a technique called whole-genome shotgun sequencing. We will come back to the technical details of shotgun in the next segment. For now, the point is: TIGR demonstrated, while the official Human Genome Project was still arguing about clone maps, that you could sequence an entire free-living organism's genome end-to-end in a matter of months using a fast, cheap, parallel approach.

[Pause.]

Then, in May 1998, Venter dropped a bomb. He announced the formation of a new for-profit company, Celera Genomics, funded by Perkin-Elmer — the company that made the dominant automated DNA sequencer of the era, the ABI 3700 — and Celera's stated mission was to sequence the entire human genome in three years, by 2001, four years ahead of the public project's timeline, and at a fraction of the cost, using whole-genome shotgun. Celera would buy three hundred ABI 3700 sequencers, the largest sequencing operation in the world by an order of magnitude, and run them twenty-four hours a day.

Celera's business model was not to give the data away. Celera was going to assemble the human genome, copyright the assembly, sell subscription access to pharmaceutical companies, and patent the most commercially interesting genes. This was the explicit threat to the public project — if Celera finished first, the human genome would belong to a private company.

[Pause.]

The reaction inside the public consortium was, depending on who you ask, panic, fury, or grim determination. Sulston at the Sanger Centre was incandescent. The Wellcome Trust immediately doubled the Sanger Centre's funding so that it could scale up to match Celera's industrial pace. The U.S. centers — Whitehead, Wash U, Baylor — all bought their own batteries of ABI 3700s and went into round-the-clock production. The public project's timeline was officially moved up. The draft, which had been planned for 2005, was now targeted for 2001 — a four-year acceleration in response to the Celera challenge.

What followed, from 1998 to 2000, was the most intense head-to-head race in the history of biology. Two parallel efforts, both trying to sequence the same three billion bases, one public and free, one private and profit-driven, racing toward a finish line that neither had ever defined precisely.

The race ended, as we will see, in a politically brokered tie on June 26, 2000, on a podium at the White House. But to understand why the race could happen at all, and why the two sides produced sequences that complemented each other rather than fully duplicating, you have to understand the technical fight underneath — clone-by-clone versus whole-genome shotgun.

That is the next segment.

## SEGMENT 5 — BACs, clone-by-clone, and whole-genome shotgun: the two strategies

[Pause.]

This is the most technically dense segment in the lecture, so I am going to slow down and use analogies aggressively. Stick with me.

Here is the problem. Sanger sequencing, even with the best automated ABI 3700 machines of 1998, can only read about seven hundred to nine hundred bases of contiguous DNA at a time. The human genome is three billion bases. So you cannot just take a chromosome and sequence it end-to-end — there is no machine on earth that can do that. You have to break the genome into pieces small enough to sequence, then sequence the pieces, then computationally stitch the pieces back together based on overlapping ends.

The question is: how do you break it up, and how do you stitch it back together?

[Pause.]

The public consortium chose what is called the clone-by-clone or hierarchical approach. Here is the analogy. Imagine you want to copy a giant book — three thousand pages — using a photocopier that only copies one page at a time. The careful, slow way is: first, you make a chapter outline. You number every chapter. Then you photocopy chapter one, page by page, in order, and you reassemble chapter one. Then chapter two. And so on. You always know where you are in the book.

That is clone-by-clone. The public consortium first built a physical map of the human genome — a low-resolution map of large overlapping DNA fragments, each about 150,000 base pairs long, cloned into things called Bacterial Artificial Chromosomes, BACs. A BAC is a circular plasmid-like vector that can hold and faithfully replicate a chunk of human DNA up to about 200 kilobases inside a bacterial cell. You will remember plasmids from the previous lecture — BACs are the heavyweight industrial-scale cousins of plasmids, engineered to carry far larger inserts.

The consortium fingerprinted tens of thousands of BACs, figured out which ones overlapped, and built a tiling path — a minimum set of overlapping BACs that covered each chromosome. Then, for each BAC in the tiling path, they shotgunned that one BAC — sequenced random fragments of it — and assembled the BAC's 150,000 bases as a self-contained unit. Then they stitched the assembled BACs together along the tiling path.

The advantages: every assembled BAC is anchored to a known physical location on a known chromosome. Repetitive DNA — and remember, half the human genome is repetitive — is contained within the small space of a single BAC, so repeats from chromosome 1 cannot get confused with repeats from chromosome 14. The assembly is local, controlled, and verifiable.

The disadvantages: it is slow. Building the BAC map alone took years. Every step has overhead. And BACs are not free to make — each one is a cloned construct in bacteria, with all the labor of plasmid prep.

[Pause.]

Celera chose the opposite approach: whole-genome shotgun.

Here is the shotgun analogy. Take the same giant three-thousand-page book. Now feed it through a paper shredder — not once but multiple times, at different shred sizes. You now have a giant pile of confetti — millions of overlapping fragments. You read every confetto. Then you computationally figure out how the confetti pieces overlap, and you reassemble the book purely from overlap information, with no chapter outline at all.

That is whole-genome shotgun. You take the whole genome, fragment it randomly, sequence millions of small fragments, and let a computer figure out how they fit together. No physical map, no BACs, no tiling path. Just sequence and assemble.

The advantages: it is fast. You skip the entire map-building phase. The wet-lab pipeline is simpler and more parallelizable.

The disadvantages: the assembly problem becomes nightmarish, especially for a repeat-rich genome like human. If you have ten thousand nearly identical copies of a transposable element scattered across the genome, how does the computer know which copy goes where? The answer, for Celera, turned out to be that you needed paired-end reads — sequence both ends of a longer fragment, separated by a known distance — and you needed very deep coverage, six to ten times the genome size in total bases sequenced.

And here is a fact that Celera was quiet about at the time but is now widely acknowledged: Celera's assembly used the public consortium's data. Because the public data was being deposited daily into GenBank under the Bermuda Principles — that is the next segment — Celera was able to anchor its shotgun assembly against the public BAC map. Some analyses suggest Celera could not have produced a usable assembly without the public data. The public project, working clone by clone, could have finished without Celera. The reverse is not clearly true.

[Pause.]

The point of this segment is not that one approach was right and the other was wrong. The point is that the two approaches were complementary, and the existence of both — the rivalry between them — pushed each side to be better. The clone-by-clone approach gave us a reliably anchored backbone. The shotgun approach proved that you could industrialize sequencing at a scale nobody had imagined. Modern next-generation sequencing is shotgun on steroids — every Illumina run is whole-genome shotgun, with billions of short reads, assembled or aligned by descendants of the algorithms Celera pioneered.

The shotgun approach won the technological future. The clone-by-clone approach won the 2003 finished sequence. Both were necessary.

## SEGMENT 6 — The 1995 H. influenzae proof: shotgun works on a real genome

[Pause.]

I want to spend a short segment on a single 1995 paper, because that paper is a hinge point in the history of sequencing.

The paper is Fleischmann et al., 1995, in Science, titled "Whole-genome random sequencing and assembly of _Haemophilus influenzae_ Rd." The authors are essentially the TIGR group — Robert Fleischmann, Mark Adams, Craig Venter, Hamilton Smith, and about thirty others.

What they did: they took the genome of _Haemophilus influenzae_, a small bacterial pathogen that causes ear infections and meningitis, total genome size 1.83 million base pairs, and they shotgunned it. They sheared the DNA randomly, cloned the fragments into plasmid libraries, sequenced about twenty-eight thousand reads — at the time, an enormous number — and assembled the reads into a single circular chromosome using a TIGR-developed assembler.

The result: the first complete genome of a free-living organism, ever. Period. Full stop.

[Pause.]

Why this matters for the Human Genome Project story is twofold.

First, it was a proof of concept. Before 1995, shotgun assembly had been done on much smaller targets — individual cosmids, individual BACs — but nobody had ever shotgunned a whole genome and assembled it cleanly. The skepticism within the public consortium was strong; many senior people, including Sulston and Lander, believed shotgun would fail catastrophically at the scale of a real chromosome because of the repeat problem. The _H. influenzae_ paper did not directly refute that — bacterial genomes have far fewer repeats than human — but it demonstrated that the wet-lab pipeline worked at the scale of a real organism.

Second, it gave Venter the credibility to propose Celera three years later. Without the _H. influenzae_ paper, Venter is a controversial figure with a track record of ESTs and patent fights. With the _H. influenzae_ paper, Venter is the man who actually delivered the first complete bacterial genome. When he stood up in 1998 and said "I am going to shotgun the human genome," people had to take him seriously, because he had done it before, just at a thousand-fold smaller scale.

[Pause.]

A small technical note that I want you to carry forward, because it will come up in the T2T segment. The _H. influenzae_ assembly was clean because bacterial genomes are mostly unique sequence — about ninety percent unique, ten percent repetitive at most. The human genome is the opposite — roughly fifty percent repetitive, with massive blocks of near-identical repeats in centromeres and telomeres. Shotgun assembly's performance degrades sharply with repeat content, which is why the Celera draft and the public draft both left the centromeres and telomeres unassembled. Those regions remained gaps in the human reference until the Telomere-to-Telomere project finally closed them in 2022, using sequencing technologies — PacBio HiFi and Oxford Nanopore — that did not exist in 1995 or 2003.

That is the through-line. Shotgun works. Shotgun has limits. Long reads fix the limits. Long reads did not exist yet. So the 1990s and 2000s genome was a clone-by-clone-plus-shotgun hybrid, and it had known gaps that would have to wait twenty years to be filled.

We have covered the proposal, the players, the rivalry, the two strategies, and the bacterial proof of concept. Now we turn to the cultural revolution that the Human Genome Project produced almost as a byproduct: the Bermuda Principles, and the founding of modern biology's open-data culture.

## SEGMENT 7 — The 1996 Bermuda Principles: open data within 24 hours

[Pause.]

In February 1996, the heads of the major sequencing centers in the international consortium met at a Wellcome Trust strategy meeting at the Princess Sophia Hotel in Hamilton, Bermuda. The meeting was called to address a practical problem: as sequencing scaled up, sequence data was piling up in individual labs faster than it could be published in papers. What was the data-sharing policy supposed to be?

The answer they reached — over three days of argument, mostly driven by John Sulston and Robert Waterston, with Michael Morgan of Wellcome chairing — was radical, and it changed biology.

[Pause.]

The Bermuda Principles, agreed in February 1996 and reaffirmed at follow-up meetings in 1997 and 1998, stated three things.

One: all human genomic sequence information generated by any center funded by the public consortium would be freely available in the public domain, with no patent restrictions, to encourage research and development and maximize benefit to society.

Two: sequence assemblies of one kilobase or larger would be released to the public databases — GenBank, EMBL, DDBJ — within twenty-four hours of generation.

Three: finished annotated sequences would be submitted immediately upon completion.

[Pause.]

Twenty-four hours. Read that again. Twenty-four hours from the moment a sequencer spit out a read to the moment that read was in a global public database that anybody on earth could download.

In 1996, this was utterly unprecedented. The norm in molecular biology, and in science generally, was that you held onto your data until you published your paper. Publication was the moment of release. Data generated months or years before publication sat in lab notebooks and local databases, accessible only to the generating lab and its collaborators. The Bermuda Principles tore that norm apart.

[Pause.]

Here is the analogy. Imagine a world in which every new mathematical theorem had to sit in the prover's desk drawer until a peer-reviewed paper came out three years later. Now imagine somebody passing a rule that says every new theorem must be posted on a public bulletin board within twenty-four hours of being proven, even before it has been written up, even before the prover has had time to figure out what it means. That is the size of the cultural shift that Bermuda represented for sequence data.

The argument for the principles was scientific. Genome sequence is infrastructure. If everybody has it, everybody can use it to do their own science, and the scientific community as a whole moves faster. Holding genome data hostage to individual publication timelines would slow down all of biology. The genome belongs to the species, not to the people who happen to sequence it first.

The argument was also strategic, although nobody said this out loud at the time. Open data was a weapon against Venter. If every base the public consortium generated was in GenBank within twenty-four hours, and Celera's data was proprietary and behind a subscription paywall, then the public effort had a moral high ground and a practical scientific value that Celera could not match. Researchers who wanted to use human sequence in their own work would default to the public data because it was free.

[Pause.]

The Bermuda Principles are arguably the single most consequential policy decision of the entire Human Genome Project. They established that publicly funded genome data is a public good. They established that immediate release, not publication-gated release, is the right default. And they set a precedent that has since been extended to other large-scale data: protein structures in the Protein Data Bank, microarray data in GEO, sequencing reads in the Short Read Archive, clinical trial data in ClinicalTrials.gov, COVID-19 genome sequences in GISAID and the public archives.

When you go to GenBank today and download a sequence, you are exercising a right that John Sulston and Robert Waterston and the others fought for in a hotel conference room in Bermuda in February 1996.

That open-data culture is one of the two great cultural legacies of the Human Genome Project. The other is the ELSI program, which we will get to in Segment 16.

## SEGMENT 8 — June 26, 2000: the White House announcement and the brokered tie

[Pause.]

By the spring of 2000, the race between the public consortium and Celera had become politically unmanageable. Both sides claimed they were nearly done with a draft. Both sides were briefing journalists and lawmakers against each other. The Wall Street Journal, the New York Times, Nature, and Science were running competing stories every week. Inside Celera, the assembly team — led by Eugene Myers — was racing to integrate their shotgun reads with the public BAC scaffold. Inside the public consortium, the assembly team — led by Jim Kent at UC Santa Cruz, who in a famous all-nighter wrote the GigAssembler program on a homemade Linux cluster in four weeks — was racing to integrate the BAC fragments into a draft.

Both sides knew the truth: neither side had a finished sequence. Both had drafts. Both drafts had gaps. Both drafts disagreed with each other in many regions.

If one side declared victory first, the other side would dispute the claim, and the resulting fight would tarnish the entire achievement in the eyes of the public, Congress, the press, and the scientific community.

[Pause.]

The solution was political. Ari Patrinos, the head of the Department of Energy's genome program, hosted a series of quiet dinners at his home in suburban Maryland in the spring of 2000, with Francis Collins and Craig Venter as the guests. Over wine and pizza, Patrinos brokered a truce. Both sides would announce a working draft of the human genome simultaneously, jointly, at a White House event chaired by President Bill Clinton, with Prime Minister Tony Blair joining by satellite from 10 Downing Street.

The event happened on Monday, June 26, 2000, in the East Room of the White House. Clinton, Blair, Collins, and Venter stood on a podium together. Clinton's speech included one of the most quoted lines in the history of biology: "Today, we are learning the language in which God created life."

Collins announced that the public consortium had completed a working draft covering about ninety percent of the genome at four-to-five-fold coverage. Venter announced that Celera had independently assembled a draft of similar coverage and would publish the details. They shook hands. The race, officially, was a tie.

[Pause.]

What was actually announced that day was not a finished genome. It was a draft — full of gaps, full of low-confidence regions, with the centromeres and telomeres entirely missing and substantial portions of the euchromatin still fragmentary. The completion would not come until 2003, and even then with substantial caveats we will address. But the June 2000 announcement was a real and important milestone, and the politics of joint announcement turned out to be wise. It defused the rivalry just enough that both sides could publish their drafts as companion papers in February 2001 — the public draft in Nature on February 15, 2001, the Celera draft in Science on February 16, 2001 — without one side undercutting the other.

The two papers, taken together, were a turning point. They made the front page of every major newspaper in the world. They established beyond any question that the human genome could be sequenced, that the era of genomics had begun, and that the technical and political infrastructure for whole-genome biology was now in place.

[Pause.]

There is an aftermath note worth telling. Celera's business model — patenting genes and selling subscription database access — turned out not to work. The pharmaceutical industry was unwilling to pay for genome data that was nearly identical to what was freely available from GenBank under the Bermuda Principles. Celera as a sequencing company effectively collapsed within a few years of the 2001 publication. Venter himself left Celera in 2002, in conflict with the company's board, and went on to found the J. Craig Venter Institute, which did fascinating later work including the first synthetic bacterial genome in 2010.

The lesson, if you want a lesson, is that the open-data principle of the public consortium was not just morally satisfying — it was also strategically devastating to the for-profit alternative. By making the data free, the public consortium removed the foundation of Celera's business. Bermuda won.

## SEGMENT 9 — April 2003: the finished sequence, what "finished" actually meant

[Pause.]

The completion of the Human Genome Project was officially announced on April 14, 2003. The date was deliberately chosen: April 1953 was when Watson and Crick published the double helix structure in Nature. Fifty years to the month between the structure and the sequence. A symbolic bookend.

The 2003 announcement was that the International Human Genome Sequencing Consortium had produced a "finished" sequence covering approximately ninety-nine percent of the gene-containing portion of the genome — the euchromatin — at an accuracy of better than one error in ten thousand bases, which is to say 99.99 percent accurate, or what is called Q40 in sequencing quality terms.

That sounds spectacular. And it is. But you need to understand what "finished" did and did not mean.

[Pause.]

"Finished" meant: euchromatin only. Euchromatin is the gene-rich, less-repetitive portion of the genome — roughly ninety-two percent of the total three billion bases. The remaining eight percent was heterochromatin: the centromeres, the telomeres, the long arms of the acrocentric chromosomes, and the highly repetitive satellite DNA. Those regions were not finished in 2003. They were known to exist, they were estimated in size, but their sequence was largely a black box, because the BAC-and-shotgun approach simply could not assemble through long stretches of near-identical repeats.

"Finished" also meant: one haploid reference. The 2003 sequence was a mosaic, not a sequence of any single person's genome. It was assembled from BAC libraries derived from about a dozen anonymous donors, blended together. The reference represented one possible human genome — not the genome of any actual individual, not a population, not an average. We will return to this in Segment 11, because the inadequacy of the haploid reference for studying human variation was one of the major motivations for the follow-up HapMap and 1000 Genomes projects.

[Pause.]

The published quality of the 2003 sequence — Q40, less than one error in ten thousand bases — was an extraordinary technical achievement. For comparison, a single Sanger read in 1990 had a quality of maybe Q20, one error in a hundred bases. The public consortium achieved Q40 across two-point-eight billion bases by sequencing each region multiple times, on average eight to ten times, and using the redundancy to cross-correct errors. They also did extensive manual curation — actual humans looking at problem regions and resolving ambiguities by hand. The "finishing" phase of the project, from the 2000 draft to the 2003 completion, was largely about closing gaps and resolving low-confidence regions through targeted re-sequencing and manual review.

[Pause.]

The total cost of the Human Genome Project, when the books were closed, was approximately $2.7 billion in 1991 dollars — slightly under the originally projected $3 billion budget. The total time was thirteen years from official start in October 1990 to completion announcement in April 2003 — two years under the original fifteen-year schedule.

Under budget. Ahead of schedule. The largest collaborative biology project ever undertaken, delivered.

The next segment is where we shift from the project itself to its scientific findings — what we actually learned from looking at the sequence. And the headline finding, the one that surprised almost everybody, is in the gene count.

## SEGMENT 10 — Only 20,000 genes, and 50% of the genome is repeats: the great surprises

[Pause.]

Welcome back. We are now into the aftermath — what we found when we actually read the sequence, and what those findings have meant for biology.

The single most surprising result of the Human Genome Project, the one that has reshaped how we think about genetics, was the gene count.

[Pause.]

Going into the 1990s, the standard textbook estimate for the number of protein-coding genes in the human genome was around one hundred thousand. Some estimates went higher — one hundred twenty thousand, one hundred fifty thousand. The reasoning was straightforward and seemed obvious: humans are complex, we have hundreds of cell types, an immune system that generates astronomical antibody diversity, a brain with eighty-six billion neurons, the most sophisticated cognition of any organism on earth. Surely we needed a lot of genes to encode all that.

There was even a famous betting pool, organized at a Cold Spring Harbor meeting in 2000, called GeneSweep. Scientists put money down on what the final gene count would be. The estimates ranged from twenty-six thousand on the low end to one hundred fifty thousand on the high end. The median bet was around sixty thousand.

When the 2001 draft papers were analyzed, the estimate came in at around thirty-two thousand genes. When the 2003 finished sequence was annotated, the estimate dropped to around twenty-five thousand. As gene annotation has been refined over the subsequent two decades, the current consensus estimate is approximately nineteen to twenty thousand protein-coding genes.

Twenty thousand.

[Pause.]

For context, the nematode worm _Caenorhabditis elegans_ — a microscopic creature with exactly 959 somatic cells and a nervous system of 302 neurons — has about twenty thousand protein-coding genes. The fruit fly _Drosophila melanogaster_ has about fourteen thousand. A rice plant has about thirty-five thousand. The unicellular protozoan _Paramecium_ has about forty thousand. By gene count, you are about the same as a worm and substantially less complicated than a grain of rice.

This was a genuine shock to the field. It violated a deep intuition that complexity at the organism level should correspond to complexity at the gene level.

[Pause.]

How do you reconcile this? Where does human complexity actually come from, if not from more genes? The answer is what genomics has been working out for the last twenty years, and it has three main components.

First: alternative splicing. A single gene can produce multiple different protein products by splicing its exons together in different combinations. The average human gene produces about four distinct splice isoforms, and some genes produce dozens. So twenty thousand genes can produce on the order of a hundred thousand or more distinct proteins.

Second: regulation. The human genome contains an enormous amount of regulatory sequence — promoters, enhancers, silencers, insulators, microRNA binding sites — that controls when, where, and how much each gene is expressed. The regulatory complexity is vastly greater in humans than in worms or flies, even when the protein-coding gene count is similar. Complexity lives in the wiring diagram, not in the parts list.

Third: networks. Genes do not act alone; they act in networks of interactions, feedback loops, and pathways. The combinatorial complexity of a network grows much faster than the number of nodes in the network. Twenty thousand genes can produce far more biological complexity through their interactions than the number twenty thousand might suggest.

Here is the analogy. Twenty thousand genes is the parts list of a piano — eighty-eight keys, a few thousand internal mechanical parts. The number of melodies you can play on that piano is essentially infinite. Complexity is not in the parts; it is in the patterns of activation.

[Pause.]

The second great surprise of the Human Genome Project was the amount of the genome that is not protein-coding.

Of the three billion bases, the protein-coding sequences — the actual exons of the actual genes — total roughly sixty million bases. That is about two percent of the genome. Two percent.

The other ninety-eight percent breaks down roughly as follows. About half the genome — fifty percent — is repetitive DNA derived from transposable elements: LINEs, SINEs, Alu repeats, endogenous retroviruses. These are sequences that at some point in our evolutionary past were active transposons, jumping around the genome and copying themselves, and most of them are now defective relics. Another twenty-five percent is intergenic sequence of various kinds. About twenty percent is intronic — within genes but not protein-coding. A few percent is regulatory and structural sequence. And the rest is various other categories.

The Alu repeats alone account for about ten percent of the entire genome. Ten percent of your DNA is roughly one million copies of a single three-hundred-base-pair repeat element derived from a primate-specific transposon.

[Pause.]

This was, at the time, often described as "junk DNA," a term coined in 1972 by Susumu Ohno to describe non-coding sequence that appeared to have no function. The HGP findings sharpened the question: is ninety-eight percent of your genome actually junk, or does it do something we have not figured out yet?

That question is what ENCODE, the next segment, was designed to answer. And the ENCODE answer turned out to be controversial — productively controversial.

## SEGMENT 11 — ENCODE and the 80% question

[Pause.]

The Encyclopedia of DNA Elements, ENCODE, was launched by NHGRI in 2003, the same month the finished human genome was announced. The premise was: we now have the sequence, but we do not know what most of it does. ENCODE would systematically map the functional elements in the genome — all the regions that are transcribed, all the regions where transcription factors bind, all the regions of open chromatin, all the regions with specific histone modifications, all the regions that act as enhancers or promoters.

ENCODE was an international consortium of dozens of labs, ran in three phases — pilot, scale-up, and ENCODE 3 — and used a combination of high-throughput sequencing assays: RNA-seq for transcription, ChIP-seq for transcription factor binding, DNase-seq and ATAC-seq for open chromatin, bisulfite sequencing for DNA methylation. The final ENCODE 3 dataset, published in a coordinated series of thirty papers across multiple journals in September 2012, integrated data from one hundred forty-seven cell types and tissue samples.

The headline finding, the one that made every newspaper, was this. ENCODE assigned a "biochemical function" to approximately eighty percent of the human genome.

Eighty percent. Up from the two percent that is protein-coding.

[Pause.]

If you took the eighty percent number at face value, it overturned the junk DNA hypothesis. Most of your genome was actually doing something. Junk was a misnomer.

The eighty percent claim immediately ignited a firestorm in the scientific community, and the firestorm is instructive because it illustrates how careful you have to be about defining "function" in biology.

[Pause.]

Here is the criticism, articulated most sharply by evolutionary biologists like Dan Graur, Ford Doolittle, and Sean Eddy in a series of pointed responses. ENCODE's definition of "function" was operational and biochemical — a region of DNA was counted as functional if it was transcribed, or bound by any transcription factor, or in open chromatin, or had any kind of detectable biochemical activity. By that definition, you would expect a large fraction of the genome to be "functional" simply by chance, because transcription factors bind sequences promiscuously, transcription happens at low levels almost everywhere, and the genome is not biochemically silent in regions that do nothing important.

The evolutionary biologists pointed out that the more rigorous definition of function — sequence that is conserved across species by purifying selection, meaning that mutations in it are weeded out because they reduce fitness — yields a much smaller fraction. By the conservation criterion, only about eight to ten percent of the human genome shows evidence of being under selection, and therefore only about eight to ten percent is "functional" in the evolutionarily meaningful sense.

So which is it? Eighty percent functional, or ten percent functional?

[Pause.]

The honest answer, which most working genomicists now accept, is that the question is partly semantic. ENCODE measured biochemical activity, which is real but does not by itself imply phenotypic importance. Evolutionary conservation measures phenotypic importance — sequences that matter to the organism's fitness — but it underestimates function because some functional sequences are species-specific or lineage-specific and would not show conservation across the deep mammalian tree.

The truth is probably that something like ten to twenty percent of the genome has direct functional importance to human biology, another large fraction is biochemically active but not phenotypically critical, and the remaining majority is some mixture of dead transposons, decayed pseudogenes, and structural filler that the cell tolerates but does not require.

Here is the analogy. Imagine a city. There are buildings that are demonstrably essential — the power plant, the hospital, the water treatment facility. There are buildings that have biochemical activity — they are occupied, lights on, doors opening — but whose removal would not really change anything about the city's function. And there are empty lots, abandoned buildings, and demolished foundations that nobody uses. ENCODE counted everything with lights on. The evolutionary biologists counted only the buildings whose removal you would notice. Both numbers tell you something, but they are not measuring the same thing.

The lasting contribution of ENCODE is not the eighty percent number. It is the catalogue. ENCODE produced the most comprehensive map ever made of where, in the human genome, transcription happens, where regulatory factors bind, where chromatin is open, where methylation occurs. That catalogue is used every day by working scientists trying to interpret what specific regions of the genome do. The eighty percent number became a famous controversy, but the underlying maps are what genomics actually uses.

## SEGMENT 12 — The haploid reference problem and the rise of variation studies

[Pause.]

I want to spend this segment on a foundational limitation of the 2003 reference genome that drove the entire next generation of human genomics projects.

The 2003 reference was, as I mentioned in Segment 9, a mosaic of about a dozen anonymous donors blended into a single haploid sequence. Haploid means one copy of each chromosome — like reading half of your inherited DNA, with the other half ignored. The reference also did not capture variation: it was one possible sequence, presented as if it were the human sequence, when in fact every actual human differs from every other actual human at millions of positions across the genome.

[Pause.]

Here is the scale of human genetic variation. On average, two unrelated humans differ from each other at roughly four to five million positions across the genome — that is about one variant per six hundred to seven hundred bases. Most of these variants are single-nucleotide polymorphisms, SNPs, where one base substitutes for another. About a million of the differences are insertions or deletions of one to fifty bases. And then there are larger structural variants — duplications, inversions, copy-number changes — that can involve thousands or millions of bases at a time.

The 2003 reference captures none of this variation. It is a single point in a multidimensional space of possible human genomes. If you want to study disease — and remember, the long-term promise of HGP was that the genome would help us understand disease — you need to know not just the reference sequence, but the catalogue of variants and their frequencies in the population.

This problem was recognized from the start. As early as 2002, while the finishing phase of the HGP was still ongoing, planning began for what became the International HapMap Project.

[Pause.]

The HapMap Project, launched in 2002 and producing its main outputs in 2005 and 2007, was designed to catalogue common SNPs and the patterns of linkage disequilibrium — which variants co-occur on the same chromosomal segments — across major human populations. HapMap genotyped about three million SNPs in samples from populations in Europe, Africa, East Asia, and later additional groups, and produced a public database of haplotype blocks — segments of the chromosome that are inherited as units.

The HapMap data made possible the first wave of genome-wide association studies, GWAS, which began in 2005 and 2006. We will discuss GWAS more in Segment 17, when we talk about what HGP did not solve. For now, the point is that HapMap was the bridge between the static 2003 reference and the dynamic study of human variation.

[Pause.]

HapMap was followed by the 1000 Genomes Project, launched in 2008, which aimed to sequence the genomes of one thousand individuals — eventually expanded to about two thousand five hundred individuals — from twenty-six populations worldwide, at low coverage, with the goal of cataloguing essentially all human genetic variants with frequencies above one percent in any population.

The 1000 Genomes Project, completed in 2015, produced a catalogue of about eighty-eight million variants. It was the first population-scale genome sequencing effort, and it transformed how human geneticists thought about variation. Instead of asking "is this variant present in the reference," you could now ask "what is the frequency of this variant in Europeans, in Africans, in East Asians, in South Asians, in Americans?" The 1000 Genomes data underlies essentially every modern GWAS imputation reference panel, every clinical variant interpretation pipeline, and every population genetic study of recent human history.

[Pause.]

But even 1000 Genomes was not enough. With twenty-five hundred samples, you could catalogue common variation well, but rare variation — variants present in less than one percent of any population — remained undersampled. And the 1000 Genomes data was based on short-read sequencing, which missed many structural variants and could not resolve the difficult repetitive regions of the genome.

Two follow-up efforts addressed these limitations, and they bring us into the modern era.

The first is the Telomere-to-Telomere consortium, which finally completed the human genome in 2022. The second is the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium, which in 2023 began replacing the single linear reference with a graph-based pangenome. Those are the next two segments.

## SEGMENT 13 — T2T-CHM13 in 2022: the truly complete genome

[Pause.]

I told you in Segment 9 that the 2003 "finished" genome was not actually finished. About eight percent of it — roughly two hundred million bases — was left unassembled, in the centromeres, the telomeres, the long arms of the acrocentric chromosomes, and various other heterochromatic regions. Those gaps stayed gaps for nearly twenty years, because the short-read sequencing technologies of the 2000s and 2010s could not span the long, near-identical repeat arrays that fill those regions.

The technology that finally cracked the problem is long-read sequencing. Two platforms in particular: PacBio HiFi, which uses single-molecule real-time circular consensus sequencing to produce reads of fifteen to twenty kilobases at greater than 99.9 percent accuracy, and Oxford Nanopore, which threads single DNA molecules through a protein pore and measures the electrical current changes as each base passes through, producing reads of one hundred kilobases to over a megabase, with accuracy improving steadily into the high-99-percent range.

Combined, these technologies can read across centromeric satellite arrays that are hundreds of kilobases long, made up of a single repeat unit copied tens of thousands of times. The 2003 short-read approach simply could not do this — the reads were too short to span the repeats, and the assembler could not figure out how many copies were present or in what order.

[Pause.]

The Telomere-to-Telomere consortium, T2T, was formed around 2019 by an informal group of long-read sequencing experts, led by Adam Phillippy at NIH and Karen Miga at UC Santa Cruz. They chose to focus on a single sample, called CHM13, which is a hydatidiform mole cell line. A hydatidiform mole is an abnormal pregnancy in which a single sperm fertilizes an egg that has lost its own nucleus; the resulting cells are essentially haploid, with two identical copies of every chromosome. Sequencing a haploid sample dramatically simplifies the assembly problem because you do not have to deal with two different parental haplotypes.

In April 2022, the T2T consortium published the first truly complete sequence of a human genome — every chromosome, end to end, including all centromeres, all telomeres, all acrocentric short arms, all of it. T2T-CHM13 added approximately two hundred million new bases compared to the previous reference, corrected thousands of errors in the older assembly, and uncovered roughly two thousand new protein-coding gene candidates, mostly in regions that had been black boxes.

[Pause.]

The analogy I want you to hold for T2T is this. The 2003 reference was like a map of the United States that had every city, every highway, and every river — but with about eight percent of the map covered by clouds, because the satellite imagery could not see through them. You knew where the clouds were, you knew roughly how big the regions were, but the actual terrain was invisible. T2T finally lifted the clouds. We can now see the centromeres — the structural anchors that hold chromosomes together during cell division. We can see the telomere ends. We can see the ribosomal DNA arrays. None of these regions were terra incognita in the literal sense, but they had been sequence incognita for the entire history of human genomics.

The discoveries from T2T are still being worked out. The centromeric satellite arrays turn out to have far more structural variation between individuals than anyone expected. The acrocentric short arms contain unexpected gene families. The regions that had been assumed to be inert repeat junk turn out to harbor active transcription and to vary between people in ways that may have functional consequences.

T2T-CHM13 is now widely used as the reference of choice for new analyses. The old GRCh38 reference is still in heavy use because of inertia and tool compatibility, but the field is migrating. By the end of the 2020s, T2T-CHM13 will likely be the standard.

## SEGMENT 14 — The Human Pangenome Reference Consortium

[Pause.]

T2T-CHM13 is a complete genome, but it is still one genome — and as we said in Segment 12, no single genome can represent human variation. The next frontier, and this is where the field is actively living right now in 2026, is the Human Pangenome.

The Human Pangenome Reference Consortium, HPRC, was launched in 2019 with funding from NHGRI, and published its first draft pangenome in Nature in May 2023.

[Pause.]

The idea of a pangenome is to replace the single linear reference sequence with a graph. Instead of one string of three billion bases, you have a graph in which each chromosome is represented as a network of nodes — shared sequence that everybody has — and branches — alternate sequences carried by different individuals or populations.

The 2023 draft pangenome contains forty-seven assemblies, each from a different individual, each sequenced and assembled to near-T2T quality using long reads. Together, those forty-seven assemblies represent ninety-four haplotypes — two per individual — drawn from diverse populations around the world. The pangenome graph captures variation that simply cannot exist in a single linear reference: regions where some people carry sequence that other people lack entirely, structural rearrangements that differ between haplotypes, large insertions that are present in some populations and absent in others.

[Pause.]

Here is the analogy. The 2003 linear reference is a single recipe for chocolate cake — flour, sugar, eggs, butter, cocoa, in specific amounts and a specific order. The pangenome is a cookbook of forty-seven variant cake recipes from forty-seven bakers around the world, with the shared steps consolidated and the divergent steps shown as alternative paths. Some bakers add cinnamon. Some substitute coconut flour. Some swap milk chocolate for dark. The pangenome lets you see, at every point in the recipe, what the actual range of human variation looks like, rather than treating one baker's recipe as canonical and everyone else's as deviation.

The HPRC's stated goal is to scale up to three hundred and fifty individual assemblies by the late 2020s, with deliberate inclusion of historically underrepresented populations — African, Indigenous American, South Asian, Oceanic. This matters scientifically because the original 2003 reference and most subsequent variation databases have been skewed toward European-ancestry samples, which means that clinical variant interpretation has been more accurate for people of European descent than for everybody else.

The pangenome is the future of the human reference. It is harder to work with than a linear reference — graph-based alignment tools are still maturing, and most existing bioinformatics pipelines assume a linear coordinate system — but the field is steadily building the infrastructure to make graph references the default.

## SEGMENT 15 — The spin-offs: TCGA, GTEx, UK Biobank, the omics revolution

[Pause.]

We have walked through the history of the reference genome itself, from 2003 through T2T and the pangenome. Now I want to step back and talk about the spin-off effects — the projects and infrastructures that exist because HGP existed, and that have arguably had larger practical impact than the reference sequence itself.

[Pause.]

The Cancer Genome Atlas, TCGA, was launched in 2006 as a joint NHGRI and National Cancer Institute project. The idea was simple in concept and enormous in execution: take thousands of tumors, sequence the cancer genomes, sequence the matched normal tissue from the same patients, and catalogue the somatic mutations that drive cancer. Over its life, TCGA characterized more than eleven thousand tumors across thirty-three cancer types, producing petabytes of genomic, transcriptomic, methylation, and clinical data, all of it freely available.

TCGA changed cancer biology. It established that almost every cancer is genetically distinct at the molecular level, that most cancers are driven by a small number of recurrent driver mutations layered over many passenger mutations, that cancers can be classified more meaningfully by their molecular profile than by their tissue of origin, and that targeted therapies designed against specific driver mutations can produce dramatic clinical responses. The modern era of precision oncology — sequencing a patient's tumor to choose their treatment — is built directly on TCGA infrastructure.

[Pause.]

The Genotype-Tissue Expression project, GTEx, launched in 2010, took the same logic in a different direction. GTEx collected matched samples from up to fifty-four tissue types in nearly a thousand deceased donors and measured gene expression — what genes are turned on at what levels — in every tissue. The result is the definitive atlas of where in the body every human gene is expressed. If you want to know whether a gene is on in liver, in brain, in pancreas, in testis — GTEx tells you. Combined with the variation catalogues from 1000 Genomes, GTEx allowed researchers to map expression quantitative trait loci, eQTLs — genetic variants that affect how much of a particular gene is expressed — across the human genome.

[Pause.]

The UK Biobank is perhaps the most consequential single resource produced in the post-HGP era. Launched in 2006, the UK Biobank recruited five hundred thousand participants aged forty to sixty-nine, collected baseline health data and biological samples, linked the participants to their electronic health records over decades of follow-up, and then — beginning in 2017 — genotyped all five hundred thousand on dense SNP arrays, and beginning in the early 2020s, generated whole-exome and whole-genome sequencing on the entire cohort.

The UK Biobank is the largest publicly available genotype-phenotype dataset in the world. Researchers anywhere on earth can apply for access, and within a few months they can be running analyses on data from half a million people linked to comprehensive lifetime medical records. The number of papers using UK Biobank data is now in the tens of thousands and growing exponentially.

[Pause.]

Other biobanks are now coming online at similar or larger scales. The All of Us Research Program in the U.S. is enrolling a million participants with explicit emphasis on demographic diversity. The FinnGen project in Finland leverages that country's exceptional population registry data. The China Kadoorie Biobank covers half a million Chinese participants. Japan's Tohoku Medical Megabank covers regions affected by the 2011 tsunami.

The point is this. The Human Genome Project produced a reference. The reference enabled a set of techniques — SNP genotyping arrays, then short-read sequencing, then long-read sequencing — to be applied at scale. Those techniques enabled population-scale biobanks. The biobanks are where modern human genetics actually lives. We are now in the era where the unit of analysis is not one genome but a million genomes, linked to a million sets of medical records, analyzed across decades.

None of that exists without HGP. The whole edifice rests on the foundation poured between 1990 and 2003.

## SEGMENT 16 — ELSI: the 5% set-aside and the ethics infrastructure

[Pause.]

There is one more legacy of HGP that I have to insist on covering, because it is unique to this project and it has had effects far beyond genomics. It is the ELSI program.

ELSI stands for Ethical, Legal, and Social Implications. From the very beginning of the Human Genome Project, NHGRI committed a fixed percentage of its annual budget — originally three percent, later raised to five percent — to research and policy work on the ethical, legal, and social implications of genomic research.

[Pause.]

This was unprecedented for a major federal research project. The reasoning, articulated by Watson and then carried forward by Collins, was that genomic information is uniquely sensitive. It is heritable. It is shared with family members. It can predict disease risk. It can identify individuals uniquely. The technologies that would be enabled by HGP — genetic testing, prenatal screening, forensic DNA, ancestry inference, employment and insurance applications — all raised serious ethical and policy questions that science alone could not answer.

The five percent set-aside funded a sustained, decades-long research program on these questions. ELSI-funded scholars include lawyers, bioethicists, sociologists, historians, philosophers, and policy analysts as well as scientists. ELSI funded studies on genetic discrimination, on informed consent, on privacy, on the special considerations for indigenous and historically marginalized communities, on the implications of direct-to-consumer genetic testing, on the use of forensic DNA, on prenatal and newborn screening, and on the equitable distribution of genomic medicine.

[Pause.]

The single most concrete legislative outcome of the ELSI program was the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act, GINA, signed into U.S. federal law in May 2008. GINA prohibits health insurers from using genetic information to determine eligibility or premiums, and prohibits employers from using genetic information in hiring, firing, or promotion decisions. GINA passed Congress with overwhelming bipartisan support — ninety-five to zero in the Senate, four hundred fourteen to one in the House — which is almost unheard of for any substantial piece of legislation, and it was the direct product of more than a decade of ELSI-funded research, advocacy, and coalition-building.

[Pause.]

GINA has real limitations. It does not cover life insurance, long-term care insurance, or disability insurance — three areas where genetic information could still legally be used against individuals. It does not cover all employer activities, especially in small employers. And its enforcement track record has been uneven.

But the principle that genetic information deserves special legal protection, and that society should think carefully about how genomic data is used, is now embedded in law and in policy. That principle did not exist in 1990. ELSI created it.

[Pause.]

The other lasting effect of ELSI is institutional. Almost every major genomic research project today — UK Biobank, All of Us, the Pangenome Consortium, the Population Genomics Initiative — has an ethics and community engagement component built into its design from the start. The notion that you cannot just collect human genomic data without thinking about consent, governance, community involvement, and benefit sharing has become the standard. That standard was set by ELSI.

This is also, by the way, a model that other emerging technology areas — AI, gene editing, synthetic biology, neurotechnology — have explicitly looked to. The idea of dedicating a fixed budget percentage to social and ethical research alongside the technical research is now widely cited as a best practice. Whether it is consistently implemented elsewhere is another question, but the model exists, and HGP is where it came from.

## SEGMENT 17 — What HGP did NOT solve: polygenic disease, regulatory variants, missing heritability

[Pause.]

This is the honest segment. I have been mostly celebrating HGP for nine segments. Now I have to be candid about what HGP promised, what it did not deliver, and what we are still struggling with.

The 1990s rhetoric around HGP, including some of Collins's own speeches and a great deal of media coverage, suggested that sequencing the genome would crack the genetic basis of common diseases. Heart disease, diabetes, schizophrenia, autism, Alzheimer's — these would all yield their genetic secrets once we had the sequence. Personalized medicine, in which your treatment is tailored to your genome, would arrive within a decade or two.

[Pause.]

Twenty-three years after the 2003 finished sequence, that vision has been only partially realized. Here is the honest accounting.

For Mendelian disorders — diseases caused by single-gene mutations with simple inheritance patterns — HGP has been transformative. Diseases like cystic fibrosis, sickle cell anemia, Huntington's disease, the rare childhood metabolic disorders, the rare immunodeficiencies — these are diagnosed routinely now through clinical sequencing. The rare disease diagnostic odyssey, which used to take families an average of five to seven years and dozens of specialists, can now often be resolved in weeks with whole-exome or whole-genome sequencing. Thousands of new disease genes have been identified, and an increasing number of rare diseases have targeted treatments.

For common complex diseases — heart disease, type 2 diabetes, schizophrenia, depression, most cancers, autoimmune diseases — the story is far more humbling.

[Pause.]

When genome-wide association studies, GWAS, became feasible around 2005 to 2007, the field expected to find a small number of common variants of large effect that would explain most of the genetic risk for common diseases. The first GWAS results, around 2007, found something very different.

What GWAS found, repeatedly, across disease after disease, was that the genetic architecture of common disease is polygenic — meaning that risk is influenced by hundreds or thousands of common variants, each of small individual effect, scattered across the genome. Schizophrenia: more than three hundred associated loci, each contributing a tiny fraction of risk. Height — a non-disease trait studied as a model — more than twelve thousand associated variants, together explaining maybe forty percent of the heritability. Diabetes, hypertension, obesity, depression: all polygenic, all with hundreds of loci, all with the same picture.

This was not what people expected, and it was not what HGP rhetoric had implicitly promised. The world of single-gene tests for common diseases largely did not materialize, because there are no single genes to test.

[Pause.]

The second uncomfortable finding from GWAS is that the vast majority of associated variants — somewhere between eighty-five and ninety percent — are not in protein-coding sequence. They are in regulatory regions, in introns, in intergenic space. These are variants that presumably affect when and how much specific genes are expressed in specific tissues, but tracing the path from a non-coding variant to a specific functional consequence is enormously harder than tracing a coding variant that changes a specific amino acid in a specific protein.

The third uncomfortable finding is what is called missing heritability. For many traits, twin studies suggest that genetic factors explain, say, seventy or eighty percent of variation in the population — meaning the trait is highly heritable. But the GWAS-identified variants together explain only ten or twenty or thirty percent. Where is the missing heritability?

The answer is some combination of: rare variants not captured by SNP arrays, structural variants not captured by short-read sequencing, gene-by-gene interactions not captured by single-variant association tests, gene-by-environment interactions not captured at all, and overestimates of heritability from twin studies due to shared environment confounding. Some of the missing heritability is being recovered as biobank-scale sequencing reveals rare variants. Some of it may never be recoverable in the form that twin studies suggested it exists.

[Pause.]

The honest summary is this. HGP gave us the reference. The reference enabled GWAS. GWAS revealed that common disease is polygenic, regulatory, and genuinely complex. That is not what was promised in the 1990s, but it is what is true. Personalized medicine has arrived in some specific high-leverage areas — pharmacogenomics, oncology, rare disease — but the broader vision of "your genome tells your doctor exactly what will happen to you and what to do about it" has not arrived and may never arrive in that form. The genome is one input. Environment, behavior, age, and stochastic biological events are also inputs, and they cannot be inferred from the sequence alone.

This is not a failure of HGP. It is a correction of an overpromise. The science is harder than the rhetoric suggested. The Human Genome Project did exactly what it set out to do — produce the reference sequence. It is everything we have learned from that reference that has shown us how complicated the path from sequence to disease actually is.

## SEGMENT 18 — The lasting legacy: the $100 genome, polygenic risk scores, AI-genomics integration

[Pause.]

We end on legacy and on where the field is going. Five threads I want to leave you with.

[Pause.]

Thread one: the cost curve, and the $100 genome. In 1990, sequencing a human genome cost $3 billion. In 2003, the second human genome would have cost about $300 million. By 2008, the first individual personal genomes were costing about $1 million each. In 2014, the cost crossed the $1,000 genome threshold, the symbolic target Illumina had been chasing for years. Today in 2026, a clinical whole genome runs about $200, and research-grade genomes from major sequencing centers can be produced for well under that. The $100 genome is essentially here. The next target, often discussed, is the $10 genome — at which point sequencing becomes cheap enough to be a routine clinical commodity, like a blood test.

Thread two: polygenic risk scores. We talked in the previous segment about how common disease is polygenic. The clinical translation of that finding is the polygenic risk score, PRS — a single number computed from a person's genotype across hundreds or thousands of disease-associated variants, weighted by their effect sizes from GWAS. For some diseases — coronary artery disease, breast cancer, type 2 diabetes — polygenic risk scores can now identify the top few percent of the population as having genetic risk equivalent to a strong monogenic mutation. PRS is starting to be used clinically for risk stratification, screening prioritization, and prevention. The accuracy and equity issues are still being worked out, but PRS is the most credible operationalization of the personalized medicine vision that HGP originally promised.

Thread three: biobank-scale science. We covered this in Segment 15. The unit of analysis is now a million genomes, linked to clinical records, imaging, wearable data, environmental exposures. This is a fundamentally different kind of human genetics than what existed before 2010, and it is producing findings on a scale that simply was not possible in the original HGP era.

Thread four: AI-genomics integration. The most exciting and rapidly developing frontier in genomics right now is the integration of machine learning with genomic data. Deep learning models trained on genomic sequence can now predict, with substantial accuracy, which variants will affect splicing, which regions of the genome are likely to be regulatory, which non-coding variants are likely to disrupt enhancer function. Protein structure prediction — AlphaFold and its successors — has solved the problem of going from amino acid sequence to three-dimensional structure for nearly every known protein. Foundation models trained on biological sequence are starting to generalize across tasks in ways that resemble what large language models do for text. The intersection of AI and genomics is where a lot of the next decade's progress will happen, and that intersection is only possible because HGP and its successors produced the foundational datasets that the AI models need.

Thread five: equity and global genomics. The greatest open issue in genomics right now is equity. The vast majority of genomic data, the reference itself, the variant catalogues, the GWAS samples, the biobank cohorts — have been heavily biased toward European-ancestry samples. This has direct clinical consequences: variant interpretation is more accurate for European patients than for everyone else, polygenic risk scores trained in European cohorts perform substantially worse when applied to other populations, and the benefits of genomic medicine have been unequally distributed. The Human Pangenome Consortium, the All of Us program, the African and Latin American biobank initiatives, the H3Africa consortium, and several other efforts are working to correct this imbalance. The next decade of genomics will be defined in part by how successfully the field shifts from a European-centric to a globally representative dataset.

[Pause.]

So where does that leave us, looking back on HGP from 2026?

The Human Genome Project was biology's moon landing — and like the moon landing, its most important legacy is not the artifact itself but the infrastructure, the techniques, the culture, and the political and institutional precedents it left behind. The 2003 reference sequence has been superseded by T2T-CHM13 and by the Human Pangenome. The original twenty-thousand-gene catalogue has been refined and re-annotated thousands of times. The technologies of 1990 — Sanger sequencing, BAC cloning, gel electrophoresis — are essentially obsolete, replaced by short-read sequencing platforms, then long-read platforms, and now whatever comes next.

But the open-data culture established by the Bermuda Principles is still the default in genomics. The ELSI framework is still the model for thinking about emerging biotechnology ethics. The pattern of large, internationally coordinated, publicly funded biology — once thought to be impossible, then thought to be a one-off — is now routine. The cost curve of sequencing has dropped by seven orders of magnitude. Genomic medicine, while it has not delivered everything that was promised, has delivered real things — Mendelian disease diagnosis, precision oncology, pharmacogenomics, polygenic risk stratification — that did not exist before HGP.

[Pause.]

If you walk out of here remembering one thing, remember the moon landing analogy. The audacious goal, the decade-long collaborative effort, the political commitment, the spinoff technologies that mattered more than the original artifact. That is what HGP was, and that is why it is the foundational story of modern biology.

Thank you.

[Pause.]

### Key concepts to retain

- **Sanger sequencing**: the chain-termination method used throughout HGP; ~500-900 bases per read.
- **BAC**: Bacterial Artificial Chromosome; ~150 kb vector used in clone-by-clone mapping.
- **Clone-by-clone vs whole-genome shotgun**: the two competing strategies; both turned out to be necessary.
- **Bermuda Principles**: 24-hour public data release; arguably the most consequential single policy decision.
- **Euchromatin vs heterochromatin**: ~92% vs ~8% of the genome; the 2003 finished sequence covered euchromatin only.
- **Polygenic**: hundreds or thousands of small-effect variants influencing a trait — the dominant pattern for common disease.
- **Missing heritability**: the gap between twin-study heritability and GWAS-explained heritability.
- **ELSI**: the 5% set-aside for Ethical, Legal, and Social Implications research.
- **T2T**: Telomere-to-Telomere consortium; 2022 completion using long reads.
- **Pangenome**: graph-based reference replacing the single linear reference.
