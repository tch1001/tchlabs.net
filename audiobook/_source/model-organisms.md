## SEGMENT 1 — Why model organisms, and what they bought us

Alright, settle in. For the next two hours we are going to talk about model organisms — and specifically about three of them: a tiny transparent worm called _Caenorhabditis elegans_, a fruit fly called _Drosophila melanogaster_, and a small striped fish called _Danio rerio_, the zebrafish. These three are not the only model organisms that biology uses — there's yeast, there's _E. coli_, there's the mouse, there's _Arabidopsis_ for plants — but these three cover an extraordinary amount of what we know about animal genetics, animal development, and animal nervous systems. And if you walk out of here remembering nothing else, I want you to walk out remembering this: **modern genetics rests on a small set of model organisms, each chosen for the questions it answers best, and the choice was never accidental — it was a deliberate evolutionary strategy by the field itself, picking animals whose biology matched the technical questions of the era.**

Let me start with the analogy I'm going to come back to over and over again today, because if you internalize it, half the lecture becomes intuitive. **The model organisms of biology are a small-animal Olympic team.** Each one chosen for the questions it answers best. The worm runs the cell-lineage event — because it has 959 somatic cells and you can see every one of them. The fly runs the classical genetics event — because it has chromosomes you can map and a century of accumulated mutant strains. The zebrafish runs the vertebrate-development event — because the embryo is transparent and a single pair of fish lays two hundred eggs in a morning. None of them is the best athlete overall. But each one is unbeatable in its event. And modern biology is what you get when you put the team together and let each member compete in the discipline they were built for.

\[Pause.\]

Now let me back up and ask: why model organisms at all? Why don't we just study humans directly? Or, if we're being more humane, why don't we just pick one animal and study only that one?

The answer is a list of practical constraints, every one of which the field figured out by painful trial and error over the twentieth century. Let me run through them, because every one of these constraints is a reason somebody chose somebody chose worm, or fly, or fish, in the first place.

**One: generation time.** Genetics is a science of crosses. You take two animals with different genotypes, you mate them, you look at the offspring, you mate those offspring, you look at the next generation, and so on. The faster the organism reproduces, the faster you do science. A human takes twenty-five years per generation. A mouse takes ten weeks. A zebrafish takes three months. A fruit fly takes ten days. A worm takes three days. You can do, in worm, ten generations in the time it takes a mouse to do one. Ten generations is the difference between answering a question and not answering it, between a PhD thesis and a permanent mystery. Generation time is everything.

**Two: ease of genetics.** Some organisms are wildly easier to manipulate genetically than others. Some have small genomes, simple chromosomes, well-defined developmental stages where you can inject DNA. Others have huge complicated polyploid genomes and no obvious place to grab the embryo. Worms and flies have famously tractable genetics. Zebrafish have just enough tractable genetics to make vertebrate development possible at scale. Humans, by contrast, are essentially genetically intractable — you can't cross humans on demand, you can't knock out human genes in living humans, you have to take whatever variation nature happens to provide. The model organisms gave us the experimental dial that humans took away.

**Three: conservation of biology.** The deep secret of comparative biology is that almost everything that matters at the molecular level is conserved across enormous evolutionary distances. The gene that controls eye development in a fly — _eyeless_ — is the same gene that controls eye development in a mouse, called _Pax6_. Knock it out in either organism and you lose eyes. The Hox genes that pattern the body plan of a fly are the same Hox genes that pattern the body plan of a vertebrate, in the same order along the chromosome, doing the same job along the body axis. The insulin signaling pathway that controls metabolism in a worm is the same pathway that controls metabolism in a human. We are, deep down, all running variations on the same biochemical operating system. So a discovery made in a fly often translates to mice, and a discovery made in mice often translates to humans. Not always. But often enough. That's the bet the entire model organism enterprise is built on, and over fifty years it has paid off enough times to keep being made.

**Four: ethics and cost.** A worm experiment costs essentially nothing. The animals live on a petri dish, eat bacteria, and reproduce in three days. You don't need ethics committee approval to kill a worm. A fly experiment is slightly more expensive but still tiny. A fish experiment is more substantial — you need a fish facility with circulating water and temperature control — but still vastly cheaper than a mouse facility, and the ethical framework is more permissive because fish are far enough from humans that the regulatory regime is lighter. A mouse experiment costs ten or twenty times what a fish experiment costs. A monkey experiment costs ten or twenty times what a mouse experiment costs. The model organism scale lets you do enormous experiments — hundreds of thousands of animals, genome-wide screens, decades-long aging studies — that you simply could not afford in a more expensive organism.

\[Pause.\]

So those are the constraints. Now the strategic question. If you had to pick a small handful of organisms to do most of animal biology with, which would you pick? The historical answer — and it's not random, it's a series of deliberate choices made by specific people in specific decades — converged on these three:

The worm, for cell lineage, aging, and the simplest neuroscience. Three hundred and two neurons. Completely mapped. Transparent. Three-day generation time. The simplest animal you can imagine that still has differentiation, behavior, and learning.

The fly, for classical genetics, developmental patterning, and complex behavior. Chromosomes you can see in the salivary glands. A hundred years of accumulated mutant lines. A nervous system with about a hundred thousand neurons that nonetheless supports learning, memory, sleep, courtship, circadian rhythm — the full repertoire of brain function in miniature.

The fish, for vertebrate development at scale. Same vertebrate body plan as humans — same notochord, same neural tube, same somites, same fins where our limbs would be. Transparent embryos so you can watch every cell of development under a microscope. Two hundred eggs per pair per week. The vertebrate that scaled.

These three are not competitors. They are complements. They cover, between them, the questions you can ask in a small animal — from gene function to neural circuitry to vertebrate organogenesis — and for fifty years they have been the primary substrate on which the genetic logic of animal biology has been worked out.

\[Pause.\]

So here is the contract for the next two hours. By the end, you should be able to do three things. **One:** explain, with the Olympic team analogy, why we use a portfolio of model organisms rather than a single one, and why each of these three was chosen for the work it does. **Two:** walk through, in rough terms, the major discoveries that came out of each — Sulston's cell lineage, Brenner's worm, Morgan's chromosomes, Nüsslein-Volhard's screen, Streisinger's fish, the Boston-Tübingen big screen, fish CRISPR — and have a feel for which discoveries came from which organism and why. **Three:** look at a paper, a press release, or a news article about a new genetic finding, a new developmental gene, a new disease model, and have real intuition for how the underlying model-organism work led to it. Because almost every modern genetic story, somewhere upstream, has a worm or a fly or a fish in its history.

Let's start where the worm started — with a single scientist in 1965 making a deliberate decision to bet his career on a nematode that fits on a fingernail.

## SEGMENT 2 — Sydney Brenner and the deliberate choice of the worm

In 1965, a molecular biologist named Sydney Brenner sat down in his office at the MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology in Cambridge, England, and wrote what is now one of the most famous documents in twentieth-century biology: a memo, addressed to Max Perutz, the head of the lab, in which he announced that he wanted to switch his research program from the molecular biology of bacteria and bacteriophages — which by then was a mature field — to something much harder. He wanted to understand how a complete nervous system is built. How a body plan is laid out. How the genes specify the cells, how the cells assemble into tissues, how the tissues into an animal. He wanted, in short, to understand development and nervous systems at the level of individual cells and individual genes.

Brenner had a track record by then. He had been part of the team that worked out the genetic code, that established the existence of messenger RNA, that nailed down many of the basic facts of molecular biology in the 1950s and 1960s. He had earned the right to bet his career on a question. And the question he chose — how a multicellular animal is built, gene by gene, cell by cell — was, at the time, considered essentially unanswerable. Nobody knew where to start.

Brenner's bet — and this is the bet that, when it paid off, transformed the field — was that to answer that question, **you had to pick the right animal.** Not the most famous animal. Not the animal whose biology you cared about emotionally. The right animal — meaning the one whose biology was simple enough that you could actually do the experiments, but rich enough that the answer would matter.

\[Pause.\]

So Brenner went looking. He surveyed possibilities. Sea urchins were transparent and easy to fertilize but adults were too big. Flatworms had a nervous system but were a mess to manipulate. Various nematodes were considered. Brenner eventually settled on a particular species of soil nematode called _Caenorhabditis elegans_ — a tiny worm, about one millimeter long, that lives in temperate soils and eats bacteria. Almost nobody outside of taxonomy had ever heard of it. Brenner picked it on purpose.

Let me give you the list of features that made him pick it, because every one of these is a reason you can do experiments in worms that you can't do anywhere else.

**One: it's tiny.** About one millimeter long as an adult, roughly the diameter of a pencil lead. You can keep ten thousand worms on a single petri dish. You can fit a year of experiments into a desk drawer. The space constraints that limit mouse and zebrafish work essentially disappear in worms.

**Two: it's transparent.** Under a microscope, you can see straight through the worm's body. Every internal cell — every neuron, every muscle fiber, every gut cell — is visible without dissection. You can watch the animal develop, watch its embryo divide, watch its cells migrate, all without cutting anything open. The whole animal is its own histology slide.

**Three: it has a tiny number of cells.** An adult hermaphrodite has exactly 959 somatic cells. Exactly. Every healthy adult worm has the same number, in the same positions, doing the same jobs. That's an absurd statement for any other animal. A mouse has tens of billions of cells, of which any two are arranged slightly differently. A fly has tens of thousands. A worm has 959, in the same arrangement every time. We'll see in the next segment what you can do with that.

**Four: it has a fast life cycle.** Three days from fertilized egg to reproductive adult, at room temperature. A worm laid this morning is laying its own eggs by the weekend. Compare with mouse at ten weeks, fish at three months, fly at ten days. Worms are the speed runners of the animal kingdom for genetics.

**Five: it has simple genetics.** Six chromosomes — five autosomes and one sex chromosome. About a hundred million base pairs of genome — small for an animal. About twenty thousand genes — comparable to humans, despite the simpler body. Hermaphroditic — most adults are hermaphrodites that self-fertilize, which means a single founder animal can produce an entire homozygous line without you doing any crosses, and which makes maintaining mutant strains trivial. The occasional male can be used for outcrossing when you want to combine genotypes.

**Six: it freezes.** You can take a tube of worms, put them in glycerol, freeze them in liquid nitrogen, and thaw them out years later. They come back to life and start reproducing. So you can archive every mutant strain you ever isolate, indefinitely, in a freezer. There is no comparable trick in mouse or fly. The worm community maintains a stock center, the _Caenorhabditis_ Genetics Center in Minneapolis, that holds tens of thousands of frozen strains, and any lab in the world can request a vial.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to hold for Brenner's choice: **Brenner's pick of the worm was the deliberate decision in 1965 — he picked the worm because it was simple enough to map every cell, complex enough to have interesting biology.** That's the sweet spot. Too simple and you don't have the biology you care about — bacteria don't have development or nervous systems. Too complex and you can't see what's going on — a mouse has too many cells, too many genes, too long a generation time to map at single-cell resolution. The worm sits exactly in the middle. Simple enough to map. Complex enough to matter.

And Brenner didn't just pick the worm and start running experiments. He spent years building the infrastructure — figuring out how to grow worms reproducibly on agar plates seeded with _E. coli_ as food, working out the mutagenesis protocols, isolating the first mutant strains, building the genetic map. He recruited a small group of colleagues and students who became, over the next decade, the founding generation of worm biology: John Sulston, Robert Horvitz, John White, and others whose names are going to come up in the segments ahead.

\[Pause.\]

It's worth pausing on the strategic genius of this choice, because it's a model for how foundational science gets done. Brenner did not start by asking, "What animal is most interesting?" Many animals are interesting. He started by asking, "What is the simplest experimental system that still contains the phenomenon I want to understand?" The phenomenon — development, behavior, nervous system function — needed to be present. But every additional layer of biological complexity, beyond the minimum needed to host that phenomenon, was a cost. It made experiments harder, slower, more expensive. So you find the simplest organism that still does the thing, and you commit to it.

Half a century later, _C. elegans_ research has produced six Nobel Prizes, mapped a complete nervous system at the level of individual neurons and their connections, established the molecular basis of apoptosis (programmed cell death), revealed how aging is genetically controlled, and provided the system in which RNA interference was discovered. Brenner's bet, in retrospect, looks like the best career decision in twentieth-century biology. But at the time, in 1965, it looked like a senior molecular biologist deciding to study a worm nobody had heard of. Strategic genius is hard to recognize until afterwards.

Now let's go look at the most extraordinary thing that came out of Brenner's worm — the complete cell lineage map.

## SEGMENT 3 — Sulston's cell lineage: the cell-by-cell movie of the worm

So you have a worm. You have an embryo. The embryo is a single fertilized egg, sitting in a thin transparent shell, about fifty micrometers long. Over the next fourteen hours, that single cell will divide, and divide, and divide, and the resulting cells will move, differentiate, organize themselves, and become a larval worm that hatches out of the shell. Then, over the next two and a half days, that larval worm will grow, divide further, lay down its adult anatomy, and become a reproductive adult with 959 somatic cells.

The question that John Sulston asked, starting in the mid-1970s, was: **what if we wrote down, cell by cell, the entire family tree of how every adult cell descended from the single fertilized egg?**

Let me say that again, because the audaciousness of the question is easy to miss. Sulston's goal was to construct, for the worm, a complete cell-lineage diagram — a chart, like a family tree, showing for every one of the 959 adult cells which cell it came from, which cell came from before that, all the way back to the fertilized egg. Every division. Every cell death. Every migration. The complete, exhaustive, cell-by-cell movie of how a worm gets built.

The analogy: **the Sulston lineage is the cell-by-cell movie of how every adult cell came from a fertilized egg. An absurdly heroic feat of microscopy and patience.** Sulston, working with collaborators including Einhard Schierenberg and John White, sat at a microscope for hours every day, for years, watching individual worm embryos develop. He drew diagrams. He named cells. He tracked their daughters. He noted which cells died — because in worms, exactly 131 cells are produced during development and then deliberately killed by programmed cell death, a tightly stereotyped process that Sulston also catalogued. He produced, eventually, a chart that looks like a phenomenally detailed family tree, with the fertilized egg at the top and the 959 adult cells at the bottom, every connection drawn in.

\[Pause.\]

Why was this such a big deal? Two reasons, one biological and one technical.

The biological reason: **the worm cell lineage is invariant.** Every worm goes through exactly the same divisions, in exactly the same order, producing exactly the same cells in exactly the same positions, every single time. That's astonishing. In a mouse or a human embryo, no two embryos are wired exactly the same way. There's stochasticity in cell division, in cell migration, in cell fate choice. Two genetically identical mouse embryos develop into mice with slightly different brain wiring, slightly different organ structure. In the worm, that's not true. Two worm embryos, grown side by side, will produce two worms whose cells are arranged in the same positions, dividing in the same order, dying at the same times, doing the same jobs. The worm is, in a sense, **a deterministic developmental program**. The cell lineage is the trace of that program. Reading the lineage is like reading the source code.

The technical reason: once you have the lineage, you can ask, for any mutant worm, **exactly which cell did something wrong.** If a worm with a particular mutation lacks a certain neuron, you can look at the lineage and ask, "Which division should have produced that neuron? Did the division happen? Did it produce the right daughter cells? Did one daughter die when it should have lived?" You can pinpoint, at single-cell resolution, the developmental step at which the mutation acted. That's a level of precision in developmental analysis that simply cannot be achieved in any other animal.

\[Pause.\]

Sulston's lineage was published in pieces through the late 1970s and early 1980s, and the complete adult lineage appeared in a landmark 1983 paper. It is, to this day, the only complete cell-lineage map of any multicellular animal. No other organism has been mapped this thoroughly. The worm is unique not just because Brenner picked it, but because Sulston had the patience to do this work — to sit at a microscope for what amounted to a decade and draw, by hand, the developmental program of an animal.

Alongside the lineage came the discovery of **programmed cell death**, or **apoptosis**, as a stereotyped developmental phenomenon. Sulston noticed that of the cells produced during worm development, exactly 131 were always killed — and always the same 131, in the same lineages, at the same developmental times. The deaths were not accidents. They were part of the program. Subsequent genetic work, primarily by Robert Horvitz, identified the genes that controlled this death program — _ced-3_, _ced-4_, _ced-9_, and others — and showed that those genes have direct mammalian homologs that control apoptosis in humans, including in cancer (where apoptosis is often dysregulated) and in neurodegenerative disease (where apoptosis is often inappropriately activated). The molecular logic of cell death, in your body right now, was worked out in worms.

\[Pause.\]

The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for 2002 went jointly to Sydney Brenner, John Sulston, and Robert Horvitz, "for their discoveries concerning genetic regulation of organ development and programmed cell death." It was, in a real sense, the Nobel for choosing the worm and then doing what the worm made possible. Brenner had picked it. Sulston had mapped it. Horvitz had used it to find the cell-death genes. Three Nobel laureates from one nematode and one decision in 1965.

The big idea to leave this segment with: **the worm cell lineage is the first and only complete cellular ancestry of a whole animal.** It set the standard for what developmental biology could aspire to — single-cell resolution, complete coverage, deterministic mapping. Modern single-cell transcriptomics, which we'll touch on in Segment 18, is in some sense an attempt to replicate the Sulston-style level of resolution in organisms much larger than the worm. We're not there yet for the mouse or human. But the worm has been there for forty years.

Now let's open up how you _do_ genetics in this system — how you find which gene does which job, when you have a transparent worm with a complete cell map and a freezer full of mutant strains.

## SEGMENT 4 — The unc/lin/mab gene namespace and forward genetic screens

Here's the basic technique of forward genetics, which is the engine that powered most of what came out of the worm in its first two decades. The logic goes like this. You have an animal with normal anatomy and behavior. You introduce mutations at random — typically by feeding the animals a chemical mutagen like EMS, ethyl methanesulfonate, which causes point mutations roughly uniformly across the genome. You let the mutagenized animals reproduce. You screen the resulting offspring for any that look or behave abnormally. Then, having found an interesting mutant, you ask: what gene was mutated? You map the mutation, you clone the gene, and now you have identified, by phenotype-first reasoning, a gene that's required for whatever process you were screening for.

This is **forward genetics**, and the name comes from the direction of the inference: you go from phenotype to gene. The opposite — pick a gene first, knock it out, and see what happens — is **reverse genetics**, which we'll get to later.

In worms, forward genetic screens were so productive that within a few years there were dozens, then hundreds, of named mutants. To keep track of them, the worm community settled on a beautifully systematic naming convention. Every gene gets a three-letter code that describes the phenotype its mutants display, followed by a hyphen and a number. The number is just the order of discovery within that phenotype class. So:

**_unc_ genes** — "uncoordinated." Mutants in _unc_ genes have abnormal movement. They twitch, they coil, they paralyze, they back up when they should go forward. There are hundreds of _unc_ genes: _unc-1_, _unc-2_, _unc-3_, on up. Each one encodes a protein required for some part of the motor or neural machinery — ion channels, neurotransmitter receptors, structural proteins of muscle, components of the synaptic vesicle cycle. The _unc_ class is, in effect, **a genetic dissection of the worm's nervous system and musculature**, named by the visible phenotype the mutants display.

**_lin_ genes** — "abnormal cell lineage." Mutants in _lin_ genes have defects in the stereotyped cell-lineage program. Specific cells fail to divide when they should, or divide when they shouldn't, or adopt the wrong fate. The _lin_ class is the genetic toolkit of **developmental cell-fate decisions**, and includes some of the most famous worm genes — _lin-4_ and _lin-14_, for instance, which were the first microRNA and its target ever discovered, leading to a whole new field of small-RNA biology.

**_mab_ genes** — "male abnormal." Mutants in _mab_ genes have defects specific to males — abnormal tail anatomy, mating defects. Because the worm is hermaphroditic but produces occasional males, you can specifically study male-only structures and male-only behaviors.

**_dpy_ genes** — "dumpy." Mutants are short and fat, instead of long and slender. Often involved in cuticle structure.

**_let_ genes** — "lethal." Mutants die. Often used for essential genes where the only phenotype is death.

**_clk_ genes** — "clock." Mutants have altered developmental timing or lifespan.

**_age_ genes** — affecting lifespan. We'll come back to these in Segment 6.

**_daf_ genes** — "dauer-formation abnormal." Worms can enter a developmentally arrested, stress-resistant state called the dauer larva when conditions are bad. _daf_ mutants either fail to enter dauer or enter dauer when they shouldn't. Some of the _daf_ genes turned out to control aging, and we'll meet them in Segment 6.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy: **the worm gene namespace is a phenotype-first dictionary of an animal's genome.** Every gene name tells you, at a glance, what category of phenotype its mutants display. You read "_unc-13_" and you know immediately: this is a gene whose mutants are uncoordinated, and there are at least twelve other _unc_ genes that came before it in the discovery order. The naming convention is a kind of compressed scientific history. Every name encodes both a phenotype and a place in the chronology.

Compare this with the human gene naming convention, which is a chaotic mess of abbreviations from different historical periods, different naming committees, different fields claiming the same gene under different names. The worm community, because it was small and started fresh, was able to enforce a single convention from the beginning. The result is that a worm geneticist can read a gene list and immediately understand what kind of biology is being studied. Few other organisms have anything like this clarity.

\[Pause.\]

The mechanics of a forward screen in worms look something like this. You take a few thousand hermaphrodite worms, you feed them mutagen for a few hours, you wash them off, and you put them back on plates to let them recover. The mutagen has introduced random point mutations throughout the genome — usually on the order of one mutation per ten million base pairs, so each treated worm carries a handful of new mutations. The treated worms self-fertilize, and their progeny are the **F1 generation** — each F1 carrying a unique set of mutations from one mutagenized parent. The F1 generation looks normal, because most mutations are recessive and the F1 worms are heterozygous for them.

You let the F1 worms self-fertilize. Their progeny — the F2 generation — segregate the mutations. About a quarter of the F2 from each F1 will be homozygous for any given new mutation. So in the F2 generation, recessive phenotypes finally appear. You scan plates of F2 worms looking for anything weird — anything paralyzed, anything misshapen, anything that doesn't behave right. Any animal that catches your eye is a candidate mutant.

You pick the candidate, you let it self-fertilize, and you check whether the phenotype breeds true. If it does, you've isolated a heritable mutation in some gene. Then comes the hard part: mapping it. Where in the genome is the mutation? Which gene is broken? In the 1970s and 80s, this was done by careful genetic crosses against marker strains, narrowing the mutation to a chromosomal region, then to a small interval. By the 1990s, with the worm genome sequenced, you could combine genetic mapping with sequencing to pinpoint the mutation at single-base resolution within a few months. By the 2010s, with high-throughput whole-genome sequencing, you can identify the mutation in a new mutant within a few weeks.

\[Pause.\]

What did all this produce? A few examples will give you the flavor.

**_unc-86_** — a gene whose mutants have defects in specific neuronal lineages. It encodes a POU-homeodomain transcription factor that's a master regulator of neuronal identity. The mammalian homolog, Brn-3, plays the same role in mammalian neuronal specification. The neural fate-determination logic worked out in worm transfers, with modification, to mouse.

**_lin-14_** — a gene whose mutants get developmental timing wrong. It encodes a protein regulated by a tiny RNA, _lin-4_, which doesn't encode a protein at all. _lin-4_ binds to the 3' UTR of _lin-14_ mRNA and represses translation. This was the first microRNA ever discovered. Today there are hundreds of microRNAs known in humans, controlling everything from cancer to development. The whole field started with a forward screen for developmental timing mutants in worms.

**_unc-13_** — mutants are paralyzed because they have defects in neurotransmitter release. The gene encodes a vesicle-priming protein essential for synaptic transmission. The mammalian homolog, Munc13, plays the same role in mammalian synapses. The molecular machinery of synaptic transmission, much of it, was worked out in worm.

The big idea to leave this segment with: **the worm provided the first genome-wide phenotype-to-gene dictionary of an animal.** Every category of biology — movement, development, sex determination, aging, sensory perception, learning — has its own collection of _unc_, _lin_, _mab_, _daf_, _clk_ genes, named for the phenotype they break. That collection has been the substrate for forty years of mechanistic biology, much of which has translated, gene by gene, to vertebrates and humans.

Now let's go to the discovery that probably did more for the field than any other in the last twenty-five years — the discovery of RNA interference, which happened in worm and changed everything.

## SEGMENT 5 — RNAi in worm: Fire and Mello, 1998

In 1998, two scientists named Andrew Fire and Craig Mello published a paper in _Nature_ that, in retrospect, is one of the most important papers in modern biology. The title was unassuming: "Potent and specific genetic interference by double-stranded RNA in _Caenorhabditis elegans_." The result was world-changing. They had discovered a previously unknown mechanism by which animal cells could silence specific genes, on demand, using small pieces of double-stranded RNA. The mechanism is called **RNA interference**, or **RNAi**, and it earned Fire and Mello the Nobel Prize in 2006 — only eight years after the discovery, one of the fastest Nobel timelines in history.

There's a separate book in this series on RNAi and siRNA, and if you want the full mechanistic and therapeutic story, read that lecture. Today, in the model-organism context, I just want to focus on one thing: **the discovery happened in worm, and that's not a coincidence.** Of all the systems where one could have found RNAi, the worm was probably the only one in which the discovery was easy enough to make in a single set of experiments.

Let me explain why.

\[Pause.\]

Going into the 1990s, the worm community had a puzzle. People were trying to do **gene knockdown** by introducing antisense RNA — RNA whose sequence is the complement of a target mRNA — into worms, on the theory that antisense RNA would base-pair to the target mRNA and block its translation. The technique worked, sort of, sometimes. The results were variable. Some labs reported strong knockdown; others reported nothing.

Fire and Mello did the controls. The standard controls for an antisense experiment are: introduce the antisense RNA, introduce the sense RNA (which shouldn't do anything because it has the same sequence as the mRNA, just doubled), and introduce both together (a mixture). When they ran these controls, they noticed something strange. The mixture — sense plus antisense — gave **much stronger silencing** than antisense alone. The sense RNA, which by itself did nothing, somehow potentiated the antisense effect when mixed in.

The explanation, after some more experiments, turned out to be that the active agent wasn't the single-stranded antisense RNA. It was the **double-stranded RNA** formed when sense and antisense annealed together in the prep tube before injection. When they purified pure double-stranded RNA and injected it on its own, the silencing was dramatic. A few molecules of double-stranded RNA, injected into a worm, could silence the corresponding gene throughout the entire animal, for the entire lifetime, and the silencing was inherited by the worm's offspring.

The mechanism, worked out over the following years by many labs, turned out to involve a set of proteins — Dicer, the RISC complex, Argonaute — that recognize double-stranded RNA, chop it into ~22-nucleotide pieces called siRNAs, and use those siRNAs as guides to direct cleavage of any messenger RNA that matches the siRNA sequence. It is, in effect, **a cellular machine that takes a double-stranded RNA input and produces sequence-specific mRNA destruction**. Every animal cell, including human, has this machinery. It evolved to fight viruses, which often produce double-stranded RNA during their replication. Fire and Mello had discovered an entire ancient antiviral defense system that doubled as a programmable gene-silencing tool.

\[Pause.\]

Why did this happen in worm rather than fly or mouse or zebrafish? Three reasons.

**One: dose-response was easy.** You could inject precise amounts of RNA into worm gonads with a fine glass needle and observe the effects in F1 progeny. The transparency made phenotype scoring effortless. In a mouse, an analogous experiment would have required a hundred times more labor and would not have been runnable as a screening exercise.

**Two: worms ate the RNA.** This was the bonus discovery. You could clone the gene of interest into a special _E. coli_ strain engineered to produce double-stranded RNA, you could feed those bacteria to worms, and the worms — eating the bacteria as food — would silence the corresponding gene. **Feeding RNAi.** A trivially simple way to do genome-wide reverse genetics. Within a few years, Julie Ahringer's lab at Cambridge had built a library covering essentially every worm gene with a feeding-RNAi construct, and anybody could do a genome-wide RNAi screen for any phenotype by feeding the right bacteria to the right worms. Forward genetics inverted — you start with the genes, and you find the ones whose silencing produces the phenotype you care about. In any other organism, this would have required years of construction; in worm, it was three years from RNAi discovery to whole-genome library.

**Three: systemic spread.** RNAi in worms is **systemic** — inject it into one tissue, it spreads through the whole animal. Inject it into the parent, it shows up in the offspring. The worm has a set of proteins that physically transport RNAi signals between cells and between generations. That's not true in fly, where RNAi is more cell-autonomous, and it's not true in mammals at all. The worm's exceptional RNAi machinery made the technique vastly more powerful in worm than in any other animal.

\[Pause.\]

The big idea: **RNAi was discoverable in worm because of features specific to worm biology — transparency, ingestible silencing, systemic spread, fast generation time — that made the relevant experiments cheap and the readout clear.** Once discovered, the underlying mechanism turned out to be universal, present in essentially all eukaryotes, including humans. RNA interference is now a major therapeutic modality — drugs like patisiran, approved by the FDA in 2018, use synthetic siRNAs to silence disease-causing genes in human patients. Every RNAi drug in clinical use today traces its lineage back to two scientists studying gene silencing in worms in the mid-1990s.

This is the recurring story of model organisms. A weird quirk of one organism's biology turns out to expose a universal mechanism that no one would have found in a more "normal" animal, because the universal mechanism's effects are too subtle in the more complex organism to detect. The worm was the right place to find RNAi, and once found, it generalized. That's the bet, paying off again.

Now let's see another example of the worm finding a universal mechanism — this time, the genetic control of aging.

## SEGMENT 6 — Worms that live twice as long: daf-2, age-1, and the genetics of aging

For most of the twentieth century, biologists had a tacit assumption about aging. They assumed it was, basically, an accumulation of damage. Random wear and tear. Mutations piling up in your DNA. Oxidized proteins. Misfolded aggregates. Telomere shortening. A general slow decay of cellular function that there wasn't, in any meaningful sense, a _program_ for. You aged because you wore out, the same way a car wears out from being driven.

In the early 1990s, a researcher named Cynthia Kenyon, working at UCSF, did an experiment in worms that shattered that assumption. Her question was simple: could you find single-gene mutations that made worms live longer? The conventional wisdom said no — aging was systemic, distributed, not the kind of thing a single gene could control. But Kenyon ran the screen anyway. She mutagenized worms and looked for ones that died later than normal.

Wild-type worms live about two to three weeks at 20 degrees Celsius. That's their natural lifespan. Kenyon screened for worms still alive at three or four weeks — the kind of age at which essentially all wild-type worms are dead. And she found them. Mutations in a gene called **_daf-2_** — a _daf_ gene we mentioned in the last segment, related to dauer formation — produced worms that lived **twice as long** as wild type. About four to five weeks instead of two to three. The worms looked normal. They moved, they fed, they laid eggs. They were just on a different aging timeline.

\[Pause.\]

This was, when she published it in 1993, an extraordinary result. **A single-gene mutation, doubling lifespan.** It implied that aging was not just damage accumulation. There was a genetic program — or at least, a genetic dial — that controlled the rate of aging. Set the dial one way, the worm lives two weeks. Set it the other way, the worm lives four. And by changing one gene, you could turn the dial.

The next question was: what did _daf-2_ encode? When it was eventually cloned and sequenced, _daf-2_ turned out to encode the worm homolog of the **insulin and insulin-like growth factor receptor**. The worm version of the receptor that, in mammals, binds insulin and IGF-1 and triggers a signaling cascade that controls metabolism, growth, and many other things.

The implication was startling. The same signaling pathway that, in mammals, controls glucose metabolism — the insulin pathway — controls aging in worms. And subsequent work showed that the downstream components of the pathway were also conserved. _age-1_, another gene whose mutants are long-lived, encodes a PI3-kinase that's activated by _daf-2_. Downstream of that sits a transcription factor called _daf-16_, the worm homolog of the mammalian FOXO transcription factors. When insulin signaling is active, _daf-16_ is phosphorylated and held in the cytoplasm. When insulin signaling is reduced (as in _daf-2_ mutants), _daf-16_ moves into the nucleus and turns on a battery of stress-resistance and longevity genes. The worm lives longer.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy: **the insulin pathway is a genetic dial that worms — and possibly we — use to set the pace of aging.** When food is abundant and signaling is high, worms grow fast, reproduce fast, age fast. When food is scarce or signaling is impaired, worms slow down, become stress-resistant, and live long. The dial is evolutionary — it lets worms survive bad conditions by waiting them out. Kenyon's _daf-2_ mutation is, in effect, a stuck dial: the worm thinks conditions are bad, so it goes into long-lived mode, even though food is fine.

The huge question, when this was first published, was: **does the dial exist in mammals?** The answer turned out to be: yes, partially. Mice with reduced insulin/IGF-1 signaling — mutants in the IGF-1 receptor, mutants in growth-hormone signaling — live longer than wild-type mice. The Ames dwarf mouse and the Snell dwarf mouse, both of which have impaired growth-hormone signaling and reduced IGF-1, live about 50% longer than normal mice. The yeast homolog of the same pathway, Sch9, controls aging in yeast. Human centenarians are enriched for genetic variants in FOXO3 and IGF-1R that reduce insulin signaling activity. The pathway is conserved. The dial is real. In every organism we've looked at — worm, fly, mouse, human — turning down insulin signaling extends lifespan.

\[Pause.\]

The mechanistic detail is fascinating and beyond what we can cover here, but the headline is this: **aging has a genetic logic, and that logic is conserved across animals.** Kenyon's worms were the first hint. The follow-up work, in worm, fly, and mouse, has built up a coherent picture: insulin/IGF-1 signaling, TOR signaling, AMPK, sirtuins — a small set of nutrient-sensing pathways that, together, set the pace at which an animal ages. Caloric restriction extends lifespan in every organism tested, from yeast to monkeys, because it dials down these pathways. Drugs like rapamycin and metformin extend lifespan in mice because they inhibit components of these pathways. Senolytics, the new class of drugs that clear senescent cells, are downstream of the same regulatory logic. Whether any of this translates into human longevity therapy is still being worked out — there are ongoing clinical trials of metformin for healthspan, of rapamycin analogs for aging-related decline — but the basic biology was nailed in the worm.

The big idea to leave this segment with: **the worm gave us the first proof that aging is genetically programmable, and the conservation of that program across animals means worm aging research is a serious candidate for translation to human medicine.** Cynthia Kenyon's worms that live twice as long are not just a curiosity. They are the leading edge of a research program that might, eventually, give us drugs that extend human healthy lifespan. Three days per worm generation, two to three weeks per worm lifespan, transparent and cheap — the worm is the platform on which the genetic logic of aging has been worked out. That is what model organisms do.

Now let's switch organisms. We've spent five segments on the worm. The other half of the invertebrate workhorse pair is the fly, and the fly story starts a half-century earlier, with a man named Thomas Hunt Morgan and a fly room at Columbia University.

## SEGMENT 7 — Thomas Hunt Morgan and the chromosomal theory of inheritance

If the worm was the genetic organism of the late twentieth century, the fly — _Drosophila melanogaster_, the common fruit fly — was the genetic organism of the entire twentieth century, and its dominance started in 1908 in a small, cluttered, banana-smelling room at Columbia University in New York, where a zoologist named Thomas Hunt Morgan and his students began the experiments that would establish, for the first time, the physical basis of heredity.

Let me set the scene. In 1900, Gregor Mendel's laws of inheritance had just been rediscovered. Three independent investigators had stumbled across Mendel's 1866 paper on pea genetics and recognized that he had worked out the basic mathematical rules of how traits are inherited. So by 1908, geneticists knew that there were discrete units of heredity — what would be called genes — that segregated cleanly in crosses and recombined in predictable ratios. But nobody knew **where in the cell** these units lived. There were theories — some people thought they were in the cytoplasm, some in some general kind of "hereditary substance" — but no proof. The physical basis of heredity was an open question.

Morgan set out to attack the problem with flies. Why flies? For the same reasons Brenner picked worms half a century later, scaled to the technology of 1908.

\[Pause.\]

**One: flies breed fast.** A fly goes from egg to adult in about ten days, and a single female lays hundreds of eggs in her lifetime. Compared to mice, rabbits, or guinea pigs — the other "small lab animals" of the era — flies provided dozens of generations per year and thousands of offspring per cross.

**Two: flies are cheap.** They live in milk bottles with a thin layer of food at the bottom — yeast paste, banana mash, eventually a defined cornmeal-molasses medium. You can keep a thousand flies in a bottle on a shelf. The Morgan lab famously stank of overripe bananas, and that smell was the smell of a serious genetics enterprise running on essentially no budget.

**Three: flies have clear morphology.** Eye color, wing shape, body color, bristle pattern — flies have many easily visible traits that can serve as genetic markers. You can sort them under a low-power microscope. You can score a thousand flies in an afternoon.

**Four: flies have a manageable chromosome number.** Four pairs. That's it. Compared to the dozens of chromosomes in many other animals, four pairs is tractable. You can keep track of them.

Morgan started by looking for visible variants — mutant flies with abnormal traits. For the first two years, he found very little. Then, in 1910, one of his students spotted a male fly with **white eyes**, in a population where every other fly had the normal red eye color. That single white-eyed fly turned out to be one of the most important specimens in the history of genetics.

\[Pause.\]

Here's what Morgan did with it. He bred the white-eyed male to a red-eyed female. All the F1 offspring had red eyes — so red was dominant. When he bred those F1 flies to each other, the F2 generation showed a 3:1 ratio of red to white — standard Mendelian segregation for a recessive trait. So white-eye was acting like a normal recessive mutation. So far, so Mendelian.

But here's the twist that broke the case open. Of the white-eyed flies in the F2, **all of them were male**. None were female. White-eye was inherited in a way that linked it tightly to the sex of the fly. This was something Mendel had not seen and could not have predicted.

Morgan and his students worked out the explanation. Flies, like mammals, have sex chromosomes — X and Y. Females are XX, males are XY. And the white-eye gene, Morgan inferred, must live **on the X chromosome**. So females have two copies of it (one on each X), and a single normal copy is enough to give red eyes. Males have only one copy (on their single X), and if that copy is mutant, the male has white eyes — there's no second copy to mask it.

This was the first concrete demonstration that **a specific gene was located on a specific chromosome**. The white-eye gene was on X. Genes were physical things, sitting on physical chromosomes, segregating according to the rules of chromosome segregation in meiosis. The **chromosomal theory of inheritance** — the idea that chromosomes are the physical carriers of genetic information — had been proposed before, but Morgan's white-eye work was the first ironclad experimental evidence.

\[Pause.\]

Over the next two decades, Morgan and his students — Alfred Sturtevant, Calvin Bridges, Hermann Muller, and many others — used the same logic to map dozens, then hundreds of fly genes to specific chromosomes and to specific positions on those chromosomes. Sturtevant, as a Columbia undergraduate in 1913, was the one who realized that the frequency of recombination between two linked genes — how often they get separated during meiosis — could be used as a measure of how far apart they are on the chromosome. Genes close together on the same chromosome get separated rarely; genes far apart get separated often. So you could turn recombination frequency into a **genetic map** — a physical map of where genes sit along the chromosome — using nothing but crosses and counting offspring.

The Morgan group's fly room produced, by 1925, the first detailed genetic map of any animal. It showed dozens of fly genes laid out along the four chromosomes, at measured distances from each other, in defined orders. It was the beginning of genetic mapping as a science. Every subsequent map of every genome — including the human genome — uses the recombination-frequency logic that Sturtevant worked out at age 19 in a fly room in 1913.

The analogy: **the fly room was the chromosome school of the early twentieth century — Thomas Hunt Morgan's flies established that genes sit on chromosomes; this is where the textbook genetics came from.** The whole structure of how we think about inheritance — linkage, recombination, sex linkage, genetic maps — was worked out in flies between 1908 and 1925. Morgan won the Nobel Prize in 1933 for it. It is the foundational period of classical genetics, and almost every concept you learned in introductory biology about how genes behave traces back to those banana-smelling milk bottles at Columbia.

The big idea to leave this segment with: **before molecular biology, the fly was the organism in which genetics became a quantitative science.** The chromosomal theory of inheritance, the genetic map, the concept of linkage, the rules of sex linkage — all of it came from Drosophila. The fly was, in 1925, what the worm became in 1985: the small animal in which the experimental apparatus of genetics was forged.

Now let's look more closely at the actual mutants Morgan and his students were collecting, because the stories of those mutants tell you how fly genetics actually got done.

## SEGMENT 8 — The eye-color revolution: white, vermilion, rosy, and recombination mapping

You will, I hope, indulge me spending a whole segment on fly eye color. It sounds like an absurdly narrow topic, but it's actually a window into how the entire apparatus of classical genetics was built. The fly eye-color genes — and there are dozens of them — became the workhorse markers of Drosophila genetics for the first fifty years of the field, and the techniques developed using them became the techniques that mapped every other gene.

Here's why eye color was such a useful trait to work with. A fly's eyes are about the most conspicuous thing about the animal. They're large, bright red, easily visible under low magnification, and they sit right on the front of the head where you can't miss them. Any mutation that changes eye color is, in effect, **a giant flashing sign on the fly's face** that says "I'm different." Score eye color and you can sort hundreds of flies in minutes. Eye-color mutants are the perfect phenotypic markers for crosses.

And there are a lot of them. Eye-color mutations have been discovered in dozens of different genes, producing a rainbow of phenotypes: white, ivory, ochre, apricot, eosin, cherry, coral, blood, garnet, raspberry, ruby, brown, scarlet, vermilion, cinnabar, sepia, cardinal, rosy, claret, purple, prune. Every word for a shade of red, yellow, or brown, somewhere in the fly literature, names a gene. The naming convention here is the opposite of the worm's: instead of phenotype categories with numbered alleles, fly genes are named by **the specific phenotype of the mutant**, often using poetic or whimsical descriptors. (We'll see this naming style continue in later segments — the famous gap genes _Krüppel_ and _hunchback_ are named for the shapes of the mutant embryos.)

\[Pause.\]

The biology underneath these eye-color genes is, in retrospect, beautiful. Fly eyes are red because they contain two pigments: a red pigment called drosopterin and a brown pigment called ommochrome. The two pigments mix to produce the wild-type "red" eye color. Mutations in genes that affect either pigment shift the eye color visibly. _white_ mutants make no pigment at all — both pathways are broken at the level of pigment transport. _vermilion_ mutants block the brown pigment pathway, leaving only the red pigment, so the eye looks orange-red instead of dark red. _rosy_ mutants do something similar in a different step. _brown_ mutants block the red pigment pathway, leaving only the brown, so the eye looks brown.

So the eye-color genes are, in molecular terms, **genes encoding enzymes and transporters of a small-molecule biosynthesis pathway**. That's a perfectly respectable kind of biology, and the fly eye-color pathway turned out to be a model system for understanding how cells make and traffic small molecules of all kinds. _white_, for instance, encodes an ABC transporter — a protein that pumps molecules across membranes. ABC transporters in humans include the cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator (CFTR, mutated in cystic fibrosis) and the multidrug resistance pump (mutated in some cancers). The molecular family was first characterized in flies. Again, that recurring story: fly biology, generalized to mammalian biology.

\[Pause.\]

Now, the reason eye-color was so important for **mapping** — for figuring out which gene sits where on which chromosome — was that the visible phenotypes let you do crosses on a massive scale. Sturtevant's recombination-mapping logic, the one I mentioned in Segment 7, works like this. You take two recessive eye-color mutants on the same chromosome — say, _white_ and _miniature_, both on the X chromosome. You cross them to make double-heterozygous females. Those females have one X carrying _white_ and another X carrying _miniature_. During meiosis, the two X chromosomes can recombine — they can swap segments. If they swap a segment between the _white_ and _miniature_ loci, you get chromosomes carrying both mutants or neither. If they don't swap, you get chromosomes carrying just _white_ or just _miniature_.

So when you cross those double-heterozygous females to wild-type males and look at the male offspring (which only have one X chromosome, so they show whichever mutations are on the X they inherited), you can count how often you see double mutants and neither-mutants (the recombinants) versus single mutants of each kind (the parentals). The ratio of recombinants to parentals is the **map distance** — and Sturtevant's insight was that map distance is proportional to physical distance along the chromosome. Genes near each other recombine rarely. Genes far apart recombine often. By counting thousands of fly offspring, you can measure these distances with reasonable precision.

In the fly room, by the early 1920s, this technique had been used to map dozens of genes along all four fly chromosomes. The map was crude — distances given in "centimorgans," where one centimorgan is roughly 1% recombination frequency — but it was real. Every fly gene that had a visible phenotype could be placed somewhere on the map. The fly chromosome started to look less like an abstract concept and more like a physical object with addresses on it.

\[Pause.\]

Then in the 1930s came an extraordinary additional tool: **polytene chromosomes**. Fly larvae have salivary glands whose cells contain unusually large chromosomes — the chromosomes have replicated themselves a thousandfold without separating, producing thick banded structures that are visible under a regular light microscope. Under proper staining, polytene chromosomes show characteristic patterns of dark and light bands — thousands of bands per chromosome, each band corresponding to a specific region of the genome. So now, when you mapped a gene by recombination to a particular centimorgan position, you could also look at the corresponding band on the polytene chromosome and physically see where it lived. The genetic map and the cytological map of the chromosome merged.

This was, in 1930s terms, **a complete physical and genetic map of a complete animal genome.** Nothing else in biology came close. The fly had become the test-tube animal — a system in which you could move from a phenotype to a gene to a chromosomal position to a physical band on the chromosome, all using techniques available to a graduate student with a microscope and a few thousand flies.

\[Pause.\]

The big idea to leave this segment with: **the fly eye-color genes were not just biology — they were the experimental scaffolding that let classical genetics become a quantitative, physically grounded discipline.** Every concept of linkage, recombination, map distance, and chromosomal location was worked out using flies whose primary visible trait was the color of their eyes. The work generalized: those mapping concepts apply to humans, mice, plants, fungi — every organism with chromosomes. Modern human genome-wide association studies use exactly the same recombination logic, scaled to millions of markers and billions of base pairs, that Sturtevant worked out with eye-color crosses in 1913.

Now let's fast-forward to the molecular era and the techniques that turned the fly from a classical-genetics organism into a fully modern molecular-biology platform.

## SEGMENT 9 — Drosophila in the molecular era: homeobox, P-elements, and UAS-Gal4

For a long time — from Morgan in 1908 through the 1970s — fly genetics was almost entirely a phenotype-and-mapping discipline. You could find mutants, map them, draw inferences about what genes did. But you couldn't really get inside the gene at the molecular level. You couldn't clone a fly gene, sequence it, or move it around. The transition from classical fly genetics to molecular fly genetics happened in the late 1970s and early 1980s, and it involved a series of techniques that, together, turned the fly into one of the most powerful experimental platforms in molecular biology.

I want to highlight three of these techniques, because they remain central to fly research today.

\[Pause.\]

**Technique one: the discovery of the homeobox.**

In the 1940s, a series of bizarre mutations had been described in flies — mutations called _Antennapedia_, _Bithorax_, and others — in which body parts grew in the wrong places. _Antennapedia_ flies have legs growing out of their heads, where the antennae should be. _Bithorax_ flies have four wings instead of two — the third thoracic segment, normally bearing balance organs, has been transformed into a copy of the second thoracic segment, which bears the wings. These were called **homeotic mutations** — mutations that transform one body part into another. They suggested, in the genetic terms of the era, that there were master regulators specifying which segment of the fly grew which appendages.

In 1983 and 1984, two groups — Walter Gehring's lab at Basel and Matthew Scott and Amy Weiner at Indiana — cloned the homeotic genes and discovered that they all contained a 180-base-pair sequence in common, encoding a 60-amino-acid protein domain that came to be called **the homeodomain**, encoded by what was named **the homeobox**. The homeodomain turned out to be a DNA-binding domain — these were transcription factors that bound to specific DNA sequences and turned on or off the genes that built each body segment.

Then came the spectacular cross-species result. When the homeobox sequences were used as probes to look for related genes in mice and humans, the searchers found that **mammals have the same genes**. Mammalian homeobox genes are arranged in the genome in the same linear order as the fly homeotic genes, expressed along the body axis in the same order, and doing the same job — specifying which body segment grows which structures. The Hox genes are conserved from fly to human. The molecular logic of the vertebrate body plan was, deep down, the molecular logic of the fly body plan. Walter Gehring's lab even showed, much later, that the master eye-development gene of the fly — _eyeless_ — could induce eye formation in a mouse, and the master eye gene of the mouse — _Pax6_ — could induce eye formation in a fly. The conservation went all the way down.

This was the moment fly genetics convinced everyone, even the skeptics, that **what we learn in flies translates to vertebrates**. The Hox discovery is the single most powerful piece of evidence for the model-organism bet. The genes that pattern your body are the genes that pattern a fly's body. They have the same names, the same chromosomal arrangement, the same job.

\[Pause.\]

**Technique two: P-element transposition.**

In the late 1970s, geneticists noticed that certain fly crosses produced a burst of mutations in the offspring — a phenomenon called **hybrid dysgenesis**. It turned out to be caused by a class of mobile DNA elements called **P elements** — transposons that could jump around the fly genome, inserting themselves at new locations and disrupting whichever gene they landed in.

A natural reaction to discovering a mutation-causing parasite might be: damn, this is annoying, let's get rid of it. Allan Spradling and Gerald Rubin had the opposite reaction. They realized that **a controllable transposon is exactly the tool you want for inserting DNA at random into a genome.** In 1982, they showed that you could engineer P elements — strip them of their natural ability to jump on their own, and put in a separately encoded transposase that you could supply or withhold at will — to create a system where you could put any gene you wanted into a P element, inject the P element into fly embryos along with the transposase enzyme, and have the P element insert your gene into the fly's genome.

This was **the first reliable method for making transgenic flies**. Suddenly you could put any cloned gene into a fly, with regulatory sequences of your choice, and ask how it behaved. You could overexpress genes. You could put in marked versions of genes to track where they were expressed. You could make collections of random insertion lines — "enhancer trap" libraries — to discover new genes by where they landed.

For a decade, P-element transgenesis was the workhorse of molecular fly genetics. It has since been supplemented by more refined methods — site-specific integration using bacteriophage integrases like phiC31, CRISPR-based insertion — but the original P-element system is what put the fly on a level molecular playing field with bacterial and yeast genetics.

\[Pause.\]

**Technique three: the UAS-Gal4 binary system.**

This one is so beautiful that it deserves its own paragraph. In 1993, Andrea Brand and Norbert Perrimon adapted a yeast transcription system for use in flies, and the result is what's now called the **UAS-Gal4 binary system** — the genetic toolkit that defines modern fly research.

Here's the logic. Yeast has a transcription factor called Gal4, and Gal4 binds to a specific DNA sequence called the UAS — Upstream Activating Sequence. When Gal4 is present, it binds UAS and turns on whatever gene is downstream. When Gal4 is absent, nothing happens. The system is exquisitely specific — Gal4 doesn't interact with any normal fly DNA, and UAS sequences don't interact with any normal fly transcription factor.

Brand and Perrimon split the system in two. They built **driver lines**: fly strains that express Gal4 under the control of a tissue-specific or stage-specific promoter. So a "_neuronal Gal4_" driver line expresses Gal4 only in neurons. An "_eye Gal4_" driver line expresses Gal4 only in the developing eye. A "_heart Gal4_" driver line expresses Gal4 only in the heart. There are now thousands of driver lines, covering essentially every cell type and developmental stage in the fly.

Then they built **responder lines**: fly strains that have UAS sites followed by whatever gene you want to express. UAS-_geneX_. The responder line, by itself, doesn't express geneX, because there's no Gal4 around to activate it.

When you cross a driver line to a responder line, the offspring carry both the Gal4 and the UAS-_geneX_. In any tissue where the driver expresses Gal4, the UAS becomes active and geneX gets expressed. In every other tissue, geneX stays off. So you can express any gene in any tissue at any time, **just by picking the right driver and the right responder and crossing them**.

The analogy: **the UAS-Gal4 binary system is the fly genetic toolkit — drive any gene in any tissue at any time using two crosses.** Want to know what happens when you overexpress _tau_ specifically in motor neurons? Cross _motor-neuron-Gal4_ to _UAS-tau_. Want to silence a gene specifically in the eye? Cross _eye-Gal4_ to _UAS-shRNA_-against-that-gene. Want to activate neurons by light? Cross _neuron-Gal4_ to _UAS-channelrhodopsin_. You can mix and match drivers and responders to assemble essentially any genetic experiment in a few weeks.

This kind of cellular-level genetic manipulation is far harder in mouse or fish. In flies it's routine. Every fly geneticist has a list of preferred drivers and a list of responders and combines them like Lego. The system has been extended with temperature-sensitive variants, drug-inducible variants, intersectional logic that requires the simultaneous presence of two drivers — every conceivable refinement. The fly is, today, the easiest animal in which to do precise spatial-temporal genetic experiments. Not because the fly biology is uniquely amenable, but because forty years of accumulated tool-building have made it so.

\[Pause.\]

The big idea to leave this segment with: **the fly went from a classical-genetics organism to a fully modern molecular platform through a series of technical revolutions — homeobox conservation, P-element transposition, UAS-Gal4 binary expression — that together turned every fly experiment into a precise molecular intervention.** A modern fly paper looks nothing like a 1925 Morgan paper. Same animal, completely different toolset. And every piece of that toolset is itself a product of the model-organism community — built by fly biologists, for fly biologists, on a foundation of shared resources and shared techniques.

## SEGMENT 10 — The Heidelberg screen: Nüsslein-Volhard, Wieschaus, and the day systematic genetics was born

Welcome back. We left off with the fly as a fully modern molecular platform. Now I want to take you back to 1979, to a lab in Heidelberg, Germany, where two scientists named Christiane Nüsslein-Volhard and Eric Wieschaus were about to do an experiment that, when it was published in 1980, would rewrite developmental biology.

The question they asked was simple and audacious. **Of all the genes in the fly genome, which ones are required for laying out the embryonic body plan?** Which genes specify the head, the thorax, the abdomen? Which ones determine that each segment looks different from its neighbors? Which ones tell the embryo which end is front and which is back? They didn't want to identify just a few key genes. They wanted to identify **all of them** — the complete genetic toolkit for embryonic patterning. Every gene whose loss disrupted the basic body plan, they wanted to catalogue.

To do this, they ran what is now called **the Heidelberg screen** — and what was, at the time, the most ambitious systematic mutagenesis screen ever attempted in any animal. They mutagenized tens of thousands of male flies with the chemical EMS. They crossed those males through a defined breeding scheme that segregated mutations to homozygosity over three generations. They looked at the resulting embryos under a microscope. **Hundreds of thousands of embryos.** Every embryo, scored for defects in body plan: missing segments, fused segments, mirror-image duplications, polarity reversals, anything weird.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy: **the Heidelberg screen is the day systematic genetics was born. Nüsslein-Volhard and Wieschaus mutagenized hundreds of thousands of flies, scored every embryonic pattern defect, found the maternal-effect and segmentation genes — and in doing so rewrote developmental biology from the bottom up.** It was the first proof that you could **saturate** a genetic system — find essentially every gene required for a particular biological process — by sheer brute-force screening, if you picked the right organism and asked the right question.

The screen identified about 120 genes whose mutants had embryonic patterning defects. The genes fell into beautifully clean categories:

**Maternal-effect genes** — genes whose function in the mother sets up the embryo's basic axes. _bicoid_ specifies the anterior end. _nanos_ specifies the posterior end. Embryos from _bicoid_-mutant mothers have two posterior ends and no head. Embryos from _nanos_-mutant mothers have two anterior ends and no abdomen. These genes encode mRNAs that the mother deposits in specific locations in the egg, where they translate into protein gradients that pattern the body axis. The first complete molecular model of an axis-setting morphogen — Bicoid — came out of this screen.

**Gap genes** — genes whose mutants are missing big chunks of the body. _Krüppel_, _hunchback_, _knirps_, _giant_. These genes are turned on in broad bands across the embryo by the maternal gradients, and they themselves activate the next layer of patterning.

**Pair-rule genes** — genes whose mutants are missing every other segment. _even-skipped_, _fushi tarazu_, _hairy_, _runt_. These are expressed in seven stripes along the body, each stripe corresponding to a pair of future segments.

**Segment-polarity genes** — genes whose mutants have polarity defects within each segment. _engrailed_, _wingless_, _hedgehog_. These define the front-versus-back orientation within each segment.

\[Pause.\]

What's astonishing about this hierarchy is not just that Nüsslein-Volhard and Wieschaus found all these classes — it's that the **molecular biology, worked out over the following two decades, matched the genetic logic exactly**. The maternal gradients turn on the gap genes. The gap genes, in overlapping combinations, turn on the pair-rule genes in their seven-stripe patterns. The pair-rule genes turn on the segment-polarity genes within each segment. The hierarchy you can infer from the mutant phenotypes is the hierarchy of the actual molecular cascade. The genetic screen, run in 1979, predicted the molecular logic that would be discovered piece by piece through the 1980s and 1990s.

And — here's the punchline that should be familiar by now — **almost every one of these genes has a vertebrate homolog doing roughly the same job.** Hedgehog signaling in flies turned out to be Sonic Hedgehog in vertebrates, controlling limb patterning, neural tube specification, and a host of other developmental processes. Wingless in flies is Wnt in vertebrates, one of the major signaling pathways in development and cancer. Engrailed, hunchback, even-skipped — all have vertebrate counterparts. The molecular grammar of embryonic patterning, worked out in fly, generalizes to vertebrates. Mutations in the human homologs of these genes cause major birth defects.

Nüsslein-Volhard and Wieschaus shared the Nobel Prize in 1995 with Edward Lewis, who had done parallel pioneering work on homeotic genes. The Heidelberg screen is the textbook example of a **saturation screen**: a screen ambitious enough that it identifies essentially every gene required for a given biological process. Almost every subsequent forward genetic screen in any organism — including, as we'll see, the Boston-Tübingen zebrafish screen in 1996 — was modeled on the Heidelberg approach.

The big idea: **systematic mutagenesis, on a sufficiently large scale, can identify the complete genetic toolkit for a biological process.** The fly was the first animal in which this was demonstrated. The screen logic generalizes; the patterning genes generalize; the strategy is now standard. Heidelberg was the proof of concept.

## SEGMENT 11 — Drosophila for behavior and neuroscience: circadian rhythms and the 2017 Nobel

If the fly showed us the genetics of body plan, it also showed us, over the following decades, the genetics of **behavior** — the most surprising biology to come out of an animal that, intuitively, you wouldn't expect to do anything interesting. Flies don't have brains, you might think. Flies are barely conscious. What could a fly possibly teach us about behavior, learning, and memory?

The answer, as it turned out, is: a lot. And the cleanest example, and the one that earned a Nobel Prize in 2017, is the genetics of **circadian rhythm**.

Every animal on Earth has a roughly 24-hour internal clock. You feel sleepy at night and awake during the day, even in the absence of external cues, because your body has an internal oscillator that runs on a 24-hour period. The question that occupied a small group of scientists starting in the 1970s was: **what genes encode this clock?** Where does the 24-hour period actually come from, at the molecular level?

In the early 1970s, Ronald Konopka and Seymour Benzer ran a screen in flies. They mutagenized flies, they raised the offspring in constant darkness, and they looked for flies whose **eclosion** — the time at which adult flies emerge from their pupal cases — was off-schedule. Wild-type flies eclose preferentially in the early morning. Konopka and Benzer found mutants that eclosed at random times, mutants that eclosed too fast, mutants that eclosed too slowly. They mapped the mutations and discovered that they were all in one gene, which they named **_period_**, or **_per_**.

\[Pause.\]

For the next twenty years, _period_ was the leading edge of circadian biology. In the 1980s, Jeffrey Hall and Michael Rosbash at Brandeis cloned the _period_ gene and showed that the _period_ protein levels in fly brains oscillated with a 24-hour rhythm. Make protein, levels go up, somehow this represses _period_ transcription, transcript goes down, protein goes down, repression released, transcript goes up — a self-sustaining negative feedback loop with a 24-hour period.

Then came more clock genes from more screens. **_timeless_** — Michael Young's lab — encoded a partner of _period_. **_doubletime_** — also Young — encoded a kinase that phosphorylated _period_, regulating its turnover rate. **_clock_** and **_cycle_** — discovered later — encoded the transcription factors that drove _period_ and _timeless_ expression. Together, these genes formed a coherent molecular oscillator: a transcription-translation negative feedback loop in which CLOCK and CYCLE turn on _period_ and _timeless_, the resulting proteins accumulate, dimerize, and repress their own transcription, then get degraded by DOUBLETIME, and the cycle restarts.

The 24-hour period emerges from the kinetics of this loop — how fast the proteins accumulate, how fast they get phosphorylated, how fast they get degraded. It's a molecular oscillator, encoded by half a dozen genes, that runs continuously in every cell of the fly.

\[Pause.\]

And — you can probably guess where this is going — **the same loop runs in your cells right now**. Mammals have homologs of _period_, _timeless_, _clock_, and _cycle_, doing essentially the same job, with essentially the same architecture. Your suprachiasmatic nucleus — the small region of your hypothalamus that orchestrates daily rhythms — runs on Per/Cry/Clock/Bmal1, the mammalian versions of the fly clock genes. Jet lag, shift work, seasonal affective disorder, and the metabolic consequences of disrupted sleep are all downstream of this molecular oscillator.

The analogy: **fly chronobiology is the place where the clock was opened. _period_, _timeless_, _doubletime_ — the clock genes were all found in flies first, then conserved across mammals.** Hall, Rosbash, and Young shared the Nobel Prize in 2017 for this work. The fly was, again, the organism in which a universal mechanism was first dissected at the molecular level. Once dissected, it generalized.

Beyond circadian rhythm, flies have been used to dissect learning and memory (the gene _dunce_, a cAMP phosphodiesterase, was the first learning gene found in any animal — by Seymour Benzer's lab in the 1970s), courtship behavior (the gene _fruitless_ specifies male-typical courtship), aggression, sleep, addiction. Every category of complex behavior that we used to think required a vertebrate brain turns out to have a fly version that can be genetically dissected.

The big idea: **fly genetics turned behavior from a black-box phenomenon into a molecular biology question.** The clock genes are the cleanest example, but the same logic runs through learning, sleep, and many other behaviors. The fly proved that even complex behavior has molecular handles you can grab.

Now let's leave invertebrates entirely and meet the vertebrate that became the workhorse of developmental biology — the zebrafish.

## SEGMENT 12 — George Streisinger and the transparent vertebrate

In 1981, a molecular biologist named George Streisinger at the University of Oregon published a short paper that, in retrospect, marks the founding of zebrafish biology. The paper was titled "Production of clones of homozygous diploid zebra fish." It was technically modest. But it represented Streisinger's bet — a deliberate decision, in the spirit of Brenner's worm bet sixteen years earlier — that **the zebrafish was the right vertebrate for genetics**.

Streisinger had spent his career in phage genetics — bacteriophages, the viruses that infect bacteria — and had been part of the heroic generation of molecular biologists who established the basic facts of gene structure and function. But by the late 1970s, he wanted to do something harder. He wanted to do **vertebrate genetics**. Not bacterial, not yeast, not invertebrate — vertebrate, with the same body plan as humans. And he wanted to do it at the scale and speed that the molecular geneticists were used to.

The challenge was: vertebrates are not, in general, friendly to genetics. Mice take ten weeks per generation, produce a handful of pups per litter, and are expensive. Frogs are big and slow. Chicks are easy to embryo-manipulate but hard to breed for genetics. What Streisinger wanted was a vertebrate that bred like a fly — fast, in huge numbers, in tanks you could keep on a shelf.

\[Pause.\]

He picked the zebrafish, _Danio rerio_, a small striped tropical freshwater fish native to South Asia. Aquarium hobbyists had been keeping them for decades. Streisinger's lab adapted them for genetics, and the list of features he chose them for is striking.

**One: they breed fast and abundantly.** A breeding pair lays about 200 eggs in a morning, every week. A small colony of a few hundred fish can produce tens of thousands of embryos per week.

**Two: they're cheap.** Tanks of water, dry food, a temperature-controlled room. Compared to mice, zebrafish facilities cost a tenth as much per animal.

**Three: they generate fast.** Generation time is about three months — slower than fly or worm, but fast for a vertebrate, far faster than mouse.

**Four — and this is the killer feature — the embryos are transparent.** Zebrafish embryos are essentially clear blobs of cells, in a thin clear chorion, sitting in a dish of water. You can put one under a microscope and watch every cell divide, migrate, and differentiate, in real time, without dissecting anything. The vertebrate equivalent of the worm's transparency — and almost as informative.

**Five: external fertilization.** Female fish lay eggs into water; males fertilize them externally. You can collect the fertilized eggs immediately, manipulate them, inject them with DNA or RNA, and watch development proceed. No surgery, no implantation, no womb. The embryo is on a microscope slide.

**Six: vertebrate body plan.** Notochord, neural tube, somites, fins where our limbs would be, two-chambered heart that pumps blood through a circulatory system — all the standard vertebrate features. Most of what zebrafish do at the molecular level, mammals also do.

The analogy: **the zebrafish is the transparent vertebrate — Streisinger's idea: you want a vertebrate, you want lots of embryos, you want to SEE them develop; zebrafish gives you all three.** The combination of fast breeding, external fertilization, and transparent embryos is unique. No other vertebrate has all three. And the body plan is conserved enough with humans that what you learn in fish often translates to clinically relevant biology.

Streisinger died in 1984, only three years after his founding paper, but his lab and his students carried the project forward. By the mid-1990s, zebrafish was an established model organism, with stock centers, standardized protocols, and a growing community.

The big idea: **Streisinger's choice of zebrafish was the second great organism choice of late-twentieth-century biology, after Brenner's choice of the worm.** Brenner gave us invertebrate genetics at single-cell resolution. Streisinger gave us vertebrate genetics at embryo-watching scale. Different niches, different organisms, both deliberately chosen.

## SEGMENT 13 — Why fish: vertebrate development you can watch

Let me spend a segment on the specific biological advantages of zebrafish for developmental biology, because they're worth dwelling on. Once you see what you can do with a fish embryo, the appeal becomes obvious.

A zebrafish embryo, fertilized at time zero, looks like this. Hour 1: a single cell sitting on top of a yolk. Hour 4: a small ball of about a thousand cells. Hour 6: a flat disk of cells starting to spread over the yolk. Hour 10: gastrulation, cells moving inward and outward to form germ layers. Hour 14: the body axis has formed, the head and tail are visible. Hour 24: a recognizable little fish, with a beating heart, twitching muscles, and visible eyes. Hour 72: the fish hatches out of the chorion and starts swimming.

At every one of these stages, **you can see what's happening, in real time, in a live embryo, under a microscope, without dissection.** That is a vertebrate biologist's dream. Mouse embryos at the equivalent stages are buried inside the mother's uterus, accessible only by dissection that kills the embryo. Frog embryos are larger and easier to access but slower-developing. Chicken embryos are accessible through an egg window but you can only do this once per embryo. Zebrafish, by contrast, give you continuous, real-time, non-destructive imaging of every stage of vertebrate development. You can take a movie of a beating heart at hour 30. You can track individual neurons as they migrate through the developing brain. You can watch blood cells emerge from the dorsal aorta at the moment of definitive hematopoiesis.

\[Pause.\]

A few specific advantages worth naming:

**Live imaging of cardiac development.** Zebrafish embryos can survive for the first few days of development without a functional circulation, because they're small enough that oxygen reaches their tissues by diffusion. So mutants with major cardiac defects, which would be embryonic-lethal in mice at very early stages, survive long enough in zebrafish for you to study the underlying gene functions. The molecular genetics of heart development — _gata4_, _nkx2.5_, the heart-tube formation machinery — has been substantially worked out in zebrafish for exactly this reason.

**Live imaging of neuronal migration.** You can put a fluorescent protein under the control of a neuron-specific promoter and watch individual neurons crawl through the developing brain over hours of microscopy. The molecular logic of axon guidance, dendrite branching, and circuit formation has been imaged in zebrafish in ways simply not possible in mouse.

**Hematopoiesis.** The developmental origins of blood cells, including the emergence of hematopoietic stem cells from the dorsal aorta, were imaged for the first time in zebrafish. The molecular pathways involved turned out to be conserved with mammalian hematopoiesis, and zebrafish are now a major model for blood disorders.

**Regeneration.** Adult zebrafish can regenerate fins, heart, retina, and parts of the spinal cord after injury. Mammals can't. Why? Working that out in fish — and figuring out which signaling pathways are activated during fin regeneration but suppressed in mammalian wound healing — is one of the great open questions and a major research frontier.

\[Pause.\]

The technical infrastructure for fish genetics has grown over the past four decades. Methods for **microinjection** — injecting DNA, RNA, or other molecules into single-cell embryos — became routine in the 1990s. Transgenic fish, with fluorescent reporters under tissue-specific promoters, are now standard. Imaging facilities use multiphoton and light-sheet microscopy to capture entire embryos in three dimensions over time. The "Zebrafish Information Network," ZFIN, catalogues every published mutant, every reagent, every protocol.

The big idea: **the zebrafish is the only animal in which you can watch a vertebrate develop, cell by cell, from fertilization to hatching, under a microscope, without dissecting anything.** That property — visible development of a conserved vertebrate body plan — is unique. And it's the reason the fish became the developmental biology workhorse for vertebrate-specific questions that worms and flies couldn't address.

## SEGMENT 14 — The Boston-Tübingen big screen: scaling Heidelberg to a vertebrate

In December 1996, a special issue of the journal _Development_ appeared, devoted to the results of the **Boston-Tübingen zebrafish screen**. Two large consortia of fish geneticists — one led by Wolfgang Driever at Boston (then at MGH, later Freiburg), and one led by Christiane Nüsslein-Volhard at Tübingen, Germany — had carried out the largest systematic mutagenesis screen ever attempted in a vertebrate. The plan: mutagenize zebrafish, screen tens of thousands of embryos for any visible developmental defect, and identify the genes required for each step of vertebrate development.

Nüsslein-Volhard, you'll notice, was one of the leaders. The Heidelberg screen in flies had been her work. Now, sixteen years later, she was doing the same thing in fish — taking the saturation-mutagenesis strategy that worked in fly and applying it to the simplest vertebrate she could find. The intellectual lineage is direct: Heidelberg in flies in 1980 became Boston-Tübingen in fish in 1996.

The scale was enormous. The two consortia together processed over a million embryos. They identified about 4,000 mutants with visible developmental defects, falling into hundreds of complementation groups — meaning hundreds of distinct genes required for vertebrate embryonic development. The mutants were organized by phenotype: defects in body axis, defects in somite formation, defects in heart development, defects in brain patterning, defects in eye development, defects in pigmentation, and on and on.

\[Pause.\]

Some highlights. The screen identified mutants in essentially every major signaling pathway: Sonic hedgehog mutants (which gave embryos with cyclopia, a single fused eye); Bmp pathway mutants (with dorsoventral patterning defects); Wnt pathway mutants; FGF pathway mutants. The pathways were the same ones identified in flies, mice, and other systems, but the fish made it possible to identify them all systematically, in a single coordinated screen, over a few years.

The mutants gave names to the field: _one-eyed pinhead_, _cyclops_, _no tail_, _floating head_, _silberblick_, _trilobite_, _knypek_. (Notice the Drosophila-style poetic naming carried over into fish.) Each one is now a well-characterized gene, often with a direct human homolog implicated in a developmental disorder. _one-eyed pinhead_, for instance, encodes a co-receptor for Nodal signaling, and Nodal pathway mutations in humans cause heterotaxy syndromes — defects in the left-right asymmetry of internal organs.

\[Pause.\]

The screen, like Heidelberg before it, demonstrated that **saturation mutagenesis works in vertebrates**. Until 1996, many people thought vertebrate development was too complex, too redundant, too distributed across cell types to be amenable to single-gene forward genetics in the way fly development was. Boston-Tübingen disproved that. Vertebrate development could be dissected gene by gene. The fish was the right vertebrate to do it in.

The Boston-Tübingen screen is one of the great achievements of model-organism biology and a landmark for the strategy of scaling up genetic tools. It established the gene catalogue for vertebrate development that subsequent molecular work has been mapping in detail for two decades.

The big idea: **the Boston-Tübingen screen scaled the Heidelberg strategy to a vertebrate and showed that the systematic genetics that worked in fly also worked in fish.** Plus, by extension, in any vertebrate. The patterning genes of zebrafish embryos are largely the same genes that pattern mouse, human, and chick embryos. The catalogue, once built, generalizes.

## SEGMENT 15 — Fish CRISPR and the fastest vertebrate genetics

Forward genetics gave the field its first generation of fish mutants. Reverse genetics — picking a gene first, knocking it out, and asking what happens — was much slower to arrive, because the homologous-recombination techniques that worked in mouse ES cells didn't work in fish. For about fifteen years, fish reverse genetics relied on awkward workarounds: morpholinos (modified antisense oligos that block mRNA processing or translation, but produce off-target effects and don't make heritable mutants) and TILLING (looking through large mutagenized libraries for random point mutations in your gene of interest, then breeding them out).

Then, around 2013, **CRISPR-Cas9 arrived in fish**, and fish reverse genetics suddenly became, in some ways, the fastest reverse genetics of any vertebrate.

Here's why fish are exceptionally well-suited to CRISPR.

\[Pause.\]

**One: injection is routine.** Fish embryos at the one-cell stage are sitting in a dish of water, externally fertilized, easily accessible to a micropipette. You can inject CRISPR reagents — a Cas9 protein or mRNA plus a guide RNA — into hundreds of embryos in a morning. In mouse, injection requires hormonal superovulation, embryo collection by dissection from sacrificed females, and reimplantation into pseudopregnant foster mothers. In fish, it's just water.

**Two: F0 phenotyping.** Because zebrafish are diploid and a fresh CRISPR injection produces mosaic mutations across the embryo's cells, you can often see a phenotype in the **founder generation itself** — the F0, the directly injected embryos. If Cas9 cuts the gene in enough cells, the resulting tissue is mostly mutant, and any phenotype shows up immediately. You don't have to breed for two generations to see effects, the way you do in mouse.

**Three: speed.** From "I want to knock out gene X" to "I am holding embryos with gene X knocked out" can take a couple of weeks in fish. From "I want to knock out gene X" to "I have homozygous knockout mouse embryos for studying" takes several months even with CRISPR streamlining. The fish system is just faster.

**Four: scale.** You can run a CRISPR-based reverse-genetic screen across hundreds of genes in fish, in a year, in one lab. The equivalent screen in mouse would take decades and cost millions.

\[Pause.\]

The result is that zebrafish, in 2026, is the fastest vertebrate genetics platform available. If you have a candidate gene from a human GWAS — a gene you suspect contributes to a human disease — you can clone the fish ortholog, knock it out with CRISPR, and have phenotype data in a vertebrate within a month. That kind of cycle time is changing how human disease genetics gets validated. Every time a new candidate gene comes out of a clinical sequencing project, somebody asks: what does it do in fish?

The big idea: **CRISPR turned zebrafish from a screen-driven forward-genetic system into a hypothesis-driven reverse-genetic system, while keeping all the fish's advantages.** Same fast generation time, same transparent embryos, same ease of injection — now with the ability to target any gene on demand. Fish reverse genetics is, in some sense, the natural home for rapid functional validation of human disease genes.

## SEGMENT 16 — Fish for cancer: transgenic models of melanoma, leukemia, and retinoblastoma

One application of zebrafish has grown enormously over the past twenty years: cancer modeling. The idea is straightforward — take a human oncogene, put it into a fish under a tissue-specific promoter, and watch the fish develop a cancer of that tissue. Because the fish is transparent at embryonic and larval stages and partially transparent as an adult (especially in pigment-mutant strains like _casper_, which lack body pigment), you can watch tumors form, grow, invade, and metastasize **in a live vertebrate, in real time, under a microscope**.

A few specific examples:

**Melanoma.** Leonard Zon's lab at Harvard made a transgenic zebrafish carrying the human oncogenic BRAF-V600E mutation under control of a melanocyte-specific promoter. The fish develop melanoma. Crossed with a _p53_ mutant background, they develop melanoma rapidly and aggressively. The model has been used to identify modifier genes that accelerate or suppress melanoma — including genes that have led to new therapeutic candidates being tested in human melanoma trials.

**Leukemia.** Transgenic fish expressing the human oncofusion MYC-ER in lymphocyte precursors develop T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia, which can be tracked by GFP-labeling the leukemic cells and watching them spread through the live fish.

**Retinoblastoma and other pediatric tumors.** Fish models have been built for retinoblastoma, neuroblastoma, rhabdomyosarcoma, and several other rare pediatric cancers — diseases for which mouse models exist but are expensive enough that screening large numbers of candidate genes is impractical. The fish enables faster modifier screens.

\[Pause.\]

The advantage of fish cancer models over mouse models is throughput and visibility. You can screen thousands of fish for drug efficacy in a way you can't screen thousands of mice. You can watch a single tumor grow over weeks in a live animal, taking daily fluorescence images. The disadvantages: fish lack adaptive immunity exactly as it works in mammals, fish don't have lungs (a common metastasis site in human cancer), and some fish biology — pigmentation, fin regeneration, scale biology — has no human equivalent. The model is partial. But for the questions it's good at — early tumor initiation, modifier genetics, drug screening — it's increasingly important.

The big idea: **fish are now a serious cancer modeling platform, particularly when speed, visibility, and modifier screening matter more than perfect mammalian fidelity.** The mouse remains the gold standard for translational cancer biology, but the fish covers the questions the mouse can't address at scale.

## SEGMENT 17 — Cross-species translatability: the central bet

We've now toured three model organisms across half a century, and the recurring story has been the same: **a mechanism discovered in worm or fly or fish turns out to be conserved across animals, including humans.** Apoptosis in worms. Hox genes in flies. Circadian clocks in flies. Patterning genes in fish. Insulin signaling and aging. RNAi. The list goes on.

The central bet of model-organism biology is that **what's true in worm is often true in mouse is often true in human; not always, but enough of the time to make the model approach work for fifty years.** Let me make this concrete.

**Cases where the translation worked beautifully.** The Hox gene cluster of vertebrates does the same thing as the Hox cluster of flies — they pattern the anterior-posterior body axis using the same genes in the same chromosomal order. The clock genes of flies (_period_, _timeless_, etc.) have direct mammalian homologs running the same molecular oscillator. The cell-death genes of worms (_ced-3_, _ced-9_, etc.) are the homologs of the human caspases and Bcl-2 proteins. The Wnt, hedgehog, Notch, and BMP signaling pathways were largely worked out in flies and fish, and they are the same pathways operating in human development and disease.

**Cases where the translation is partial.** Drosophila has only a single Hox cluster, while vertebrates have four — duplicated and divergent. The fly has no adaptive immune system, while mammals do. Zebrafish have a partially functional p53 pathway that differs in important ways from mammalian p53. The TGF-beta superfamily has many more members in vertebrates than in flies, and not all of them have direct invertebrate counterparts. Where the translation is partial, you have to be careful — the fly experiment gives you a hypothesis that needs to be validated in mouse before you trust it in human.

**Cases where the translation fails.** Aging in mammals involves stem-cell exhaustion, cellular senescence, and immunoaging — processes that have no direct worm equivalent. Many neurological diseases involve specific neuronal cell types that don't exist in flies. The mammalian gut microbiome plays an enormous role in physiology with no real fly or worm equivalent. When the failure happens, model-organism work can mislead — a mechanism that looked critical in worm or fly turns out to be irrelevant or different in mammals.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy: **cross-species translatability is the central bet of model-organism biology. What's true in worm is often true in mouse is often true in human; not always, but enough of the time to make the model approach work for fifty years.** It's a probabilistic bet, not a guarantee. Each individual finding in a model organism is a hypothesis about human biology, not a fact. The hypothesis has to be tested in increasingly complex systems — first mouse, then human cells, eventually clinical trials — before it becomes therapeutic.

The strategic value is that **model organisms generate the hypotheses cheaply and quickly**. The fly or worm or fish points you at a gene, a pathway, a mechanism. Then you go to mouse to validate. Then to human cells. Then to patients. Without the model organism work upstream, you wouldn't know which of the twenty thousand human genes to investigate. The model organisms are the search engine, the prioritization layer, the funnel.

The big idea: **the model-organism enterprise is a probability machine. It feeds the rest of biomedicine a stream of high-probability candidate mechanisms drawn from cheap, fast, tractable animals.** Some of the candidates fail when tested in mammals. Most succeed at least partially. The success rate is high enough that fifty years of work has built much of modern biomedicine on this foundation. It is, on net, the best bet any field has ever made.

## SEGMENT 18 — Frontier: comparative genomics, behavior at scale, single-cell vertebrates, and AI phenotyping

Let me close with a look at where the model-organism field is heading, because the frontier is interesting and unsettled.

**Comparative genomics in the _Caenorhabditis_ family.** _C. elegans_ has many close relatives — _Caenorhabditis briggsae_, _Caenorhabditis remanei_, _Caenorhabditis japonica_, and others. By sequencing and analyzing these closely related species, we can ask: which features of worm biology are general nematode features, and which are quirks of _elegans_ specifically? Comparative genomics adds a new dimension to model-organism work — instead of one worm, you have a phylogeny of worms, each one a slightly different natural experiment.

**Drosophila eclosion mapping and high-resolution behavior.** Modern fly labs are using high-throughput video recording to track every behavior of every fly in a population for days at a time, generating terabytes of behavior data that machine learning algorithms can mine for patterns. The granularity of behavioral phenotyping has gone from "this mutant doesn't move right" to "this mutant has a 3.7% reduction in grooming bout frequency between 14:00 and 16:00." Behavior is becoming a quantitative phenotype on the same scale as gene expression.

**Zebrafish single-cell transcriptomics.** The combination of fish embryos (small, transparent, all stages accessible) with single-cell RNA sequencing is producing complete cell-by-cell atlases of vertebrate development. Every cell of a 24-hour fish embryo, transcriptionally profiled. The ambition is the Sulston lineage map, scaled to a vertebrate — every cell, every gene, every developmental moment. We're not there yet, but the fish is the closest we've come to a complete vertebrate developmental atlas.

**AI-driven phenotyping.** Across all three organisms, machine learning systems are being trained to recognize subtle phenotypes that humans miss. A neural network looking at zebrafish embryos can pick up developmental defects that a human screener would call normal. AI-driven phenotyping is, in effect, a new screening modality — one that can scan millions of animals and pick out the interesting ones automatically. It's going to make screens of unprecedented scale possible over the next decade.

\[Pause.\]

The big picture: **the model-organism enterprise is alive, evolving, and integrating with the broader genomics revolution.** Worms, flies, and fish are not historical artifacts. They are the active substrate on which we test the functional consequences of every interesting human genetic variant that comes out of the clinic. As human genetics generates more candidate disease genes, the model organisms become more important, not less — they're the platforms on which those candidates get rapid functional testing.

Brenner, Morgan, Streisinger, Nüsslein-Volhard, Sulston, Kenyon, Fire, Mello — these are the names. The animals — worm, fly, fish — are the platforms. The bet they made, fifty to a hundred years ago, that you could understand biology by picking the right simple animal and studying it relentlessly — that bet has paid off beyond what anyone could have imagined. And the bet is still paying off, every day, in labs around the world. If you walked into a modern biomedical research building right now, somewhere in the basement there are tanks of zebrafish, somewhere there are bottles of flies, and somewhere there are petri dishes of worms. The Olympic team is still competing. Still winning. Still answering questions nothing else can answer.

That's the model organism story. Worm, fly, fish. Three animals. A century of genetics. Most of what we know about how life works at the molecular level. Thanks for staying with me. Go read some primary literature.
