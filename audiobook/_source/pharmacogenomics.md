## SEGMENT 1 — Why the same drug does different things to different people

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. For the next two hours we're going to talk about one of the most clinically immediate fields in all of modern genetics — pharmacogenomics, the science of how your DNA shapes your response to drugs. And before I tell you a single mechanism, I want to plant a scene in your head that I want you to carry through the whole lecture.

\[Pause.\]

Imagine three patients. They're all the same age. They're all roughly the same weight. They all have the same diagnosis, let's say a blood clot in the leg, and their doctor prescribes them all the same dose of the same drug — warfarin, a blood thinner that's been on the market since the 1950s and is still one of the most prescribed anticoagulants in the world. Same drug. Same dose. Same indication. Three patients.

Patient one takes their pill every day for a month and shows up to the clinic with their blood beautifully thinned, exactly in the therapeutic range, clot dissolving, doctor happy.

Patient two takes the exact same pill, every day for a month, and shows up to the clinic with blood that's barely thinned at all. The clot hasn't moved. The drug is, in their body, essentially doing nothing. Their doctor doubles the dose. Now they're in range.

Patient three takes the exact same pill, and after a week they show up in the emergency room bleeding from their gums, bruising spontaneously, with blood that's so thin it won't clot if they nick themselves shaving. Their dose, for them, was a near-fatal overdose. If they hadn't gotten lucky and noticed, they could have bled into their brain and died.

Same drug. Same dose. Same patient profile on paper. Three radically different outcomes.

\[Pause.\]

This isn't a hypothetical. This is what warfarin clinics looked like for sixty years, and to a significant extent still do. The drug has a fivefold range of doses across the population, and figuring out which patient needs which dose has historically been done by trial and error, with bleeding events and recurrent clots as the way you learn.

Now here's the question that pharmacogenomics asks. Why? Why do three people, given the same chemical, experience such different outcomes? What is different about them, biologically, that produces these different responses?

There are several answers, and we'll go through them. Some of the variation is environmental — what you eat, what other drugs you're on, how much you drink. Some of it is physiological — your age, your weight, your kidney function, your liver function. Some of it is disease-related — a patient with congestive heart failure metabolizes drugs differently than a patient without. But a very large, often dominant, chunk of the variation has nothing to do with any of that. It has to do with the DNA you inherited from your parents. Specifically, it has to do with single-letter differences in a handful of genes — genes that code for the enzymes that activate, deactivate, and clear drugs from your body. Change one letter in one of those genes, and warfarin can be life-saving at one milligram per day or a near-fatal overdose at the same dose.

That insight — that drug response is, in large part, a genetic trait — is the founding insight of pharmacogenomics. And once you accept it, the clinical implication is enormous. If we could read those genes _before_ we ever prescribed the drug, we could match the dose, or even the choice of drug, to the patient's actual metabolic biology. We could replace trial and error with a lookup table. We could stop killing people with morphine toxicity, stop letting clots throw to people's brains because their drug isn't working, stop giving children with leukemia bone-marrow-killing doses of a perfectly normal medicine.

That's the promise. And for about thirty drug-gene pairs, we now actually deliver on it routinely.

\[Pause.\]

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, explain to a friend without jargon why the same drug can have wildly different effects on different people, and roughly which enzymes are doing the varying; two, name a few of the canonical drug-gene pairs and what genotype predicts what outcome; three, look at a news headline about a "personalized medicine breakthrough" and understand whether what's being described is real, useful, and implementable, or whether it's somebody selling vapor — then we've succeeded. Everything else is detail hung onto that scaffold.

The single-sentence definition I want you to carry through the whole lecture is this. **Pharmacogenomics is the genetics of drug response — same dose, different person, different outcome, often because of a single variant in a single enzyme.**

Let's build the scaffold.

## SEGMENT 2 — The many reasons people respond to drugs differently

Before we go deep into the DNA, I want to spend a few minutes giving you the full menu of reasons people respond differently to the same medicine. Because if you walk away thinking it's all genetics, you'll be wrong, and you'll miss things in the clinic that matter. Genetics is the biggest single explanatory variable for a lot of drugs, but it lives in a noisy environment of other variables, and good prescribing requires considering all of them.

So here's the list, roughly in order of how variable they are across patients.

**Genetics.** The big one for this lecture, and we'll spend the rest of the time on it. Inherited differences in the genes coding for drug-metabolizing enzymes, drug transporters, and drug targets. Stable across your entire life. Easy to measure once. Predictive for many drugs.

**Age.** Newborns and very young children have immature liver enzymes. Their CYP450 system — the metabolic kitchen we'll spend the next several segments inside — doesn't reach adult activity levels until somewhere between age two and adolescence, depending on the specific enzyme. Elderly patients, on the other end, have declining liver mass, declining kidney function, declining metabolic capacity. Both extremes are vulnerable to drug accumulation. A standard adult dose of morphine in a one-year-old can be lethal. A standard adult dose of a benzodiazepine in a ninety-year-old can put them in a confused, fall-prone state for days.

**Sex.** Men and women metabolize many drugs differently, partly because of body composition — women on average have a higher fraction of body fat, which changes the volume of distribution for lipophilic drugs — and partly because of hormonal modulation of liver enzymes. Estrogen, in particular, modulates several CYP enzymes. This is why women have, statistically, more adverse drug reactions than men for many drug classes. It's also one of the reasons drug doses, when first approved, are often calibrated to men and then quietly adjusted later for women.

**Body weight and body composition.** Important, but cruder than people often think. A 120-kilogram patient and a 50-kilogram patient given the same dose will have quite different blood concentrations of a water-soluble drug, but the relationship is not always linear, because the drug doesn't necessarily distribute into fat tissue.

**Kidney function.** Many drugs are cleared by the kidneys. If your kidneys aren't working well — chronic kidney disease, acute injury, age-related decline — those drugs build up. Renal dosing tables exist for a reason. Get the dosing wrong on something like vancomycin or digoxin in a patient with bad kidneys, and you have a serious problem on your hands within a day or two.

**Liver function.** Most drugs are metabolized by the liver. Cirrhosis, hepatitis, fatty liver, all reduce metabolic capacity. The same dose of the same drug in a healthy liver and a cirrhotic liver are not the same exposure to the body. The cirrhotic patient gets, effectively, more drug per dose.

**Disease state.** Inflammation itself, surprisingly, alters drug metabolism. When you're sick — really sick, with significant systemic inflammation — your liver downregulates many of its metabolic enzymes. Patients in the ICU with sepsis often metabolize their drugs much more slowly than they would if they were healthy, which is part of why ICU pharmacology is its own demanding subspecialty.

**Other drugs.** Drug-drug interactions are an enormous source of variability. Some drugs induce the metabolic enzymes — they turn the engine up — so they make other drugs clear faster. Some drugs inhibit the metabolic enzymes — they turn the engine down — so they make other drugs hang around longer. Grapefruit juice, famously, contains compounds that inhibit a specific liver enzyme, CYP3A4, and that's why certain medications carry a warning not to take them with grapefruit. A glass of grapefruit juice with the wrong statin can raise the statin's blood concentration five or tenfold, with predictable consequences for your muscles.

**Diet and lifestyle.** Smokers metabolize certain drugs faster because compounds in cigarette smoke induce a specific CYP enzyme. Heavy drinkers chronically induce another. People on extreme high-fat or high-protein diets shift their metabolism. Even when you take a drug — fasting versus with food — matters for absorption.

**Adherence.** Trivial, but in practice the biggest reason a drug "doesn't work." Patients miss doses. Patients take them at the wrong time. Patients stop taking them when they feel better, or when they don't, or when the bottle gets put in the wrong drawer.

\[Pause.\]

Now, all of those matter. But here's the point of this segment. Even after you control for every single one of those variables — same age, same sex, same weight, same kidneys, same liver, same diet, same other drugs, perfect adherence — you still see massive variation in drug response across patients. There is a residual, irreducible variability that those non-genetic factors don't explain. And that residual is, in many cases, the genetics.

For some drugs the genetic contribution is small. For others — warfarin, clopidogrel, codeine, certain chemotherapies — the genetics dominates. That's where pharmacogenomics earns its keep, and that's where pre-prescription genotyping has clinical impact you can measure in lives saved.

So that's the framing. Genetics is one variable among many, but for the drug-gene pairs we care about most, it's the one variable that, if you knew it, would change what you prescribed.

## SEGMENT 3 — The liver's metabolic kitchen: CYP450 enzymes

Now let's go into the engine room. Because if I told you there was one family of enzymes in your body that, more than any other, is responsible for how you handle prescription drugs — one family that determines whether a drug works, whether it builds up to toxic levels, whether it gets activated or destroyed before it can do its job — it would be this family. The cytochrome P450 enzymes. CYP450, often just written CYP. Pronounced "sip" by people in the field, although you'll hear "see-why-pee" too.

Here's the analogy I want you to carry. Think of your liver as a giant metabolic kitchen. Every drug you swallow gets absorbed from your gut, goes into your bloodstream, and the very first stop is the liver — through what's called the hepatic portal vein. Before the drug ever reaches the rest of your body, it has to pass through this kitchen. And in this kitchen are dozens of chefs, each with a slightly different specialty. Some chefs are slicing, some are chopping, some are turning ingredients into something edible, some are turning food scraps into garbage that the kidneys can flush. Each chef is an enzyme. And the CYP450 family is the dominant set of chefs in this kitchen.

Roughly seventy-five percent of all prescription drugs are metabolized, in some way, by CYP450 enzymes. Three-quarters. If you pulled up a random drug from a random pharmacy and asked "which enzyme handles this in the liver?" — three times out of four, the answer is a CYP.

Why is one enzyme family so dominant? Because of what they do mechanistically. CYP450 enzymes use an iron-containing heme group to insert an oxygen atom into a target molecule. It sounds simple, but adding a single oxygen has profound consequences. It usually makes the molecule more water-soluble, which is the first step toward getting it out of your body through the urine. It often inactivates a drug's pharmacological activity. Sometimes — this is critical — it _activates_ a drug, taking a chemically inert "prodrug" and converting it into the form that actually does the therapeutic work. The same oxygen insertion can wear many hats.

There are dozens of CYP enzymes encoded in your genome. About fifty-seven in humans, organized into families. But for drug metabolism, a small handful do most of the work. Let me introduce you to the cast.

**CYP3A4.** The biggest workhorse. CYP3A4 metabolizes somewhere between thirty and fifty percent of all prescription drugs, depending on whose count you trust. It's promiscuous — it'll take on a huge variety of substrates. Statins, many chemotherapies, immunosuppressants like cyclosporine and tacrolimus, many benzodiazepines, certain HIV drugs. CYP3A4 is the chef on the line who can cook anything, and so most of the orders come through him. Genetic variation in CYP3A4 itself is relatively modest in its impact — it's not the gene that explains most of the variability — but the gene is heavily influenced by drug-drug interactions, which is why CYP3A4 inhibitors and inducers are such a big deal in clinical pharmacology.

**CYP2D6.** Probably the most genetically variable CYP, and the one that's been studied most in pharmacogenomics. CYP2D6 metabolizes about twenty-five percent of prescription drugs, including many psychiatric medications — SSRIs like fluoxetine and paroxetine, tricyclic antidepressants, certain antipsychotics — and importantly, the opioid codeine. We'll spend an entire segment on what goes wrong when CYP2D6 doesn't function normally.

**CYP2C9.** Major handler of the blood thinner warfarin, the diabetes drug glipizide, the painkiller ibuprofen, and several others. Variants in CYP2C9 are the main genetic reason warfarin dosing varies so dramatically.

**CYP2C19.** Activates the prodrug clopidogrel — the blockbuster antiplatelet drug used after stents — and metabolizes proton pump inhibitors like omeprazole and several antidepressants. We'll come back to clopidogrel; the CYP2C19 story is one of pharmacogenomics' clearest cases.

**CYP2B6.** Smaller but increasingly important. It metabolizes the HIV drug efavirenz, the smoking-cessation drug bupropion, and certain anesthetics. Variants in CYP2B6 are particularly relevant in HIV pharmacology, especially in African populations where certain variants are common.

That's your starting cast. CYP3A4, CYP2D6, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP2B6. Memorize these five names. If you remember nothing else specific about the CYP family, remember these five, and you'll be able to follow virtually any pharmacogenomics discussion.

\[Pause.\]

Now, one more thing about the kitchen analogy that I want to drive home, because it sets up everything that comes next. Different people have, genetically, different chefs. Some people have a fully staffed kitchen with all the chefs working at full speed. Some people have a chef who works incredibly slowly — they're competent, they just take forever. Some people have a chef who's missing entirely. Some people have an extra chef on the line, working twice as fast as normal. And the same dish — the same drug, in the same kitchen — comes out completely differently depending on the staffing.

That's pharmacogenomics. The variation isn't in the recipe. It isn't in the ingredients. It's in the staffing of the kitchen.

## SEGMENT 4 — Star alleles and metabolizer phenotypes

So how do we, as a field, talk about which version of which enzyme you have? Because every CYP gene comes in dozens of variants in the human population, and we need a way to refer to them without losing our minds.

The convention is called **star allele nomenclature**, and it's elegant once you get used to it. Every variant of a gene gets a star, then a number. The wild-type, fully-functional reference allele is always star one. So CYP2D6\*1 is the reference, fully-functional CYP2D6 enzyme. CYP2D6\*2 is the second-described variant. CYP2D6\*3 is the third. And so on, up into the dozens for the most-studied genes — CYP2D6 has well over one hundred star alleles defined.

Each star allele represents a particular combination of variants — usually one or a few SNPs, sometimes a deletion, sometimes a structural change — that occurs in the population at a meaningful frequency, and that has a characterizable functional consequence. Some star alleles produce a normally-functional enzyme. Some produce a slow enzyme. Some produce no enzyme at all. Some produce extra copies of the gene, which means extra enzyme.

Let me walk you through the CYP2D6 example, because it's the cleanest illustration of how the whole system works.

**CYP2D6\*1.** The wild-type. Codes for a fully-functional CYP2D6 enzyme. If you have two \*1 alleles, one from each parent, you've got two working copies and you metabolize CYP2D6 substrates at the expected rate. This is what the textbook patient looks like.

**CYP2D6\*2.** Also functional, encodes an enzyme that works essentially normally. The difference from \*1 is a SNP that doesn't meaningfully affect activity. So functionally, \*2 behaves like \*1.

**CYP2D6\*4.** This is the classic poor-metabolizer allele in European populations. It's a single SNP at a splice site that completely abolishes enzyme production. If you carry a \*4 allele, that copy of the gene produces _no functional enzyme_. About 20% of Europeans carry at least one \*4. And if you're unlucky enough to be \*4/\*4 — both copies — you have no CYP2D6 function at all. You're a complete poor metabolizer.

**CYP2D6\*5.** A deletion. The entire gene is gone. Same functional consequence as \*4 — no enzyme from that copy.

**CYP2D6\*10.** Common in East Asian populations, produces an enzyme with reduced activity. Not zero, just slower. If you're \*10/\*10, you're what's called an intermediate metabolizer.

**CYP2D6\*17.** Common in African populations, also reduced function.

**CYP2D6\*2xN.** This is the ultrarapid metabolizer notation. The "xN" means the gene has been duplicated N times — sometimes two copies on a single chromosome, sometimes three, sometimes thirteen. Yes, thirteen. There are documented cases of patients with thirteen functional copies of CYP2D6 on a single chromosome. If you carry one of these duplications, you produce dramatically more CYP2D6 enzyme than normal, and you tear through CYP2D6 substrates at remarkable speed.

So you can see how this is going. Two alleles per person, one from each parent. The combination — your **diplotype** — determines your enzyme function. And the field has developed a standardized way to convert diplotypes into a small set of **metabolizer phenotypes**.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you those phenotypes. There are five of them, and they're the language clinicians use to describe a patient's metabolic status for a given enzyme.

**Poor metabolizer (PM).** No functional enzyme. Both alleles are no-function alleles. For CYP2D6, this might be a \*4/\*4 patient. For substrates of that enzyme, a poor metabolizer cannot metabolize the drug normally. If the drug is active in its parent form and gets inactivated by the enzyme, a PM accumulates the drug to high blood levels — they're at risk of toxicity at a standard dose. If the drug is a prodrug that needs the enzyme to be activated, a PM gets no drug effect — the drug doesn't work.

**Intermediate metabolizer (IM).** Reduced enzyme function. One no-function allele plus one normal, or two reduced-function alleles. The patient metabolizes substrates more slowly than normal, but not zero. They're somewhere in between.

**Normal metabolizer (NM).** Sometimes called "extensive metabolizer" in older literature. Two functional alleles. The patient metabolizes the drug at the rate the drug label assumes. This is what the standard dose is calibrated for.

**Rapid metabolizer (RM).** Faster than normal, but not extreme. Often this is a patient with one fully-functional allele and one allele with elevated activity, or two normal alleles in someone with environmental induction.

**Ultrarapid metabolizer (UM).** Dramatically elevated enzyme activity. Often due to gene duplication, like CYP2D6\*2xN. For active drugs that get inactivated by the enzyme, an ultrarapid metabolizer chews through the drug so fast that it doesn't reach therapeutic concentrations — the drug effectively doesn't work, even though they're taking it. For prodrugs, ultrarapid metabolizers convert the prodrug to the active drug so efficiently that they can hit toxic levels of the active form.

Now here's where the analogy from earlier becomes load-bearing.

**The poor metabolizer is the slow chef.** Orders pile up at their station. The drug, which the body is trying to get rid of, just sits in the blood, building up to higher and higher concentrations with each dose, eventually reaching toxic levels. Or, if the chef's job was to activate a prodrug, the drug never gets activated and nothing happens.

**The ultrarapid metabolizer is the fast chef.** They tear through orders so quickly that by the time anyone else can react, the food is gone. For an active drug, this means the drug never accumulates enough to work — they need higher doses than normal. For a prodrug, it means they convert the inactive form to the active form so fast that toxic levels of the active drug pile up downstream. The fast chef is dangerous in a way that's exactly opposite to the slow chef — and which is more dangerous depends entirely on whether the drug they're handling is the active form or the inactive form.

\[Pause.\]

This is the conceptual core of pharmacogenomics. The genotype tells you the diplotype, the diplotype tells you the phenotype — poor, intermediate, normal, rapid, ultrarapid — and the phenotype tells you what's likely to happen when you give the patient a standard dose of a drug metabolized by that enzyme. From a single buccal swab worth of DNA, you can predict, before you ever prescribe, which of those five categories the patient falls into for each major enzyme. And once you know the category, the dose adjustment falls out of published guidelines.

The promise, in a sentence: one genetic test, done once, lets you dose-adjust hundreds of drugs over the patient's lifetime.

## SEGMENT 5 — Warfarin: the original pharmacogenomics poster child

Now let's make all of this concrete. The drug we're going to use to learn the field's methodology is warfarin. The same warfarin from the opening scene in Segment 1. The most-studied, most-litigated, most-clinically-painful pharmacogenomics target in the history of the field. The drug that taught the world what variant-driven dosing actually looks like, and also the drug that taught the world how hard it is to actually implement pharmacogenomics in the clinic. Both lessons matter.

Warfarin is a blood thinner. Its job is to prevent or treat blood clots. The mechanism is beautiful. Your body needs vitamin K to make certain clotting factors — proteins in your blood that, when activated in a cascade, form clots. Warfarin blocks an enzyme called VKORC1 — vitamin K epoxide reductase complex subunit 1 — that recycles used vitamin K back into the active form. Block VKORC1, and you starve the clotting factor production line of vitamin K, and clotting slows down.

It's also a phenomenally dangerous drug, because the window between effective and lethal is small. Too little warfarin: you don't prevent the clot, the patient throws a clot to their lung or their brain. Too much warfarin: you cause a bleed — a brain hemorrhage, a GI bleed, an intracranial bleed during a minor head injury. Warfarin is, by some estimates, the drug responsible for more emergency-room visits per prescription than almost any other drug on the market. The standard practice is to monitor a blood test called the INR every few weeks for the rest of the patient's life and titrate the dose endlessly.

Here's the genetic part. The dose of warfarin a particular patient needs, to land in their therapeutic window, varies fivefold across the population. Some patients need half a milligram a day. Some patients need ten milligrams a day. Same blood thinning effect. Vastly different doses.

About forty percent of that variability is explained by two genes.

**CYP2C9.** The enzyme that metabolizes warfarin, breaking it down so the body can clear it. The two major non-functional variants are CYP2C9\*2 and CYP2C9\*3. Patients with these variants metabolize warfarin more slowly than normal — their slow chef can't break down the warfarin at standard rates, so the warfarin builds up, and they need a lower dose to achieve the same blood-thinning effect. A \*3/\*3 patient may need a third of the standard dose.

**VKORC1.** The target of warfarin itself. There's a common variant in the promoter region of VKORC1 — a single base change — that reduces the amount of VKORC1 enzyme the cell makes. Less enzyme means less warfarin is needed to block it. Patients with this variant are warfarin-sensitive and need lower doses.

Now, the rest of the variability comes from non-genetic factors — age, weight, diet (vitamin K intake from leafy greens), other drugs, kidney and liver function. Plus another genetic factor or two we now know matter, like CYP4F2 in some populations. But CYP2C9 and VKORC1 are the big two, and together they're enough to make a substantial difference in initial dose prediction.

So in the early 2000s, when these associations were nailed down, the field got excited. We can sequence two genes, plug the genotype into a dosing formula along with age, weight, and a few other variables, and predict the right starting dose for each patient. We can stop the dangerous trial-and-error period. We can prevent bleeds and clots in the first month of therapy.

\[Pause.\]

And then, in a moment that I think every pharmacogenomics researcher remembers, came the disappointment.

A large clinical trial called COAG — Clarification of Optimal Anticoagulation through Genetics — was run in the early 2010s. Patients about to start warfarin were randomized to two arms. One arm got the standard clinical algorithm — dose by age, weight, comorbidities, the usual. The other arm got the genetic algorithm — same clinical factors, plus CYP2C9 and VKORC1 genotyping, plugged into a formula.

The result: no significant difference in time spent in therapeutic INR range. The genetic arm did not, in this trial, beat the clinical arm.

This was, depending on how you read it, either a catastrophe for pharmacogenomics or a lesson about how the field works. There's a parallel trial called EU-PACT that ran in Europe at the same time and did find a benefit. The trials differed in important ways — the patient populations, the control arm algorithm, the time frame. The honest answer is that the warfarin pharmacogenomics story is complicated, and the marginal benefit of genotyping for warfarin specifically, in the era of the alternative drugs we now have, is real but modest.

But — and this is the part that matters for the rest of the lecture — by the time COAG and EU-PACT were running, the broader field had already shown that for _other_ drug-gene pairs, the benefit was huge and unambiguous. Clopidogrel, codeine, abacavir, carbamazepine, thiopurines, 5-FU. Warfarin was the proof of concept that pharmacogenomics could work in principle. The other drugs proved it works in practice.

There's a coda. The newer anticoagulants — drugs like apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran — don't require monitoring and have much wider therapeutic windows. In many patients they've replaced warfarin entirely. So even where warfarin pharmacogenomics didn't dramatically reshape practice, the broader pharmaceutical pipeline gave us alternatives. Sometimes the answer to a hard pharmacogenomics problem is "use a different drug." We'll come back to that.

So that's warfarin. The original poster child. CYP2C9 plus VKORC1, ~40% of dose variability, dosing nomograms still in use, modest benefit from genotyping demonstrated in some trials, the drug that taught the field both the promise and the difficulty of clinical implementation. Carry it with you as the prototype. Most of what follows is variations on this theme.

## SEGMENT 6 — Clopidogrel and CYP2C19: the prodrug that doesn't work for everyone

Now let me tell you a pharmacogenomics story where the clinical benefit is, by contrast, unambiguous. Clopidogrel — brand name Plavix — and the enzyme CYP2C19.

Clopidogrel is an antiplatelet drug. Platelets are the little sticky cell fragments in your blood that initiate clotting. After certain cardiovascular procedures — most famously, after a doctor places a stent in a coronary artery to keep it open — you absolutely must keep your platelets from clumping onto the stent, because if they do, the stent itself becomes the trigger for a clot, and that clot in a coronary artery is a heart attack. Stent thrombosis. It can kill you within hours.

Clopidogrel is the standard drug for preventing this. It binds to a receptor on the platelet called P2Y12 and blocks platelet activation. Hundreds of millions of prescriptions a year. One of the top-selling drugs in pharmaceutical history.

Here's the catch. Clopidogrel, as you swallow it, is _inactive_. It's a prodrug. It does nothing to your platelets in the form you take it. To work, it has to be metabolically activated in the liver — specifically, it has to be processed through CYP2C19 to generate the active metabolite that binds the P2Y12 receptor. No CYP2C19, no active drug. No active drug, no antiplatelet effect.

\[Pause.\]

Now think about what this means. Remember the metabolizer phenotypes from Segment 4. A patient who is a CYP2C19 poor metabolizer — two no-function alleles, the most common of which is CYP2C19\*2 — barely activates clopidogrel. The prodrug they swallow stays a prodrug. It circulates in their blood, gets cleared by other pathways, and never converts efficiently to the active form. So the drug, in their body, doesn't work. They are getting essentially a placebo dose of antiplatelet medication. And they don't know it. Their cardiologist doesn't know it, unless they tested. And a couple of weeks after the stent goes in, those patients have a substantially higher rate of stent thrombosis, heart attack, and death.

How common is this? CYP2C19\*2 is one of the most common loss-of-function variants in human pharmacogenomics. In European populations, about 2% of people are \*2/\*2 — full poor metabolizers — and another 25% or so carry one \*2 allele, making them intermediate metabolizers with reduced but not zero activation. In East Asian populations, the numbers are dramatically higher. About 14% of East Asians are CYP2C19 poor metabolizers — \*2/\*2 or \*2/\*3. One in seven. In a busy cardiology practice in Beijing or Seoul, you can do the math: a substantial fraction of patients leaving the hospital with a fresh stent and a prescription for clopidogrel are receiving a drug that won't work for them.

In 2010, after a series of studies and meta-analyses confirmed this effect, the FDA put a **boxed warning** on clopidogrel. A boxed warning, also called a black box warning, is the strongest cautionary label the FDA can attach to a drug short of pulling it from the market. The clopidogrel boxed warning explicitly states that CYP2C19 poor metabolizers may not benefit from the standard dose and that alternative therapy or alternative strategies should be considered.

What are the alternative strategies? There are two main ones. You can give a higher dose of clopidogrel — but this doesn't fully fix the problem and increases bleeding risk. Or you can switch to a different antiplatelet — prasugrel or ticagrelor, both of which are activated through different pathways and aren't dependent on CYP2C19. These drugs cost more and have their own side effect profiles, but for a CYP2C19 poor metabolizer they're the right choice.

\[Pause.\]

So now the workflow. A patient comes into the cath lab, gets a stent placed, and is about to be discharged with a prescription for antiplatelet therapy. Before that prescription gets written, a CYP2C19 genotype is run — buccal swab, saline rinse, blood draw, doesn't matter, just need a few cells. The result comes back, usually within a day. If the patient is a normal or rapid metabolizer, clopidogrel is fine. If the patient is an intermediate or poor metabolizer, they get prasugrel or ticagrelor instead.

This protocol is now standard at many major cardiology centers. It's one of the cleanest pharmacogenomics implementations in practice. The clinical benefit is measurable: fewer stent thromboses, fewer heart attacks, fewer deaths. The test is cheap — a few tens of dollars for genotyping the relevant CYP2C19 SNPs. The turnaround is fast. The decision rule is simple: PM or IM, use a different drug.

If you want a single example of what pharmacogenomics, done well, looks like in real clinical practice — what it looks like when the science actually translates into changed care — this is the example. Clopidogrel-CYP2C19. The prodrug that doesn't work for everyone, but for which we can identify in advance who it won't work for.

There's one more thing about this story worth emphasizing. Notice the population genetics. The fact that CYP2C19\*2 is twice as common in East Asian populations as in European populations means that the clinical benefit of testing is distributed unequally across ancestries. In a European population, a small fraction of patients will be identified as poor metabolizers and re-routed. In an East Asian population, a much larger fraction will be. We'll come back to this — the ancestry dimension of pharmacogenomics — because it's one of the deepest equity issues in the field.

## SEGMENT 7 — Codeine, CYP2D6, and the children who died

I want to tell you a story now that is, I think, the most haunting case in all of pharmacogenomics. It's the story that, more than any other single case, drove regulatory action and made the field's case to skeptical clinicians who thought pharmacogenomics was an academic curiosity. It's the story of codeine and CYP2D6 ultrarapid metabolizers. And it involves dead babies.

Let me set up the pharmacology first.

Codeine is an opioid pain reliever. It's been around for two centuries. It's relatively mild — less potent than morphine, less abused historically, used for moderate pain and as a cough suppressant. It was, until quite recently, considered safe enough to give to children. Many of you have probably had it after a tooth extraction or for a bad cough.

Here's the thing about codeine that, for most of its history, almost nobody outside of pharmacology knew. Codeine itself, the molecule you swallow, has almost no opioid activity. It's not the drug. It's a prodrug. To produce its pain-relieving effect, codeine has to be converted in your liver to morphine — yes, morphine, the strong opioid — by an enzyme. And that enzyme is CYP2D6.

About ten percent of the codeine you swallow gets converted to morphine in a normal metabolizer. That's enough to give you mild pain relief. Most of the rest gets converted to inactive metabolites and excreted.

Now apply the metabolizer phenotypes.

If you're a CYP2D6 poor metabolizer — about 7% of Europeans — you convert essentially no codeine to morphine. Codeine doesn't work as a painkiller for you at all. You'd be better off with acetaminophen. This was actually known clinically for decades, in a vague way; some patients reported codeine just didn't help them. Nobody knew why until the genetics was worked out.

If you're a CYP2D6 ultrarapid metabolizer — about 1% to 10% of Europeans depending on the population, and up to 30% in some North African populations — you convert codeine to morphine _far_ more efficiently than normal. We're not talking about a small uptick. We're talking about producing morphine levels that, from a standard codeine dose, can be several times higher than expected. Plasma morphine concentrations that, in some documented cases, have approached lethal levels.

\[Pause.\]

And here is where the story turns dark.

In 2006, a case was published of a healthy newborn who died, in their first weeks of life, of morphine poisoning. The baby was being breastfed. The mother had been prescribed Tylenol with codeine for postpartum pain — a routine prescription, given to millions of women every year. The mother was a CYP2D6 ultrarapid metabolizer. She converted her codeine to morphine at an unusually high rate, the morphine concentrated in her breast milk, and the breastfeeding infant absorbed enough morphine to develop opioid toxicity and stop breathing. The autopsy found morphine concentrations consistent with a fatal opioid overdose in a baby who had never been directly given an opioid.

This was the wakeup call. Other cases were reported subsequently — infants who died after surgery from codeine given for post-tonsillectomy pain, where the infants themselves were ultrarapid metabolizers and converted their dose to dangerous morphine levels. Children who stopped breathing after standard pediatric doses. A pattern emerged that, in retrospect, had probably been killing children for decades but had been written off as unexplained deaths or sudden infant death syndrome.

In 2012 and 2013, the FDA issued a boxed warning on codeine and required label changes on tramadol — another opioid with similar CYP2D6 metabolism. The recommendations: codeine is contraindicated in children under 12, contraindicated for post-tonsillectomy or post-adenoidectomy pain in children up to 18, and to be used with caution in breastfeeding mothers. The European regulators issued parallel warnings.

This is the cautionary tale that, in my view, every clinician should know. **The ultrarapid metabolizer is dangerous, even from a standard dose, even of a drug that's been on the market safely for two hundred years, if the drug is a prodrug that gets converted to a more potent active form.** The fast chef converts the inactive ingredient into the active poison faster than the body can handle, and the patient downstream — sometimes a patient who's not even the one taking the drug, like a breastfeeding infant — pays the price.

The pharmacogenomics implication is direct. Before you prescribe codeine — or tramadol, or any prodrug opioid metabolized by CYP2D6 — you should know the patient's CYP2D6 status, or you should default to a different drug. For most clinical contexts now, this means we just don't use codeine in pediatrics anymore, and we're cautious with it in adults. The risk of an undetected ultrarapid metabolizer is too high.

\[Pause.\]

There's a beautiful, awful symmetry in the codeine story. Poor metabolizers get no benefit from the drug; ultrarapid metabolizers get a near-overdose. The "right" dose, in the middle, is calibrated for the average patient, and the people on the tails of the genetic distribution — both tails — are at risk in opposite directions. Same drug, same dose, different chefs, different outcomes. Sometimes the difference between "doesn't work" and "kills your baby."

This is why pharmacogenomics is not academic.

## SEGMENT 8 — Abacavir and HLA-B\*5701: the prevent-toxicity test

I want to switch gears now to a completely different kind of pharmacogenomics test. Up to this point, we've been talking about variants in metabolic enzymes — chefs in the kitchen, faster or slower or absent. The next two segments are about variants in a completely different system: the immune system, specifically HLA genes, and how variants in HLA can predict catastrophic immune reactions to particular drugs.

The first example is abacavir, a drug used for HIV.

Abacavir is a nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor. It's part of the backbone of many combination HIV regimens. Very effective. Very well-tolerated, for most patients. But about 5% of people who took it in the early years of its use developed something called a **hypersensitivity reaction** — a multi-system inflammatory syndrome that started a few days to a few weeks after starting the drug. Fever, rash, gastrointestinal symptoms, sometimes respiratory symptoms. If the patient kept taking the drug, the reaction could progress to a life-threatening, multi-organ syndrome. If they stopped the drug and were re-exposed later, the reaction came back faster and worse — sometimes fatally.

So abacavir had this awful problem. It was a very good drug for the 95% of people who could take it, and a potentially deadly drug for the 5% who couldn't, and you couldn't tell in advance which patient was which. Clinicians would start abacavir and watch for the first signs of hypersensitivity, ready to stop the drug at the first hint. It worked, mostly. But it was nerve-wracking, and the 5% who reacted got sick, sometimes severely, before the drug could be stopped.

In 2002, a research group did a genome-wide association study comparing patients who had developed hypersensitivity to patients who had tolerated the drug. And they found a single, dramatic association. Almost every patient who had reacted to abacavir carried a particular variant of an HLA gene — HLA-B\*5701. And almost no patient who hadn't reacted carried it.

The numbers, when properly tabulated, were extraordinary. About 5% of European populations carry HLA-B\*5701. Of those who do, the risk of hypersensitivity to abacavir is around 50% — a coin flip. Of those who don't, the risk is essentially zero. The variant accounted for nearly all the hypersensitivity cases.

\[Pause.\]

Why does HLA-B\*5701 cause this? The mechanism, worked out subsequently, is fascinating. HLA-B molecules are part of the immune system's antigen-presenting machinery — they hold up little peptide fragments for T cells to inspect, and if the T cell sees a peptide it considers foreign, it triggers an immune response. The HLA-B\*5701 variant has a binding groove with a particular shape, and abacavir, the small drug molecule itself, fits into that groove. When abacavir occupies the groove, it changes the spectrum of peptides the molecule can hold, which causes the immune system to suddenly see normal self-peptides as foreign and attack them. It's an autoimmune-like reaction driven by a small molecule altering antigen presentation. Drug-induced T-cell activation by altered peptide repertoire.

A clinical trial called PREDICT-1 was run to test whether pretreatment HLA-B\*5701 genotyping would reduce hypersensitivity. The trial randomized patients to standard care or HLA-B\*5701 screening before abacavir initiation. The result was, by pharmacogenomics standards, spectacular. The screened arm had _zero_ confirmed hypersensitivity reactions. The standard care arm had the expected ~5% rate. A simple, cheap genetic test, done once before prescribing, eliminated a deadly adverse drug reaction entirely.

Since 2008, HLA-B\*5701 testing has been the **standard of care** before prescribing abacavir, worldwide. It is mandatory in many jurisdictions. If you cannot demonstrate that your patient is HLA-B\*5701 negative, you should not prescribe abacavir.

This is the prototype "prevent-toxicity" pharmacogenomics test. It doesn't tell you about metabolism. It doesn't tell you about dose. It tells you, binary, whether a patient is at high risk of a specific catastrophic adverse reaction, and therefore whether to use the drug at all. Positive: pick a different drug. Negative: proceed with confidence.

Notice the value proposition. The test costs maybe $50. It prevents an adverse event that, when it happens, is severe enough to require hospitalization and can be fatal. Even on pure cost grounds, before you weigh the human suffering, the test pays for itself many times over. This is the cleanest cost-effectiveness case in pharmacogenomics. The abacavir-HLA-B\*5701 story is the example I would pull out if a hospital CFO ever asked me whether pharmacogenomics testing was worth the money.

And it generalized. The success with abacavir launched a search for other drug-HLA associations, and we found them. Some are even more dramatic than abacavir.

## SEGMENT 9 — Carbamazepine and HLA-B\*1502: Stevens-Johnson syndrome

The next HLA-drug story is even more clinically dramatic, and it's a perfect case study in why pharmacogenomics has to think globally about ancestry.

The drug is carbamazepine. It's an anticonvulsant — used to treat epilepsy and trigeminal neuralgia, among other conditions. It's been on the market for over fifty years. For most patients it's a workhorse drug, effective and reasonably tolerated.

For some patients — fortunately rare — carbamazepine triggers one of the most terrifying adverse drug reactions in all of medicine. **Stevens-Johnson syndrome** and its more severe form, **toxic epidermal necrolysis**, often abbreviated SJS/TEN. These are immune-mediated reactions in which the skin and mucous membranes essentially undergo massive cell death. The patient develops a rash that progresses to blisters, then sheets of skin actually slough off the body, including the lining of the mouth, eyes, esophagus, and airway. The patient ends up looking, and being treated, like a severe burn victim. Mortality, even with modern intensive care, is around 25% for full toxic epidermal necrolysis. Survivors often have permanent eye damage, scarring, and chronic pain.

Carbamazepine is one of the most common drug causes of SJS/TEN. The reaction is rare — maybe 1 in 10,000 prescriptions in unselected populations — but catastrophic when it happens.

\[Pause.\]

In 2004, a Taiwanese research group made a critical observation. In their patient population, virtually every case of carbamazepine-induced SJS/TEN occurred in patients carrying a specific HLA variant: **HLA-B\*1502**. The association was tight — over 90% of cases were HLA-B\*1502 positive, and the overall risk in carriers was on the order of several hundred-fold elevated relative to non-carriers.

And here's the population genetics. HLA-B\*1502 is rare in European populations — well under 1%. It's much more common in many Asian populations: Han Chinese, Thai, Malaysian, Filipino, Indonesian, Vietnamese. In some of these groups, 10 to 15% of people carry the allele. So while the overall risk of SJS/TEN with carbamazepine in a European patient is low and somewhat unpredictable, the risk in a Han Chinese patient — driven by the high allele frequency — is substantially higher, and largely concentrated in the genetic carriers.

The FDA, in 2007, issued an alert and updated the carbamazepine label to recommend HLA-B\*1502 screening in patients of Asian ancestry before initiating the drug. In Taiwan, HLA-B\*1502 testing before carbamazepine became standard. A nationwide implementation study showed a substantial reduction in SJS/TEN cases following the policy change. This is one of the great pharmacogenomics public health victories.

There's a related story for another anticonvulsant, allopurinol — used for gout, but also a major cause of SJS/TEN — and a different HLA allele, HLA-B\*5801, common in Han Chinese and Korean populations. Same general pattern. Same general intervention.

These HLA-drug associations have taught the field two lessons.

**Lesson one.** HLA variants matter for drug safety in a way that's completely independent of metabolism. The HLA gene doesn't metabolize anything. It presents peptides to T cells. But certain HLA variants, in combination with certain drugs, trigger T-cell responses that cause severe immune-mediated adverse reactions. It's a different mechanism than CYP, and the testing infrastructure is somewhat different too.

**Lesson two — and this one is huge.** Most early pharmacogenomics research was done in European populations. The HLA-B\*1502/carbamazepine story is a clear case where the European data totally missed the most important association in the disease, because the variant is rare in Europeans. The risk allele was hiding in plain sight in Asian populations the entire time. If we had only studied Europeans, we would have, and in fact for years did, miss this association — and the people who paid the price were Asian patients. We'll return to this theme in Segment 16, because it's one of the central equity problems in modern pharmacogenomics.

\[Pause.\]

That's where we'll take our break. In the first half of the lecture we've covered why people respond to drugs differently, the CYP450 enzymes that handle most of those drugs, the star allele system for naming variants, the metabolizer phenotypes that variants translate into, and four worked examples — warfarin, clopidogrel, codeine, abacavir, and carbamazepine — that show how genetic variation translates into clinical decisions about dose, drug choice, or whether to prescribe at all. When we come back, we'll talk about the chemotherapies where pharmacogenomics is now mandatory, the global infrastructure that makes pharmacogenomics implementable, the implementation problem itself, somatic versus germline pharmacogenomics, the ancestry equity issue, and where the field is going next. Stretch, get some water, and I'll see you in a few minutes.

## SEGMENT 10 — Chemotherapy pharmacogenomics: TPMT, DPYD, and UGT1A1

Welcome back. Now I want to spend a segment on a corner of pharmacogenomics where the testing is, by current consensus, simply **mandatory before prescribing** — a corner where the science is so well-established, and the consequences of getting it wrong so severe, that essentially no responsible clinician initiates these drugs without testing. That corner is the metabolism of certain chemotherapy and chemotherapy-adjacent drugs.

Let's start with the cleanest example. **6-mercaptopurine** and the enzyme **TPMT**.

6-mercaptopurine, often abbreviated 6-MP, and its closely related cousin azathioprine, are thiopurine drugs. They've been around since the 1950s. They're used in the maintenance phase of childhood leukemia — particularly acute lymphoblastic leukemia, ALL, the most common childhood cancer — and they're used as immunosuppressants for autoimmune conditions like inflammatory bowel disease, after organ transplants, and so on.

The mechanism is that thiopurines get incorporated into DNA in dividing cells, where they interfere with cell division. In leukemia, this kills the malignant lymphoblasts. In autoimmune disease, this suppresses proliferating immune cells.

To do their job, thiopurines have to be metabolized into active intracellular metabolites. They also get inactivated by competing pathways, the most important of which is methylation by an enzyme called **thiopurine S-methyltransferase**, TPMT.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the thing. TPMT activity varies dramatically across the population, and the variation is largely genetic. About 1 in 300 people is **TPMT-deficient** — they carry two no-function alleles, the most common being TPMT\*3A or TPMT\*3C. In a TPMT-deficient patient, the inactivation pathway is gone. The thiopurine drug, given at a standard dose, accumulates the active intracellular metabolite to ten or twenty times the normal level. The result is **profound, life-threatening bone marrow suppression**. White blood cells, red blood cells, platelets — all wiped out. The patient becomes severely immunocompromised, anemic, and at risk of bleeding. In a child being treated for leukemia, this can be fatal within days.

The intermediate metabolizers — about 10% of the population, with one no-function allele — also experience excess toxicity at standard doses, though less severe.

The testing infrastructure here is mature and the dosing adjustments are precise. Patients identified as TPMT poor metabolizers get dramatically reduced thiopurine doses — sometimes as low as 10% of the standard dose. Intermediate metabolizers get something like 30-70% of the standard dose. Normal metabolizers get the full dose. With proper dosing, all three groups can be treated safely and effectively.

In pediatric oncology — childhood leukemia treatment — TPMT genotyping (and increasingly, NUDT15 genotyping, which is particularly important in Asian populations) before thiopurine therapy is **standard of care**. It's no longer optional. Every major pediatric oncology center in the developed world tests every patient before they start 6-MP, and adjusts the dose to genotype. This is one of the success stories.

Now let me do two more in quick succession.

**Irinotecan and UGT1A1\*28.** Irinotecan is a chemotherapy drug used for colorectal cancer and some others. Its active metabolite is detoxified by an enzyme called UGT1A1, which adds a glucuronic acid group and makes the molecule water-soluble for excretion. Patients with a variant called UGT1A1\*28 — a polymorphism in the gene's promoter, which by the way is also responsible for Gilbert's syndrome, a benign condition causing mild hyperbilirubinemia — have reduced UGT1A1 activity. They detoxify irinotecan more slowly. At standard doses, they have substantially elevated rates of severe diarrhea and neutropenia, both of which can be life-threatening. The FDA recommends genotype-guided dose reduction in UGT1A1\*28 homozygotes.

**5-fluorouracil and DPYD.** 5-FU is one of the most commonly used chemotherapy drugs in the world — backbone of treatment for colorectal, breast, and several other cancers. It's metabolized and inactivated by an enzyme called dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase, DPYD. About 3-8% of the population carries a partial-deficiency variant, and a very small fraction (~0.2%) are essentially DPYD-deficient. A DPYD-deficient patient given a standard 5-FU dose can die — and has died — from overwhelming toxicity. Severe mucositis, neutropenia, cardiotoxicity, neurotoxicity. There are documented cases of patients receiving their first 5-FU infusion and dying within two weeks from the drug.

In 2020, after a series of fatal cases and a successful trial, DPYD genotyping became **mandatory** before 5-FU treatment across much of Europe. The United States has been slower to mandate it, but most major cancer centers now do it routinely. The drug has saved millions of lives, but it has also killed people who could have been identified by a $100 genetic test done before the first infusion.

\[Pause.\]

Step back and look at the pattern. Three chemotherapy or chemotherapy-related drugs — thiopurines, irinotecan, 5-FU. Three different enzymes — TPMT, UGT1A1, DPYD. Three different genetic variants. But the same underlying phenomenon: a fraction of patients with reduced metabolic clearance, who at standard doses develop life-threatening toxicity, and who can be identified by a cheap, fast, one-time genetic test before the first dose.

This is what mature, clinically-implemented pharmacogenomics looks like. The science is settled. The guidelines are clear. The infrastructure exists. The patients are tested. Lives are saved. There's no debate.

The remaining question, for these and other drug-gene pairs, is implementation — how do we make sure every patient who needs the test gets it, at every hospital, in every country? And that's the question we'll spend the next several segments on.

## SEGMENT 11 — PharmGKB and CPIC: the global resource for pharmacogenomics

I've been throwing drug-gene pairs at you, with associated guidelines for dosing or drug choice. Where do these guidelines come from? Who curates them? How does a clinician, in the middle of a busy day, look up "patient is a CYP2C19 poor metabolizer and needs an antidepressant, what should I prescribe?" and get a clear, evidence-based answer?

The answer is two acronyms you need to know: **PharmGKB** and **CPIC**.

**PharmGKB** stands for the Pharmacogenomics Knowledge Base. It's a project based at Stanford, funded by the NIH, and it serves as the central global repository for the evidence base of pharmacogenomics. Every published association between a genetic variant and a drug response — every paper, every clinical study, every case report — gets curated into PharmGKB. Each variant-drug-outcome association is assigned a level of evidence, from one (strong, multiple replicated studies) down to four (preliminary, single study, may or may not hold up). PharmGKB is, in practice, the Cochrane Reviews of pharmacogenomics.

If you want to know what's known about a particular drug-gene pair — say, fluoxetine and CYP2D6 — you go to PharmGKB and you read the curated summary. It will tell you what variants matter, in what populations, what the effect sizes are, and what the supporting studies say.

\[Pause.\]

But PharmGKB is a knowledge base. It tells you what's known. It doesn't tell you what to _do_ at the bedside. For that, you need **CPIC**.

**CPIC** is the Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium. It's an international collaboration of clinicians, pharmacologists, geneticists, and informaticians whose explicit mission is to translate pharmacogenomics evidence into actionable clinical guidelines. CPIC writes the guidelines that say, in plain language: if your patient is a CYP2C19 poor metabolizer and they need treatment for major depression, here is the dosing recommendation, here is the drug choice, here is what to do. Specific. Actionable. Bedside-ready.

As of the mid-2020s, CPIC has published guidelines for approximately 25-30 drug-gene pairs at the highest evidence level, with more in development. The guidelines are open-access, free to read, hosted at cpicpgx.org, updated regularly as evidence evolves. They are the gold standard for clinical pharmacogenomics worldwide.

The CPIC guideline structure is consistent across drug-gene pairs. They give you the metabolizer phenotype categories, the recommended action for each phenotype, the strength of the recommendation, the level of evidence supporting it, and links to the underlying data. A clinician implementing pharmacogenomics for the first time can essentially treat CPIC as their playbook.

There are also other guidelines bodies — the Dutch Pharmacogenetics Working Group, DPWG, publishes parallel European guidelines that overlap heavily with CPIC. The FDA maintains a table of pharmacogenomics-relevant drug labels. In Canada, in Australia, in Japan, in the UK, similar national or regional groups exist. The guidelines mostly agree with each other, though they sometimes differ in details of dose adjustment or population specifics.

The point is: if you, as a clinician, want to do pharmacogenomics responsibly, the evidence base and the guidelines have been curated for you. You don't have to read the primary literature. You go to CPIC, you find the drug, you find the genotype, you find the recommendation. The work has been done.

That's a profound thing. Pharmacogenomics has gone from an academic discipline where you had to be a specialist to use it, to a clinical discipline where any prescriber, with a few hours of training, can follow guidelines and improve patient care. The bottleneck is no longer the knowledge. The bottleneck is the infrastructure to deliver genotype results to the prescriber at the moment of prescribing. And that's the next segment.

## SEGMENT 12 — Preemptive vs reactive: when do you genotype the patient?

There's a critical strategic question in clinical pharmacogenomics, and it's deceptively simple. _When_ do you test the patient?

There are two basic models.

**Reactive testing.** A patient has a clinical problem — they had a bad reaction to a drug, or a drug isn't working, or they're about to start a high-risk drug like abacavir or thiopurines — and you order a genetic test specifically for the relevant gene at that moment. Get the result, adjust the prescription, move on. This is how most pharmacogenomics testing happens today.

**Preemptive testing.** You genotype the patient _before_ any drug-prescribing decision arises. You sequence a panel of pharmacogenomically-relevant genes — typically a few dozen of them, covering the major drug-metabolizing enzymes and HLA alleles — and you store the result in the patient's electronic health record. Then, every time the patient is prescribed any drug in the future, the system automatically checks their stored genotype against the drug's pharmacogenomics guidelines and alerts the prescriber if a dose adjustment or alternative drug is indicated.

\[Pause.\]

The trade-offs are obvious. Reactive testing is cheaper per-encounter — you only pay for the test when you need it. Preemptive testing is more efficient over a lifetime — you pay once, and the result is used potentially hundreds of times.

But the deeper argument for preemptive testing is timing. Reactive testing is often _too late_. Consider the codeine case. By the time a baby has died from morphine toxicity, ordering a CYP2D6 test on the mother is academic — the harm has already happened. By the time a 5-FU patient is in the ICU with multi-organ failure, ordering DPYD doesn't help them. By the time the stent has thrombosed in the clopidogrel poor metabolizer, the heart attack has happened. Pharmacogenomics, done reactively, often fixes the next prescription but can't undo the damage from the current one.

Preemptive testing avoids this. The genotype is sitting in the chart, ready, before any drug is ever prescribed. The first time a patient walks into any prescriber's office, the relevant information is already there.

So the field has been moving, gradually, toward preemptive testing. Several major programs have demonstrated feasibility and clinical benefit.

**eMERGE.** The Electronic Medical Records and Genomics Network is a U.S. NIH-funded consortium that, since 2007, has been integrating genotyping into the electronic medical records of large patient cohorts and measuring clinical outcomes. eMERGE has demonstrated that pharmacogenomics alerts in the EHR change prescribing behavior, and that preemptive panel testing is cost-effective at scale.

**All of Us.** The NIH's flagship precision medicine cohort — over a million participants, deep genotyping, longitudinal health records, deliberately oversampling populations that have been underrepresented in past genomics research. All of Us is, among many other things, building the infrastructure for population-scale pharmacogenomics in the U.S.

**National implementation programs.** The UK has been piloting pharmacogenomics integration through the NHS Genomic Medicine Service. The Netherlands has the longest-running national preemptive pharmacogenomics program. Estonia genotyped a substantial fraction of its population through a biobank initiative. Several Asian countries are deploying national panels focused on locally-relevant variants like CYP2C19 and HLA-B\*1502.

\[Pause.\]

The economic argument for preemptive testing keeps getting stronger as the cost of sequencing falls. In 2026, a pharmacogenomics panel covering the major drug-metabolizing genes, deployable from a buccal swab, costs in the low hundreds of dollars. If you amortize that cost across a patient's lifetime of prescriptions — and over a typical adult life, the average patient takes dozens of different drugs through dozens of different prescribers — the per-prescription cost of pharmacogenomics is trivial.

And the clinical case is overwhelming. The published estimates are that something like 90% of all patients carry at least one clinically-actionable pharmacogenomic variant. Not 10%. Not 50%. Ninety percent. Almost everyone has, in their genome, at least one variant that should be informing at least one drug prescription somewhere in their life. Preemptive panel testing finds those variants once and uses them many times.

The remaining barriers are not scientific. They're infrastructural. Getting the genotype into the EHR. Getting the EHR to actually flag drug-gene mismatches at the moment of prescribing. Training the prescriber to act on the flag. Building the lab capacity to genotype at scale. Reimbursing the testing. Those are the next decade's problems, and we'll dig into them in the next segment.

The vision: every patient is genotyped once, ideally in childhood or at a first adult primary care visit. The result lives in their record forever. Every prescription, the system checks. Most of the time, nothing happens — the genotype is irrelevant for that drug. Occasionally, a flag goes up — patient is a poor metabolizer for the prescribed drug, here's the adjusted dose or alternative recommendation. The prescriber acts, the patient gets the right drug at the right dose, and nobody ever knows how many bad outcomes were silently averted.

That's where we're going.

## SEGMENT 13 — The implementation problem

Let me talk about why we're not already there. Because the science of pharmacogenomics is, for the canonical drug-gene pairs, settled. We know what to test. We know what to do with the results. CPIC has written the guidelines. The labs can do the assays. And yet, in 2026, most patients who would benefit from pharmacogenomics testing are not getting it. Why?

The honest answer is that delivering a genetic test result to a busy clinician at the right moment in their workflow, in a form they can actually act on, turns out to be one of the hardest informatics problems in medicine.

\[Pause.\]

Let me walk you through the implementation pipeline as it has to work.

**Step one. You have to genotype the patient.** This is the easy part. The lab work is mature. Commercial assays cover the major variants for the major pharmacogenes for under $300. Some hospital systems do it in-house; many send specimens to commercial reference labs. Turnaround is days, sometimes weeks. Not great for an urgent prescription, but fine for preemptive testing.

**Step two. You have to get the result into the patient's electronic health record.** This is where it starts to get hard. Pharmacogenomics results are not a single number like a glucose level. They're a list of variants, which translate into a list of star alleles, which translate into a list of phenotypes, which translate into recommendations for specific drugs. The EHR has to store all of this in a structured way that other parts of the system can query — not as a PDF attached to the chart, which is, embarrassingly, where many pharmacogenomics results currently live in many hospital systems. A PDF in the chart is invisible to the prescribing module. If the prescriber doesn't open it, the information might as well not exist.

**Step three. You have to integrate the genotype with the prescribing decision-support system.** This is the holy grail. When a doctor in the EHR types a prescription for clopidogrel, the system has to automatically check the patient's CYP2C19 status, and if the patient is a poor metabolizer, fire an alert in real time — not buried in some sidebar that nobody reads, but actually interrupting the prescribing workflow with "this patient is a CYP2C19 poor metabolizer; clopidogrel is not recommended; consider prasugrel or ticagrelor." That's what you need. Building that in a way that works across hundreds of drug-gene pairs, doesn't fire so many alerts that prescribers ignore all of them (the "alert fatigue" problem), and updates as the guidelines update — that is genuinely hard software engineering. The major EHR vendors have started rolling out pharmacogenomics decision-support modules, but uptake has been uneven and the user experience is, frankly, often clunky.

**Step four. You need clinicians who know how to interpret and act on the results.** Most physicians in practice today were trained before pharmacogenomics was clinically routine. Continuing medical education has tried to fill the gap, but knowledge varies enormously. This is where **clinical pharmacists** have stepped into a critical role. Many hospital systems now have pharmacogenomics-trained pharmacists who function as consultants — the prescriber writes the initial order, the pharmacist checks against the patient's genotype, the pharmacist contacts the prescriber if there's a mismatch, the order gets adjusted. Pharmacist-mediated pharmacogenomics is, in many places, what's actually making the system work.

**Step five. You need reimbursement.** In the U.S., insurance coverage for pharmacogenomics testing has been patchy. Some payers cover specific drug-indication pairs (HLA-B\*5701 before abacavir, for instance). Some cover panel testing for psychiatric indications. Some don't cover much of anything. In single-payer systems — the UK, the Netherlands, Estonia, parts of Asia — coverage decisions are made centrally and tend to be more uniform. The economics are clearly favorable when you do the analysis at the population level, but getting from "clearly favorable in the analysis" to "actually paid for in this prescriber's clinic" is its own challenge.

\[Pause.\]

So the implementation problem is real. It is not a "science isn't ready" problem. The science has been ready for years. It is a "the healthcare system is not yet structured to use the science" problem. And that's slowly changing — major academic medical centers have led the way, large integrated systems like Mayo, Vanderbilt, the VA, Geisinger, Mount Sinai have built world-class pharmacogenomics programs and demonstrated what's possible — but the diffusion to community medicine and to global health settings has been gradual.

The optimistic view, which I share, is that we're at the inflection point. EHR integration is catching up. Sequencing costs are falling. Pharmacist-led models are scaling. CPIC and equivalent bodies are aggressively expanding guidelines. Patient demand is increasing as direct-to-consumer testing makes more people aware that their genotype matters for their prescriptions. The trajectory is toward universal preemptive testing being the standard of care within a decade, maybe two.

The pessimistic view is that healthcare informatics has been almost-there for twenty years, and these problems are persistently harder than they look. Both views have evidence on their side. But the direction of travel is clear.

## SEGMENT 14 — Somatic vs germline pharmacogenomics: a tale of two tests

I want to introduce a critical distinction now that often confuses people coming into pharmacogenomics — and that confused the field itself for years. There are actually two completely different things we call "pharmacogenomics," and they operate on different genomes, with different logistics, in different clinical contexts.

The first — and the one we've been discussing this entire lecture so far — is **germline pharmacogenomics**. This is testing the patient's _inherited_ DNA, the DNA they were born with, the DNA in every cell of their body. CYP2D6, CYP2C9, TPMT, HLA-B\*5701 — these are all germline tests. The result is stable across the patient's lifetime. Test once, use forever. The variants you're looking for are inherited from your parents and present from birth.

The second is **somatic pharmacogenomics**, which is mostly the territory of oncology. Cancer cells have their own genome — actually, a corrupted derivative of the patient's germline genome, with accumulated mutations specific to the tumor. Many modern cancer drugs are targeted to specific somatic mutations in the tumor. To know whether the drug will work, you don't test the patient — you test the tumor.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you a couple of examples.

**BRAF V600E and vemurafenib.** In melanoma, about half of all tumors carry a specific mutation in the BRAF gene — the substitution of glutamate for valine at amino acid position 600. This V600E mutation produces a hyperactive BRAF protein that drives uncontrolled cell growth. The drug vemurafenib is a small molecule that specifically inhibits BRAF V600E — not normal BRAF, just the mutant form. If you have melanoma and your tumor carries BRAF V600E, vemurafenib can produce dramatic, often life-saving, responses. If your tumor doesn't carry V600E — if you have wild-type BRAF in your tumor — vemurafenib won't work, and may actually make things worse by paradoxically activating wild-type BRAF. So before prescribing vemurafenib, you sequence the tumor for V600E. Tumor genotyping. Somatic pharmacogenomics.

**EGFR exon 19 deletions and erlotinib.** In non-small-cell lung cancer, a subset of tumors carry mutations in the EGFR gene — most commonly small in-frame deletions in exon 19, or a specific point mutation in exon 21. These mutations make the EGFR receptor hyperactive and drive tumor growth. Drugs like erlotinib, gefitinib, and the next-generation osimertinib specifically inhibit EGFR with these activating mutations. If your lung cancer carries the right EGFR mutation, these drugs work spectacularly well — multi-year responses, in some cases. If your tumor has wild-type EGFR, they barely work. So before prescribing, you sequence the tumor. Somatic pharmacogenomics.

The list of these somatic-genotype-to-drug-choice pairings has expanded enormously over the past 15 years. ALK rearrangements in lung cancer and crizotinib. HER2 amplification in breast and gastric cancer and trastuzumab. KRAS G12C and sotorasib. BCR-ABL fusion in CML and imatinib. PIK3CA mutations and alpelisib. The entire field of "precision oncology" is, fundamentally, somatic pharmacogenomics scaled up.

\[Pause.\]

The critical distinction is workflow. Germline pharmacogenomics is patient-centered — you test the patient once, the result is permanent, it informs many future drug decisions. Somatic pharmacogenomics is tumor-centered — you test the tumor each time, because each new tumor (or each new biopsy of a progressing tumor) may have a different mutational profile. The infrastructure is different. The labs are different. The turnaround time, the cost structure, the clinical context — all different.

Some practical consequences. Germline pharmacogenomics testing is moving toward upfront, panel-based, preemptive deployment integrated into primary care. Somatic pharmacogenomics testing is delivered through pathology, often through "tumor sequencing panels" that cover dozens or hundreds of cancer-relevant genes at a time, typically ordered when a cancer is first diagnosed or when it progresses.

Both fields use the same word — pharmacogenomics — and both depend on the same fundamental insight, that genetic variation in genes that interact with the drug determines whether the drug will work. But operationally they're parallel disciplines. When you read about pharmacogenomics in the news, it helps to ask: is this a germline test, looking at the patient's inherited DNA, or a somatic test, looking at the tumor? The clinical workflow, and the implications, will differ significantly.

For the rest of this lecture I'm going to focus mostly on germline pharmacogenomics, because that's the field with the broadest implications across all of medicine. But just know that somatic pharmacogenomics is a huge and rapidly-growing parallel discipline, and that for cancer patients in particular, both kinds of test may matter — somatic to choose the targeted drug, germline to dose the chemotherapy backbone.

## SEGMENT 15 — The ancestry problem in pharmacogenomics

I've alluded to this several times, and now I want to confront it directly. Pharmacogenomics has a serious population genetics problem, and it is one of the deepest equity issues in modern medicine.

Here's the situation. The variants we test for in clinical pharmacogenomics, and the guidelines that translate variants into recommended actions, were largely derived from studies conducted in European populations — predominantly White Europeans and European-descended Americans. This is a consequence of who gets enrolled in clinical research, where the major research institutions have historically been located, and which populations have been the focus of large-scale genomic studies.

The consequence is twofold, and both consequences are damaging.

\[Pause.\]

**Consequence one. Allele frequencies vary dramatically by ancestry.** The CYP2D6\*4 allele that's common in Europeans is rare in East Asians. The CYP2D6\*10 allele that's common in East Asians is rare in Europeans. The HLA-B\*1502 allele that causes Stevens-Johnson syndrome with carbamazepine is rare in Europeans and common in many Asian populations. The CYP2B6 variants that affect efavirenz metabolism are particularly common in African populations. The CYP2C19 variants that determine clopidogrel response are very different in frequency between European, Asian, and African populations. So a test panel calibrated for European patients — covering the variants most common in Europeans — will miss the most clinically-relevant variants in patients of other ancestries.

If you test an East Asian patient for CYP2D6 and only look for the European variants, you'll miss the CYP2D6\*10 allele and underestimate the prevalence of intermediate metabolizers in that patient. If you test an African patient for CYP2D6 and only look for the European variants, you'll miss several common African variants entirely.

**Consequence two. The clinical guidelines may not be validated in non-European populations.** Even when we know the variants, we often don't have large enough studies in non-European populations to know how the variants behave clinically — whether the dose adjustments derived from European data are correct for other groups, whether there are interactions with other variants that are common in one population but not another, whether the metabolizer phenotype categories translate cleanly across ancestries.

There are some specific failures that have caused real harm. The classic carbamazepine SJS/TEN story — where the most important HLA association for the world's most populous countries was missed for decades because the early research was done in Europeans — is one. The classic CYP2C9 warfarin story is another: the dosing algorithms derived from European patients perform poorly in African American patients, partly because additional CYP2C9 variants that are common in African ancestry but rare in European ancestry weren't included in early panels.

\[Pause.\]

The field has been making progress, but slowly. **All of Us** is deliberately oversampling non-European populations. The **H3Africa** consortium is building African genomics infrastructure. Several Asian countries have built national pharmacogenomics databases focused on locally-relevant variants. CPIC guidelines now explicitly call out where the evidence base is thin for particular populations, and recommend testing for population-relevant variants. Major commercial pharmacogenomics panels increasingly include the major non-European variants.

But the legacy effect is real. For decades, the pharmacogenomics infrastructure was built around European patients, and the rest of the world has been catching up. If you're a clinician serving a non-European population — or, more realistically, any population that's not exclusively European — you have to be aware of this. The default panels and the default guidelines may have gaps for your patients. The right answer is to use comprehensive panels that include population-relevant variants, and to be aware of ancestry-specific guidance where it exists.

This is the ancestry problem. It's not unique to pharmacogenomics — it pervades all of medical genetics — but it bites particularly hard here, because the consequences are immediate, concrete, and measured in adverse drug reactions and treatment failures. A drug that's safe and effective in a European patient may be neither in an Asian or African patient, for reasons that come down to allele frequencies the early studies didn't capture.

The solution is straightforward to state and slow to implement: build the evidence base across all populations, design panels to detect the variants that matter in each population, and update the guidelines as the evidence accumulates. The field is doing this. It's not done.

## SEGMENT 16 — Where pharmacogenomics is going: the integrated prescription

Let me bring all of this together and tell you where the field is heading over the next ten to twenty years.

The trajectory, in one sentence, is: **pharmacogenomics is becoming a routine part of every prescription, invisibly, in the background.**

Here's what that looks like in practice. A patient walks into any clinical setting — primary care, urgent care, ER, specialty clinic — and their preemptive pharmacogenomics panel has been done, sometime in their past, and lives in their record. Maybe it was done as part of a routine adult wellness visit. Maybe it was done at birth as part of newborn screening, extended beyond the classic metabolic disease screening into pharmacogenomics. Maybe it was done as part of a national population genomics program. The mechanism doesn't matter. The result is there.

When the clinician writes a prescription, the EHR checks the patient's relevant genotypes against the drug's pharmacogenomics profile, automatically, in milliseconds. Most of the time, nothing happens — the drug doesn't have a clinically-significant pharmacogenomic interaction, or the patient's genotype doesn't require adjustment. Occasionally, an alert fires — and the alert is intelligent, contextual, and actionable, not a generic warning the prescriber will dismiss without reading. "This patient is a CYP2D6 ultrarapid metabolizer; the standard dose of this antidepressant may not achieve therapeutic concentrations; consider an alternative drug or a higher starting dose. Click here for the CPIC guideline." The prescriber acts. The right drug at the right dose goes out the door.

\[Pause.\]

This vision requires several things to mature in parallel.

**Sequencing has to be cheaper and more comprehensive.** We're already mostly there. A whole-genome sequence, in 2026, costs a few hundred dollars. The pharmacogenomically-relevant content can be extracted from any whole genome, so once a patient has a sequence done for any reason, pharmacogenomics is essentially free as a byproduct. The cost trajectory continues downward.

**EHR integration has to mature.** This is happening, slowly. The major EHR vendors have rolled out pharmacogenomics decision-support modules. Adoption is uneven. The user experience is improving. Standards bodies like HL7 FHIR are developing data exchange formats specifically for pharmacogenomics.

**Polygenic dose prediction.** Right now, most pharmacogenomics dosing is single-gene or two-gene — warfarin uses CYP2C9 and VKORC1, clopidogrel uses CYP2C19. But emerging research suggests that for some drugs, dose response is influenced by many genes at small effect sizes, in addition to the major-effect genes. Polygenic risk scores for drug response — analogous to polygenic risk scores for disease — are an active research area. The idea is to predict a patient's optimal dose by summing contributions across hundreds or thousands of genetic variants, not just the canonical pharmacogenes. This is early days, but for some drugs it could materially improve prediction.

**Pediatric and developmental pharmacogenomics.** A whole subfield is emerging around how pharmacogenomics interacts with developmental changes in enzyme expression. Neonates and infants have immature CYP systems; the pharmacogenomic implications differ from adults. The same poor metabolizer phenotype may have different consequences at different ages. Pediatric guidelines are catching up to adult guidelines.

**Pharmacogenomics for non-prescription products.** Many over-the-counter drugs, dietary supplements, and recreational drugs have pharmacogenomic profiles too. Caffeine metabolism is largely controlled by CYP1A2 — fast metabolizers tolerate caffeine differently than slow metabolizers. Alcohol metabolism is shaped by ADH and ALDH variants — the famous "Asian flush" reaction. These extend pharmacogenomics from the prescription pad into daily life.

The endpoint is a world where prescribing without considering pharmacogenomics is as obviously inappropriate as prescribing without considering kidney function. Where every patient's genotype-relevant profile is part of their basic medical information, alongside their allergies and their problem list. Where adverse drug reactions of the kind that fill pharmacology textbooks become rare events, identifiable in advance, preventable through routine practice.

We're not there yet. But every year, we're noticeably closer.

## SEGMENT 17 — The frontier: AI-guided dosing and novel ADR prediction

In this last content segment before the appendix, I want to give you a glimpse of the next-generation frontier — what the bleeding edge of pharmacogenomics research is doing right now, and where it might take the field in the next decade.

\[Pause.\]

**AI-guided dosing.** Current pharmacogenomics uses relatively simple decision rules: poor metabolizer gets a 30% dose reduction, intermediate gets 70%, normal gets 100%, ultrarapid gets a different drug. These are derived from population averages applied to phenotype categories. They're useful, but they're crude. They don't account for the interaction between multiple pharmacogenes, between pharmacogenetics and other clinical variables, between pharmacogenetics and concomitant medications, in their full combinatorial complexity.

Machine learning models trained on large datasets — millions of patient encounters with linked genotype, drug, dose, and outcome data — are starting to outperform the simple rules. The model can take in a patient's genotype across dozens of pharmacogenes, their clinical state, their other medications, their lab values, and predict the optimal starting dose and the optimal titration trajectory for a specific drug. Some early studies suggest these models reduce time to therapeutic range, reduce adverse events, and improve outcomes compared to standard guideline-based dosing.

This is, in a sense, the natural extension of CPIC guidelines. Where CPIC has hand-curated rules from human pharmacogenomics experts, AI-guided dosing learns the rules from data, including patterns the human experts haven't recognized yet. It's not a replacement — the CPIC guidelines remain essential as interpretable, evidence-based foundations — but a refinement, a way of personalizing within the categories the guidelines establish.

**AI predicting novel adverse drug reactions from genotype.** This one is even more ambitious. The current catalog of clinically-actionable drug-gene associations covers maybe a hundred drugs at most. The pharmacopeia has thousands of drugs. There are almost certainly hundreds of clinically-relevant drug-gene associations that have not yet been discovered, because the relevant cases are rare, the genetic studies haven't been done, or the signals are subtle enough that classical statistical methods haven't picked them up.

Machine learning models trained on millions of patients' genetic and pharmaceutical data — using the kind of nationwide biobank infrastructure that the UK Biobank, All of Us, FinnGen, Estonia, and others have built — can search for novel signals across the entire drug-gene combinatorial space. Some early results suggest these methods can identify candidate associations that subsequently replicate in independent cohorts, raising the prospect of discovering pharmacogenomics knowledge faster than the traditional one-drug-one-gene-one-study workflow.

**Pharmacogenomics-integrated drug development.** Increasingly, when pharmaceutical companies design new drugs, they're building pharmacogenomics into the development pipeline from day one. Phase I trials genotype participants. Phase II and III trials stratify outcomes by relevant pharmacogenotypes. Drug labels at approval already specify recommended genotyping, where applicable, rather than waiting for postmarketing surveillance to discover that 5% of patients shouldn't be taking the drug. This shifts pharmacogenomics from a retrospective discipline — finding out who shouldn't take an already-approved drug — to a prospective one, built into the drug from approval.

**In-line clinical workflow integration.** The longer-term vision is that pharmacogenomics doesn't require any conscious effort by the prescriber. They write the prescription; the system silently checks, silently adjusts, silently flags. The prescriber sees the relevant pharmacogenomics information only when there's something they need to decide. The cognitive load is zero unless action is required. This is the only realistic path to universal implementation in busy clinical practice.

**Pharmacogenomics-aware drug discovery.** A frontier area: design new drugs whose efficacy doesn't depend on a polymorphic enzyme, to avoid creating new drug-gene response problems. Or design drugs that are activated through multiple parallel pathways, so a defect in any one pathway doesn't kill efficacy. This is pharmacogenomics-informed pharmacology — using what we've learned to make the next generation of drugs more uniformly effective across patients.

\[Pause.\]

So that's the frontier. AI-guided dosing, AI-driven adverse event discovery, integrated drug development, invisible clinical workflows. The end state, ten or twenty years out, is a pharmacology that is fundamentally personalized — where every drug is dosed and chosen for the specific person taking it, using their actual genetic biology rather than a population average.

## SEGMENT 18 — Closing: what to remember

We have covered a lot. Let me close by giving you the things I most want you to remember.

\[Pause.\]

**One.** Pharmacogenomics is the genetics of drug response. The same dose of the same drug produces different outcomes in different people, and a significant fraction of that variation is explained by inherited variation in the genes coding for drug-metabolizing enzymes, drug transporters, and drug targets. Genetics is one variable among many — age, weight, kidney function, other drugs, adherence — but for the drugs we care about most, it is the variable that, once known, changes what you should prescribe.

**Two.** The cytochrome P450 enzymes — the liver's metabolic kitchen — handle about 75% of all prescription drugs. The five names to remember are CYP3A4, CYP2D6, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, and CYP2B6. Each has known variants, organized by the star allele system, that translate into one of five metabolizer phenotypes: poor, intermediate, normal, rapid, and ultrarapid. The poor metabolizer is the slow chef; the ultrarapid metabolizer is the fast chef; both are dangerous in opposite directions, and which direction depends on whether the drug is active in its parent form or is a prodrug needing activation.

**Three.** The canonical drug-gene pairs every clinician should know are: warfarin with CYP2C9 and VKORC1; clopidogrel with CYP2C19; codeine and tramadol with CYP2D6; abacavir with HLA-B\*5701; carbamazepine with HLA-B\*1502; thiopurines (6-MP, azathioprine) with TPMT and NUDT15; 5-FU with DPYD; irinotecan with UGT1A1. For some of these, testing is now mandatory before prescribing. For all of them, testing changes patient outcomes.

**Four.** The pharmacogenomics infrastructure — PharmGKB for the evidence base, CPIC for the clinical guidelines, preemptive panel testing for the workflow — has matured to the point where any prescriber willing to follow the guidelines can practice pharmacogenomics effectively. The bottleneck is no longer the science. It's the integration of genotype data into clinical workflows, the EHR systems that have to deliver the right information at the right moment, the reimbursement that has to make testing accessible, and the training that has to bring practicing clinicians up to current standards.

**Five.** Pharmacogenomics has an ancestry problem. Most of the field's evidence base was developed in European populations, and allele frequencies and clinical guidelines may not transfer cleanly to other groups. The field is catching up, but slowly, and in the meantime non-European patients are systematically less well-served by default panels and default guidelines. The solution is to use comprehensive panels that include population-relevant variants, and to be aware of population-specific guidance where it exists.

**Six.** The future is preemptive, universal, and invisible. Genotype every patient once. Store the results. Check automatically with every prescription. Alert the prescriber only when action is needed. Let pharmacogenomics become as routine, and as taken-for-granted, as checking a patient's allergies or their kidney function before writing the script. We're not there yet, but the trajectory is clear, and the destination is one where adverse drug reactions of the preventable kind become rare events.

\[Pause.\]

The last thing I want to say is this. Pharmacogenomics is one of the rare areas of medicine where what we learn from genetics translates almost immediately into action that helps a specific patient in front of you. It's not a research promise. It's not a maybe-someday breakthrough. For abacavir, for thiopurines, for 5-FU, for clopidogrel, for codeine in children, the genetic test before the prescription is what stands between a fine outcome and a catastrophic one. People are alive today because their CYP genotype was checked before the chemotherapy infusion. People are alive today because their HLA-B\*5701 status was tested before they were started on abacavir.

Pharmacogenomics is, in this sense, one of the cleanest cases of precision medicine actually delivering on its promise. Not for every drug. Not for every patient. But for a growing list of drug-gene pairs, the answer is unambiguous: test before you prescribe, and you will help more people and harm fewer. The rest is implementation.

That's the field. Thanks for sticking with me for two hours.

Thanks, everyone. Take care of yourselves. Good work today.

## APPENDIX — Quick-reference summary (not for reading aloud)

**Core idea**: Pharmacogenomics is the genetics of drug response. Same dose, different person, different outcome — often because of a single variant in a drug-metabolizing enzyme, drug transporter, drug target, or HLA gene. Predictable. Testable. Actionable.

**Why people respond differently to drugs**: genetics (the big one for many drugs), age (immature or declining enzyme function), sex, body weight/composition, kidney function, liver function, disease state (inflammation downregulates CYPs), drug-drug interactions (CYP3A4 inhibitors/inducers, grapefruit juice), diet/lifestyle, adherence.

**CYP450 enzyme family** (~75% of all prescription drugs metabolized here):
- **CYP3A4**: ~30-50% of drugs; statins, immunosuppressants, many chemotherapies; heavily affected by drug-drug interactions (grapefruit juice).
- **CYP2D6**: ~25% of drugs; codeine, tramadol, many SSRIs, tricyclics, antipsychotics, tamoxifen; most genetically variable; star alleles \*1 (wild type), \*4 (PM, common in Europeans), \*5 (deletion), \*10 (IM, common in East Asians), \*17 (Africans), \*2xN (UM, gene duplication).
- **CYP2C9**: warfarin, glipizide, ibuprofen; \*2 and \*3 are reduced-function.
- **CYP2C19**: clopidogrel (activates prodrug), PPIs, some antidepressants; \*2 and \*3 are loss-of-function (common in Asians).
- **CYP2B6**: efavirenz, bupropion; relevant in HIV pharmacology, especially Africans.

**Metabolizer phenotypes**: PM (poor) → IM (intermediate) → NM (normal/"extensive") → RM (rapid) → UM (ultrarapid). For active drugs, PMs accumulate to toxic levels and UMs sub-therapeutic. For prodrugs, the reverse — PMs get no effect, UMs over-activate to toxic levels of active metabolite.

**Canonical drug-gene pairs**:
- **Warfarin** + CYP2C9 + VKORC1: ~40% of dose variability explained; dosing nomograms; COAG vs EU-PACT trials; alternative direct oral anticoagulants now reduce clinical relevance.
- **Clopidogrel** + CYP2C19: prodrug, \*2/\*2 PMs are non-responders, 2% of Europeans, 14% of East Asians; FDA boxed warning 2010; switch to prasugrel or ticagrelor.
- **Codeine/tramadol** + CYP2D6: prodrug to morphine; UMs at risk of fatal opioid toxicity; case reports of infants dying from breastfeeding UM mothers; FDA contraindicated in children <12 (2013).
- **Abacavir** + HLA-B\*5701: 5% of carriers get severe hypersensitivity reaction; PREDICT-1 trial eliminated reactions with screening; testing mandatory before prescribing.
- **Carbamazepine** + HLA-B\*1502: Stevens-Johnson/TEN risk; common in Asian populations; mandatory screening in patients of Asian ancestry. Allopurinol + HLA-B\*5801 parallel.
- **6-MP/azathioprine** + TPMT (and NUDT15 in Asians): 1/300 are PMs at risk of fatal bone marrow suppression; mandatory in pediatric oncology and most adult use.
- **Irinotecan** + UGT1A1\*28: severe diarrhea/neutropenia in homozygotes (also Gilbert's syndrome).
- **5-FU** + DPYD: ~3-8% partial deficiency, ~0.2% complete; fatal toxicity in DPYD-deficient patients; mandatory in much of Europe since 2020.

**Infrastructure**:
- **PharmGKB** (Stanford, NIH-funded): central knowledge base of all pharmacogenomics evidence.
- **CPIC** (Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium): clinical guidelines for ~30 drug-gene pairs, open-access at cpicpgx.org.
- **DPWG** (Dutch): parallel European guidelines.
- **FDA**: maintains table of pharmacogenomic biomarkers in drug labels.

**Preemptive vs reactive**: reactive testing is too late for many drugs (codeine, 5-FU); preemptive panel testing (genotype once, use forever) is the direction of travel. ~90% of patients carry at least one clinically-actionable variant. Programs: eMERGE, All of Us, UK NHS Genomic Medicine Service, Estonia, Netherlands.

**Implementation barriers**: EHR integration (pharmacogenomics results often live as PDFs invisible to prescribing modules), clinical decision support and alert fatigue, prescriber training (pharmacist-led models filling the gap), reimbursement, lab capacity.

**Somatic vs germline**: germline pharmacogenomics = inherited DNA, test patient once (CYP, HLA, TPMT, DPYD); somatic pharmacogenomics = tumor DNA, test the tumor (BRAF V600E + vemurafenib, EGFR exon 19 + erlotinib/osimertinib, ALK + crizotinib, HER2 + trastuzumab, KRAS G12C + sotorasib). Both are "pharmacogenomics" but operationally distinct.

**Ancestry problem**: most pharmacogenomics evidence base from European populations; allele frequencies and clinical guidelines may not transfer (CYP2D6\*10 in East Asians, HLA-B\*1502 in Asians, CYP2B6 variants in Africans, additional CYP2C9 alleles in African Americans for warfarin). H3Africa, All of Us, and national programs working to close the gap.

**Frontier**: AI-guided dosing (machine learning models outperforming simple decision rules); AI-driven discovery of novel ADR-genotype associations from biobank data; pharmacogenomics-integrated drug development from Phase I; invisible in-line clinical workflow integration; pediatric and developmental pharmacogenomics; polygenic dose prediction.

**Two organizing principles**: (1) genetic variation in drug-metabolizing enzymes, drug targets, and HLA genes drives a major fraction of inter-individual drug response variability, and is testable in advance of prescribing; (2) implementation — getting the right genotype information to the right prescriber at the right moment — is the rate-limiting step for pharmacogenomics' translation from clear science to universal practice.
