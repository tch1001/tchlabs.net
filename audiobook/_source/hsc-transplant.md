## SEGMENT 1 — The central idea: replace the blood-forming system

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. For the next two hours we're going to talk about one of the oldest, most consequential, and — in a quiet way — most underrated procedures in modern medicine. It's called **hematopoietic stem cell transplantation**, abbreviated **HSCT**, and you may have heard it under its older name, **bone marrow transplant**, or BMT. The two names refer to essentially the same thing. The procedure has been done in human patients since the late 1950s. It got its founder, E. Donnall Thomas, a Nobel Prize in 1990. It is the curative therapy for several otherwise-fatal leukemias. And — this is the part I want you to hold onto — **it is also the infrastructure on which essentially every modern gene therapy for blood disease is built.**

So before any details, before any history, before any names, here is the single sentence I want you to leave this room with, in case you remember nothing else:

**Hematopoietic stem cell transplant means replacing a patient's blood-forming system with new hematopoietic stem cells — either someone else's (allogeneic) or the patient's own, possibly engineered (autologous) — by first wiping out the existing bone marrow and then infusing the new cells, which find empty niches, settle in, and rebuild the entire blood and immune system from scratch.**

That's it. That's the procedure. Every detail we cover today — the conditioning regimens, the donor matching, the engraftment biology, the graft-versus-host disease, the haploidentical revolution, the cord blood banks, the gene therapy products — all of it is engineering and refinement built on top of that one idea. Wipe out the marrow. Put in new stem cells. Let them rebuild the blood.

\[Pause.\]

Now, take a second to notice how strange this is. We are talking about a procedure where the standard of care, for decades, has been to deliberately destroy a vital organ system in a patient — the bone marrow, the factory that makes every red blood cell, every white blood cell, every platelet in your body — and then bet that an infusion of someone else's stem cells, or the patient's own banked-and-edited stem cells, will rebuild that factory from scratch in time to save the patient's life. The window between destruction and rebuilding is on the order of two to four weeks. During that window, the patient has essentially **no immune system, no clotting cells, and a collapsing red blood cell count.** A common cold can kill them. A paper cut can be dangerous. We keep them in isolation rooms, on prophylactic antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals, and we watch the blood counts every day, waiting for the new cells to engraft.

That's the procedure. It works. It has cured tens of thousands of leukemia patients who would otherwise have died. And every time you read in the news about a sickle cell patient cured by CRISPR, or a beta-thalassemia patient liberated from transfusions by a gene therapy called Zynteglo, or a baby with severe combined immunodeficiency given a normal immune system — what you are reading about, underneath the headline, is hematopoietic stem cell transplant. The gene therapy is the new payload. The transplant is the chassis.

\[Pause.\]

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, sketch on a napkin what hematopoietic stem cells are, where they live, and why they are uniquely transplantable; two, explain in plain English what conditioning is, what engraftment is, what graft-versus-host disease is, and why those three together define the entire risk-benefit profile of the procedure; three, look at a news headline about a "new gene therapy for sickle cell" or a "new CAR-T for leukemia" and recognize which steps are HSCT infrastructure and which are the actual novel science — then we've succeeded. Everything else is detail hung onto those three.

Let me set up the structure of the rest of the lecture. We'll start with the hematopoietic stem cell itself — what it is, why it sits at the top of a hierarchy of blood-forming cells, and why this particular cell type, almost uniquely among adult tissues, can be transplanted. Then we'll tell the story of **E. Donnall Thomas**, the first successful bone marrow transplants in the late 1950s and 1960s, and the long road from those early near-failures to a Nobel Prize. Then we'll spend a segment on **HLA matching** — the immunological wall that determines whether a transplant works or kills the patient. Then we'll separate the two big flavors, **allogeneic** versus **autologous**, and walk through why each one exists and what it's for. Then **indications** — the cancers and the genetic diseases that HSCT is used to treat. Then **conditioning** — the deliberate destruction of the marrow, which is the most dangerous part of the whole procedure. That's the first hour.

After the break, we'll come back to **stem cell sources** — bone marrow harvest, mobilized peripheral blood, cord blood — because the technology of how you actually collect stem cells has evolved enormously and matters for patients. Then **engraftment biology** — what actually happens after the infusion, the homing, the niche occupation, the reconstitution. Then the great chronic complication, **graft-versus-host disease**, and its mirror-image silver lining, the **graft-versus-tumor effect**. Then the **HLA matching landscape**, where donors actually come from in practice — siblings, registries, cord banks, and the access revolution called **haploidentical transplant**. Then a deep dive on **HSCT for sickle cell disease**, which is the oldest cure we've had for that condition and the bridge to modern gene therapy. Then the new wave — **Casgevy, Zynteglo, Skysona, Lyfgenia, Strimvelis** — the FDA-approved gene therapies that all run on transplant infrastructure. And we'll end with the frontier — antibody-based conditioning, in vivo CAR generation, the convergence of HSCT and gene editing.

That's the map. Now let's start with the cell itself.

## SEGMENT 2 — The hematopoietic stem cell: the cell that rebuilds the blood

To understand why this procedure works at all, you have to understand what a **hematopoietic stem cell** actually is, because this cell is the single biological reason the whole transplant paradigm exists. Without it, none of this would be possible. With it — and only with it — you can take cells out of one person's body, put them into another person's body, and have those few cells repopulate an entire organ system in a matter of weeks. There is almost nothing else in the adult human body that you can do this with. You cannot transplant heart cells like this. You cannot transplant liver cells like this. You can — and we'll come back to this — transplant whole organs through surgery, but you can't take a few million isolated heart muscle cells, infuse them into a recipient's bloodstream, and have them home to the heart and rebuild it. You can do exactly that with hematopoietic stem cells and the bone marrow. It is, in a real sense, a property of this one cell type that defines an entire field of medicine.

\[Pause.\]

So let's build up what an HSC actually is, in three pieces.

**Piece one: hematopoietic stem cells sit at the top of a hierarchy.** Your blood is, at any moment, a population of trillions of cells of many distinct types — red blood cells carrying oxygen, neutrophils chasing bacteria, lymphocytes patrolling for viruses and cancers, platelets sealing wounds, monocytes, eosinophils, basophils, dendritic cells, and so on. Every one of those cells is short-lived. A neutrophil lives a few days. A red blood cell lives about 120 days and then gets destroyed in the spleen. Platelets live about a week. You replace, very roughly, **a hundred billion blood cells every single day** — meaning your bone marrow runs a daily production line bigger than the population of the planet by an order of magnitude. And all of those new cells, every single one of them, descend from a small population of cells at the top of the hierarchy called **hematopoietic stem cells**. Maybe ten or twenty thousand truly long-term HSCs sit quietly in your bone marrow at any given moment. Below them is a pyramid of progenitor cells, each more committed than the last, fanning out into the many mature blood lineages. The HSCs themselves divide rarely — sometimes once every few weeks or months — but each division produces both a copy of itself, by self-renewal, and a downstream progenitor that eventually generates millions of mature blood cells. **One stem cell at the top; an entire downstream factory beneath it.**

**Piece two: HSCs have two defining properties — self-renewal and multipotency.** Self-renewal means that when an HSC divides, at least one daughter can be another HSC, identical to the parent. This is the property that lets the stem cell pool persist for the entire human lifespan; otherwise it would deplete itself over time. Multipotency means that the other daughter, or both daughters in some divisions, can give rise to all the mature blood and immune lineages — myeloid, lymphoid, erythroid, megakaryocytic. Together, self-renewal and multipotency are the formal definition of a stem cell. The hematopoietic stem cell was, historically, the first adult stem cell ever rigorously characterized — by Till and McCulloch in Toronto in the early 1960s, who showed that single cells from mouse bone marrow could form clonal colonies in the spleens of irradiated mice, with each colony containing multiple blood lineages. That experiment, in 1961, is one of the founding documents of stem cell biology as a field.

**Piece three: HSCs live in a specialized niche.** They are not floating freely in your bone marrow. They live nestled against the walls of specialized blood vessels — sinusoids — and in close contact with supporting cells called **mesenchymal stromal cells** and **CXCL12-expressing reticular cells**. The niche provides chemical signals — stem cell factor, thrombopoietin, CXCL12, and others — that keep the HSCs quiet, healthy, and self-renewing rather than rushing into differentiation. This niche concept matters enormously for transplant because the niches are finite. When you transplant new HSCs into a recipient, those cells need empty niches to occupy. If the recipient's own HSCs are still sitting in their niches, the new ones have nowhere to settle, and the transplant fails. That's the entire reason for the violent process called **conditioning** that we'll cover later — conditioning's job is to empty the niches.

\[Pause.\]

Now, here's the piece that makes this medically magical. HSCs naturally know how to find their niche. If you draw HSCs from a donor, put them in a bag, hang the bag on an IV pole, and let them drip into a recipient's vein, those cells will circulate through the bloodstream, find their way to the bone marrow vasculature, squeeze through the vessel walls, and settle into empty niches. This is called **homing**, and it's driven by a chemokine called CXCL12 — also known as SDF-1 — that's produced by the marrow stroma and bound by a receptor called **CXCR4** on the HSCs. The stem cells follow the chemical gradient home. You don't have to do surgery. You don't have to inject into the marrow directly. You just put the cells in the blood, and they find the marrow on their own.

This is **not normal** for transplantation. If you wanted to transplant pancreatic islet cells to cure type 1 diabetes, you'd have to physically deliver them to the liver or pancreas via catheter and hope a fraction take up residence. If you wanted to transplant cardiomyocytes for heart failure, you'd need direct injection or some kind of engineered scaffold. Bone marrow stem cells are unique among adult cells in having a working, evolved homing program that lets them find their target tissue from a peripheral IV infusion. The cellular GPS is built in. It is one of those facts of biology that turns out, almost by accident, to make an entire branch of medicine possible.

That property — homing — combined with self-renewal and multipotency, is what makes HSCT a thing. You put a few million cells in. They find their seats. They start dividing. Within two to four weeks, they have rebuilt the entire blood production line. It is one of the most remarkable demonstrations of regenerative biology in adult medicine, and it is the foundation of everything we'll talk about today.

## SEGMENT 3 — E. Donnall Thomas and the long road to the first successful transplants

The history of HSCT is the story of one person, more than any other, refusing to give up on an idea that looked completely insane for the better part of twenty years. That person is **E. Donnall Thomas** — usually just "Don Thomas" — who won the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1990 for developing bone marrow transplantation. He shared the prize with Joseph Murray, who pioneered kidney transplantation. The Nobel committee, in essence, gave that prize to the founders of transplant medicine as a whole.

\[Pause.\]

Let me set the scene. In the years after the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in 1945, doctors and biologists began to understand that ionizing radiation, in sufficient doses, kills people primarily by destroying the bone marrow. Radiation damages all rapidly dividing tissues, but the most acutely lethal effect at moderate doses is the wipeout of the blood-forming cells. People exposed to several grays of whole-body radiation die within a few weeks, not because their hearts stop or their brains fail, but because their bone marrow stops making white cells and platelets — they bleed out, or they die of infection. This was the bone marrow syndrome of acute radiation sickness.

In the late 1940s and early 1950s, a series of mouse experiments showed something remarkable: if you irradiated a mouse to a normally lethal dose, but then injected it with bone marrow cells from a healthy mouse, the recipient mouse lived. The bone marrow cells homed, engrafted, and rebuilt the blood. This was the proof of principle, in mice, that bone marrow could be transplanted between individuals.

The leap to humans was made — slowly, painfully — by a small number of clinicians in the late 1950s. Thomas, then a young hematologist at Cooperstown, New York, did the very first attempted human marrow infusions in 1956, treating patients with end-stage leukemia after high-dose chemotherapy and radiation. The early results were almost uniformly fatal. The patients died of infections, or of bleeding, or of what we would now recognize as graft-versus-host disease, although the concept hadn't been articulated yet. The marrow either failed to engraft, or it engrafted and then attacked the recipient. Or the recipient died of the chemo and radiation conditioning itself. By the early 1960s, only a handful of patients had survived. The field was, by any normal medical standard, a failure.

What kept it alive was a small number of people — Thomas chief among them — who believed the underlying biology had to be right. The mice worked. The principle was sound. They just hadn't yet figured out the immunology.

\[Pause.\]

The immunology turned out to be the key, and it came in the form of a discovery made in parallel by Jean Dausset in France, George Snell in Maine, and Baruj Benacerraf in New York: the existence of a set of cell-surface proteins called the **human leukocyte antigens**, or **HLA**, which serve as the body's "self" markers. We'll spend the next segment on HLA in detail, but for now: every cell in your body carries HLA proteins on its surface, and your immune system is trained to recognize those specific HLA shapes as "self." Anyone else's HLA proteins, with different sequences, look like "non-self" — and the immune system attacks them. This is the central reason organ transplants are rejected. It is also the central reason bone marrow transplants either fail or, worse, cause graft-versus-host disease.

Once HLA was discovered and methods to type donors and recipients were developed, in the mid-1960s, Thomas — now at the Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center in Seattle, where he would spend the rest of his career — could finally do something his predecessors couldn't: match donors to recipients. In 1968 and 1969, he and his Seattle team performed the first successful HLA-matched allogeneic bone marrow transplants. Long-term survivors emerged. Patients with previously incurable leukemias began to live for months, then years, then decades. By the mid-1970s, the Seattle group could publish series of patients with acute leukemia in whom HSCT had produced durable, drug-free remissions. The therapy worked, in the right patients, with the right donors.

Thomas built the Fred Hutchinson into the world center for transplantation. His protocols — the conditioning regimens, the GVHD prophylaxis, the post-transplant care — became the global standard. His trainees seeded transplant programs at academic centers around the world. By the time he won the Nobel in 1990, more than ten thousand patients had been transplanted at his institution alone, and bone marrow transplantation was an established curative therapy for several leukemias, severe aplastic anemia, severe combined immunodeficiency, and a handful of genetic blood disorders.

Thomas himself was, by all accounts, a quiet and modest man, more comfortable at the bedside than on the lecture circuit. He died in 2012, at age 92, having lived long enough to see the field he founded extend its reach into gene therapy, into pediatric cures for sickle cell, into the early CAR-T era. He had built a procedure that, today, is performed on tens of thousands of patients every year worldwide, and that serves as the chassis for an entire generation of new cellular medicines.

\[Pause.\]

There's a lesson in this history worth pausing on. The first decade of human bone marrow transplants was a catastrophe by ordinary clinical standards. Almost every patient died. There were calls within the medical community to abandon the approach as obviously dangerous and unworkable. The reason it didn't get abandoned is that a small number of people had a clear mechanistic understanding of why it _should_ work and a stubborn belief that the missing piece — turned out to be HLA matching — was findable. They were right. But they had to wait twenty years to be vindicated.

This pattern, where a procedure looks like a failure until a key piece of biology gets discovered and changes everything, will repeat in the modern story of gene therapy and cellular medicine. Keep it in mind. The most transformative therapies often look like dead ends for years before they suddenly work.

## SEGMENT 4 — The matching problem: HLA and the immunological wall

Let's stop and look in detail at the immunological problem that almost killed the field in its infancy, because understanding it is essential to understanding how modern transplant works. The problem is called **HLA matching**, and it is the central challenge of allogeneic transplantation — the kind of transplant where the donor cells come from a different person.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy. Imagine that every cell in your body wears a uniform — a distinctive set of patches on its surface that tells your immune system "I belong to this team." The patches are protein molecules, encoded by a cluster of genes on chromosome 6 called the **major histocompatibility complex**, or **MHC**. In humans, we usually call them by their alternative name, **HLA**, the human leukocyte antigens. The two names refer to the same molecules. HLA is just the human-specific term.

There are two big classes of HLA. **Class I** is on essentially every cell in your body. It includes the loci called HLA-A, HLA-B, and HLA-C. **Class II** is mostly on professional immune cells — dendritic cells, macrophages, B cells, activated T cells — and includes HLA-DR, HLA-DQ, and HLA-DP. Each of these loci has dozens to hundreds of alleles in the human population — variant sequences that differ from one person to the next. The combinatorics are staggering. Your particular set of HLA alleles, inherited from your mother and father, is essentially a unique molecular fingerprint, shared with very few other people on earth — unless you have a sibling who, by chance, inherited the same set from your shared parents.

The biological purpose of HLA is to display chunks of protein — peptides — on the cell surface, where T cells can inspect them. Class I HLA displays peptides from inside the cell, including viral peptides if the cell is infected. Class II displays peptides from things the cell has engulfed from outside. T cells walk up, sample the displayed peptides, and decide whether anything looks dangerous. This is the foundation of adaptive immunity.

But here's the catch. T cells are trained, during their education in the thymus, to recognize peptides displayed on **your own HLA** as "self." Anyone else's HLA, with a slightly different shape, looks foreign — almost regardless of what peptide is being displayed. The HLA itself is the immune target. **The shape of the molecule is the "team uniform," and uniforms from other teams trigger attack.**

\[Pause.\]

So now imagine you're transplanting bone marrow from one person to another. Two immunological wars can break out.

**War one: host-versus-graft.** The recipient's residual immune system sees the donor cells, recognizes their HLA as foreign, and attacks them. This is conventional graft rejection — the same reason a kidney transplant from an unmatched donor gets rejected. In bone marrow transplant, this typically results in **graft failure** — the donor stem cells get killed before they can establish residence, and the patient is left with no functioning bone marrow. Fatal, unless rescued by another transplant or by autologous backup cells.

**War two: graft-versus-host.** This is the unique problem of bone marrow transplant. Because what you're transplanting includes a fully functional donor immune system — T cells, primarily — those donor T cells will see the recipient's tissues, recognize them as foreign, and attack. This is **graft-versus-host disease**, or **GVHD**, and we'll spend a whole segment on it. It is the central chronic complication of allogeneic HSCT and the reason matching matters so much.

So HLA matching reduces both wars. The closer the match between donor HLA and recipient HLA, the less the recipient's immune system attacks the graft, and the less the donor's immune system attacks the host.

\[Pause.\]

In practice, the field looks at typically ten HLA loci — two alleles each at HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C, HLA-DRB1, and HLA-DQB1 — and counts matches out of ten. A **10/10 match** is the gold standard. **9/10** is acceptable. Less than that gets risky fast. And the matching has to be at high resolution, at the DNA sequence level, because even single-amino-acid mismatches in the right position can trigger massive immune reactions.

The hardest part of HLA matching is the math. You inherit one copy of the whole HLA cluster — called a haplotype — from each parent. Since HLA is essentially co-inherited as a unit, with very rare recombination within the cluster, you and a sibling have a 25 percent chance of being a full HLA match, a 50 percent chance of being half-matched (sharing one haplotype), and a 25 percent chance of being completely unmatched. So if you have one sibling, you have a 25 percent chance they're a perfect donor. If you have three siblings, your odds rise to roughly 58 percent that at least one is a match. Most patients, especially in small families, do not have a matched sibling, which has historically been the central access problem of HSCT.

For patients without a sibling donor, you turn to unrelated donor registries — institutions like the National Marrow Donor Program in the US, operating the **Be The Match** registry, which has on the order of forty million potential donors registered globally. The chance of finding a 10/10 unrelated match depends heavily on ancestry. For patients of Northern European descent, the registry hit rate is around 75 percent. For patients of African, Asian, Hispanic, or mixed ancestry, the hit rate is dramatically lower — sometimes under 25 percent — because the registries are dominated by donors of European ancestry, and HLA diversity is much higher in people whose ancestors evolved in Africa.

This **inequity in access** is one of the central ongoing problems of transplant medicine, and it is the central motivator for two of the modern innovations we'll cover after the break: **cord blood transplant**, which has looser matching requirements, and **haploidentical transplant**, which uses half-matched family donors and has opened HSCT to essentially every patient with a living relative. These are not just clinical advances. They are equity advances. They take a therapy that, for decades, worked best for white European patients with large families, and extend it to populations that the registries failed.

\[Pause.\]

So when you read about HSCT, anywhere, the HLA matching problem is the deep structural issue underneath every clinical decision. It determines who can be transplanted, how well they do, what side effects they suffer, and — crucially — whether the transplant is even available to them at all. We'll come back to it. For now, hold the picture: every cell has team uniforms; transplant works only when the uniforms more or less match; mismatching causes immune wars in both directions; and the registries, until very recently, made the procedure dramatically more accessible to some populations than others.

## SEGMENT 5 — Allogeneic versus autologous: two different procedures, same name

I've been using two words, sometimes interchangeably, and now I want to draw a sharp distinction between them, because they describe genuinely different procedures with different purposes, different risks, and different patient populations. The two words are **allogeneic** and **autologous**.

\[Pause.\]

**Allogeneic** means "from someone else." An allogeneic HSCT uses bone marrow or stem cells from a separate donor — a sibling, a family member, a registry donor, or a cord blood unit. The donor's cells are HLA-matched to the recipient as closely as possible, but they are genetically a different person's cells. The recipient's blood and immune system, after transplant, are essentially the donor's. If you sequenced a successful allogeneic transplant recipient's white blood cells a year after transplant, they'd come up as the donor's DNA. The recipient is, in a very real biological sense, a **chimera** — two genomes living in one body, with one in the blood and the other everywhere else.

**Autologous** means "from yourself." An autologous HSCT uses the patient's own stem cells, harvested before high-dose treatment, frozen, and then re-infused after the treatment has destroyed the marrow. The patient is, in this case, both donor and recipient. There is no HLA matching problem because the cells are perfectly matched to themselves. There is no graft-versus-host disease because there's no foreign donor immune system. And — crucially for the gene therapy era — there is the option to **engineer the cells in the lab** between harvest and re-infusion. The cells go out as one genome and come back as a slightly edited version of the same genome.

These two flavors of transplant exist for genuinely different clinical purposes, and I want you to understand the logic.

\[Pause.\]

**Why do allogeneic transplant?** Two reasons. First, the patient's own marrow is diseased — for example, in leukemia, the marrow contains the cancer; you can't use the patient's own cells because they're cancerous. So you need a separate, healthy donor. Second — and this is the subtle one — the donor's immune system, once engrafted, can attack any residual cancer cells in the recipient. This is the **graft-versus-tumor effect**, which we'll cover in detail later, and it is one of the most powerful therapeutic forces in oncology. The donor's T cells see the recipient's leukemic cells as foreign and kill them. This effect is, in a real sense, an early version of immunotherapy that long predates checkpoint inhibitors or CAR-T. The trade-off is that those same donor T cells can also attack the recipient's normal tissues — graft-versus-host disease — and balancing those two effects is the central clinical art of allogeneic transplant.

**Why do autologous transplant?** Also two reasons. First, in certain cancers — multiple myeloma, lymphoma — the standard of care is to give a dose of chemotherapy so high it would normally be lethal to the bone marrow. The autologous transplant is a **rescue** — you collect the patient's stem cells beforehand, give the high-dose chemo, and re-infuse the cells to rebuild the marrow. You're not using the transplant to replace diseased cells; you're using it as a safety net that lets you give an otherwise impossible dose of chemo. Second — and this is the modern reason that's exploded over the last decade — autologous HSCT is the chassis for **gene therapy**. You take the patient's own HSCs, edit them in the lab to fix a genetic disease — Casgevy edits the BCL11A enhancer to reactivate fetal hemoglobin in sickle cell, Zynteglo adds a functional beta-globin gene for beta-thalassemia — and re-infuse the edited cells. The autologous step is the delivery mechanism. The gene editing is the therapy.

\[Pause.\]

So when you read about HSCT or gene therapy, the first question to ask is always: is this allogeneic or autologous? Because the entire risk profile flows from the answer.

Allogeneic risks: graft failure, graft-versus-host disease (acute and chronic), opportunistic infections (especially CMV, EBV reactivation), all the complications of immunosuppression. Treatment-related mortality at one year for allogeneic transplant is, depending on patient age and conditioning intensity, somewhere between 10 and 30 percent. It is a major procedure with real death risk.

Autologous risks: the conditioning chemotherapy toxicity (mucositis, infections during the cytopenia period), but no GVHD. Treatment-related mortality at one year is typically 1 to 3 percent. Much safer.

But autologous can't, by itself, treat cancers where the bone marrow itself is diseased, because you'd be collecting contaminated cells. So allogeneic remains the standard for most acute leukemias, while autologous is standard for myeloma, certain lymphomas, and the gene therapy indications.

\[Pause.\]

There's a third category I'll mention here and we'll come back to: **syngeneic** transplant, which means donor and recipient are identical twins. Same genome, perfect HLA match, no GVHD risk and no rejection risk. Clinically it's like autologous — no immune incompatibility — but the donor cells are guaranteed not to carry the original disease (because they came from a separate body). In the rare cases where a leukemia patient has an identical twin, this is essentially the ideal transplant scenario. But identical twins are rare, so syngeneic is a tiny fraction of the field. I mention it mostly to complete the taxonomy.

So the taxonomy: allogeneic, from a different person; autologous, from yourself; syngeneic, from an identical twin. Three flavors. Allogeneic for blood cancers where you need the graft-versus-tumor effect and a clean marrow source. Autologous for chemo rescue and for gene therapy. Syngeneic for the rare lucky patient.

That's the architecture of the field. Let's now go through the actual diseases this procedure treats.

## SEGMENT 6 — Indications: what HSCT is actually used for

The list of conditions for which hematopoietic stem cell transplant is the standard of care, or a major treatment option, is long, but it falls into a few clean categories. Let me walk through them, because each one teaches you something about why the procedure works the way it does.

\[Pause.\]

**Category one: the acute leukemias.** These are the cancers that started the field and remain its largest single indication. **Acute myeloid leukemia**, AML, is a cancer of myeloid lineage progenitors in the bone marrow. **Acute lymphoblastic leukemia**, ALL, is the analogous cancer of lymphoid progenitors, more common in children. Both diseases are characterized by the accumulation of immature, non-functional white cell precursors that crowd out normal hematopoiesis. Untreated, both are rapidly fatal. With standard chemotherapy, many patients achieve initial remission, but for high-risk subtypes or for patients who relapse, the only curative option is allogeneic HSCT. The transplant works by two mechanisms: the high-dose conditioning chemo kills residual leukemia, and the donor immune system mops up whatever survives via the graft-versus-tumor effect. AML and ALL together account for a large fraction of all allogeneic transplants performed worldwide.

**Category two: the chronic leukemias and the myeloproliferative neoplasms.** **Chronic myeloid leukemia**, CML, was historically a major transplant indication — driven by a defining genetic abnormality, the Philadelphia chromosome, which creates a fusion gene called BCR-ABL. For decades, allogeneic HSCT was the only curative therapy for CML. Then, in 2001, a small-molecule drug called **imatinib** (Gleevec) was approved that specifically inhibited the BCR-ABL fusion protein, and it turned CML from a transplant disease into a pill-managed chronic condition for most patients. This is one of the great success stories of targeted therapy, and it also illustrates how the indications for transplant evolve. CML transplants are now rare and reserved for patients whose disease has become resistant to multiple tyrosine kinase inhibitors. The other myeloproliferative diseases — myelofibrosis, polycythemia vera, essential thrombocythemia in advanced phases — still occasionally come to transplant when other options are exhausted. **Chronic lymphocytic leukemia**, CLL, was historically transplanted in younger, fit patients, but with the rise of BTK inhibitors and now CAR-T, transplant for CLL has also become uncommon.

**Category three: the lymphomas and myeloma.** **Non-Hodgkin lymphomas** and **Hodgkin lymphoma**, when they relapse after initial chemotherapy, are typically treated with **autologous** HSCT — the high-dose chemo rescue paradigm. The lymphoma cells are killed by intensified chemo, and the patient's own stem cells, collected beforehand, are re-infused to rebuild the marrow. **Multiple myeloma**, a malignancy of plasma cells, is the single largest indication for autologous HSCT worldwide. Patients typically receive induction chemotherapy, undergo autologous transplant with high-dose melphalan, and then continue on maintenance therapy. The transplant doesn't cure myeloma — it delays relapse — but it remains a key part of the standard treatment paradigm for fit patients. There are tens of thousands of autologous transplants done annually for myeloma globally.

**Category four: severe aplastic anemia.** This is a non-malignant condition where the bone marrow simply fails — typically due to an autoimmune attack on the patient's own HSCs. The marrow becomes empty, and the patient becomes pancytopenic — low in red cells, white cells, and platelets. Without treatment, severe aplastic anemia is fatal. Allogeneic HSCT, especially from a matched sibling, is curative in 80 to 90 percent of patients, and it is the standard of care for young patients with a matched donor. For older patients or those without a donor, the alternative is immunosuppression with antithymocyte globulin plus cyclosporine, but transplant is more durable.

**Category five: the inherited immune deficiencies.** **Severe combined immunodeficiency**, SCID — also called "bubble boy disease" — is a group of genetic diseases in which infants are born essentially without a functional immune system. Untreated, they die in the first year of life from opportunistic infections. Allogeneic HSCT, especially when done in the first three months of life with an HLA-matched sibling, is curative in over 90 percent of cases. SCID was one of the very first diseases for which bone marrow transplant was established as a cure, and it remains a paradigm. Other primary immunodeficiencies — Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome, chronic granulomatous disease, hyper-IgM syndrome — are also treated with allogeneic HSCT in many cases.

**Category six: the inherited blood disorders.** This is the category that is currently being transformed by gene therapy. **Sickle cell disease**, caused by a single-base mutation in the beta-globin gene producing abnormal hemoglobin that causes red cells to deform and cause vaso-occlusive crises, has been curable by allogeneic HSCT from a matched sibling for decades — with cure rates around 85 percent, but with substantial GVHD and mortality risk, and with the access constraint that only about 20 percent of patients have a matched sibling. **Beta-thalassemia**, caused by mutations that reduce beta-globin production and lead to severe anemia requiring lifelong transfusions, is also curable by allogeneic HSCT. Both diseases, until very recently, had transplant as their only definitive cure. We'll spend a whole segment on this, because the new gene therapies — Casgevy, Zynteglo, Lyfgenia — are reshaping the treatment landscape for these conditions.

**Category seven: the inherited metabolic and storage diseases.** Some rare inherited diseases — adrenoleukodystrophy, certain mucopolysaccharidoses, Krabbe disease — are treated with allogeneic HSCT or with autologous gene therapy (Skysona for adrenoleukodystrophy is an example) because donor-derived myeloid cells can cross into tissues and deliver missing enzymes to deficient cells, partially reversing the metabolic defect. This use of transplant as a kind of long-term enzyme delivery system is one of the more elegant applications of the procedure.

**Category eight, emerging: autoimmune diseases.** This is newer and still mostly experimental, but for the most severe cases of systemic sclerosis, multiple sclerosis, Crohn's disease, and lupus, autologous HSCT after immunoablative conditioning is being used to "reset" the immune system. The idea is to wipe out the autoreactive immune cells and let the marrow rebuild a more naive, possibly tolerant immune system. Results have been mixed but encouraging in selected high-risk patients.

\[Pause.\]

So that's the indication map. Leukemias and lymphomas, the cancers. Aplastic anemia and SCID, the bone marrow failures and inherited immunodeficiencies. Sickle cell and thalassemia, the inherited red cell disorders. Storage diseases, where transplant becomes enzyme delivery. Autoimmune disease, the experimental frontier. In every case, the procedure follows the same skeleton — wipe out the existing marrow, infuse new stem cells, wait for engraftment — but the purpose changes. Sometimes you're replacing cancerous cells. Sometimes you're delivering a missing gene. Sometimes you're resetting an autoimmune system. The chassis is the same. The payload is what defines the treatment.

Now we have to talk about the chassis — specifically, the most dangerous part of it — which is conditioning.

## SEGMENT 7 — Conditioning: wiping the slate clean

Conditioning is the most dangerous part of the entire transplant procedure, and I want you to understand exactly what it is and why it has to be done. The conditioning regimen is the chemotherapy, and often the radiation, that the patient receives in the days **before** the stem cell infusion. Its job is to do two things at once: kill any residual disease in the patient — leukemia cells, lymphoma cells — and **empty out the bone marrow niches** so that the incoming donor stem cells have somewhere to settle.

\[Pause.\]

Hold the analogy. Remember that hematopoietic stem cells live in specialized niches in the marrow — those little pockets along the sinusoid walls where supporting cells provide the chemical signals that keep stem cells healthy. The number of niches is finite. If the recipient's own HSCs are still sitting in those niches, the donor HSCs have nowhere to go. They circulate, they fail to engraft, and the transplant fails. So conditioning is, at its root, **eviction.** You have to violently empty out the existing tenants so the new ones can move in.

The trouble is that the techniques we use to empty the niches — high-dose chemotherapy and radiation — don't only kill HSCs. They kill every rapidly dividing cell in the body. The lining of your gut, which turns over every few days, gets stripped, causing mucositis — painful sores throughout the mouth, esophagus, stomach, and intestines. The hair follicles get killed; the patient goes bald within two weeks. The gonads get damaged, often permanently, causing infertility. The lungs, liver, kidneys, and heart can sustain damage that ranges from temporary to permanent. And of course the very cell types you're trying to wipe out — the white cells, platelets, and red cell precursors — get wiped out, leaving the patient with **profound pancytopenia** for the two to four weeks until the new stem cells engraft. During that gap, the patient is exquisitely vulnerable: a fever can be sepsis, a nosebleed can be a bleeding crisis.

That gap is the central reason transplant is so dangerous. The conditioning kills the marrow on schedule, but the engraftment of the new cells takes its own time, and during the interval the patient is essentially defenseless. We manage it with intense supportive care — protective isolation, prophylactic antibiotics, antivirals, antifungals, transfusion support — but treatment-related mortality during this period is real.

\[Pause.\]

Now, there are two broad flavors of conditioning regimen, and the choice between them is one of the biggest clinical decisions in transplant medicine.

**Myeloablative conditioning** is the classical, full-dose approach. The intent is to completely destroy the recipient's marrow. The two workhorse myeloablative regimens are **busulfan plus cyclophosphamide** ("Bu/Cy") and **cyclophosphamide plus total body irradiation** ("Cy/TBI"). Busulfan is an alkylating agent that's particularly toxic to hematopoietic stem cells, and at the doses used for transplant it's effectively lethal to the marrow. Cyclophosphamide adds further immunosuppression. Total body irradiation, in older regimens, did similar work via radiation damage. Myeloablative conditioning has the advantage of being most likely to eliminate residual disease and to give the donor cells the cleanest possible runway. But it is genuinely toxic — older patients, patients with comorbidities, can't tolerate it.

**Reduced-intensity conditioning**, often abbreviated **RIC**, and its even gentler cousin **non-myeloablative conditioning**, take a different approach. Instead of trying to wipe out the recipient's marrow with chemo, you use lower doses of chemo combined with strong immunosuppression — drugs like fludarabine, with low-dose busulfan or melphalan — to suppress the recipient's immune system enough that the donor cells can engraft. The actual elimination of any residual disease then comes substantially from the **graft-versus-tumor effect** of the donor immune system after engraftment, rather than from the conditioning itself. The conditioning is gentler, treatment-related mortality is lower, and older or sicker patients can be transplanted. The trade-off is somewhat higher rates of relapse and somewhat higher rates of mixed chimerism — situations where the recipient's own marrow partially survives and coexists with the donor marrow.

A common reduced-intensity regimen is **fludarabine plus melphalan**, often written "Flu/Mel." Another is fludarabine plus low-dose busulfan. The development of RIC regimens in the late 1990s, by groups including Slavin in Israel and McSweeney and Storb in Seattle, dramatically expanded the population of patients eligible for allogeneic transplant. Patients in their 60s and even 70s could now be transplanted, where previously they would have been ineligible.

\[Pause.\]

For **autologous** transplant — and this includes the gene therapy applications — the conditioning regimen is usually a single high-dose chemotherapy agent tailored to the disease. For multiple myeloma, it's high-dose melphalan. For lymphoma, it's a regimen like BEAM (carmustine, etoposide, cytarabine, melphalan). For sickle cell gene therapy with Casgevy or Lyfgenia, it's high-dose busulfan, given as a single agent over several days to wipe out the patient's marrow before infusion of the edited cells. The intensity is enough to make room for engraftment but not as immunosuppressive as you need for allogeneic transplant, because there's no donor immune system to manage.

The point I want you to take away is that **conditioning is the price of engraftment**, and it is paid in upfront toxicity and risk. It is the most acutely dangerous component of the procedure. Almost every modern innovation in HSCT — reduced-intensity regimens, post-transplant cyclophosphamide, antibody-based conditioning that we'll talk about at the end — is, in one way or another, an attempt to reduce the cost paid in conditioning. The dream of the field, for fifty years, has been to find a way to empty those marrow niches that is precise, gentle, and non-toxic. We are, with antibody-drug conjugates targeting CD117 and CD45, finally beginning to approach that dream. But that's the frontier; the present remains busulfan, cyclophosphamide, radiation, and melphalan.

\[Pause.\]

One more concept. The **conditioning regimen interacts with everything else**. The intensity determines the speed of engraftment, the risk of graft failure, the severity of GVHD, the risk of relapse, the risk of organ damage. A patient with refractory leukemia might be pushed to the most intense regimen to maximize chance of eliminating disease, accepting higher toxicity. A patient with sickle cell, who's not fighting a cancer, might be given a less intense regimen because the goal is engraftment of the gene-edited cells rather than tumor eradication. Every conditioning choice is a balance between these competing pressures, and oncologists make it patient by patient, disease by disease.

That is the wiping of the slate. Now let's talk about what gets put back on the slate — the stem cell source.

## SEGMENT 8 — Where the stem cells come from: marrow, blood, or cord

The label "bone marrow transplant" is, in 2026, often a misnomer. Most transplants today are not bone marrow transplants in the literal sense. The cells are no longer typically harvested from the bone marrow itself. Over the last thirty years, the field has shifted to using stem cells collected from one of three sources, each with its own properties, advantages, and tradeoffs. Let me walk through them.

\[Pause.\]

**Source one: bone marrow harvest.** This is the original method, dating back to the Thomas era. The donor goes to the operating room under general anesthesia. A surgeon uses a large-bore needle to repeatedly aspirate marrow from the posterior iliac crests — the back of the hip bones — often making fifty to a hundred separate punctures to collect approximately one liter of marrow. The marrow is rich in HSCs, but also rich in mature blood cells and stromal cells; it's a complex mixture. The collection is then processed, filtered, sometimes washed or T-cell-depleted, and infused into the recipient. For the donor, the procedure means a hospital stay, general anesthesia, post-procedure pain and bruising at the harvest sites, and a recovery of a few weeks. It is not a trivial procedure for the donor, but it is well-tolerated by healthy volunteers and serious complications are rare. Bone marrow harvest is still used today, particularly for pediatric recipients and in some haploidentical regimens, but it is no longer the dominant method.

**Source two: peripheral blood stem cells, or PBSC.** This is now the most common source. Here's the trick. Under normal conditions, HSCs are anchored in the bone marrow niches by sticky interactions — chemokine signaling between CXCL12 from the niche and CXCR4 on the stem cells, plus adhesion molecules like VLA-4. But if you give the donor a few days of **G-CSF** — granulocyte colony-stimulating factor, a hormone that drives marrow proliferation — the marrow gets overstuffed, the CXCL12-CXCR4 anchoring breaks down, and HSCs are released into the bloodstream. They are "mobilized" out of their niches into the peripheral circulation. The donor then sits in a chair for four to six hours connected to an **apheresis machine** — a centrifuge that separates the white cell fraction from the blood and returns the rest to the donor. The collected white cells contain enough HSCs to make a transplant dose. No surgery. No general anesthesia. The donor experiences a few days of bone aches from the G-CSF, but recovers within a week.

This is now the most common collection method, used in roughly 75 percent of adult allogeneic transplants and the great majority of autologous transplants. The grafts contain more HSCs and many more T cells than marrow grafts, which means faster engraftment but somewhat higher rates of chronic GVHD. For autologous transplant — including the gene therapy applications — PBSC is essentially universal, because you can mobilize the patient with G-CSF, sometimes augmented with a CXCR4 antagonist called **plerixafor** that further disrupts the niche anchoring, and collect huge numbers of stem cells in one or two apheresis sessions.

**Source three: umbilical cord blood.** The blood remaining in the placenta and umbilical cord after a baby is born is rich in HSCs — these are the stem cells that just finished building the baby's blood and immune system in utero. After the baby's cord is clamped, the residual blood can be collected from the cord and placenta, HLA-typed, and frozen for later use. Cord blood banks have stored hundreds of thousands of units worldwide, both public — donated and available to any matched patient — and private, family-banked at the parents' expense for potential future use by the child or a sibling.

Cord blood as a transplant source was pioneered by Eliane Gluckman in Paris in 1988, when the first successful cord blood transplant treated a child with Fanconi anemia using cord blood from his newborn sister. Cord blood has two big advantages and one big disadvantage. **Advantages:** the HSCs are immunologically naive — they haven't yet learned to discriminate self from non-self — which means cord blood transplants tolerate more HLA mismatch than adult donor transplants. A 4/6 cord match can engraft where a 9/10 adult unrelated mismatch would not. This is enormously important for ethnic minority patients underserved by adult registries. And cord blood is available off the shelf — once banked, you can ship a unit overnight, rather than waiting weeks for an adult donor to be worked up. **Disadvantage:** the cell dose is small. A single cord blood unit contains roughly one-tenth the cell dose of an adult marrow or PBSC harvest. This means slower engraftment — sometimes 30 to 40 days versus 14 to 21 — and higher rates of graft failure, especially in adult recipients. Strategies to address this include double cord transplant (using two units together), cord blood expansion ex vivo, and intra-bone delivery.

\[Pause.\]

So the three sources, in summary: **bone marrow** — the original, requires surgical harvest, still used selectively. **Peripheral blood stem cells** — mobilized with G-CSF, collected by apheresis, dominant today. **Cord blood** — banked at birth, smaller dose but more tolerant of mismatch, particularly valuable for minority patients.

Each source produces a graft with somewhat different cellular composition — different ratios of CD34+ stem cells, T cells, NK cells, monocytes — and those differences subtly affect engraftment kinetics, GVHD risk, and graft-versus-tumor effect. It is one of the small art forms of transplant oncology to choose the right source for the right patient with the right disease.

For the gene therapy applications — Casgevy, Zynteglo, and the rest — the source is essentially always autologous mobilized peripheral blood. The patient gets G-CSF and plerixafor, undergoes apheresis to collect billions of CD34+ cells, those cells are shipped to the manufacturing facility, edited with CRISPR or transduced with a lentiviral vector, returned to the hospital, and re-infused after conditioning. The collection step is exactly the same as for autologous transplant in lymphoma. The difference is what happens in between.

## SEGMENT 9 — Engraftment: the long wait

After the conditioning has wiped out the marrow and the new stem cells have been infused, the patient enters the most fragile period of the entire transplant process. The infused cells are circulating in the blood, finding their way through the vasculature, looking for their niches. The recipient has essentially no functional marrow. Blood counts crash. The platelets drop into single digits — normal is 150 to 400 thousand per microliter; transplant patients live at 10 or 20 for weeks. The neutrophil count drops to zero. The patient is, for a window of two to four weeks, profoundly immunocompromised and at high risk of bleeding.

Then, somewhere between day 10 and day 30 after infusion — depending on the source, the cell dose, the conditioning, and the recipient — engraftment happens. The new stem cells have homed to the marrow niches, settled in, and begun to differentiate. The first detectable sign is usually a rising neutrophil count. We define **neutrophil engraftment** as the first of three consecutive days with an absolute neutrophil count above 500 per microliter — ANC > 500. **Platelet engraftment** follows, usually a few days to weeks later, defined as platelets above 20,000 without transfusion support. **Red cell engraftment** comes last, often weeks to months after, since red cells live long enough that the patient lives off transfusions during the early window.

\[Pause.\]

Let me walk through what's actually happening at the cellular level, because the biology is beautiful.

When you infuse the donor cells into the vein, they don't just sit in the bloodstream. They have somewhere to go. **CXCL12**, the chemokine produced by the bone marrow stroma, leaks out into the bloodstream at low levels. The infused HSCs express **CXCR4**, the CXCL12 receptor, on their surface. As they circulate, they sense the CXCL12 gradient and follow it back toward its source — the bone marrow. When the cells reach marrow capillaries, they bind to adhesion molecules on the endothelial cells — selectins, integrins like VLA-4 and LFA-1 — and squeeze through the vessel walls into the marrow space. This is called **transendothelial migration**, and it's mechanistically similar to how immune cells extravasate at sites of inflammation. Once in the marrow, the HSCs sample the local environment and find their niche — guided by additional signals from the stromal cells, the endothelium, and even from osteoblasts at the bone-marrow interface.

The whole process, from infusion to niche occupancy, takes hours to a few days. The cells then begin to divide and differentiate. The first daughter cells become committed progenitors, then mature blood cells, which take days to weeks to develop and exit into the bloodstream. The kinetics depend on cell dose, conditioning intensity, and the source. Peripheral blood stem cell grafts engraft faster than marrow, typically reaching ANC > 500 by day 12 to 14. Marrow takes 18 to 21 days. Cord blood is slowest, often 25 to 35 days, and graft failure is a real risk during the long window.

\[Pause.\]

This engraftment gap — the two to four weeks during which the patient has no marrow function — is when most of the major early complications happen. Bacterial infections from gut translocation (because the mucositis has stripped the gut barrier). Fungal infections like aspergillosis. Viral reactivations — CMV, EBV, HHV-6, BK virus. Bleeding into critical organs. Engraftment failure itself. Hepatic veno-occlusive disease (now called sinusoidal obstruction syndrome), a particular liver complication of conditioning that can be fatal.

The clinical infrastructure built around HSCT is essentially built around managing this gap. Patients are admitted to specialized transplant units with HEPA-filtered air, restricted visitor access, strict hand hygiene. They get prophylactic antibiotics — typically a fluoroquinolone — antivirals like acyclovir, antifungals like voriconazole, PCP prophylaxis with TMP-SMX. They get transfusions of platelets and red cells as needed. They get daily blood counts, weekly viral PCRs, frequent fungal antigen testing. Any fever triggers immediate broad-spectrum antibiotics. It is an intensive, expensive, multidisciplinary undertaking, and a major transplant center may have dozens of patients in this fragile window at any given time.

\[Pause.\]

When engraftment happens, it can be dramatic. Patients who have been bedbound and pancytopenic for two weeks suddenly start producing white cells. The fevers — often called "engraftment fevers" — flare and then resolve. Blood counts climb day by day. You can plot the daily ANC and watch the line go from 0 to 100 to 500 to 1000 to 5000 over the course of a week. There is a clinical moment, well known to transplant nurses, when a patient says "I feel better today" for the first time since admission. That moment usually coincides with neutrophil engraftment.

Once engraftment is established, the patient transitions out of the acute phase. They are discharged from the hospital, often after 30 to 60 days of inpatient care. They continue on antimicrobial prophylaxis, immunosuppression (for allogeneic), and close outpatient monitoring. The immune system rebuilds over months to years — T cell reconstitution is particularly slow, often taking a year or more for full function. During this period, the patient remains at elevated risk for opportunistic infections and, for allogeneic recipients, for **graft-versus-host disease**, which is the central topic of the second half of the lecture.

\[Pause.\]

So engraftment is the bridge across the chasm that conditioning created. New cells go in. They home. They occupy. They divide. The blood rebuilds. The patient — if they survive the gap — gets a new blood and immune system. It is one of the most concrete demonstrations in medicine that the body, given the right cells and the right conditions, can rebuild itself.

## SEGMENT 10 — Graft-versus-host disease: the central chronic complication

Welcome back. In the second half of this lecture we turn to the complications and the modern era. And the place to start, after a break, is the single most feared complication of allogeneic HSCT: **graft-versus-host disease**, abbreviated **GVHD**.

GVHD is the inverse of organ rejection. In a kidney transplant, the recipient's immune system attacks the foreign graft. In an allogeneic HSCT, the recipient's immune system has been ablated by conditioning, so the **donor's** immune system — which came with the transplanted stem cells — wakes up in a new body and attacks the **recipient's** tissues. The donor T cells see the recipient's organs, recognize their HLA shapes as foreign, and mount an immune assault. **The graft attacks the host.** That's the disease.

\[Pause.\]

GVHD comes in two clinical phenotypes, defined by timing and pattern.

**Acute GVHD** typically presents in the first 100 days after transplant. It targets three organs: the **skin** (rash, often starting on the palms and soles and spreading), the **gut** (severe diarrhea, abdominal pain, nausea), and the **liver** (cholestatic hepatitis with rising bilirubin). It's graded I through IV based on severity. Grade I is mild skin rash, manageable with topical steroids. Grade IV is multi-organ failure, often fatal. Severe acute GVHD is one of the main causes of treatment-related mortality after allogeneic transplant.

**Chronic GVHD** typically presents later — months to years post-transplant — and is more like an autoimmune disease. It can affect skin (thickening, scleroderma-like changes), eyes (dry eye), mouth (lichen planus-like lesions), lungs (bronchiolitis obliterans), joints, GI tract, liver, and genitourinary tract. Chronic GVHD is the leading cause of late morbidity in transplant survivors. Patients can live for decades with chronic GVHD requiring ongoing immunosuppression, with significant quality-of-life impact.

\[Pause.\]

The standard GVHD prophylaxis is a calcineurin inhibitor — **cyclosporine** or **tacrolimus** — combined with **methotrexate** in the early post-transplant days. This dates from the work of Storb and colleagues in Seattle in the 1980s and has been the backbone of GVHD prevention ever since. Treatment of established GVHD starts with high-dose corticosteroids, and for refractory cases involves drugs like ruxolitinib (a JAK inhibitor approved for steroid-refractory GVHD), ibrutinib, extracorporeal photopheresis, and many others. The therapeutic landscape for GVHD has expanded dramatically in the last decade.

GVHD is the price of allogeneic transplant. Reducing it without losing the **graft-versus-tumor effect** — the silver lining we'll cover next — is the central unsolved problem of the field.

## SEGMENT 11 — Graft-versus-tumor: the silver lining

The same alloreactive donor T cells that cause GVHD also kill residual cancer cells in the recipient. This is the **graft-versus-tumor effect**, or **GVT**, and it is the central therapeutic advantage of allogeneic transplant over autologous.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the evidence. In the 1980s, transplant oncologists noticed that patients who developed mild-to-moderate GVHD after allogeneic transplant for leukemia had **lower relapse rates** than patients with no GVHD. Conversely, when T cells were depleted from donor grafts to reduce GVHD, leukemia relapse rates went up. The conclusion was inescapable: the same donor T cells that attacked the recipient's normal tissues were also attacking and eliminating residual leukemic cells. The immune response was, in a sense, the therapy.

This led to a remarkable clinical maneuver called **donor lymphocyte infusion**, or **DLI**. If an allogeneic transplant patient relapses after engraftment, you can collect additional T cells from the original donor and infuse them into the patient. The donor T cells, recognizing the recipient's leukemia as foreign, attack and often eliminate it. For chronic myeloid leukemia in particular, DLI alone — without further conditioning, without further transplant — can induce molecular remissions in relapsed patients. This is one of the most striking demonstrations of the power of allogeneic immunity in oncology and was, in many ways, the conceptual ancestor of all modern cellular immunotherapy.

\[Pause.\]

So the central tension of allogeneic transplant becomes clear. **GVHD and GVT are mediated by the same cells.** You can't fully suppress GVHD without losing GVT. You can't maximally enhance GVT without unleashing GVHD. The clinical art is balancing the two.

Modern approaches try to separate them. Selective T-cell depletion strategies remove the most alloreactive T cells while preserving anti-tumor T cells. Adoptive transfer of specific virus-specific or tumor-specific donor T cells delivers antitumor benefit without broad alloreactivity. And — closing the conceptual loop — CAR-T therapy can be thought of as an attempt to deliver pure GVT, targeted at specific tumor antigens, without the GVHD that comes from a polyclonal allogeneic immune response.

GVT is one of the deepest reasons allogeneic HSCT remains the standard of care for many high-risk leukemias even in an era of targeted therapies. Nothing else delivers durable immune surveillance of residual disease the way a fully engrafted donor immune system does.

## SEGMENT 12 — Haploidentical transplant: the access revolution

For decades, the central access problem in transplant was HLA matching. If you didn't have a matched sibling (25 percent chance), you went to the registry, where most non-European patients had limited or no options. Cord blood helped but not enough. Then, in the early 2000s, a group at Johns Hopkins led by **Ephraim Fuchs**, **Leo Luznik**, and **Rick Jones** developed a regimen that changed the calculus completely. It's called the **Baltimore platform** for **haploidentical transplant**, and it has opened HSCT to essentially any patient with a living first-degree relative.

\[Pause.\]

A haploidentical donor is half-matched — sharing one HLA haplotype with the recipient. By definition, every parent of a patient is haploidentical, as are all children, and 50 percent of siblings. So **every patient with a living parent, child, or non-matched sibling has a haploidentical donor.** The access problem, in principle, disappears.

The historic obstacle was immunology. Half-matched transplants were associated with catastrophic rates of GVHD and graft rejection — the immunological wars in both directions were brutal. Multiple groups in the 1980s and 1990s tried elaborate T-cell depletion strategies with limited success.

The Baltimore breakthrough was elegantly simple. After infusion of the unmanipulated half-matched graft, on days +3 and +4 post-transplant, the patient is given a **single high dose of cyclophosphamide**. Cyclophosphamide preferentially kills proliferating T cells — and the most rapidly proliferating T cells at that moment are the **alloreactive** ones that have just woken up and started dividing in response to recipient antigen. Selective elimination of the alloreactive subset, in vivo, post-transplant. The non-alloreactive T cells, dividing more slowly, survive. Regulatory T cells, which are particularly resistant to cyclophosphamide, are spared and help establish tolerance.

The result is a haploidentical transplant with rates of severe GVHD comparable to matched unrelated donor transplants. The procedure has been refined over twenty years, has been adopted globally, and has transformed access to allogeneic HSCT. Patients of any ancestry who have any first-degree relative can now be transplanted.

\[Pause.\]

The numbers are striking. In 2005, haploidentical transplants were a tiny fraction of allogeneic procedures globally. By 2020, they were one of the fastest-growing categories, particularly in Asia, Latin America, and among minority populations in the US and Europe. The Baltimore protocol — post-transplant cyclophosphamide, or PTCy — is now also being used with matched and mismatched unrelated donors to reduce GVHD across the board. It is one of the most important clinical innovations in transplant medicine of the last twenty years.

This is what I mean by an access revolution. A procedure that was, for fifty years, structurally limited to patients with matched donors became — through a clever bit of immunology — available to almost everyone. Equity through biology.

## SEGMENT 13 — Cord blood transplant: small dose, easier match

Let me return briefly to **cord blood** as a transplant source, because it has its own remarkable history and a particular role in the modern landscape.

\[Pause.\]

The first cord blood transplant was done in 1988 by Eliane Gluckman in Paris, treating a six-year-old boy with Fanconi anemia using cord blood from his newborn sister. He survived, his bone marrow was reconstituted by his sister's cord cells, and the proof of principle was established: the small amount of blood remaining in a placenta after birth contained enough HSCs to rebuild a child's hematopoietic system.

This launched an entire industry of **cord blood banking**. Public cord banks — funded by governments, foundations, and the Be The Match registry — accept donated cord blood units, HLA-type them, store them in liquid nitrogen, and make them available to any matched patient worldwide. Private cord banks store units at parents' expense for potential future use by the family. There are now hundreds of thousands of cord blood units banked globally.

The key clinical property of cord blood is **immunological naivety**. The T cells in a cord blood unit have never seen any antigen — they haven't been educated yet. This means they're less likely to mount a severe GVHD response and, importantly, they tolerate higher degrees of HLA mismatch than adult donor cells do. A cord blood unit matched at 4 out of 6 HLA loci can engraft successfully, whereas an adult unrelated donor at the same match level would be far too risky.

This makes cord blood especially valuable for **ethnic minority patients** underrepresented in adult registries, and for patients of mixed ancestry who genuinely have no matched adult donor in the world. Cord blood is also available off the shelf — once banked, you can ship it overnight rather than waiting weeks for an adult donor workup.

\[Pause.\]

The limitation is dose. A single cord unit contains roughly one-tenth the HSCs of an adult marrow or PBSC harvest. For pediatric recipients this is plenty; for adults it can be marginal. Engraftment is slower — sometimes 30 to 40 days — and graft failure rates are higher. Strategies to address the dose problem include **double cord transplants** (using two units), **ex vivo expansion** of cord HSCs with molecules like StemRegenin-1 or UM171, and **intra-bone delivery** to place the cells directly in the marrow. The recently approved cord blood product **Omisirge** is an ex-vivo-expanded cord unit designed to engraft faster than conventional cord.

Cord blood as a transplant source has receded somewhat as haploidentical transplant has expanded — when every patient can find a family donor, the unique access value of cord blood narrows. But cord blood remains essential for patients with no family donor and is increasingly important as a source for off-the-shelf cellular therapy products being developed for cancer and autoimmune disease.

## SEGMENT 14 — Modern conditioning: antibodies replacing chemo

I told you in Segment 7 that the dream of the field, for fifty years, has been to find a way to empty the marrow niches without the toxicity of chemotherapy and radiation. We are, finally, beginning to approach that dream. The technology is **antibody-based conditioning**, and it represents one of the most exciting frontiers in transplant medicine.

\[Pause.\]

The concept is simple. Instead of using systemic chemotherapy or radiation to kill bone marrow cells nonspecifically, you use a **monoclonal antibody** that binds specifically to a protein expressed on hematopoietic stem cells, and use that antibody to selectively eliminate the HSCs from their niches. The antibody might be naked (relying on immune effector mechanisms to kill the targeted cells), or — more commonly in modern designs — it can be coupled to a cytotoxic payload, making it an **antibody-drug conjugate** or ADC.

The two main targets being pursued are **CD117** and **CD45**. CD117, also called c-Kit, is the receptor for stem cell factor and is highly expressed on hematopoietic stem cells. An anti-CD117 antibody, alone or as an ADC, can selectively deplete HSCs from the marrow without the systemic toxicity of busulfan or melphalan. CD45 is a pan-leukocyte marker, broader in its targeting, useful for combined depletion of HSCs and immune cells.

The leading company in this space has been **Magenta Therapeutics**, which developed anti-CD117 ADCs for transplant conditioning. Although Magenta itself ran into financial difficulties, the concept has been picked up by multiple groups including Jasper Therapeutics, with anti-CD117 antibodies in clinical trials for conditioning ahead of HSCT — including for the gene therapy applications, where avoiding busulfan would be a major safety improvement, especially for pediatric sickle cell patients.

\[Pause.\]

Why does this matter so much? Because the conditioning toxicity is, in many cases, the biggest barrier to wider use of HSCT and HSCT-based gene therapies. The kid with sickle cell who could be cured by Casgevy in principle faces a multi-month hospital stay, busulfan-induced infertility, and real treatment-related mortality risk. Replace the busulfan with an outpatient infusion of an antibody-drug conjugate, and the procedure becomes dramatically safer. This is the path to expanding gene therapy access from a few hundred patients per year to potentially tens of thousands.

Antibody-based conditioning is still mostly experimental in 2026, but it is the clearest near-term opportunity to remake the entire HSCT field. Watch this space.

## SEGMENT 15 — HSCT for sickle cell: the original cure

Now let's tell the story that brings all of this together. Sickle cell disease is one of the oldest known molecular diseases — Linus Pauling described its molecular basis in 1949 as the prototype of "molecular disease." It is caused by a single-base mutation in the beta-globin gene that produces hemoglobin S, which polymerizes when deoxygenated, deforming red blood cells into sickle shapes. The sickled cells clog small blood vessels, causing the agonizing vaso-occlusive crises, organ damage, strokes, and premature death that define the disease.

For decades, sickle cell disease had only supportive treatments: transfusions, hydroxyurea, pain management. Then in the 1980s, transplant pioneers began trying allogeneic HSCT — matched sibling donor transplants for selected pediatric patients — and found that **transplant could cure sickle cell**. The donor stem cells engrafted, produced normal (non-sickling) hemoglobin, and the disease was eliminated. Cure rates approached 85 to 90 percent for matched sibling pediatric transplants.

\[Pause.\]

But the catch was access. Only about 20 percent of sickle cell patients have an HLA-matched sibling. The disease is most common in patients of African ancestry, who are dramatically underrepresented in adult donor registries. So the cure existed, but most patients couldn't access it. Even those who could face significant GVHD risk and treatment-related mortality of 5 to 10 percent.

The famous case was the **Berlin patient** — Timothy Brown — who was cured of HIV in 2007 by an allogeneic HSCT from a donor with a CCR5 mutation, and who incidentally was also cured of any underlying blood condition by the transplant. But the broader picture for sickle cell was that allogeneic transplant was a powerful but limited tool.

What changed everything was the realization that you could, instead of finding a matched donor, **edit the patient's own stem cells**. Use autologous HSCT — no donor matching, no GVHD — but engineer the patient's HSCs to overcome the genetic defect. That's the foundation of the modern gene therapy era for sickle cell, which we'll cover next.

The historical lesson: HSCT was the original cure for sickle cell, it worked, it had access barriers, and those barriers are what gene therapy is solving — by replacing the donor with the patient's own edited cells.

## SEGMENT 16 — Gene therapy on HSCs: Casgevy, Zynteglo, Skysona, Lyfgenia

In December 2023, the FDA approved two gene therapies for sickle cell disease on the same day. **Casgevy**, from Vertex and CRISPR Therapeutics, is the first CRISPR-based therapy ever approved. **Lyfgenia**, from bluebird bio, is a lentiviral gene therapy. Both work by editing the patient's own hematopoietic stem cells. Both run on transplant infrastructure.

\[Pause.\]

Let me describe **Casgevy**, because it's the cleanest example. The patient is mobilized with G-CSF and plerixafor. CD34+ cells are collected by apheresis. The cells are shipped to a manufacturing facility where they're electroporated with a CRISPR-Cas9 ribonucleoprotein complex targeting the **BCL11A enhancer** — a regulatory element that normally suppresses fetal hemoglobin in adults. Editing this enhancer reactivates fetal hemoglobin production, which doesn't sickle and substitutes for the defective adult hemoglobin. The edited cells are expanded, quality-controlled, and shipped back. The patient receives myeloablative busulfan conditioning, and the edited cells are infused. They engraft, rebuild the marrow, and produce red cells with high levels of fetal hemoglobin. The disease is functionally cured.

The clinical results have been dramatic. In the pivotal trials, the great majority of patients had no vaso-occlusive crises in the year following treatment. Some patients have now been followed for five-plus years with sustained benefit. It is, in essence, autologous HSCT plus a CRISPR step.

**Zynteglo** (betibeglogene autotemcel), from bluebird, treats transfusion-dependent beta-thalassemia by using a lentiviral vector to add a functional beta-globin gene to the patient's HSCs. Same infrastructure — mobilize, collect, transduce, condition, reinfuse. Approved 2022.

**Skysona** (elivaldogene autotemcel), also from bluebird, treats cerebral adrenoleukodystrophy in boys by lentiviral addition of the ABCD1 gene to autologous HSCs. The transduced myeloid cells cross into the brain and deliver functional protein. Approved 2022.

**Lyfgenia** is bluebird's lentiviral gene therapy for sickle cell, approved 2023, adding a modified beta-globin (T87Q) that doesn't sickle.

**Strimvelis**, from GSK and originally San Raffaele in Milan, is a gamma-retroviral gene therapy for ADA-SCID, approved in Europe in 2016. Same paradigm: autologous HSCs, gene-corrected, reinfused.

\[Pause.\]

Each of these is HSCT with a gene-editing or gene-addition step. The conditioning, the harvest, the engraftment monitoring, the inpatient stay — all of that is transplant. The gene therapy is the payload. The transplant is the chassis.

## SEGMENT 17 — The HSCT-gene-therapy convergence

I want to step back and look at the structural picture, because the convergence between HSCT and gene therapy is one of the defining patterns of modern medicine, and it is hiding in plain sight.

\[Pause.\]

Every approved gene therapy for a blood disease — Casgevy, Zynteglo, Skysona, Lyfgenia, Strimvelis — is autologous HSCT plus an editing or transduction step. Every approved CAR-T therapy — Kymriah, Yescarta, Tecartus, Breyanzi, Abecma, Carvykti — is autologous lymphocyte therapy that uses the same apheresis, manufacturing, and infusion infrastructure built for transplant. The new categories of cellular medicine are not separate from HSCT. They are HSCT with new payloads.

This matters for hospitals, for regulators, for patients, for economics. The institutions equipped to deliver HSCT — transplant centers with apheresis units, cell processing facilities, isolation wards, conditioning protocols, and long-term follow-up infrastructure — are the same institutions equipped to deliver gene therapy and CAR-T. The regulatory framework — the FDA's Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research, the BLA pathway for cellular products — is the same. The training of clinicians, nurses, pharmacists is shared. The reimbursement battles are similar.

\[Pause.\]

What this convergence means, in practice, is that the HSCT infrastructure is the **delivery system** for a whole emerging class of medicine. If you want a cell-based therapy for a disease, you ask: can you collect the right cells, modify them appropriately, condition the patient to accept them, and infuse them safely? The answer is increasingly yes for blood, immune system, and lymphoid disease. It is increasingly being attempted for autoimmune disease, for muscular dystrophy, for inherited liver disease. The chassis is being adapted to new payloads at a rapid rate.

The 2020s and 2030s will be the decade in which this convergence plays out. The number of gene therapies running on HSCT infrastructure will grow. The conditioning technology will improve. The cost will, eventually, come down — although not soon. The access will expand. The procedure that E. Donnall Thomas pioneered in Seattle in the 1960s, originally for refractory leukemia, has become the foundation for an entire generation of cures.

## SEGMENT 18 — Frontier: where this is going

We'll close with a look at the frontier. Five threads worth watching.

\[Pause.\]

**One: antibody-based conditioning replacing busulfan.** I covered this in Segment 14 — anti-CD117 ADCs, anti-CD45 antibodies — that can deplete HSCs from their niches without the systemic toxicity of chemotherapy. Outpatient conditioning, no infertility, no mucositis, dramatically lower treatment-related mortality. This would transform every application of HSCT, especially pediatric gene therapy.

**Two: CAR-HSC for in vivo CAR-T generation.** Instead of harvesting T cells, editing them, and reinfusing — what if you could edit a patient's HSCs to produce CAR-T cells continuously, for the rest of their life? The HSCs would differentiate into T cells expressing the CAR, providing durable immunosurveillance. Multiple groups are exploring this. The combination of HSC editing and CAR design represents a deep integration of two cellular medicines.

**Three: AAV6 and other vectors for in situ HSC editing.** Modern gene therapy uses ex vivo editing — pull the cells out, edit them, put them back. The next step is **in vivo editing** — deliver an editing reagent directly to HSCs in the patient's body, no harvest required. AAV6 has shown some HSC tropism. Lipid nanoparticles targeting HSCs are in development. If this works, it eliminates the manufacturing burden of cellular therapy and dramatically reduces cost.

**Four: the economics of sickle cell cure.** Casgevy and Lyfgenia are priced at over $2 million per patient. The US has approximately 100,000 sickle cell patients. The arithmetic — $200 billion to cure the entire US sickle cell population — is genuinely staggering. The combination of antibody-based conditioning, manufacturing automation, and eventual in vivo editing has to bring this cost down by an order of magnitude or two for these cures to be deliverable at population scale. This is, ultimately, an engineering problem; it is also a policy problem.

**Five: the convergence with other cellular therapies.** Allogeneic universal donor HSCs, gene-edited to evade host immunity. Off-the-shelf cellular products built on cord blood or iPSC-derived HSCs. Combinations of HSCT with checkpoint inhibitors, with BiTEs, with TIL therapy. The boundaries between transplant, gene therapy, and immunotherapy are dissolving. The chassis stays. The payloads multiply.

\[Pause.\]

So let me leave you with this. Hematopoietic stem cell transplant is one of the oldest, most consequential, most underrated procedures in modern medicine. It cured the first leukemia patients. It established the concept that you can take cells from one person and rebuild a vital organ system in another. It demonstrated for the first time that immunology — graft-versus-tumor — could be harnessed as a cancer therapy. It built the regulatory, clinical, and manufacturing infrastructure on which CAR-T, gene therapy, and the next generation of cellular medicines run. E. Donnall Thomas and his colleagues built a chassis. We are still, sixty years later, putting new things on it.

That's where we'll stop. Thanks for your attention. Next week we'll pick up with gene therapy in solid organs — the AAV revolution, the eye, the muscle, the brain — and we'll see how the HSCT model translates, and how it doesn't, to tissues that don't have a built-in homing mechanism.

\[End of lecture.\]

## APPENDIX — Quick-reference summary (not for reading aloud)

**Core idea**: Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) — wipe out a patient's bone marrow, infuse new hematopoietic stem cells (donor or own, edited or unedited), let them home, engraft, and rebuild the entire blood and immune system. The chassis for modern cellular medicine.

**The HSC**: hematopoietic stem cell. Self-renewing, multipotent, lives in marrow niches, expresses CXCR4 to follow CXCL12 chemokine gradient home from the bloodstream. Roughly 10-20K long-term HSCs per adult; produces ~100B blood cells/day downstream.

**Founder**: E. Donnall Thomas — first attempts 1956 (Cooperstown), first successful HLA-matched allogeneic BMT 1968-69 (Seattle/Fred Hutchinson); Nobel Prize 1990 (shared with Joseph Murray for solid organ transplant).

**HLA matching**: MHC class I (HLA-A/B/C, all cells) and class II (HLA-DR/DQ/DP, immune cells). Typically 10/10 matching across A, B, C, DRB1, DQB1. Sibling: 25% chance of match. Registry (Be The Match): ~75% for European ancestry, <25% for African/mixed. Inequity is the central access problem.

**Allogeneic vs autologous vs syngeneic**:
- **Allogeneic** — donor cells. Used for leukemias, aplastic anemia, SCID, sickle cell. Risks: GVHD, graft failure, opportunistic infection. TRM ~10-30% at 1 year.
- **Autologous** — patient's own. Used for myeloma, lymphoma (high-dose chemo rescue) and for gene therapy. TRM 1-3%. No GVHD.
- **Syngeneic** — identical twin. Rare.

**Indications**:
- Leukemias (AML, ALL, CML pre-imatinib, CLL pre-BTKi)
- Lymphomas (NHL, Hodgkin) — autologous standard for relapsed
- Multiple myeloma — autologous standard
- Severe aplastic anemia — allo cure
- SCID and other primary immunodeficiencies
- Sickle cell, beta-thalassemia (allo or gene therapy)
- Storage diseases (ALD, MPS) — gene therapy delivery
- Autoimmune (SSc, MS, IBD) — experimental "immune reset"

**Conditioning**:
- **Myeloablative** — busulfan + cyclophosphamide (Bu/Cy); Cy + TBI. Full destruction, more toxicity.
- **Reduced-intensity (RIC)** — fludarabine + melphalan (Flu/Mel); fludarabine + low-dose busulfan. Relies on graft-vs-tumor.
- **Non-myeloablative** — minimal chemo, max immunosuppression.
- For autologous: single high-dose agent (melphalan for myeloma, BEAM for lymphoma, busulfan for gene therapy).

**Stem cell sources**:
- **Bone marrow harvest** — OR under GA, posterior iliac crests, ~1 L collected. Original method, still used selectively.
- **Peripheral blood (PBSC)** — G-CSF mobilization ± plerixafor, apheresis. Dominant source today. Faster engraftment, higher chronic GVHD.
- **Cord blood** — banked at birth (Gluckman 1988). Naive cells tolerate HLA mismatch (4/6 acceptable), but small dose, slower engraftment, higher graft failure. Critical for minority patients.

**Engraftment**:
- Homing via CXCR4-CXCL12, transendothelial migration into marrow.
- ANC > 500 (neutrophil engraftment): PBSC ~day 12-14, marrow ~day 18-21, cord ~day 25-35.
- Platelet engraftment > 20K, then red cells. Full immune reconstitution takes months to years.
- Gap period: profound pancytopenia, isolation, prophylactic antimicrobials, transfusion support.

**GVHD**:
- Donor T cells attack recipient tissues.
- **Acute** (<100 days) — skin (rash), gut (diarrhea), liver (cholestasis). Grades I-IV.
- **Chronic** (>100 days, often months-years) — skin scleroderma, dry eye, mouth lesions, lung BO, etc.
- Prophylaxis: tacrolimus/cyclosporine + methotrexate (Storb regimen). PTCy increasingly used.
- Treatment: steroids first; ruxolitinib for steroid-refractory; ECP, ibrutinib, etc.

**Graft-versus-tumor (GVT)**:
- Same alloreactive T cells that cause GVHD also kill residual cancer.
- T-cell-depleted grafts → less GVHD, more relapse.
- **Donor lymphocyte infusion (DLI)** exploits GVT directly for relapse post-transplant; classic for CML.
- Central therapeutic value of allogeneic HSCT; the conceptual ancestor of modern immunotherapy.

**Haploidentical transplant (Baltimore platform)**:
- Half-matched family donor (parent, child, half-matched sibling).
- Fuchs, Luznik, Jones at Johns Hopkins, early 2000s.
- **Post-transplant cyclophosphamide (PTCy)** on days +3/+4 selectively kills proliferating alloreactive donor T cells.
- Transformed access — every patient with a first-degree relative has a donor.
- Now also applied to matched/mismatched unrelated donor transplant for GVHD reduction.

**Modern conditioning frontier**:
- **Anti-CD117 ADCs** (Magenta, Jasper) — selective HSC depletion without busulfan.
- **Anti-CD45 antibodies** — broader leukocyte depletion.
- Goal: outpatient conditioning, no infertility, dramatically lower TRM.
- Key enabler for pediatric gene therapy and population-scale sickle cell cure.

**HSCT for sickle cell**:
- Curative since 1980s with matched sibling allogeneic HSCT (~85% cure).
- Access barrier: only ~20% have matched sibling, registry coverage poor for African ancestry.
- Berlin patient (Timothy Brown, 2007) — HIV cure via CCR5-mutant allo HSCT.
- Solved by autologous gene therapy.

**Gene therapy on HSCs (all BLA cellular therapies)**:
- **Casgevy** (Vertex/CRISPR, 2023) — CRISPR KO of BCL11A enhancer → fetal hemoglobin reactivation. Sickle cell, beta-thal.
- **Lyfgenia** (bluebird, 2023) — lentiviral T87Q anti-sickling beta-globin. Sickle cell.
- **Zynteglo** (bluebird, 2022) — lentiviral functional beta-globin. Transfusion-dependent beta-thal.
- **Skysona** (bluebird, 2022) — lentiviral ABCD1. Cerebral adrenoleukodystrophy.
- **Strimvelis** (GSK/San Raffaele, EU 2016) — gamma-retroviral ADA. ADA-SCID.
- All: mobilize CD34+, edit/transduce ex vivo, myeloablative busulfan, reinfuse.

**HSCT-gene therapy convergence**:
- Same infrastructure: apheresis, GMP manufacturing, conditioning, infusion, engraftment monitoring.
- Same regulatory pathway: FDA/CBER BLA for cellular therapy.
- Same clinicians, same wards, same workflows.
- Gene therapy = HSCT + editing step.
- CAR-T = autologous lymphocyte therapy on transplant rails.

**Frontier**:
- Antibody-based conditioning (CD117 ADCs) replacing busulfan.
- CAR-HSC for in vivo, durable CAR-T generation.
- In vivo HSC editing via AAV6 or LNP, eliminating ex vivo manufacturing.
- Cost reduction toward population-scale sickle cell cure (~$2M → manageable).
- Allogeneic universal donor HSCs, off-the-shelf cellular products.

**Two organizing principles**: (1) the hematopoietic stem cell's unique combination of self-renewal, multipotency, and chemokine-driven homing makes IV infusion of stem cells a workable transplant strategy — almost uniquely among adult tissues; (2) HSCT is the chassis, and modern cellular medicine (gene therapy, CAR-T) is a series of new payloads built on that chassis. Understand the chassis, and the new payloads make sense as variations.
