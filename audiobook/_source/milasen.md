## SEGMENT 1 — Why this case matters — the central question of "can we build a medicine for one person?"

Alright. Let's begin. Today, for the next two hours, we are going to walk through a single medical case. One patient. One drug. One paper. And by the end of those two hours, I want you to understand why this particular case — published in the New England Journal of Medicine in October of 2019, the lead authors Kim, Hu, and Moufawad El Achkar, working out of Boston Children's Hospital under Tim Yu — why this single case may turn out to be one of the most important medical papers of the early twenty-first century, even though it concerns a sample size of exactly one human being.

The patient's name was Mila Makovec. She was six years old when she received her first dose of the drug that was made for her. The drug was called **milasen** — m-i-l-a-s-e-n — and the name is exactly what it sounds like. It is named after her. It will never be given to anyone else. It was designed, manufactured, tested in animals, cleared by the FDA, and infused into a child's spinal fluid in less than one calendar year. And there has never, in the history of medicine, been a drug like that before.

\[Pause.\]

I want to start with the question the paper implicitly asks, because if you carry one question with you through the whole lecture, this is the one. **Can we build a medicine for one person?**

That sounds like a strange question. Of course we build medicines. Pharmacies are full of them. But every drug you have ever taken — every aspirin, every antibiotic, every insulin shot, every chemotherapy infusion — was built for a population. A drug company spent ten or fifteen years and a billion dollars or more developing it, ran it through three phases of clinical trials with thousands of subjects, got it approved by a regulator that demands evidence of efficacy across many patients, and then sold it to the millions of people who happen to share whatever disease it treats. The economics, the ethics, the regulatory machinery, the manufacturing — all of it is designed for the many. Not the one.

Now think about what that means for someone with a truly rare disease. If you have a disease that only ten thousand people in the world share, the math of drug development still kind of works — barely — because ten thousand people times a high price per dose can still pay back the development cost. But what if you have a disease that only a hundred people share? Or ten? Or — and this is Mila's case — exactly one? Your specific mutation has been seen, in the entire history of medical literature, in you and in no one else. The economic engine that built every drug on every pharmacy shelf does not work for you. It cannot work for you. There is no market. There is no patient population. There is no commercial drug company that will ever invest the money to make your medicine, because they will sell, at most, one dose to one patient and then never again.

For most of medical history, that meant such patients were simply out of luck. We knew their diagnosis, we knew the molecular cause of their disease, we even sometimes had a theoretical idea of what kind of drug would fix it — and we couldn't do anything for them, because the system wasn't built to treat a single person.

The milasen paper is the first published proof that this is no longer necessarily true. For a certain class of mutations — a certain class of molecular problems — we now have a technology platform flexible enough, fast enough, and cheap enough to design and deliver a brand-new medication for an individual patient. Not in fifteen years. In one. Not for a billion dollars. For a few million. Not after thousands of clinical trial subjects. After one. And the paper we are studying today is the case report that proved it could be done.

\[Pause.\]

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, sketch on a napkin what an antisense oligonucleotide is and explain in plain English how a splice-switching ASO works; two, walk through Mila's diagnostic odyssey and explain why standard genetic testing missed half of her disease and what kind of testing finally caught it; three, articulate honestly what the milasen treatment did for her, what it didn't do, and what kinds of patients in the future this approach can and cannot help — then we have succeeded. Everything else hangs off those three.

There is one more thing I want to say before we go further, and I want to say it now because it shapes the tone of everything we will discuss for the next two hours. Mila Makovec died on February 18, 2021, at the age of ten. The paper we are studying was published in October of 2019. The drug we will spend most of this lecture admiring — its molecular cleverness, the speed of its development, the elegance of its mechanism — slowed her seizures and stabilized parts of her clinical picture, but it did not, in the end, save her life. Her brain continued to atrophy. Her disease continued to progress, more slowly than it would have without the drug, but it progressed. And the day came when the slowing wasn't enough.

I tell you this now because I do not want you to leave this lecture with a feeling that medicine has solved the problem of rare disease. It hasn't. What this paper proved is that we have, for the first time, the technical capacity to try. That is not the same as a cure. It is, however, an extraordinary new beginning, and the rest of this lecture is the story of how that beginning happened, what it required, and what it teaches us about the future of medicine for the rarest patients on Earth.

## SEGMENT 2 — Meet Mila — the clinical story of a healthy child becoming sick

So let me introduce you to the patient. Because before this becomes a story about molecules and splicing and oligonucleotides, it is a story about a child.

Mila Makovec was born healthy. By every account, by every measure her pediatrician had, she developed normally through the first three years of her life. She walked on time. She talked on time. She was, to use the phrase that appears over and over in case reports of children with late-onset neurodegenerative diseases, "previously normal." Photographs from her early childhood look like every other child's early childhood. There was no warning.

At about age three, the first thing went wrong. Her left foot started turning inward when she walked. A small thing. The kind of thing a parent notices, a pediatrician examines, an orthopedist looks at, and most of the time it turns out to be nothing — a tight muscle, a developing arch, a minor orthopedic quirk that resolves on its own. Sometimes it's the first sign that something is happening inside the developing nervous system, but you usually can't tell which one of those it is until time passes and you see whether other things start going wrong.

In Mila's case, other things did start going wrong.

\[Pause.\]

Over the next two years — between ages three and five — Mila's clinical picture changed in ways that her family and her physicians watched with growing alarm. Her vision began to deteriorate. Not gradually-needing-glasses deterioration. Real, progressive vision loss, the kind where a child who could see her parents' faces last year cannot see them this year. She started bumping into furniture. She would walk past toys on the floor she'd loved last week. Examinations of the back of her eye — the retina — showed degeneration of the light-sensing cells.

Then her language began to regress. This is one of the most chilling things in pediatric neurology — when a child who has been speaking, sometimes speaking quite well, begins to lose words. Not the normal "I forgot how to say it" of a small child reaching for vocabulary they don't yet have, but the heartbreaking reverse — words she used to use, freely and correctly, simply stopped coming out of her mouth. The vocabulary shrank. Sentences shortened. Eventually they almost stopped altogether.

She became clumsy. Her movements lost their precision. She had difficulty with the fine motor tasks she had previously been able to do. And she began to have seizures.

By the time she was six years old — the age at which the milasen treatment began, and the age at which most of the clinical description in the paper was written — here is what Mila's life looked like, day to day. She was blind. She had what we call ataxia, which means her movements were uncoordinated; she could not walk without support. She was having approximately thirty seizures per day. Thirty. Per day. We will come back to that number, because the eventual measurement of how the drug affected those seizures is the cleanest piece of evidence in the entire paper that milasen was doing something real.

She had developed dysphagia — that's the medical term for difficulty swallowing — to the point that she required a gastrostomy tube. A G-tube. A surgically placed feeding tube that delivers food directly into her stomach because she could no longer safely eat by mouth without the risk of aspirating food into her lungs. She was minimally verbal. She was alert — and this is an important and painful clinical detail — she was alert. The lights were on. She was, by every neurological sign her doctors could measure, aware of the people around her, present in the room, recognizable as the same child her parents had always loved. She just could no longer move or see or speak the way she used to.

An MRI scan of her brain showed atrophy. Both the cerebrum — the big folded outer part of the brain that does most of what we think of as conscious thought, sensation, and motor planning — and the cerebellum, the smaller structure in the back that handles coordination and balance, had shrunken visibly compared to what a normal six-year-old's brain looks like. The brain was, literally, losing tissue.

\[Pause.\]

The doctors at Boston Children's took a tiny biopsy of her skin. Why skin? Because skin is easy to access and skin cells contain the same machinery — the same lysosomes, the same enzymes — as cells throughout the rest of the body. They put the skin cells under an electron microscope and looked at the lysosomes, which are tiny compartments inside cells that we'll discuss in detail in the next segment. And they saw something diagnostic.

Inside the lysosomes were dense, layered deposits arranged in patterns that pathologists describe with a vivid, almost poetic word: **fingerprints**. Curvilinear, whorled deposits that look, under the electron microscope, like the swirls of a human fingerprint pressed into the cell. These are not normal. They appear in a specific family of diseases collectively called **neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis** — a mouthful, also called by the older eponymous name **Batten disease** — in which cellular waste material that should be broken down and recycled by the lysosomes instead accumulates and accumulates until the cell, eventually, dies. The fingerprint inclusions are diagnostic for one particular subtype of Batten disease, the form caused by problems in a gene called CLN7.

So now her doctors knew, on clinical grounds, what category of disease they were looking at. Neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis, CLN7 type. Batten disease. A relentless, fatal, childhood neurodegenerative disorder. The next step would be genetic confirmation — finding the actual DNA mutations responsible. And that, as we will see, is where the case got strange. And eventually, where it got remarkable.

## SEGMENT 3 — Batten disease — the lysosomal storage disorder family, NCL forms, what CLN7 normally does

Let me back up and zoom out for a moment, because to understand what is wrong in Mila's cells, you have to understand a part of cellular biology that I suspect you have not yet encountered in much depth: the lysosome.

I want you to picture a cell — any cell, doesn't really matter which kind, but let's say a neuron, since that's the cell type that matters here. Inside the cell, in addition to all the parts you've heard about — nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes — there is a population of small membrane-bound compartments called **lysosomes**. They are roughly spherical, they are filled with about sixty different digestive enzymes, and their interior is highly acidic — about pH 4.5, similar to lemon juice. Their job is, very simply, to break things down. Anything in the cell that needs to be recycled — old proteins, damaged organelles, bacteria the cell has engulfed, debris from worn-out membranes — gets delivered into the lysosome, where the acidic environment and the digestive enzymes chew it apart into raw building blocks that can be reused.

Here is the analogy I want you to keep. **The lysosome is the cell's recycling center.** Every city needs one. If the recycling center stops working, garbage starts accumulating in the streets, and pretty quickly the city becomes unlivable. Inside cells, the consequence of a broken lysosome is exactly the same. Cellular waste piles up. The cell becomes congested with material it cannot dispose of. And in the cells that are most sensitive to that congestion — and as it turns out, neurons are among the most sensitive cells in the body to lysosomal dysfunction — the cells start to die.

\[Pause.\]

A whole family of human genetic diseases, collectively called **lysosomal storage disorders**, are caused by inherited defects in one or another of the proteins required for lysosomes to function properly. There are dozens of them. Tay-Sachs disease is one. Gaucher disease is another. Niemann-Pick. Pompe. Hurler. Mucopolysaccharidoses. Some are mild. Many are devastating. Most begin in childhood. Many are fatal. Each one is caused by a defect in a single gene encoding a single enzyme or transporter or membrane protein critical for lysosomal function, and the specific clinical picture depends on which protein is broken and which cell types depend most heavily on it.

One subgroup of lysosomal storage disorders is called the **neuronal ceroid lipofuscinoses**, or NCLs. The name comes from two words. "Ceroid" and "lipofuscin" are both old histological terms for the kind of waxy, yellowish-brown pigment that accumulates inside cells that are failing to clear their own waste. So a "neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis" is, literally, a disease in which neurons accumulate that waxy pigment. It is also called, in older literature and still informally, **Batten disease**, after a British physician named Frederick Batten who described one form of it in 1903.

There are at least thirteen genetically distinct forms of NCL. They are numbered CLN1 through CLN14, with one number skipped. Each one is caused by mutations in a different gene — CLN1, CLN2, CLN3, and so on — and each one has slightly different clinical features, slightly different age of onset, slightly different rate of progression. But the common thread is the same: a defect in some part of the lysosomal machinery, leading to accumulation of those characteristic waxy deposits, leading to progressive neurological decline, leading, in most cases, to early death.

\[Pause.\]

Mila has the CLN7 form. CLN7 is caused by mutations in a gene called **MFSD8** — that stands for "major facilitator superfamily domain-containing 8" — and the gene name tells you something. The major facilitator superfamily is a big family of membrane proteins that move small molecules across membranes. So MFSD8 is, structurally, a transporter. It sits in the lysosomal membrane. Its precise substrate — what it actually transports — was, at the time of the paper, not fully known. Honestly, it is still incompletely understood. But its function clearly matters, because losing it causes a particularly aggressive form of NCL: typical age of onset around two to seven years, with progressive vision loss, language regression, seizures, ataxia, and death usually before adolescence ends. That clinical course exactly matches what was happening to Mila.

Now — here is the genetic detail that will become essential to the rest of the story. The CLN7 form of Batten disease, like almost all the NCLs, is **autosomal recessive**. That means you need two broken copies of the MFSD8 gene to get the disease — one inherited from each of your parents. A person with only one broken copy is a **carrier** — clinically normal, because their one good copy makes enough of the transporter to do the job, but able to pass the broken version to their children. If two carriers have a child together, there is a one-in-four chance, with each pregnancy, that the child will inherit both broken copies and develop the disease. That's exactly what happened in Mila's case. Both her parents are carriers. Mila inherited a broken copy from each.

But here is where the story turns. The standard, accepted clinical workflow when you suspect Batten disease in a child is to send a blood sample to a genetic testing lab and ask them to sequence the genes known to cause NCL — CLN1 through CLN14 — and identify the specific mutations the patient carries. That's called a **gene panel**. It's the routine, well-validated, insurance-covered way to confirm a clinical diagnosis at the molecular level. And when Mila's family did this — and they did — the panel came back with a finding, but only a partial one. The panel identified one of her two broken copies of MFSD8. The other was missing. Invisible. Not where the panel was looking.

That gap — the missing second mutation — is what set the entire rest of this story in motion. We turn to it next.

## SEGMENT 4 — The diagnostic odyssey, panel testing, and why one mutation was missing

I want to spend this segment on a phenomenon that you will not have heard much about, but that is utterly central to the experience of families dealing with rare genetic disease. It is called the **diagnostic odyssey**.

Here's what a diagnostic odyssey looks like. A child starts showing symptoms — say, the foot turning inward, the early vision changes, the loss of vocabulary I described in Segment 2. The parents take the child to the pediatrician. The pediatrician sees something is wrong, but doesn't know what, and refers the child to a specialist — maybe a neurologist. The neurologist runs some tests, sees something is still wrong, and refers the child to another specialist — maybe a geneticist, maybe a metabolic disease specialist, maybe an ophthalmologist if the vision is involved. Each specialist orders their own tests. Each set of tests takes weeks or months to come back. Some come back normal. Some come back ambiguous. Some come back suggesting a particular disease, which then requires confirmation testing, which takes more weeks or months. Meanwhile the child is getting worse. Meanwhile the family is making round-trip drives to academic medical centers, paying out of pocket for tests insurance won't cover, getting on waiting lists for the next specialist, and absorbing the staggering psychological weight of not knowing what is happening to their child or why.

In the rare-disease community, the typical diagnostic odyssey lasts somewhere between five and ten years. Some families never get a diagnosis at all. Many families see twenty or thirty specialists before someone finally lands on the right test or the right gene. The journey is exhausting, expensive, and — most cruelly — usually does not, even when it ends in a diagnosis, lead to any treatment, because for most rare diseases there is no treatment to offer. The diagnosis is just a name. A label. An explanation. Sometimes that name itself is a kind of relief, because not-knowing is worse than knowing, but the label rarely changes the trajectory of the disease.

\[Pause.\]

Mila's family was, by the standards of the rare-disease community, comparatively lucky in the speed of their diagnosis. The clinical picture — the early-childhood onset, the vision loss, the seizures, the regression — pointed Boston Children's neurologists toward Batten disease relatively quickly. The skin biopsy showing fingerprint inclusions confirmed it. And the gene panel identified, within MFSD8, the gene for the CLN7 subtype, one specific mutation: a single-letter change at position 1102 in the coding sequence, where a G — guanine — had been replaced by a C — cytosine. We write that as **c.1102G>C**. This is a known pathogenic mutation. It has been seen in other CLN7 patients. It alters a single amino acid in the protein and renders that protein non-functional. So one of Mila's two MFSD8 copies was definitively, identifiably broken.

But — and here is the puzzle — the other copy looked normal. The panel sequenced every exon of MFSD8 on the second copy, and every exon was wild-type. Every single one of the protein-coding stretches of the gene, on her second copy, was indistinguishable from a healthy person's. Yet she clearly had the disease. The skin biopsy was unambiguous. The clinical course was textbook. She could not have CLN7 with only one broken copy of MFSD8 — recessive diseases don't work that way. The second mutation had to exist. The panel was simply not finding it.

\[Pause.\]

This is a moment to pause and explain why a gene panel can miss a mutation, because the explanation tells you something deep about how genetic testing actually works in clinical medicine.

Here is the analogy I want you to use. **A gene panel is like checking a list of usual suspects.** You have a crime, you have a list of people who fit the profile, you go down the list, you interview each one in turn, and you see whether any of them confess or have alibis that don't hold up. It's efficient. It's cheap. It works well most of the time because the usual suspects really are usual — most pathogenic mutations in known genes fall in well-characterized parts of those genes, and a panel is designed to look exactly where mutations are commonly found.

But what does a panel actually sequence? Almost all clinical gene panels sequence only the **exons** of the targeted genes — the protein-coding stretches — plus a small amount of the immediately flanking sequence at the boundaries between exons. They do not sequence the **introns** — the non-coding stretches in between exons, which get spliced out during the production of the mature messenger RNA. And they do not sequence anything outside the gene itself — none of the regulatory regions, none of the regions far upstream or downstream, and crucially, none of the intronic interior.

For most patients, that is fine, because most pathogenic mutations really are in the exons. But not all of them. Sometimes a mutation hides somewhere a panel does not look. And when that happens, the panel comes back with a partial answer — like the answer for Mila — and the clinician has no way of telling, just from the panel result, whether there is a second mutation that exists but was missed, or whether the patient really does only have one mutation and therefore must have a non-recessive form of the disease, or some other explanation entirely.

So Mila's family was, at this point, in clinical limbo. The diagnosis was clear. The first mutation was identified. The second mutation was missing. And without finding the second mutation, no one could be certain enough about the precise molecular pathology to even speculate about what kind of treatment might help. The path forward required going beyond the panel. It required reading the entire genome — every base of her DNA, exons and introns and intergenic stretches alike — and looking for whatever the panel had missed.

That brings us to **whole-genome sequencing**.

## SEGMENT 5 — Whole-genome sequencing as the detective tool — chimeric reads, careful eyes

So in January of 2017, Mila's case was referred to a researcher at Boston Children's Hospital named Timothy Yu. Yu's lab specialized — and still specializes — in using whole-genome sequencing to identify previously undiagnosable forms of childhood neurological disease. The decision was made to take Mila's DNA, sequence the entire thing, and go looking for whatever the panel had missed.

Let me explain the difference between a panel and a whole-genome sequence.

A gene panel, as we just discussed, reads about a hundred to a few hundred specific genes — the exons of those genes, mostly — at high accuracy, and reports any mutations it finds. The total amount of DNA examined is, in genomic terms, tiny — a few hundred thousand bases out of three billion in the human genome.

**Whole-genome sequencing** reads everything. Every chromosome, every exon, every intron, every regulatory region, every stretch of repetitive sequence that no one knows the function of, every base. All three billion bases. It produces, in raw form, an enormous file — usually several hundred gigabytes for a single human genome — containing the entire DNA sequence of the patient, with every base typically read multiple times for accuracy. To use the analogy I promised in the opening — **a panel is checking a list of usual suspects. Whole-genome sequencing is reading the whole book and looking for anything unusual.**

\[Pause.\]

But here is the catch, and this is the part that makes whole-genome sequencing harder than it sounds. Reading the whole book gives you a lot of information. Three billion bases worth. The vast majority of which — for any given patient — are completely normal, identical to a reference human genome, irrelevant to the diagnosis. To find the actual disease-causing variant, you have to filter that ocean of normal sequence down to the small set of differences from the reference, and then further filter down to the differences that are likely to actually cause disease. The bioinformatic pipelines that do this filtering are sophisticated, but they are not perfect, and the kinds of mutations they catch most easily are simple point mutations and small insertions or deletions — single letters changed, a handful of letters added or removed. Those are easy to recognize because they look like clean differences from the reference genome.

Bigger, weirder mutations — large rearrangements, big insertions of foreign sequence, complex structural variants — are much harder for the automated pipelines to call confidently. They tend to produce, in the raw sequencing data, what are called **chimeric reads** — individual sequencing reads in which the front half of the read matches one location in the genome and the back half of the read matches somewhere else entirely. Chimeric reads are often discarded by quality filters as artifacts, or flagged but ignored, because most chimeric reads really are artifacts of the sequencing process. But sometimes — sometimes — a chimeric read is the signature of a real, large, complex mutation that happens to span the boundary between two different DNA elements. And recognizing those takes careful human eyes looking at the raw data.

\[Pause.\]

This is what Yu's team did. When the automated pipeline came back with nothing definitive in MFSD8 on Mila's second copy, they did not give up. They went into the raw sequencing reads at the MFSD8 locus and they looked, by eye, at every read mapping there. And in February of 2017 — about a month after the referral — they found something unusual.

In a region within intron 6 of MFSD8 — that is, in the non-coding stretch between exon 6 and exon 7, exactly the kind of region a standard panel would never have looked at — there was a cluster of chimeric reads. The front halves of these reads matched MFSD8 intron 6 normally. The back halves matched something completely different. And that something completely different had three very specific features. First, on one end, a long stretch of T's — a so-called **poly-T tail**. Second, on the other end, a series of repeating six-letter motifs that read AGAGGG, AGAGGG, AGAGGG, over and over. Third, surrounding the entire insertion, the genomic sequence of MFSD8 was duplicated — fourteen base pairs of the gene were present at both ends of the inserted material, a feature called a **target site duplication**.

To someone trained in genome biology, those three features together are a diagnostic signature. They are the unmistakable fingerprint of a particular kind of mobile DNA element called an **SVA** — an SVA retrotransposon. Roughly two thousand bases long. A piece of jumping DNA that does not belong in the MFSD8 gene, that originally lived somewhere else in the genome, and that, at some point in Mila's recent ancestry, had copied itself out of its original location and pasted itself into MFSD8 intron 6 on what would become Mila's second copy.

That was the missing mutation. That was the second hit. And the panel had never seen it — could never have seen it — because the SVA was sitting in an intron, deep inside non-coding DNA that the panel did not even attempt to read.

In Segment 6 we are going to talk about what SVAs are, where they come from, and why they exist at all. But I want to pause here, at the end of Segment 5, on a methodological point. Because this is the moment in the story where it became clear that finding the diagnosis required not just better technology, but better attention. The automated pipeline could not, on its own, identify what had happened in Mila's gene. It took human researchers, looking at raw reads, recognizing a pattern, and trusting their suspicion enough to investigate. The chimeric reads were there in the data the whole time. They just needed someone who knew what they were looking at.

And that, in a way, is the deeper lesson of the diagnostic phase of this paper. The molecular cause of Mila's disease was knowable. The data existed. The tools existed. What was required was someone willing to do whole-genome sequencing on a case the panel had already mostly answered, and someone willing to scrutinize the raw output for signs of something the pipeline did not flag. Without those choices, the diagnosis would have stopped at "one MFSD8 mutation, second one unknown," and the rest of the story — the design of milasen, the FDA submission, the treatment of the patient — would never have happened.

## SEGMENT 6 — Jumping genes — SVA retrotransposons, the "junk DNA" that wasn't junk

So let me tell you about SVAs, because these things are weird and wonderful and they say something interesting about what your genome actually is.

An SVA stands for **SINE-VNTR-Alu**. That's an acronym made of acronyms, which is unfortunate. SINE means "short interspersed nuclear element," VNTR means "variable number of tandem repeats," and Alu refers to a particular family of repetitive sequences named after a restriction enzyme cut site within them. The name is a Frankenstein because the element itself is a Frankenstein — a hybrid composite of three different repetitive DNA elements that got fused together somewhere in primate evolutionary history into a single chimeric mobile element. SVAs are unique to great apes; you find them in humans, chimps, gorillas, orangutans, but not in monkeys further down the primate tree, not in other mammals. They are evolutionarily recent. There are perhaps three thousand of them scattered throughout the human genome.

But the deeper context you need to know is that an SVA is one example of a much larger phenomenon called **transposable elements** or **retrotransposons** or, casually, **jumping genes**. Roughly half of the human genome — fifty percent — is made up of retrotransposon-derived sequence. Half. Of your DNA. Is the result of a particular kind of molecular parasite that has been spending hundreds of millions of years copying itself around inside the genomes of your ancestors.

Let me give you the analogy. **A retrotransposon is a parasitic Word macro.** Imagine you have a document — a long, important text document — and there is a macro inside it whose only job is to find itself and paste a copy of itself somewhere else in the document, at random. Every time the macro runs, the document gets a little longer, with one more copy of the macro spliced in at a new location. Over time, if you run the macro enough times, the document fills up with copies of the macro. The original text is still in there somewhere, but it is increasingly surrounded by, and in some cases interrupted by, copies of the parasitic macro that has been replicating itself for as long as the document has existed.

That is, more or less, what has happened to your genome. The vast bulk of the three billion bases you carry around is not genes. It is not regulatory sequence. It is repetitive copies of mobile elements — LINEs and SINEs and SVAs and endogenous retroviruses — that have, over evolutionary time, copied themselves and pasted themselves into your DNA, and into your ancestors' DNA, over and over and over. Most of these copies are now defunct, broken, immobile. They cannot copy themselves anymore. They are, in evolutionary terms, fossils — the wreckage of past invasions. But some, a small minority, are still active. They can still copy themselves. And when they do, they paste somewhere new in the genome, occasionally — rarely — in a place where they disrupt a gene.

\[Pause.\]

How do they copy themselves, mechanistically? Retrotransposons replicate through an RNA intermediate. The DNA copy of the retrotransposon gets transcribed into RNA. The RNA gets reverse-transcribed back into DNA, by a reverse transcriptase enzyme that the retrotransposon itself encodes — or, in the case of SVAs, by an enzyme borrowed from another retrotransposon called L1. That new DNA copy is then inserted back into the genome, often at a new location. The original copy is unchanged. There is now one more copy in the genome than there was before. Over generations, copies accumulate.

The reason about half of the human genome is retrotransposon-derived is that this process has been going on for an extremely long time, the active elements have inserted millions of copies, and natural selection generally cannot remove a retrotransposon insertion that does not cause overt harm. Most insertions are harmless — they land in non-coding sequence and don't disrupt anything. They get inherited along with everything else. So the count just goes up over evolutionary time. Your genome is, in a real sense, mostly an archaeological site of past retrotransposon activity.

For most of the twentieth century, biologists referred to all this material as **junk DNA**. The thinking was that since it was repetitive, since it didn't code for protein, since it had no obvious function, it must be useless filler — junk that the genome was carrying around for no good reason. That view has shifted considerably in the last twenty years. Some retrotransposon-derived sequences have been co-opted by the genome for regulatory functions; some have evolved into novel genes; some seem to play roles in chromosome structure or recombination. The "junk DNA" framing was always too dismissive. But for the most part, your retrotransposon load is genuinely doing nothing useful in your day-to-day biology. It's just there.

\[Pause.\]

Most of the time. But sometimes, the retrotransposon machinery is still active enough in human cells — particularly in germ cells, the cells that produce sperm and eggs — that a new insertion occurs, and that new insertion is then passed to the next generation. Most new insertions, again, are harmless. But occasionally one lands in a gene and disrupts it. And when that happens, you have a new genetic disease, born in that one generation, often unique to that one family, sometimes unique to that one person.

This is what happened in Mila's case. At some point — perhaps in her parents, perhaps a generation or two earlier, the timing is impossible to know precisely — an active SVA somewhere in the genome was transcribed, reverse-transcribed, and pasted into intron 6 of MFSD8 on one chromosome. That insertion was harmless in the carrier who first received it, because they still had a normal copy of MFSD8 on their other chromosome. The insertion was passed down. It eventually reached one of Mila's parents, who passed it to Mila. From her other parent, Mila received the c.1102G>C point mutation. Now she had no working copy of MFSD8 at all. And the disease unfolded.

The reason this matters for our story — beyond just the elegance of the molecular detective work — is that an SVA insertion in an intron is exactly the kind of mutation that is hardest to find with standard testing, hardest to interpret when you do find it, and, as we are about to see, easiest to fix with a specifically targeted ASO. Because what the SVA actually did, mechanistically, inside the MFSD8 gene, is a problem with a precise molecular shape — and that precise shape is something we can design a drug to mask. Which is the topic of Segment 7.

## SEGMENT 7 — The molecular crime scene — how the SVA insertion created a cryptic splice site

So we have an SVA — about two thousand bases of foreign sequence — sitting inside intron 6 of MFSD8 on Mila's second chromosome. Now I want to walk you through, in slow motion, what that insertion actually does to the gene. Because to understand the drug, you have to understand the broken machinery the drug is trying to compensate for. And the broken machinery is, in this case, a problem with **RNA splicing**.

Quick refresher, in case it has been a while since your intro biology class. When a gene is transcribed, the initial RNA product — called the **pre-mRNA** — contains both the exons and the introns of the gene, in their original order. Before that RNA can be translated into protein, the introns have to be cut out, and the exons have to be joined together end to end, to produce the **mature messenger RNA** that the ribosome will actually read. This cut-and-paste operation is called **splicing**, and it is performed by a large molecular machine in the nucleus called the **spliceosome**.

The spliceosome recognizes the boundaries between exons and introns by reading short sequence motifs in the pre-mRNA. The end of each exon, going into an intron, is marked by what we call a **splice donor site**. The start of each exon, coming out of an intron, is marked by a **splice acceptor site**. These sites are short — only a few bases of recognizable motif — and they are recognized by complementary sequences in the spliceosome's RNA components. When the spliceosome reads a donor site, it knows where one cut has to be made. When it reads an acceptor site, it knows where the next cut has to be made and where the next exon begins. It makes the cuts, throws the intron away, and welds the two exons together. This is going on, in your cells, thousands of times per second, across every gene that's being expressed.

\[Pause.\]

In a healthy MFSD8 gene, the splicing of intron 6 works the normal way. The spliceosome reads the donor site at the end of exon 6, reads the acceptor site at the start of exon 7, cuts both ends, throws out intron 6, and welds exon 6 directly to exon 7. The mature mRNA contains the full coding sequence, and the resulting MFSD8 protein is functional.

Now, what happens when an SVA is jammed into intron 6? Most of the time, intronic insertions are still tolerated by the spliceosome. The intron gets longer, but it still gets spliced out, because the donor at the end of exon 6 and the acceptor at the start of exon 7 are still where they're supposed to be. The spliceosome still finds them and still does its job. A longer intron is, mostly, no big deal.

But sometimes — and this is exactly what happened in Mila's gene — the inserted SVA contains, within its own sequence, a short motif that looks, to the spliceosome, _like a splice acceptor_. A fake acceptor. A motif that the spliceosome's RNA components can base-pair with, and that the spliceosome therefore reads as a legitimate place to make a cut. We call these fake sites **cryptic splice sites**. They exist all over the genome — in introns, in intergenic regions, even within exons — but they normally aren't used because the spliceosome preferentially uses the real, strong sites. When a cryptic site is introduced by a mutation in a position where the spliceosome can now reach it, however, the cryptic site can get used in preference to the real site, and the splicing pattern gets corrupted.

Here is the analogy I want you to use. **A cryptic splice acceptor is a fake doorway.** Imagine a corridor in a building, with a door at one end labeled "Exit." Everyone uses that door, because it's where the exit is. Now someone installs a fake door, made of paint and a doorknob, partway down the corridor, also labeled "Exit." People hurrying through the corridor may now hit the fake door first, try to open it, get confused, and end up in the wrong place. The legitimate exit at the end of the corridor is still there, but the fake one earlier in the corridor is now intercepting traffic that was supposed to reach the legitimate one.

That is exactly what happens, molecularly, when an SVA introduces a cryptic splice acceptor into an intron. In Mila's case, the relevant cryptic acceptor sat about 119 base pairs upstream of the real exon 7 splice acceptor. So the spliceosome, working its way through intron 6, reaches the cryptic acceptor first — and uses it. The cut happens at the wrong place. The exon being added to the growing mRNA is not exon 7. It is the chunk of intronic sequence between the cryptic acceptor and the real exon 7, which we'll call "i6.SA-derived" — meaning, derived from the intron-6-cryptic-acceptor exonization.

\[Pause.\]

And here is the lethal consequence. That mis-spliced exon, that piece of intronic sequence that has been inappropriately included in the mRNA, contains within it a **premature stop codon**. A sequence — TAA or TAG or TGA, the three nucleotide triplets that signal "stop translation here" — appears in the wrong reading frame, much earlier than it should. So when the ribosome translates this corrupted mRNA into protein, it gets a short way in, hits the premature stop, and falls off. No full-length MFSD8 protein is made from this allele. The cell tries — the gene is transcribed, the broken splicing happens, the truncated protein attempts to be produced — and the result is essentially nothing. A truncated fragment of MFSD8 that does not function.

So the SVA, sitting in intron 6, has effectively destroyed Mila's second copy of MFSD8 — not by deleting the gene, not by mutating the protein-coding sequence, but by inserting a cryptic splice site that hijacks the splicing machinery and produces a truncated, non-functional protein. The exonic sequence of MFSD8 on that chromosome is intact. Every exon, sequenced individually, looks normal. The damage is purely at the level of how the gene is processed. That is why the panel didn't see anything wrong — because the panel was looking at the exons, and the exons are fine. The crime took place not in the exons themselves but in the spliceosome's interpretation of them.

\[Pause.\]

I want to dwell on this for one more moment, because the molecular shape of this problem is what sets up the entire treatment strategy. The problem here is **not** that Mila is missing the MFSD8 gene. She has it. The problem is **not** that the protein-coding sequence has been corrupted. It hasn't. The problem is that the splicing of one specific intron is going wrong, because of one specific cryptic splice site that the SVA has introduced, that the spliceosome is now using when it shouldn't.

If you could somehow prevent the spliceosome from using that cryptic site — if you could put something over the fake doorway so that the spliceosome's reading machinery skipped over it and went on to find the real exon 7 acceptor — then the splicing would proceed normally, exon 6 would be joined directly to exon 7, and the resulting mRNA would encode a full-length, functional MFSD8 protein. From this one allele, you would get half-normal levels of working transporter, which combined with whatever the c.1102G>C allele produces should be more than enough to substantially rescue the cell's lysosomal function.

That is the therapeutic concept. And the molecule that can do this masking — the sticky note that can cover the fake doorway — is an antisense oligonucleotide. That is the topic of Segment 8.

## SEGMENT 8 — Antisense oligonucleotides — what they are and how they work

Alright. We have, hopefully, set up the problem. Now let's talk about the tool.

An **antisense oligonucleotide** — usually shortened to **ASO** — is, mechanically, an extremely simple kind of drug. It is a short piece of synthetic nucleic acid, typically between fifteen and twenty-five bases long, designed to be the exact reverse complement — the mirror image — of a chosen target RNA sequence. The idea is that because of Watson-Crick base pairing — A pairs with T or U, G pairs with C — a mirror-image piece of nucleic acid will find and bind to its target RNA inside the cell, sequence-specifically, with very high precision. Once it is bound, depending on the design, the ASO can do one of several things to that target RNA. It can recruit an enzyme called RNase H to chop the target up. It can simply sit there and prevent the target from being read by the ribosome. Or — and this is the case we care about for milasen — it can mask a sequence motif that some piece of the cellular machinery would otherwise have recognized.

Here is the analogy I want you to use, and I want you to hold this in your head for the rest of the lecture. **An antisense oligonucleotide is a sticky note that finds and silences a specific RNA sequence.** Imagine you are reading a long sentence, and someone places a small sticky note over one word in that sentence. Your eye skips over the covered word. You read the rest of the sentence without it. The word is still there, on the page, underneath the sticky note — nothing has been removed, nothing has been chemically changed — but your reading process has effectively skipped that word, because it was covered.

That is, conceptually, what an ASO does. It is a piece of synthetic mirror-image RNA — or really, a chemically modified analog of RNA, which we'll get to — that finds one specific sequence in one specific transcript inside a cell and lays itself down on top of that sequence, covering it. The cellular machinery that was supposed to recognize that sequence — the ribosome, or a splicing factor, or RNase H, depending on what kind of ASO it is — either can't get to its target because the sticky note is in the way, or interprets the sticky-note-plus-target as a different kind of substrate and does something different in response.

\[Pause.\]

Now, two practical points about ASOs as drugs that you need to know.

First, you cannot put a piece of normal, unmodified RNA into a person and expect it to do anything useful. The bloodstream is full of enzymes called RNases that chew up RNA in seconds. Even if you got it past the RNases, normal RNA does not cross cell membranes easily, and inside cells it gets degraded fast. So for ASOs to be useful as drugs, the chemistry of the nucleic acid backbone has to be modified to make the molecule resistant to nucleases, to improve its uptake into cells, and to tune its binding properties. The modifications that have become standard for therapeutic ASOs include replacing the natural phosphodiester linkages between bases with **phosphorothioate** linkages — where a sulfur atom replaces one of the oxygens — which makes the molecule nuclease-resistant and improves cellular uptake. And modifications to the sugar component of each nucleotide, the most common of which for splice-modulating ASOs is **2'-O-methoxyethyl**, abbreviated **2'-MOE**, which tightens binding to the target RNA and further protects from degradation.

If those chemistry words are washing over you, don't worry about memorizing them. The point is that a clinical ASO is not just naked RNA. It is RNA whose chemical backbone has been carefully engineered, base by base, to survive in the body, get into cells, find its target, and stick to it. The chemistry of those modifications was worked out over decades, primarily by a single company called **Ionis Pharmaceuticals** — formerly called Isis, until they changed their name for obvious reasons — and that chemistry is the platform on top of which essentially all modern therapeutic ASOs are built.

Second practical point: ASOs do not cross the blood-brain barrier when injected into the bloodstream. The blood-brain barrier is the tight junction system that lines the blood vessels of the brain and keeps most large molecules — including ASOs — out of the central nervous system. So if your target is a gene expressed in neurons, you cannot just give the ASO by IV and expect it to reach the brain. You have to inject it directly into the **cerebrospinal fluid** — the fluid that bathes the brain and spinal cord — by inserting a needle into the lower back, between the vertebrae, into the space surrounding the spinal cord. That route is called **intrathecal injection**. It is exactly the same procedure as a spinal tap, except instead of pulling fluid out, you are pushing drug in.

Here is the analogy. **Intrathecal injection is going through the lower back into the cerebrospinal fluid, where the drug bathes the brain and spinal cord directly.** The CSF circulates throughout the central nervous system, and an ASO injected into the lumbar CSF will distribute, over hours to days, to the brain and spinal cord, where it can enter neurons and do its work.

\[Pause.\]

So to summarize this segment. An ASO is a short, chemically modified piece of synthetic mirror-image nucleic acid that binds to a specific RNA target by Watson-Crick base pairing. Depending on its design, it can destroy the target, block its translation, or cover up a sequence motif within it. To get an ASO into the central nervous system, you inject it intrathecally — directly into the cerebrospinal fluid by lumbar puncture. The chemistry of the molecule has been engineered over decades to make it stable, deliverable, and effective.

Now — for milasen specifically, the kind of ASO we want is the masking kind. The covering-up kind. We want to put a sticky note on the cryptic splice site that the SVA introduced into MFSD8 intron 6, so that the spliceosome stops recognizing it and goes on to use the real exon 7 acceptor instead. That class of ASO is called a **splice-switching** or **splice-modulating** ASO, and it has a particular history of its own — including a critical precedent drug, already FDA-approved at the time of Mila's case, that we will discuss next.

## SEGMENT 9 — Splice-switching ASOs and the nusinersen precedent (Spinraza for SMA)

I want to dwell for an entire segment on one specific class of ASOs — the splice-modulating ones — and on one specific drug that had been approved by the FDA two years before Mila's treatment began. Because that one drug, and its approval, is what made milasen possible. Without it, the entire milasen story almost certainly does not happen.

The drug is called **nusinersen**, brand name **Spinraza**. It treats a disease called **spinal muscular atrophy**, or SMA, a devastating motor neuron disease that, in its most severe infantile form, used to kill affected babies before their second birthday. SMA is caused by loss of a gene called SMN1, which makes a protein called survival motor neuron, essential for the health of the motor neurons that drive your muscles. Without SMN1, motor neurons in the spinal cord die, and the affected child progressively loses the ability to move, breathe, and swallow.

Here is the molecular twist that made SMA treatable. Humans have a second, very similar gene called SMN2, which arose from a duplication of SMN1 sometime in primate evolution. SMN2 makes almost the same protein as SMN1 — but for reasons of one single-base difference in its sequence, SMN2's pre-mRNA is mostly spliced incorrectly. The spliceosome skips exon 7 of SMN2 most of the time, producing a truncated, unstable protein that doesn't work. Only about ten to fifteen percent of SMN2 transcripts are spliced correctly to include exon 7 and produce functional protein. That is not enough, by itself, to compensate for the loss of SMN1. But — and this is the brilliant insight — if you could shift the splicing of SMN2 to include exon 7 more efficiently, you could produce more functional protein from the gene the patient already has, and partially rescue the disease.

That is exactly what nusinersen does. It is a splice-modulating ASO targeted to a specific intronic regulatory element in SMN2 — an element that normally promotes the skipping of exon 7. By binding to that element and masking it, nusinersen prevents the skipping. Exon 7 gets included more often. More functional SMN protein gets made. The motor neurons of the treated patient do better. The disease progresses much more slowly, or in babies treated very early, doesn't progress at all.

\[Pause.\]

Nusinersen was approved by the FDA in December of 2016. It was the first splice-modulating ASO approved for any human disease, and it was a transformative success. Babies who would have died now grow into children who can sit up, who can walk, who can attend school. It was, in every sense, a paradigm shift. The drug is delivered intrathecally, by spinal injection, every few months for the rest of the patient's life. Its chemical backbone is exactly the chemistry I described in Segment 8 — phosphorothioate linkages, 2'-O-methoxyethyl sugar modifications, a roughly twenty-base oligonucleotide of carefully chosen sequence.

Here is why nusinersen mattered so much for milasen, beyond just being a scientific success in its own right. **It was the precedent.** It proved several things at once. It proved that splice-modulating ASOs can work clinically — that the theoretical mechanism of switching a splicing decision by base-pairing actually translates into clinical benefit in a real human disease. It proved that this specific chemistry — the 2'-MOE phosphorothioate backbone — is safe enough to give to children, repeatedly, for years, by intrathecal injection. It proved that the route of administration — spinal injection every few months — is tolerated by pediatric patients in clinical practice. And it proved that the FDA was willing to approve drugs designed against these targets with this chemistry.

In other words, nusinersen built an entire platform. The chemistry was de-risked. The delivery route was de-risked. The mechanism was de-risked. The regulatory pathway had been blazed. If, in 2017, you wanted to design a brand-new splice-modulating ASO for a different disease, you did not have to reinvent any of those wheels. You could pick up the same chemistry, design a new sequence aimed at your new target, deliver it the same way, and have a reasonable expectation that the major engineering questions — safety, deliverability, mechanism — would behave the way they had behaved for nusinersen. **The platform was portable. The sequence was the variable. Everything else was a copy.**

That is exactly how Yu and his colleagues approached milasen. They did not have to invent ASO chemistry. They did not have to invent intrathecal delivery. They did not have to invent a regulatory pathway. They had to design a new sequence — a new sticky note for a new word in a new sentence — using the existing platform that nusinersen had validated. That is a much, much smaller undertaking than developing a drug from scratch. That is what made one year possible.

\[Pause.\]

I want to name this idea explicitly because it is going to recur as a theme. **In modern drug development, platforms matter more than individual drugs.** When a new platform — a new chemistry, a new delivery route, a new molecular class — is validated for the first time in one disease, it dramatically lowers the barrier to attempting it in other diseases. mRNA vaccines were a research curiosity for two decades until the COVID-19 mRNA vaccines were authorized, and now mRNA-based therapies are being tested for dozens of conditions. CAR-T cells took thirty years to reach the first approval, and now CAR-T programs exist for every conceivable cancer. The first hard climb is the platform climb. The second climb, for the next disease using the same platform, is much shorter. Splice-modulating ASOs were validated by nusinersen. Milasen was the first follow-on after that validation, and the speed of milasen's development is a direct consequence of standing on top of the nusinersen platform.

This is also, by the way, the reason organizations like the n-Lorem Foundation — which we will discuss in Segment 17 — can now contemplate producing custom ASOs for individual patients on a regular basis. The hard chemistry work has already been done. The platform is mature. The only piece that has to be customized for each new patient is the twenty-base sequence of the oligonucleotide itself, which can be redesigned for a new target in a matter of weeks.

Alright. With nusinersen as our precedent firmly in mind, we are ready to talk about the actual design of milasen. But first — we have been at this for roughly an hour. Let's take a fifteen-minute break, get coffee, stretch, and come back ready to walk through the molecular engineering of the drug itself.

## SEGMENT 10 — Designing milasen — eight candidates, screening in patient fibroblasts, picking TY777

Welcome back. Before the break we talked about the precedent. Now let's talk about the actual design of the drug.

Yu's team had, by the spring and summer of 2017, a clear molecular picture. They knew the second mutation was an SVA in intron 6 of MFSD8. They knew the SVA had introduced a cryptic splice acceptor, called i6.SA, about 119 bases upstream of the real exon 7 acceptor. They knew the cryptic acceptor was being used in preference to the real one, producing a mis-spliced transcript with a premature stop codon. And they knew what they wanted to do — they wanted to design a splice-modulating ASO that would bind to the region around the cryptic acceptor and prevent the spliceosome from using it.

How do you actually design such a molecule? You start by considering the region around the cryptic acceptor, and you identify all the sequence motifs that the splicing machinery cares about. The cryptic acceptor itself is one such motif. Adjacent to it are likely to be other sequences called **exonic splicing enhancers** — short motifs that recruit splicing factors to nearby splice sites and increase their utilization. If you can mask the cryptic acceptor and the nearby exonic splicing enhancers, you weaken the cryptic splicing event in multiple ways at once, and you tip the balance back toward the real exon 7 acceptor being used.

The team designed **eight different candidate ASOs**, each one targeted to a slightly different region of the cryptic acceptor and its surroundings. Each candidate was a short — eighteen to twenty-two nucleotides — piece of 2'-MOE phosphorothioate nucleic acid, with a slightly different sequence chosen to base-pair with a slightly different stretch of the MFSD8 pre-mRNA around the SVA insertion. The reason for designing eight candidates rather than one is that the precise position of an ASO along a target sequence often matters a great deal for its efficacy — sometimes a shift of just a few bases can change a useless ASO into an active one or vice versa, because of factors like local RNA secondary structure, the precise position of the binding site relative to splicing factor binding sites, and so on. So the standard approach is to design several candidates, screen them empirically, and pick the best one.

\[Pause.\]

To screen them, the team needed Mila's own cells. Specifically, they needed cells from Mila that carried both her mutations and that were producing the broken splicing pattern, so that the team could measure whether each candidate ASO actually corrected the splicing in cells of Mila's specific genotype. And the standard cell type for this kind of work is the **fibroblast** — a kind of cell that lives in your skin and connective tissue, that grows readily in tissue culture, and that can be obtained from a routine skin biopsy.

In May of 2017, fibroblasts were established from Mila's skin biopsy and grown in culture. The team confirmed that those fibroblasts were producing the abnormal, mis-spliced MFSD8 transcripts — about ninety-two percent of the transcripts from her SVA-carrying allele were going through the cryptic acceptor and including the unwanted intronic sequence. Only about eight percent were being spliced correctly, exon 6 to exon 7. That baseline of eight percent correct splicing represented essentially the residual leaky output of the SVA-carrying allele, plus whatever transcription was happening from the c.1102G>C allele.

Now the team did what was, in concept, a simple experiment. They added each of the eight candidate ASOs to dishes of Mila's fibroblasts, at various concentrations, let the cells take the ASO up and process the RNA for a couple of days, and then measured what fraction of the MFSD8 transcripts in the treated cells were being spliced correctly — exon 6 directly to exon 7 — versus what fraction were still going through the cryptic acceptor.

One of the eight candidates was clearly the winner. It was called, in the lab's internal nomenclature, **TY777**. It was a twenty-two nucleotide oligonucleotide. In Mila's fibroblasts, treatment with TY777 tripled the proportion of correctly spliced MFSD8 transcripts, from a baseline of about eight percent to approximately thirty-four percent. The half-maximal effective concentration — the dose at which half of the maximum splicing correction was achieved — was in the nanomolar range, which is a very respectable potency for a clinical ASO.

TY777, ultimately renamed **milasen**, was the molecule they would advance toward the patient.

\[Pause.\]

The team did not stop with measuring splicing correction. They went on to measure whether the splicing correction translated into rescue of the actual cellular phenotypes of the disease. Remember — the underlying problem in Mila's cells is lysosomal dysfunction. The lysosomes accumulate undigested material, they swell into abnormal vacuoles, they fluoresce a particular way under the microscope because of accumulated lipofuscin pigment, and the cellular process called **autophagy** — by which cells deliver internal waste to lysosomes for breakdown — gets jammed up because the lysosomes can't process what's being delivered to them.

In Mila's fibroblasts, treatment with TY777 rescued all of these phenotypes. The abnormal lysosomal vacuoles got smaller and less numerous. The autofluorescence — the lipofuscin accumulation — went down. The autophagic flux — the rate at which material moved through the autophagy pathway — normalized. The cellular machinery, with about thirty-four percent functional MFSD8 transcripts being made, was producing enough working transporter to begin clearing the lysosomal backlog.

This is the moment in the paper where the team had concrete molecular and cellular evidence that the drug they were about to make was actually doing something useful, in cells from the actual patient who would receive it. The splicing was corrected. The downstream lysosomal phenotypes were rescued. The half-maximal dose was reasonable. The candidate had been selected. By the fall of 2017 — October — the in-vitro validation was complete.

Now they had to take this oligonucleotide out of the dish and put it into a living animal. And then, eventually, into a human child.

## SEGMENT 11 — From cells to rats — the abbreviated preclinical safety program

A standard drug, going from in-vitro validation to first-in-human dosing, normally undergoes a years-long preclinical safety program. Multiple animal species. Long-term dosing studies. Toxicology at multiple doses. Carcinogenicity studies. Reproductive toxicity studies. Pharmacokinetic studies in every relevant species. Histopathology of every organ. The total preclinical package for a typical IND submission to the FDA is often hundreds of pages and represents years of work and millions of dollars of laboratory effort. The purpose of all this is to characterize, before you put the drug into a person for the first time, every reasonably foreseeable risk the drug poses.

In milasen's case, there was no time for any of that. Mila was already having thirty seizures a day. Her brain was atrophying. Every month that passed was a month of continuing neurodegeneration that the drug, even if it worked perfectly when it arrived, could not undo. The team and the FDA had to find a way to characterize the drug's safety just enough to justify intrathecal dosing in a child, in a fraction of the time a normal preclinical program would take.

What they did, and what the FDA accepted, was a single abbreviated rat toxicology study, and they leaned heavily on the safety record of nusinersen — the precedent drug — for everything else. Because milasen had the same chemistry, the same backbone, the same general structural class, and the same delivery route as nusinersen, and nusinersen had been dosed in many hundreds of children by 2017 with an excellent safety profile, the regulatory argument was that the major safety questions about the platform itself had already been answered. The only question that needed fresh data, for milasen specifically, was whether this particular sequence — when administered intrathecally — caused any acute, dose-dependent toxicity that would change the calculus for first-in-human dosing.

\[Pause.\]

In December of 2017, the team administered milasen to rats by intrathecal injection — exactly the same route that would eventually be used in Mila — at three escalating doses: 0.06 milligrams, 0.25 milligrams, and 1.0 milligram per animal. Those doses were chosen to span and exceed the maximum doses that had been used in nusinersen clinical trials, on a body-weight basis. After dosing, the animals were observed for behavioral and neurological signs, and then sacrificed at intervals for tissue analysis.

The 0.06-milligram dose was uneventful. The animals showed no acute neurological signs and no histological abnormalities in the spinal cord or brain. The 0.25-milligram and 1.0-milligram doses produced a transient hindlimb weakness in some animals — the rats had some difficulty moving their back legs for a few hours after the injection — but the weakness resolved by about twenty-four hours, and no permanent damage was seen. The pattern of transient hindlimb weakness was, importantly, exactly the kind of finding that had been reported in preclinical studies of nusinersen at high doses — it appeared to be a consequence of bolus intrathecal injection of any phosphorothioate ASO, not something specific to milasen. So while it set an upper bound on the dose that could be considered safe, it did not raise novel safety concerns.

On the basis of this study, plus the platform safety data from nusinersen, the team and the FDA agreed on a clinical dosing strategy. Start at a low dose, well below any dose that had caused observable toxicity in the rat study. Escalate gradually, over multiple administrations, monitoring carefully for any adverse effects. The target maintenance dose was set at 42 milligrams in Mila — a dose that, in terms of the molar amount of oligonucleotide delivered to the CSF, was equivalent to about three times the standard clinical dose of nusinersen. The rationale for choosing three times higher was that antisense oligonucleotides distribute differently within the central nervous system depending on the route and rate of delivery, and the team estimated, based on existing pharmacokinetic data, that milasen would distribute about one-third as well to the brain as to the spinal cord. So a dose three times higher than nusinersen would, by their estimate, deliver to the brain roughly the same molar exposure that nusinersen achieves in the spinal cord, where its primary site of action is.

\[Pause.\]

I want to flag something important about the preclinical program here, because if you go on to work in drug development you will encounter strong reactions to it from both sides. On one hand, the rat study was abbreviated. It was a single species, a single short-duration study, a small number of animals, with no long-term toxicology, no reproductive studies, no carcinogenicity work, no detailed pharmacokinetic profiling. By the standards of a normal IND, this is a small fraction of the preclinical package a regulator would normally demand before allowing first-in-human dosing.

On the other hand — and this is the argument the FDA accepted, in the specific context of this specific patient — the chemistry was not novel. The delivery route was not novel. The molecular class was not novel. The patient was facing a rapidly fatal disease with no other treatment options. The reasonable risk of an unexpected adverse effect from a new sequence, when the underlying platform had been extensively characterized in another approved drug, was judged to be low relative to the certain harm of withholding any attempt at treatment. So the abbreviated program was, in the FDA's judgment, justified for this particular case under the **Expanded Access** regulatory pathway — which we'll discuss next.

I want you to notice the structure of this decision. **The preclinical program was not abbreviated by lowering the safety bar. It was abbreviated by inheriting safety data from a closely related precedent drug.** That distinction is important. Milasen's safety case rested heavily on the fact that the platform — the chemistry, the delivery route, the molecular class — had already been characterized for nusinersen. Without that precedent, no abbreviated rat study would have been sufficient. The platform's prior validation was the safety case. The rat study was a final, narrow confirmation that this specific sequence did not introduce some new, unexpected, sequence-specific toxicity.

This is the model that has now been adopted, more or less, by the field for individualized ASO therapies. You can move fast for individual patients, but only because the platform you are using has already been extensively safety-characterized by precedent drugs. The first drug in any new class will always require a full preclinical program. Subsequent drugs in the same class, with the same chemistry and delivery, can be developed much more quickly. Milasen was the first published example of this principle being applied to a brand-new drug for a single patient — and the speed of its development is a direct demonstration of what platform inheritance can buy you.

## SEGMENT 12 — The FDA Expanded Access pathway and the ethics review

Let me spend a segment on the regulatory and ethical framework, because the technical achievement of designing milasen would have been moot if no regulator had been willing to permit its administration to a child.

In the United States, drugs intended for human use must, normally, be approved by the FDA on the basis of extensive clinical trial data demonstrating safety and efficacy. The standard pathway from a new molecule to an approved drug involves Phase 1, Phase 2, and Phase 3 trials, typically involving thousands of patients and a decade or more of testing. Milasen could not possibly go through that pathway. There was, by definition, no patient population other than Mila. There would be no Phase 1, Phase 2, or Phase 3 trial, because the drug was for one person.

The pathway that was used instead is called **Expanded Access**, sometimes called **compassionate use**. Expanded Access is a regulatory mechanism by which the FDA can authorize the use of an investigational drug — a drug not yet approved for general use — in a specific patient with a serious or life-threatening condition who has no other satisfactory treatment options. Normally, Expanded Access is used to provide a drug that is already in clinical development for some population to an individual patient who falls outside the trial criteria but might benefit. In milasen's case, Expanded Access was used in a more radical way: to authorize the administration of a drug that had been developed specifically and exclusively for this one patient, and that was not in clinical development for anyone else.

For this to happen, the team had to submit, to the FDA, an **Investigational New Drug** application — an IND — covering the use of milasen in Mila. The IND included the molecular rationale for the drug, the in-vitro validation in Mila's fibroblasts, the rat toxicology study, the proposed dosing regimen, the consent and monitoring plan, and a discussion of the risks and potential benefits. In January of 2018, the FDA reviewed the IND and granted permission to proceed. That permission was specific to Mila. Milasen was not approved as a drug. It was approved for use in this one named patient under Expanded Access. The legal status of the molecule, as a drug, did not change. What changed was the FDA's specific authorization that it could be administered to this one patient under defined conditions.

\[Pause.\]

In parallel with the FDA submission, the case was reviewed by the **institutional review board** — the IRB — at Boston Children's Hospital. The IRB is the body responsible for reviewing all research and experimental clinical activities at the institution to ensure that they meet ethical standards: that the risks have been properly characterized and minimized, that the potential benefits justify the risks, that the patient and family have given informed consent, that the science is sound enough to justify the attempt. The IRB had to be satisfied not just that milasen was technically reasonable, but that this was an ethically appropriate use of investigational medicine.

The ethical issues were not trivial, and I want to walk through them honestly, because they will recur for every patient considered for an individualized therapy.

First, the question of informed consent. Mila, at age six, could not give consent herself. Her parents could give consent on her behalf, but they were also the family that had been pushing for this treatment to happen — they had founded the Mila's Miracle Foundation, raised significant funds, and been instrumental in connecting with Yu's lab. Their consent was real and freely given, but the IRB had to be careful that the family's understandable desperation to do something for their child did not override their ability to assess the risks soberly. The team and the family worked through extensive discussions about what was known, what was unknown, and what the realistic best- and worst-case outcomes might be.

Second, the question of benefit. Milasen had never been given to a human. The preclinical work suggested it would correct splicing in Mila's specific genotype and might therefore slow the progression of her disease. But there was no guarantee that the in-vitro effect would translate to the brain. There was no guarantee that even a perfectly corrected MFSD8 splicing pattern at this stage of her disease would slow neurodegeneration in a clinically meaningful way. The drug might do nothing. It might cause harm. The IRB had to weigh those possibilities against the certain trajectory of untreated CLN7 — a relentless decline ending in death.

Third, the question of broader implications. If the IRB approved milasen for Mila, it was implicitly creating a precedent. Other families with rare disease children would, on hearing about this case, approach Boston Children's and similar institutions asking for similar one-off treatments. How would the institution handle that demand? On what basis would some patients be offered individualized therapy and others not? Was it ethically defensible to develop a drug only for the patients whose families could fund the work and connect with the right researchers? These were not theoretical questions. They became, immediately after milasen was reported, very real institutional questions that Boston Children's responded to by establishing a formal **Oversight Committee on Personalized Experimental Therapeutics**, which now reviews all such proposals.

\[Pause.\]

I want to highlight one more point about the regulatory model, because it is now becoming a template. The FDA, in approving milasen under Expanded Access, did not commit itself to approving every individualized ASO that might be proposed in the future. It made a case-specific judgment that this particular drug, for this particular patient, with this particular safety package and this particular molecular rationale, met the standard for compassionate use. The pathway it used — Expanded Access, with an abbreviated preclinical program leveraging platform safety data from a precedent drug — has since been applied to additional individualized ASOs developed for other patients. The FDA has been working with organizations like the n-Lorem Foundation to standardize, as much as possible, the framework for these submissions, so that what was unprecedented in 2018 has become, in 2024 and beyond, a defined regulatory pathway that other patients can be considered under.

The key principle that the milasen case established, regulatorily, is that **the unit of analysis can, for sufficiently grave and rare conditions, legitimately shrink to a single patient.** A drug does not have to be a population-level intervention to be considered. The FDA can, when the circumstances justify it, consider the risks and benefits of a single use of an investigational drug in a single patient, and authorize that use on a case-by-case basis. Before milasen, this had been done for drugs that were already in development for broader populations. After milasen, it has been done for drugs developed for one patient only. That is a real and meaningful change in the regulatory landscape.

Alright. The drug was designed. The FDA had approved its use under Expanded Access. The IRB had approved the protocol. The family had consented. The first dose was scheduled. Let me tell you what happened next.

## SEGMENT 13 — First dose — the escalating intrathecal regimen

In late January of 2018, Mila Makovec received the first intrathecal injection of milasen. Roughly twelve months had elapsed between the referral of her case to Yu's lab and the moment a needle, loaded with a drug that had been designed and manufactured for her alone, was placed into her lower back and the drug was infused into her cerebrospinal fluid. Twelve months. That is, to put it gently, an unprecedented turnaround for a brand-new drug.

The clinical regimen used what is called a **dose escalation** strategy. Rather than going straight to the eventual maintenance dose, the team started low and ramped up over a series of doses, watching carefully at each step for adverse effects. The reason for this caution is that even with all the platform inheritance from nusinersen, this was the first time this specific sequence had ever been administered to a human being. If something unexpected was going to happen, the safest place to discover it was at a low dose where any adverse effect would, hopefully, be milder and more easily managed.

The escalation went, in milligrams: three point five, then seven, then fourteen, then twenty-one, then twenty-eight, then thirty-five, then forty-two. Seven loading doses, each administered roughly two weeks apart over the first several months of treatment. Then two additional doses at the full forty-two milligram level, also at two-week intervals, to complete the loading phase. After that, the regimen transitioned to **maintenance dosing**: forty-two milligrams approximately every three months, the same kind of long-interval maintenance schedule used for nusinersen.

\[Pause.\]

The forty-two milligram dose, as I mentioned in Segment 11, was chosen on the basis of two considerations. First, intrathecally administered ASOs do not distribute uniformly throughout the central nervous system. They tend to reach higher concentrations in the spinal cord and the lower CSF spaces, and lower concentrations in the brain itself, because the CSF flow patterns and the diffusion characteristics of the drug favor caudal — that is, toward-the-spine — distribution. Estimates from preclinical and pharmacokinetic studies suggested that the brain reaches roughly one-third the exposure that the spinal cord does. Since Mila's disease is primarily a brain disease — her affected neurons are in the cerebrum and cerebellum — the team needed a dose high enough that even after the discount for poor brain distribution, the drug would reach meaningful concentrations in her brain tissue. Three times the nusinersen dose, in molar terms, was the answer that fell out of that math.

Second, the dose had to be below whatever level might cause acute toxicity. The rat study had shown transient hindlimb weakness at doses corresponding to the upper end of the human nusinersen range, but no permanent damage. The forty-two milligram dose was selected to deliver a meaningful brain exposure while staying below thresholds that might cause acute adverse effects.

In total, across the loading phase and the first year of maintenance dosing reported in the paper, approximately **eighteen grams of milasen drug substance** were manufactured. Eighteen grams. For comparison, a typical small-molecule drug program might produce tens of kilograms or more across the development and commercial phases combined. Milasen is, almost certainly, the smallest production run for any individually-manufactured pharmaceutical in modern history. There was no commercial manufacturing process. The drug was synthesized to research-grade standards under a custom protocol, with quality controls appropriate for its intended use in a single patient.

\[Pause.\]

How was the dose administered, in practice? Mila came to the hospital. She was sedated, because intrathecal injection in a young child with active neurological disease is not a procedure you do on an awake, frightened patient. Under sedation, a needle was placed in her lower back, between two of her lower lumbar vertebrae, into the subarachnoid space where the cerebrospinal fluid pools. A small volume of CSF was withdrawn first — both to confirm correct needle placement and to provide a sample for laboratory analysis — and then the dose of milasen, dissolved in a small volume of saline, was slowly infused. The whole procedure took perhaps fifteen to twenty minutes from start to finish. Mila recovered from sedation, was observed in the hospital for several hours to confirm no acute adverse effects, and then went home.

This was repeated approximately every two weeks during loading, and every three months during maintenance.

The CSF samples collected at each dosing visit allowed the team to measure the pharmacokinetics of the drug — how much milasen was present in the CSF at various time points after dosing. The concentrations rose in approximate proportion to the dose given, plateauing in the range of about eighteen to twenty-seven nanograms per milliliter during the maintenance phase. There was one trough — a dip in CSF drug levels at around day forty — that was attributed to a small CSF leak following one of the procedures. Otherwise, the pharmacokinetics behaved roughly as expected from the platform precedent. The drug was getting into the CSF, persisting there, presumably reaching the neurons, and presumably being taken up by them — though of course there was no direct way to measure tissue concentrations in the patient's brain.

\[Pause.\]

Across the loading phase and the first year of maintenance dosing, there were **no serious adverse events**. That is one of the most important sentences in the paper, and I want you to register it. No serious adverse events. The drug did not, at the doses used, cause neurological deterioration, did not cause meningitis or other infectious complications of repeated intrathecal access, did not cause systemic toxicity that was attributable to the drug, did not cause any abnormality in routine laboratory monitoring that required intervention. There were minor adverse events of the kind you'd expect from any patient receiving multiple intrathecal procedures — some procedural pain, some transient changes in CSF cell counts, the CSF leak I mentioned — but nothing serious, nothing that required stopping the drug, nothing that suggested the platform was unsafe for this patient.

That is, in a sense, the first answer the paper gives to its central question. Can we build a medicine for one person? Yes, and we can deliver it without immediately harming her. The first hurdle — that the brand-new drug, when injected into a child's spinal fluid, would not cause an acute disaster — was cleared. Now the question turned to whether the drug, beyond being safe, was actually doing anything.

## SEGMENT 14 — The seizure results — parent diary + EEG, the clearest signal

The most clinically clear-cut result reported in the paper concerns seizures. So let's spend a segment on that, because it is the place where the data are cleanest and the signal is strongest.

Before treatment, Mila was having approximately thirty seizures per day. Documented by two independent measurement methods. The first was a **parent diary** — her family kept careful daily records of every seizure, its time, its duration, its character. The second was periodic **EEG monitoring** — electroencephalography, in which electrodes are placed on the scalp to record the electrical activity of the brain, allowing seizures to be detected and characterized electrographically even if their behavioral manifestations are subtle.

Both methods agreed. Mila was having a lot of seizures. The pre-treatment baseline daily count averaged thirty-one point five per day. The average duration was about one hundred and eight seconds per seizure — nearly two minutes each. And across the day, when you summed up the total time she spent in seizure activity, it averaged about three point nine percent of her waking time. That's a substantial seizure burden — both quantitatively and qualitatively. Each one of those seizures was a discrete neurological event, and thirty of them per day means her brain was repeatedly being interrupted by abnormal electrical activity throughout every day of her life.

\[Pause.\]

After milasen treatment began, and as the loading phase progressed, the seizure numbers came down. The reductions reported in the paper, comparing the pre-treatment baseline to the post-treatment period:

The daily seizure count fell by approximately **sixty-three percent**, from thirty-one point five per day to eleven point seven per day. Average seizure duration fell by approximately **fifty-two percent**, from one hundred and eight seconds to fifty-two seconds. And the percentage of time spent in seizure activity fell by approximately **eighty-five percent**, from three point nine percent to zero point six percent.

Those reductions were achieved without any changes to her antiepileptic medications. Mila had been on a stable regimen of anti-seizure drugs throughout the pre-treatment period, and that regimen was not modified during the milasen treatment. So the reduction in seizures could not be attributed to a change in her anti-epileptic management. The most parsimonious explanation was that milasen itself was reducing her seizure activity.

\[Pause.\]

I want to dwell on why this matters, because if you are not used to reading clinical neurology literature, the size of these effects may not register the way it should. A sixty-three percent reduction in daily seizure count is, in clinical terms, a large, clinically meaningful effect. For most antiepileptic drugs, the bar to declare a drug "effective" in a clinical trial is a fifty percent responder rate — meaning, the proportion of patients whose seizure count drops by at least fifty percent compared to baseline. Achieving an actual sixty-three percent reduction in a patient with this severity of disease is, by the standards of the seizure literature, a strong response. The eighty-five percent reduction in time spent in seizure activity — which captures both the count reduction and the duration reduction together — is even more striking.

These are also numbers that are relatively hard to fake. A parent diary alone could conceivably be subject to bias, especially given the family's investment in the treatment working. But the diary numbers were corroborated by EEG, which is an objective electrophysiological measurement that does not depend on observer interpretation in the same way. When the diary and the EEG agree, the signal is real.

\[Pause.\]

The seizure result was, in the paper, the most clinically compelling piece of evidence that milasen was doing something. The other measures of Mila's clinical status — adaptive behavior scores on the Vineland-II scale, gross motor function on the GMFM-88 scale, developmental status on the Bayley scale, sensory thresholds — were more mixed in their findings. They mostly stabilized, in the sense that her previously declining trajectory on those measures flattened during the treatment period. She was not getting worse on those scales as fast as she had been before. But "not getting worse as fast" is a harder thing to interpret than a clear quantitative drop in seizure frequency, because the natural history of a neurodegenerative disease is variable and the period of observation was limited.

The seizures were the clearest signal. The other measures were consistent with stabilization but not, individually, statistically conclusive in a single patient. Together, the clinical picture during the first year of treatment was: seizures substantially better, other functional measures plateaued rather than continuing to decline.

That is a hopeful picture. It is also, by itself, not a cure. And the next segment is where we have to confront, honestly, what the drug did not do.

## SEGMENT 15 — The MRI truth — atrophy continued; this slowed decline, didn't reverse it

I have, until now, emphasized the encouraging findings of the paper. Now I have to tell you the hard part. Because the milasen paper, to its enormous credit, did not hide or downplay this finding, and we should not either.

In addition to the clinical assessments of behavior, motor function, and seizures, Mila underwent serial MRI scans of the brain during the treatment period. The purpose of these scans was to monitor whether the underlying neurodegeneration — the brain atrophy that had been documented before treatment — was changing in response to the drug. The hope, ideally, would have been that the atrophy would slow or even reverse. The reality is what the MRIs showed.

Across the first year of treatment, Mila's brain continued to lose volume. The atrophy progressed. Not, perhaps, at the same alarming rate it might have without treatment — that is hard to know for certain, because there is no untreated control Mila to compare to, and the natural history of late-stage CLN7 varies between patients. But the atrophy did not stop. It did not reverse. The neurons that were dying continued to die. The brain that had already shrunk continued to shrink.

\[Pause.\]

I want you to sit with that for a moment, because it is the central honest finding of this paper, and it shapes the interpretation of everything else.

The seizures got better. The drug was doing something — something measurable, something clinically meaningful, something that improved a major and disabling symptom of the disease. But the underlying process — the progressive death of neurons due to lysosomal dysfunction — continued. Milasen reduced one consequence of the disease. It did not stop the disease itself.

Why might that be? There are several plausible explanations, none of which are mutually exclusive, and none of which we can definitively distinguish from a single patient. One possibility is that by the time treatment began — at age six, with substantial brain atrophy already present — too many neurons had already been damaged or lost for any drug to reverse the trajectory. The drug could only act on cells that were still alive. Cells that had already died were beyond its reach. The window for meaningful intervention in a neurodegenerative disease may close earlier than we would like to admit.

Another possibility is that the drug was not reaching the entire affected brain in adequate concentrations. We discussed in Segment 13 that intrathecally delivered ASOs distribute unevenly within the CNS, with the brain getting roughly one-third the exposure of the spinal cord. Even at the three-times-nusinersen dose used for Mila, some regions of her brain may have received insufficient drug to drive meaningful splicing correction in neurons.

A third possibility is that the splicing correction itself, even at maximum, was incomplete. In Mila's fibroblasts, milasen tripled the proportion of correctly spliced transcripts, from eight percent to thirty-four percent. That is a substantial improvement, but it is not a full restoration. The treated cells were still producing a majority of mis-spliced transcripts. The amount of functional MFSD8 protein being produced from her SVA-carrying allele, even with milasen present, was still well below what a healthy person produces from a normal allele. Combined with whatever functional product might come from her c.1102G>C allele — which the literature suggests produces some small amount of partially functional protein — the total functional MFSD8 in her cells, on treatment, was probably still well below normal. Perhaps enough to reduce seizures and stabilize some functions, not enough to halt or reverse the underlying neurodegeneration.

And of course it is also possible that some combination of all three explanations is correct. Probably is correct.

\[Pause.\]

The clinical implication of the MRI finding is sobering. It tells us that even when an individualized ASO works — even when it does exactly what it was designed to do, with measurable benefit on at least one major symptom — that benefit may not equate to a cure. The disease may continue to progress, more slowly perhaps, but still toward the same ultimate endpoint. For a family considering an individualized therapy for a child with a fatal disease, this is not a small consideration. The drug may extend life. It may improve quality of life. It may reduce symptom burden. It may not, in the end, save the patient.

This is the moment in the lecture where I want to remind you of something I said in Segment 1. Mila died in February of 2021, two and a half years after the publication of the paper, at age ten. The MRI findings predicted, in some sense, what eventually happened. The brain volume loss that was visible on those serial scans continued through the years after the paper was published. The disease, slowed but not stopped, eventually reached the endpoint that severe neurodegenerative diseases reach.

The drug did not fail. By any reasonable measure of what it was designed to do, it succeeded — it corrected splicing, it reduced seizures, it stabilized some clinical measures, it was tolerated safely. But succeeding at what the drug was designed to do is not the same as curing the disease. And we should not, in our enthusiasm for the platform's promise, lose sight of that distinction.

## SEGMENT 16 — The honest discussion — what worked, what didn't, what we don't know

Let me synthesize, in this segment, the picture we have at the end of the paper. Because I want you to be able to articulate, in your own words, an honest summary of what milasen did and did not do.

**What worked.** The molecular diagnosis was achieved by whole-genome sequencing plus careful manual inspection of chimeric reads, identifying a previously undescribed SVA insertion in MFSD8 intron 6. The molecular mechanism — cryptic splice acceptor activation leading to a premature stop codon — was characterized in detail. A splice-modulating ASO was designed against the cryptic acceptor and validated in patient-derived fibroblasts, where it tripled correct splicing and rescued lysosomal phenotypes. An abbreviated preclinical safety program in rats, leaning heavily on platform safety data from nusinersen, established a tolerable dose range. The FDA authorized administration under Expanded Access. The drug was manufactured, dosed in escalating fashion intrathecally, and tolerated without serious adverse events. Seizures fell substantially. Other clinical measures stabilized.

**What didn't work, or didn't work fully.** The underlying brain atrophy continued. The drug slowed but did not halt or reverse the neurodegeneration. Mila's eventual clinical decline continued, more slowly than projected from her pre-treatment trajectory, but not stopped.

**What we don't know, and could not know, from this case.** Whether starting treatment earlier — at age two or three, before substantial brain damage — would have produced a much better outcome. Whether a higher dose of milasen would have produced more complete splicing correction with acceptable safety. Whether milasen would have eventually been more effective if continued for many years rather than the one year reported in the paper. Whether the residual mis-splicing — the sixty-six percent of transcripts that were still being incorrectly spliced even on treatment — was the limiting factor or whether other aspects of the disease biology were also at play. None of these questions can be answered from a single patient. They could only be answered by treating additional CLN7 patients with milasen, which has not, to my knowledge, been pursued at scale, because the SVA insertion that drives Mila's disease is itself unique to her family — no other reported CLN7 patient has the same molecular defect, so the same drug would not be expected to help them.

\[Pause.\]

This is the deepest difficulty with individualized therapy. **Every patient is, by definition, the only data point.** There is no clinical trial. There is no statistical analysis. There is no comparison group. There is just one patient, one drug, one set of observations, and a lot of uncertainty about whether the observed effects were truly drug-related or simply within the range of natural variation in this particular disease.

The paper's authors handle this difficulty with care. They report the seizure results, which are the most quantitative and the most clearly distinguishable from baseline noise. They report the other clinical measures honestly. They report the MRI findings without trying to spin them. They acknowledge the limitations of an N-of-1 study. They make a defensible scientific case that milasen had a real clinical effect on seizures, without overclaiming about disease modification.

For the reader of the paper, the appropriate stance is to accept the seizure data as strong evidence that the drug worked at the level of its proximate molecular and clinical targets, to accept the safety data as preliminary but encouraging, to accept the MRI data as a sobering reminder that the underlying pathology was not reversed, and to hold the entire case as a proof of concept for the approach rather than as definitive evidence of efficacy for this particular drug.

\[Pause.\]

There is one more piece of the honest discussion I want to flag, because it has become a significant issue in the field. **Individualized therapies do not, in general, have a clear path to becoming approved drugs.** Milasen will never be approved by the FDA as a marketable drug, because the patient population is one. There is no clinical trial that could be run. There is no regulatory pathway by which milasen could move from compassionate use in Mila to broader availability. The drug exists only for her, and now that she is gone, the drug exists only as a manufactured substance with no intended use.

That has implications for how we think about what we are doing when we develop these therapies. We are not, in the conventional sense, developing drugs. We are doing something more like custom molecular medicine — designing a specific molecule for a specific patient, manufacturing it for that patient, and dosing it to that patient under regulatory authorization that applies only to that case. The framework looks more like surgery, in a way, or like a custom prosthetic — a one-off intervention crafted for one person — than like the population-level drug development the pharmaceutical industry was built around. And the institutions, the funding mechanisms, the regulatory frameworks, the ethical structures, and the public expectations of what a "drug" is have all been built for the population model and have to be rebuilt, or at least supplemented, to handle the individualized model.

That rebuilding has begun. Which brings us to what happened after the paper was published.

## SEGMENT 17 — After the paper — Mila's death, the n-Lorem Foundation, the institutional response

The milasen paper was published in October of 2019. By that point, Mila had been receiving the drug for almost two years. The paper covered the first year of clinical observations in detail; her treatment continued after the paper appeared. Through 2019 and into 2020, milasen was administered to her on the every-three-month maintenance schedule. The reductions in seizures were maintained. The brain atrophy continued. Her overall clinical status declined gradually but, by report, more slowly than the natural history of CLN7 would have predicted.

In February of 2021, Mila Makovec died. She was ten years old. The drug had been administered to her for approximately three years. Her death was not unexpected, given the underlying disease, but it was, for her family and her medical team, the end of a long and extraordinary fight. The Mila's Miracle Foundation, which her family had founded to support the research that led to her treatment, continued — and continues — to support work on individualized therapies for rare diseases, and on Batten disease specifically.

\[Pause.\]

The institutional response to the milasen case was, in retrospect, more important than the individual treatment itself. Three things, in particular, happened in the wake of the paper.

First, **Boston Children's Hospital established an Oversight Committee on Personalized Experimental Therapeutics.** This is a formal institutional body that reviews proposals for individualized treatments developed at the hospital, ensuring that the scientific case, the safety package, the ethical considerations, and the patient and family engagement all meet a consistent standard. The committee exists because, immediately after milasen, the hospital was approached by many families asking whether their child could receive a similar custom drug. Some of those requests were appropriate. Some were not. Without a formal review structure, the hospital had no consistent way to decide which proposals to pursue and which to decline. The committee provides that structure.

Second, **the FDA began to develop more formal guidance for individualized ASO development.** In 2020 and 2021, the FDA issued draft guidance documents on the development of individualized ASOs for severely debilitating or life-threatening genetic diseases. The guidance documents specify what kinds of preclinical data the FDA expects for these therapies, how the abbreviated preclinical program should be structured, what safety monitoring is required, and what the regulatory submissions should contain. The guidance does not eliminate the FDA's case-by-case judgment, but it does provide a framework that researchers and clinicians can work within. The milasen case was the foundational example that the guidance was developed around.

Third — and this is, in my opinion, the most significant institutional outcome of the entire story — **the n-Lorem Foundation was founded.** n-Lorem is a non-profit organization founded by Stanley Crooke, who is the founder and former CEO of Ionis Pharmaceuticals, the company whose ASO chemistry was the platform for both nusinersen and milasen. Crooke recognized, watching the milasen case, that the technical capability to develop individualized ASOs existed but that the economic and institutional infrastructure to deliver them broadly did not. He founded n-Lorem specifically to fill that gap.

\[Pause.\]

Here is what n-Lorem does. It accepts applications from clinicians on behalf of patients with rare genetic diseases caused by mutations that, in n-Lorem's expert judgment, are likely to be amenable to ASO-based therapy. If the case is accepted, n-Lorem designs and manufactures a custom ASO for that patient, conducts the necessary preclinical safety work, supports the FDA submission, and provides the drug to the patient — for free, indefinitely, for the rest of the patient's life. The organization is funded by donations and by the in-kind contributions of Ionis Pharmaceuticals, which provides the technical infrastructure. As of this lecture, n-Lorem has accepted patients into its program and produced individualized ASOs for many of them, with treatments ongoing.

The institutional achievement of n-Lorem is that it has industrialized, more or less, what milasen demonstrated could be done as a one-off heroic effort. The case-by-case medical detective work of identifying a treatable molecular target, designing an ASO, validating it in patient cells, conducting preclinical safety studies, navigating the FDA, manufacturing the drug, and delivering it to the patient — this entire workflow has been turned, by n-Lorem, into a repeatable program. Not for every rare disease — only for those where the molecular pathology is amenable to ASO therapy and where the case meets n-Lorem's scientific criteria — but for a meaningful subset of patients who would otherwise have no path to a treatment.

Without milasen, n-Lorem might not exist, or might exist in a very different form. The case proved the concept. The foundation institutionalized the workflow.

\[Pause.\]

I want to name explicitly what the milasen paper accomplished, beyond its specific clinical findings, because the dominoes it knocked over have continued to fall in important ways.

**First, it proved that a brand-new drug can be designed and dosed in one patient in less than one year.** Twelve months from referral to first dose. That timeline had never been demonstrated for a brand-new drug for any disease. It is now the working assumption for individualized ASO programs.

**Second, it established that ASO chemistry is portable across diseases when the splice defect is amenable.** The nusinersen platform — the 2'-MOE phosphorothioate chemistry, the intrathecal delivery, the splice-modulating mechanism — was shown to work for a different gene, a different mutation, a different patient. The platform is genuinely portable, and that portability is the basis of every subsequent individualized splicing therapy.

**Third, it created the FDA template for individualized IND submissions.** The regulatory framework that handled milasen has become, with refinement, the framework that handles subsequent individualized ASO submissions. The pathway exists because milasen proved it could exist.

**Fourth, it triggered the rare-disease N-of-1 movement.** n-Lorem is the most visible example, but it is not the only one. Academic medical centers have begun to develop their own individualized therapy programs. Patient advocacy organizations have begun to fund similar efforts. The broader idea that individual patients with rare diseases might be candidates for custom drugs has moved from "unprecedented" to "occasionally feasible," which is an enormous shift in the moral landscape of rare disease medicine.

**Fifth, and finally, it showed the honest limits of the approach.** The paper did not overclaim. It reported that the drug slowed but did not stop the disease. It reported the MRI atrophy honestly. It set the appropriate expectation that individualized therapies can extend or improve lives without necessarily saving them, and that the earlier they are started, the more benefit they are likely to provide. That honesty has shaped how the field thinks about its own promise. We are not selling cures. We are, when we are lucky, buying time. And time, especially for a child with a fatal disease, is not nothing — but it is also not everything we might want.

## SEGMENT 18 — Where this leads — the next generation of N-of-1 therapies, splice-switching pipeline, the limits of personalization

Let me close with a look forward, because the milasen case has implications well beyond the one patient it was made for.

The first and most concrete extension of the milasen approach is the growing pipeline of **splice-switching ASOs for other rare genetic diseases**. Whenever a patient is found whose disease is caused by a splicing mutation — a cryptic splice site activated by an intronic insertion, a pseudoexon inserted by an aberrant splice event, an exon-skipping defect — the milasen template can in principle be applied. Design an ASO against the splice site that needs to be masked. Validate in patient cells. Run an abbreviated preclinical program. Submit to FDA under Expanded Access. Dose under careful monitoring. Each one of those steps was charted by the milasen case, and each one becomes faster and easier as the field accumulates experience.

A growing number of such cases have been published since 2019. Patients with rare forms of various neurological diseases, retinal diseases, and metabolic diseases have been treated with custom-designed splice-modulating ASOs. Most of these cases have not had as much publicity as milasen, but collectively they represent a steady accumulation of evidence that the platform is robust, the regulatory pathway is workable, and the clinical outcomes — variable, modest, sometimes meaningful, never curative on their own — are real.

\[Pause.\]

Beyond splice-switching ASOs, the broader concept of **N-of-1 personalized therapy** has begun to expand into other molecular modalities. RNA editing approaches that can correct specific base changes are being adapted for individual patients. CRISPR-based genome editing is being considered for individualized in-vivo applications. Antibody-drug conjugates tailored to specific patient mutations are being explored. The general principle that the milasen case crystallized — that for a small subset of patients with rare diseases caused by molecularly tractable mutations, a fully custom drug can be feasibly designed, made, and delivered — is now being explored across multiple drug classes, not just ASOs.

The bottleneck, increasingly, is not the technology. The chemistry of ASOs is mature. The bioinformatics for identifying tractable mutations are mature. The regulatory pathway, while still case-by-case, has been blazed. The bottleneck is the workflow — the ability to identify the right patients, the funding to support the custom development, the institutional capacity to deliver and monitor the therapies, and the ethical and economic structures to decide which patients to prioritize when the available capacity is, inevitably, less than the demand.

\[Pause.\]

I want to close with what I think the honest limits of personalization are, because the milasen story can be read in two opposite ways and I want you to be able to hold both readings at once.

The optimistic reading is that we have crossed a threshold. We are no longer limited, for rare disease patients, to whatever drugs the pharmaceutical industry has happened to develop. We can, when the molecular pathology is right, design a custom drug for a specific patient, and we can do it in months. This is a real and meaningful expansion of what medicine can offer. For families who would previously have had no option, the milasen story is a promise that an option may now exist.

The sobering reading is that "may now exist" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. The patients who benefit from this approach are a small subset of all rare disease patients. They are the ones whose mutations are amenable to ASO therapy or some other tractable modality. They are the ones whose disease is at a stage where slowing progression is still meaningful. They are the ones whose families have the resources and the connections to engage with the academic medical centers and non-profit foundations that can deliver this kind of care. And even for those who do qualify and do receive treatment, the results are usually not cures. They are clinical improvements. Sometimes substantial. Often partial. Rarely complete.

The honest answer to the question I asked at the beginning of this lecture — _can we build a medicine for one person?_ — is **yes, sometimes, for some patients, with limited expectations.** That is a much smaller answer than "yes, period," but it is much larger than the answer the field would have given before milasen. And it is, I think, the answer we should hold steadily as the field continues to grow.

\[Pause.\]

Mila Makovec lived ten years. The drug that was made for her did not save her life, but it improved her last years measurably, and the case that her treatment created has now become the template for an emerging field that is, today, designing and delivering custom drugs for patients who, a decade ago, would have had nothing offered to them at all. That is, in a way, what one patient's life can do for the patients who come after her. The Mila's Miracle Foundation. The n-Lorem Foundation. The Oversight Committee on Personalized Experimental Therapeutics. The published paper itself. The trained generation of researchers and clinicians and regulators who learned, from this case, that an individualized drug development workflow is possible. All of those are, in a sense, things she left behind. The drug bore her name. The platform she made possible is now bearing other patients' names too.

If you take one thing from this lecture, take that. The question of whether we can build a medicine for one person has been answered. The much harder question — how to make sure every patient who could benefit actually does, and how to keep our promises honest about what those medicines can and cannot do — is the question the next generation of this field, your generation if you go into medicine, will spend the next several decades answering.

Thank you for your attention today. We'll take questions after the break.

\[Pause. End of lecture.\]

## APPENDIX — Quick-reference summary (not for reading aloud)

**The patient.** Mila Makovec. Symptom onset age 3, diagnosis at 6, first dose late January 2018, paper published October 2019, died February 2021 at age 10.

**The disease.** Neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis, CLN7 subtype. Caused by loss of MFSD8, a lysosomal membrane transporter. Part of the Batten disease family of lysosomal storage disorders. Fatal pediatric neurodegeneration with vision loss, language regression, seizures, ataxia.

**The mutations.** Compound heterozygous.
- Allele 1: c.1102G>C — known pathogenic point mutation, detected by gene panel.
- Allele 2: ~2 kb SVA retrotransposon insertion in intron 6 — missed by panel, found by whole-genome sequencing + manual inspection of chimeric reads (poly-T tail, AGAGGG hexamer repeats, 14 bp target site duplication = SVA signature).

**The molecular mechanism.** SVA introduced a cryptic splice acceptor (i6.SA) ~119 bp upstream of real exon 7 acceptor → mis-splicing → premature stop codon → truncated non-functional protein.

**The drug.** Milasen (research name TY777). 22-nucleotide 2'-MOE phosphorothioate antisense oligonucleotide. Splice-modulating mechanism — masks the cryptic acceptor and nearby exonic splicing enhancers so the spliceosome uses the real exon 7 acceptor instead.

**Cell validation.** In patient fibroblasts, tripled correct splicing (8% → 34%); half-maximal effective concentration in nanomolar range; rescued lysosomal vacuoles, autofluorescence, autophagic flux.

**Preclinical.** Abbreviated rat intrathecal study at 0.06, 0.25, 1.0 mg. 0.06 mg uneventful; higher doses caused transient hindlimb weakness resolving by ~24 h. Platform safety inherited from nusinersen.

**Dosing.** Intrathecal injection. Loading: escalating 3.5 → 7 → 14 → 21 → 28 → 35 → 42 mg, plus two more at 42 mg, approximately every 2 weeks. Maintenance: 42 mg every ~3 months. 42 mg = molar equivalent of 3× nusinersen, chosen because brain distribution is ~1/3 of spinal cord. Total drug substance manufactured: ~18 g.

**Outcomes (first year).**
- No serious adverse events.
- Seizures: 63% reduction in daily count (31.5 → 11.7/day), 52% reduction in duration (108s → 52s), 85% reduction in % time in seizure (3.9% → 0.6%). No change in antiepileptic regimen.
- Vineland-II, GMFM-88, Bayley, sensory thresholds: stabilized rather than continuing to decline.
- MRI: continued brain volume loss — neurodegeneration slowed clinically but not reversed.
- CSF pharmacokinetics: dose-proportional rise, plateau at 18–27 ng/ml.

**Timeline (key dates).**
- Nov 2016 — clinical diagnosis.
- Jan 2017 — referral to Yu's lab.
- Feb 2017 — SVA mutation identified.
- May 2017 — patient fibroblasts established + splice defect identified.
- Aug 2017 — ASO hit.
- Oct 2017 — ASO function validated.
- Dec 2017 — rat toxicology study.
- Jan 2018 — FDA Expanded Access permission.
- Late Jan 2018 — first patient dose.
- Oct 2019 — NEJM paper published.
- Feb 2021 — Mila died at age 10.

**Precedent drug.** Nusinersen (Spinraza), approved Dec 2016 for spinal muscular atrophy. Same 2'-MOE phosphorothioate chemistry, same intrathecal delivery, same splice-modulating mechanism. The platform validation that made milasen's abbreviated preclinical program acceptable.

**Regulatory pathway.** FDA Expanded Access (compassionate use) IND. Authorized for use in Mila specifically, not approved as a drug. Boston Children's IRB review with extensive family consent process.

**Institutional aftermath.**
- Boston Children's Oversight Committee on Personalized Experimental Therapeutics.
- FDA draft guidance on individualized ASO development (2020–2021).
- n-Lorem Foundation founded by Stanley Crooke to provide custom ASOs for free to qualifying rare-disease patients.

**Five dominoes knocked over.**
1. A brand-new drug can be designed and dosed in one patient in <1 year.
2. ASO chemistry is portable across diseases when the splice defect is amenable.
3. FDA template established for individualized IND submissions.
4. Rare-disease N-of-1 movement triggered.
5. Honest limits established: slows progression, does not reverse damage already done.

**Master analogies.**
- ASO = a sticky note placed over a word in a sentence so the reader's eye skips over it.
- Splice-switching ASO = sticky note that hides one bad word so the splicing machinery picks the right boundary.
- SVA = a parasitic Word macro that has been copying itself for millions of years.
- Cryptic splice acceptor = a fake doorway intercepting traffic meant for the real exit.
- Panel testing vs whole-genome sequencing = checking a list of usual suspects vs reading the whole book.
- Compound heterozygous = two broken copies, broken in different ways, one from each parent.
- Lysosome = the cell's recycling center.
- N-of-1 trial = a clinical study with sample size 1.
- Intrathecal delivery = spinal injection bathing the brain and spinal cord directly.
- Diagnostic odyssey = 5–10 years and dozens of specialists before a diagnosis.
