## SEGMENT 1 — The fact that broke developmental biology

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. For the next two hours we're going to talk about one of the most consequential experiments in modern biology — an experiment that, in a single 2006 paper, overturned a century of consensus about how cells work and opened up a branch of medicine we are still, twenty years later, only beginning to explore. The topic is **induced pluripotent stem cells**, or iPSCs, and the central fact is this.

In 2006, a Japanese researcher named Shinya Yamanaka, working at Kyoto University, took an ordinary adult skin cell — a fibroblast, the kind of cell sitting in your dermis right now holding your skin together — and he forced that cell to express just four genes. Four. Not four hundred, not forty. Four. The genes were called Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, and c-Myc. Within a couple of weeks, that skin cell stopped behaving like a skin cell. It changed shape. It changed which genes it was reading. It started dividing faster. And when Yamanaka looked at it under the microscope and ran the molecular tests, the cell was indistinguishable from an embryonic stem cell. It could become any tissue in the body. It had, in effect, traveled backwards in time, from a fully committed adult cell to the pluripotent state that exists for a few days in an early embryo and then never, ever, comes back. Except now, apparently, it did come back. You just had to ask in the right way.

\[Pause.\]

That one fact rewrote the rules of developmental biology. For more than a century, the consensus was that differentiation — the process by which a fertilized egg becomes a liver cell, or a neuron, or a skin cell — was a one-way street. Cells went down the developmental tree, branch by branch, becoming more specialized at each step, and once they reached the end of a branch they were stuck there forever. A skin cell could only ever make more skin cells. A neuron, even worse, couldn't really divide at all. The developmental tape only played in one direction. Yamanaka showed that you could rewind it. With four ingredients, in a dish, in your lab. That's it. That's the discovery.

So here's the analogy I want you to carry through this lecture. Think of development like recording onto a cassette tape. The fertilized egg is a blank tape — it can become anything. As the embryo develops, the tape gets recorded on, segment by segment. By the time you're an adult, every cell in your body has a fully recorded tape that says "you are a skin cell" or "you are a heart muscle cell" or "you are a neuron in the visual cortex." Once recorded, the tape was thought to be locked. The cell could play its recording — do its job — but it couldn't be rerecorded. Yamanaka found the rewind button. **You can take an adult skin cell, rewind the developmental tape, and end up with a stem cell that could become anything.** And not metaphorically. Literally. In a dish. In about three weeks.

That's why this discovery won the Nobel Prize in 2012, shared with a British biologist named John Gurdon who had, fifty years earlier, done the foundational experiment that suggested rewinding was at least biologically possible. We'll come to Gurdon in a few minutes. But first I want you to have the destination in mind.

\[Pause.\]

Why does this matter beyond the textbook? Three reasons.

First, **ethics**. Before iPSCs, if you wanted pluripotent stem cells — cells that could become any tissue in the body, the kind of cells you'd want for regenerative medicine — your only source was a human embryo. You had to destroy a 5-day-old blastocyst to extract its inner cell mass. That created an unresolvable political and ethical battle that paralyzed stem cell research in the United States for most of the 2000s. After Yamanaka, you could derive pluripotent cells from anyone's skin biopsy. Same biology. No embryo. The ethical landscape changed overnight.

Second, **personalization**. Embryonic stem cells, even if you can get them, come from somebody else. If you transplant a tissue made from those cells into a patient, the patient's immune system will recognize the foreign tissue and reject it, exactly as it would reject a kidney transplant from a stranger. iPSCs, in principle, can be made from the patient's own skin. The resulting tissues are genetically the patient's own. No rejection. No lifelong immunosuppression. The dream of replacement parts grown from your own cells, on demand, in a dish.

Third, **disease in a dish**. You can now take a skin cell from a patient with Parkinson's disease, reprogram it to iPSCs, and then differentiate those iPSCs into the very dopamine neurons that are dying in that patient's brain. You have, sitting on your bench, a population of that patient's neurons, with their exact genetic background, available to study and to test drugs on. We could not do that before iPSCs. Patient brains are off-limits while patients are alive. iPSCs put the patient's brain cells in your dish.

\[Pause.\]

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, explain to a curious friend what an iPSC is and what Yamanaka actually did, using the rewind-the-tape analogy; two, sketch why the four factors OSKM are the four factors, and what they do once they're inside a skin cell; and three, look at a news headline about a new iPSC-based therapy — say a clinical trial for diabetes, or for macular degeneration, or for spinal cord injury — and have a real intuition for what's happening underneath — then we've succeeded.

Everything else is detail hung onto that scaffold. Let's start by going back to the conventional wisdom that Yamanaka broke, because you can't appreciate how shocking his result was without knowing what people believed before.

## SEGMENT 2 — The conventional wisdom: differentiation is one-way

For most of the twentieth century, developmental biology operated under a set of intuitions that were so deeply held they were rarely even articulated. They were just the background assumptions, the water the field swam in. Let me lay them out for you, because Yamanaka's work is, in essence, a frontal assault on every one of them.

Assumption one: **differentiation is one-way**. An adult cell — a skin fibroblast, a liver hepatocyte, a cardiac myocyte — has a fixed identity. You can lesion it, you can starve it, you can put it in any culture medium you like, and it will either die or stay what it is. It won't, of its own accord, decide to become something else. The cell's identity, once committed, was thought to be locked.

Assumption two: **the embryo is the only source of pluripotency**. The pluripotent state — the state where a cell can become any tissue in the body — exists only for a brief window in the early embryo. After about day six of human development, when the inner cell mass differentiates and starts forming the three germ layers — ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm — pluripotency is gone. The cells become multipotent at best, restricted to lineages within their germ layer. By the time you're an adult, the only stem cells in your body are tissue-specific adult stem cells with very limited capabilities. Hematopoietic stem cells in your bone marrow can make blood, but not liver. Intestinal stem cells can make gut, but not neurons. The fully pluripotent state — the ability to make _anything_ — is over by the second week of life. It's gone. You can't get it back.

Assumption three: **gene expression in adult cells is essentially permanent**. Once a cell has settled into being, say, a liver cell, the network of transcription factors maintaining its liver identity is self-reinforcing. The liver-specific genes are on, the alternative-lineage genes are silenced — packed into tight heterochromatin, marked with repressive methyl groups, locked away. To switch a liver cell to a neuron, you'd have to undo all of that silencing and impose a new pattern. It's not impossible in principle, but the architecture is so deeply baked in that nobody really expected it could be done with simple molecular tools.

\[Pause.\]

There were hints, even then, that the picture might not be quite that clean. Some of the hints came from natural biology. Salamanders, famously, can regenerate amputated limbs. The cells at the wound site dedifferentiate — they lose their adult identity, become a kind of stem cell called a blastema, and then redifferentiate into all the tissues of a new limb. So at least in salamanders, the developmental tape clearly could be partially rewound. And some hints came from experiments. As early as 1958, a developmental biologist named Frederick Steward took a fully differentiated carrot root cell and showed that, under the right conditions, it could grow back into an entire carrot plant. Differentiation was clearly reversible in plants. Whether it was reversible in animals — particularly in mammals — was a much more contested question.

But the dominant intuition in mammalian biology, the textbook view, the assumption underneath every grant proposal and every lecture, was that differentiation was, for all practical purposes, irreversible. Adult cells stay adult. That was the consensus.

And then John Gurdon, in 1962, decided to test it.

\[Pause.\]

The conceptual frame I want you to hold onto for the rest of this lecture is this: in any complex system, the **information** is one thing, and the **state** is another. The information in a cell is its DNA — the full blueprint, every gene, every regulatory element. Every cell in your body, with a few exceptions, has the same DNA. The state of a cell, on the other hand, is which subset of that information is being actively used — which genes are on, which are off, which transcription factors are bound to which regulatory regions, what the chromatin looks like, what proteins are floating around. Differentiation is, fundamentally, a change in state, not a change in information. The skin cell has the same DNA as the egg it came from. It just has a different program loaded onto that DNA.

If that's true — if the DNA is the same and only the state differs — then in principle, you should be able to change the state without changing the DNA. You should be able to load a different program. You should be able to take a skin cell and convince it, by changing which transcription factors are around, to become an embryonic stem cell, or even a neuron. The conventional wisdom said this couldn't be done. The conceptual logic said it should be possible. Yamanaka's work — and Gurdon's before him — was the experimental resolution of that tension.

Let's go meet Gurdon.

## SEGMENT 3 — Gurdon's frogs: the nucleus has all the information

It is 1962. A young British biologist named John Gurdon, working at Oxford, is about to do an experiment that nobody really believes will work. He's going to test the idea that the nucleus of an adult cell — the DNA inside it — still contains all the information needed to make a whole organism. If it does, you should be able to extract that nucleus, put it into an environment that knows how to use it, and watch a whole organism develop from a cell that used to be, say, a piece of frog intestine.

The technique is called **somatic cell nuclear transfer**, often abbreviated SCNT, and the procedure is exactly as audacious as it sounds. Gurdon took a frog egg — a fertilized egg, or rather an unfertilized one ready to be fertilized — and he used ultraviolet light to destroy its own nucleus. Now he had an enucleated egg, a cell with all the cytoplasm and machinery of an egg, but no DNA. Then he took an adult frog intestinal epithelial cell — a fully differentiated cell from the gut lining of a tadpole — and he extracted its nucleus with a tiny micropipette. He injected that adult nucleus into the enucleated egg.

Now think about what's happening biologically. The egg cytoplasm is exquisitely tuned to start a new organism. It's loaded with maternal RNAs, with transcription factors, with all the molecular machinery that normally interprets the fertilized nucleus and initiates development. But the nucleus it just received isn't the nucleus of an egg. It's the nucleus of an intestinal cell. It's been carrying around the program "be a gut lining cell" for the past several weeks. Its DNA has been packaged into a chromatin state that says "I am intestine." Its non-intestine genes are silenced. The egg cytoplasm has no idea any of this. The egg cytoplasm just sees a nucleus and starts trying to do what egg cytoplasm does — initiate embryonic development.

And here's the astonishing result. In a small fraction of cases — Gurdon was very honest that his efficiency was low — the egg succeeded. The intestinal nucleus, exposed to the egg cytoplasm's reprogramming factors, was forced to dismantle its intestinal program and adopt the embryonic program. The egg started dividing. It became an embryo. It hatched into a tadpole. The tadpole was a genetic clone of the original adult frog whose intestine had donated the nucleus. Gurdon had created a frog from a gut cell.

\[Pause.\]

Now stop for a second and think about what that experiment actually proved. It proved two things, simultaneously, that were absolutely foundational for the rest of this story.

First, it proved that the **DNA in an adult cell is not damaged or deleted during differentiation**. There was an alternative theory at the time — that during development, cells permanently lost the genes they didn't need anymore. Maybe an intestinal cell had thrown away its neuron genes and its muscle genes, and that's why it couldn't become a neuron. Gurdon's frog disproved that. If the intestinal nucleus, taken alone, could produce an entire frog — with its neurons and muscles and skin and bones — then the intestinal nucleus must still contain all of those genes. The information was still in there. The DNA blueprint is preserved. What differentiation does is restrict access to that blueprint, not erase parts of it.

Second, Gurdon's experiment proved that **the cytoplasmic environment around the nucleus can reprogram the nucleus's state**. The egg cytoplasm was the reprogramming factor. Whatever molecules the egg had — maternally deposited transcription factors, chromatin-remodeling enzymes, RNA, all of it — was apparently sufficient to wipe the intestinal program off the nucleus and impose the embryonic program. The nucleus, by itself, would have stayed intestinal. Put into the right environment, it became something else.

That insight — **environment can reprogram nucleus** — is the conceptual seed of Yamanaka's work, forty-four years later. Because if the egg cytoplasm contains a set of factors that can reprogram an adult nucleus, the obvious next question is: what are those factors? Can we identify them? Can we, instead of using whole egg cytoplasm, just add the right purified molecules directly to a cell and reprogram it that way? Yamanaka's screen — which we'll get to in Segment 5 — is, in essence, a search for those factors. Forty-four years to go from "egg cytoplasm reprograms a nucleus" to "four specific transcription factors reprogram a nucleus."

\[Pause.\]

Gurdon's work, by the way, took decades to be fully accepted. His efficiency was low. People assumed his successful clones must have come from contaminating embryonic cells in his preparations. It took years of replication and refinement before the field grudgingly admitted that yes, adult nuclei could be reprogrammed by egg cytoplasm, and yes, this told us something deep about how cells work. By the time he won the Nobel Prize alongside Yamanaka in 2012, his original frog experiment was fifty years old. Science can be patient.

One more thing about Gurdon, just because it's such a nice story. As a teenager in school, his biology teacher wrote on his report card that any ambition to do science was a "sheer waste of time" because Gurdon was so manifestly bad at the subject. He kept the report card framed on the wall of his office for the rest of his career. Pin that to the wall of your office too, if you ever feel discouraged.

## SEGMENT 4 — Dolly the sheep: it works in mammals

For thirty years after Gurdon's frog, the field operated under a strange asymmetry. SCNT clearly worked in amphibians. You could take a frog gut cell and grow a frog. But every attempt to do the same trick in mammals failed. People tried mice, they tried cows, they tried rabbits. The adult nuclei could not, apparently, be reprogrammed by mammalian egg cytoplasm. Or if they could, the embryos arrested early, or developed abnormally, or just didn't work. The leading hypothesis was that mammalian differentiation was somehow more deeply locked down than amphibian differentiation. Maybe the chromatin marks were tighter. Maybe the genome was more thoroughly silenced. Maybe mammals had evolved an irreversibility that frogs hadn't. There were lots of just-so stories.

Then, in 1996, a Scottish embryologist named Ian Wilmut, working at the Roslin Institute outside Edinburgh, did the experiment that had eluded everybody. He took a cell from the mammary gland of a six-year-old adult Finn Dorset ewe. He used techniques refined over decades to enucleate a Scottish Blackface egg — a different breed, so they could later prove the genetic origin of the clone — and he fused the adult mammary cell with the enucleated egg using an electric pulse. The fused cell, treated like a fertilized egg, was implanted into a surrogate Scottish Blackface ewe.

In July 1996, the surrogate gave birth to a lamb. Finn Dorset, like the cell donor, not Scottish Blackface like the surrogate. They named her **Dolly**, after Dolly Parton, because she had been cloned from a mammary cell. (Wilmut later said it was the only name they could think of that nobody objected to. The Parton tribute was always officially intentional.)

\[Pause.\]

Dolly was, biologically, an extraordinary creature. She was the first mammal ever cloned from an adult cell. Everything she was — her genome, her wool color, her cellular machinery — derived from a six-year-old mammary cell that had previously been doing nothing more glamorous than making milk proteins for a Scottish ewe. The experiment proved, unambiguously, that mammalian adult cells could be reprogrammed by egg cytoplasm. The barrier that had been hypothesized — that mammals were somehow special, that mammalian differentiation was irreversible — was not real. It was just technically hard. Wilmut had found the right protocol. Once you knew the protocol worked, the conceptual barrier collapsed.

Dolly herself had an unremarkable life by sheep standards — she lived to be six and a half, gave birth to several lambs of her own, and was eventually euthanized due to a lung infection that may or may not have been related to her cloned origins. The scientific debate about whether clones age prematurely is still unresolved. But the proof-of-concept was undeniable. A mammal had been cloned from an adult cell.

Now think about what this added to Gurdon's story. Gurdon had shown that frog nuclei retained all the genetic information needed to make a whole organism. Wilmut showed the same was true in mammals. The DNA in your skin cell — and in mine — contains, somewhere in it, the complete blueprint for a human being. It's been there the whole time, just silenced and locked away in chromatin patterns that say "you are skin." If you could find a way to release that silencing — by SCNT into an egg, or by some other trick — you could, in principle, reset the cell back to a pluripotent state.

\[Pause.\]

But SCNT, as a tool, has serious problems. You need eggs, which are scarce and ethically complicated in humans. You need micromanipulation skills that take years to master. The efficiency is brutally low — Dolly herself was the product of 277 attempts. And for clinical applications, you'd have to do it for every patient individually, which is operationally impossible. SCNT proved a principle. It didn't give us a usable technology. What the field needed was a way to reprogram a cell without an egg — without the messy biological cytoplasm transfer — using purified, defined molecular factors. If only we knew what those factors were.

This is the question that Shinya Yamanaka took up in the early 2000s. Gurdon and Wilmut had proven that reprogramming was possible. The mechanism — the actual identities of the molecules in egg cytoplasm doing the reprogramming — was still a mystery. Yamanaka asked: can we just figure out what those molecules are and add them directly?

Spoiler. He could. And the answer turned out to be smaller, and simpler, than anyone had any right to expect.

## SEGMENT 5 — Yamanaka's screen: from twenty-four candidates to four

Shinya Yamanaka in 2003 is a researcher in his early forties, working at the Nara Institute of Science and Technology in Japan, with a small lab and a big question. He has been working on embryonic stem cells, and he has noticed that ES cells have a very distinctive genetic signature — they express a particular set of genes that other cell types don't. Some of these genes encode transcription factors. Yamanaka makes a bet. He bets that if he can identify the right transcription factors — the ones that are uniquely active in ES cells and that maintain the pluripotent state — then forcing those same factors into an adult cell might re-impose the pluripotent state. The factors do the same job in the adult cell that they do in the ES cell. The adult cell becomes an ES cell.

It is, when you state it baldly, an audacious bet. It assumes that pluripotency is maintained by a small enough set of factors that you could identify them and recapitulate them. It assumes those factors are dominant — that adding them to an adult cell can overpower the adult cell's existing program, rather than being neutralized by it. It assumes the adult cell's chromatin, however locked-down it is, can be reopened by these factors. None of those assumptions were known to be true. Most of them, frankly, the field would have bet against.

But Yamanaka had a strategy. He sat down and made a list of every gene he knew of that was expressed specifically in embryonic stem cells and not in adult tissues. The list had twenty-four candidates. Twenty-four genes that the literature, and his own work, had implicated in the pluripotent state. Some were transcription factors. Some were chromatin remodelers. Some had unknown functions. He didn't know which subset, if any, would do the reprogramming. So he decided to test them all.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the experimental design, which is gorgeous in its simplicity. Yamanaka took mouse embryonic fibroblasts — adult cells from a mouse — and he engineered them so that there was a reporter gene that would activate only if the cell entered the pluripotent state. Specifically, he used a gene called Fbx15, which is on in ES cells and off in fibroblasts. He linked Fbx15 to an antibiotic-resistance marker. So: if the fibroblast somehow became pluripotent and activated Fbx15, it would also activate the antibiotic-resistance gene, and the cell would survive antibiotic treatment. If the cell stayed fibroblast, it would die. The selection was built in. You could throw millions of cells at the assay and have only the rare reprogrammed survivors come through.

Then Yamanaka took his twenty-four candidate genes and put all of them, simultaneously, into the fibroblasts using a retrovirus — a viral vector that integrates whatever DNA you put into it into the host genome and forces the cell to express it. Twenty-four genes, all forced on at once, in a fibroblast that has a reporter for pluripotency.

The result of that first experiment, in 2005, was electrifying. Some of the fibroblasts — a small fraction — survived the antibiotic selection. They had activated the Fbx15 reporter. They had, by the criterion he was using, become pluripotent. The twenty-four-factor cocktail worked.

But twenty-four factors is way too many. It's a sledgehammer. Yamanaka needed to know which ones, specifically, were doing the work. So he did the obvious next experiment: drop out one factor at a time. Make a cocktail of twenty-three factors, missing factor number one. Then twenty-three missing factor number two. And so on. For each subtraction, check whether the cells still get reprogrammed.

If a factor is essential, taking it out destroys reprogramming. If a factor is dispensable, taking it out leaves reprogramming intact. By systematically subtracting factors, Yamanaka could identify which ones were doing the actual work.

He narrowed it from twenty-four to ten. Then from ten to four.

The four were **Oct4** (also called Pou5f1), **Sox2**, **Klf4**, and **c-Myc**. We now call this combination **OSKM** — Oct, Sox, Klf, Myc — pronounced "ozzkim," like a name. These four factors, together, were sufficient to reprogram an adult fibroblast back into something that looked, by every test Yamanaka could throw at it, like an embryonic stem cell.

\[Pause.\]

Now, what do these four factors actually do? Let me give you a brief biological tour, because the identities of OSKM are not random — they each have a job.

**Oct4** is the master regulator of pluripotency. It's a transcription factor that, in early embryos, is required to maintain the inner cell mass — the population of cells in the early blastocyst that will become the whole organism. Without Oct4, embryos arrest at a very early stage. Within an ES cell or an iPSC, Oct4 sits on top of a giant network of pluripotency genes, keeping them all turned on, while simultaneously suppressing genes that would push the cell toward any specific lineage. It is, more than any single other factor, _the_ pluripotency gene. Yamanaka quickly figured out that Oct4 was indispensable — you can swap out the other three, sometimes, but Oct4 has to be there.

**Sox2** is Oct4's partner. The two physically bind to DNA together at many pluripotency genes — they're co-regulators, and they work as a heterodimer. Sox2 stabilizes Oct4 on its target sites, broadens the set of genes the two of them turn on, and reinforces the pluripotency program. Like Oct4, Sox2 is essential — you can't reprogram without it.

**Klf4** is a transcription factor that does something a little different. It supports cell proliferation, it helps maintain a particular epithelial-like state that reprogrammed cells need to pass through, and it contributes to the chromatin opening that lets Oct4 and Sox2 access genes they couldn't reach in the original fibroblast state. Klf4 is somewhat replaceable — closely related family members can sub in — but functionally something Klf-like is needed.

**c-Myc** is the cell-cycle driver. We talked about Myc back in the cancer lecture — it's a transcription factor that pushes cells to grow and divide, and it's one of the most frequently mutated oncogenes in human cancers. Why is Myc here? Because reprogramming requires the cell to divide many times — chromatin marks get reset during division, and the cell needs to be cycling rapidly for the OSK factors to do their work. Myc revs up the cell cycle. It also helps open up chromatin in a more general way, making the genome more accessible to the other factors. Myc is the accelerator pedal. Without it, reprogramming still happens, but much more slowly. With it, things go fast — and, as we'll see, with some risk.

\[Pause.\]

So that's the cast. Four transcription factors, each doing a specific job, together able to take a skin cell and reset it to pluripotency. Yamanaka submitted his paper to Cell in 2006, the editors accepted it, and in August of that year, the world of stem cell biology had to be substantially rewritten.

Let's spend the next segment on what the actual paper showed.

## SEGMENT 6 — The 2006 Cell paper: mouse iPSCs

The paper is titled "Induction of Pluripotent Stem Cells from Mouse Embryonic and Adult Fibroblast Cultures by Defined Factors." It's published in Cell in August 2006. Two authors: Kazutoshi Takahashi and Shinya Yamanaka. Takahashi was the postdoc who did most of the experimental work; Yamanaka was the senior author who designed the strategy. The paper is twelve pages long, dense with data, and it is, without exaggeration, one of the most consequential biology papers of the twenty-first century.

Here's what they showed.

They took mouse fibroblasts — both embryonic fibroblasts and adult tail-tip fibroblasts — and they introduced the four factors using a retroviral vector. After several weeks in ES cell culture conditions, small colonies appeared that looked like ES cells. They were morphologically distinct from the original fibroblasts. They had a high nucleus-to-cytoplasm ratio. They grew in tight, dome-shaped colonies. They expressed the ES cell markers Yamanaka had been screening for.

Then they ran the full battery of pluripotency tests.

**Test one: gene expression.** Did the reprogrammed cells turn on the genes that ES cells turn on, and turn off the genes fibroblasts had been expressing? Answer: yes. The transcriptomes — the full pattern of which genes are being read by the cell — shifted dramatically from a fibroblast pattern to an ES-cell-like pattern.

**Test two: morphology and self-renewal.** Did the cells look like ES cells under the microscope, and did they grow indefinitely in culture without differentiating? Answer: yes. They could be passaged many times — meaning split into new dishes over and over again — without losing their identity.

**Test three: differentiation potential in vitro.** If you put ES cells in a dish without the right maintenance factors, they spontaneously start differentiating into all three germ layers — ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm — forming structures called embryoid bodies. Did the reprogrammed cells do this? Answer: yes. They formed embryoid bodies with cells expressing markers of all three germ layers.

**Test four: teratoma formation.** This is the gold-standard test for pluripotency, and I'll spend some time on it later because it's both elegant and a little gruesome. If you inject true pluripotent cells under the skin of an immunocompromised mouse, they form a benign tumor called a teratoma — a chaotic mass containing cells from all three germ layers. Bone, gut lining, hair follicles, neurons, muscle, all jumbled together in a single tumor. It looks like an embryo had a really bad day. Did Yamanaka's reprogrammed cells form teratomas? Answer: yes. They produced classic three-germ-layer teratomas indistinguishable from those formed by ES cells.

\[Pause.\]

By every test the field had developed, these reprogrammed cells were pluripotent. Yamanaka named them **induced pluripotent stem cells**, or iPS cells, or iPSCs. The name distinguishes them from ES cells — embryonic stem cells, which come from embryos — and emphasizes that pluripotency was _induced_, manufactured, in cells that had previously been something else.

The first round of iPSCs, frankly, were not quite as good as ES cells. They were generated using a less stringent reporter — the Fbx15 system, which selects for cells that have started down the path of pluripotency but doesn't guarantee they've fully reached it. The 2006 cells couldn't, when injected into early embryos, contribute to all tissues of the resulting mouse — a more stringent test of pluripotency called germline transmission. So there was some immediate skepticism in the field. Maybe these weren't really pluripotent. Maybe they were just partially reprogrammed cells that had picked up some ES cell features without truly resetting all the way.

Yamanaka heard the criticism, went back to the bench, and a year later — in 2007 — published a follow-up paper using a more stringent reporter, this time the Nanog gene, which is on only in fully pluripotent cells. Cells selected with the Nanog reporter were genuinely indistinguishable from ES cells. They contributed to all tissues of chimeric mice. They could even produce sperm and eggs and pass on the iPSC genome to offspring — the ultimate test, called germline transmission. iPSCs were the real deal.

\[Pause.\]

The community's response to the 2006 paper was, initially, somewhere between skepticism and disbelief. The result was so unexpected, the methodology so seemingly simple, the implications so vast, that people assumed there must be something wrong with it. Maybe Yamanaka was using contaminated ES cells. Maybe the retroviruses were doing something unexpected. Maybe the reprogramming was incomplete and Yamanaka was overclaiming.

Within months, those doubts were settled by an unusual mechanism: dozens of labs around the world tried to repeat the experiment. The protocol was published in detail. The four factors were known. The cells were available. And it worked. In lab after lab, putting OSKM into fibroblasts and waiting several weeks produced colonies of cells that looked, behaved, and tested like ES cells. The reproducibility of Yamanaka's result was, by 2007, undeniable. He had not made it up. The biology was real.

And the question on everyone's mind, immediately, was: does this work in human cells? Because mouse biology and human biology are similar in many ways but they're not identical. ES cells from the two species are subtly different. Maybe human cells were locked down in ways mouse cells weren't. Maybe OSKM wouldn't be enough. Maybe you'd need different factors entirely. The race to make human iPSCs was on, and it was going to be settled with surprising speed.

## SEGMENT 7 — Human iPSCs and the cancer problem with c-Myc

A year and a half after the mouse paper, in November 2007, two papers appeared back-to-back, one in Cell and one in Science, reporting the successful reprogramming of human fibroblasts to pluripotency. The Cell paper was from Yamanaka's group again. The Science paper was from a completely independent group — Junying Yu and James Thomson at the University of Wisconsin. Thomson, by the way, was the researcher who had first derived human embryonic stem cells in 1998, almost a decade earlier. He was, in some sense, the dean of the field, and he had been quietly working on his own reprogramming approach in parallel with Yamanaka. The two groups arrived at the same destination at almost the same moment, by different routes.

Yamanaka's human iPSC paper used the same four factors he had used in mouse — OSKM. He took skin fibroblasts from a 36-year-old woman, introduced the four factors via retroviruses, and after about a month obtained colonies that looked, behaved, and tested like human ES cells. By every available assay — gene expression, surface markers, embryoid body formation, teratoma formation in immunocompromised mice — these cells were human pluripotent stem cells, derived from an adult skin biopsy. No embryos involved. No ethical impasse.

Thomson's paper used a different cocktail. Instead of OSKM, Thomson's team used Oct4, Sox2, Nanog, and Lin28. The first two are the same. The second two are different — Nanog is another pluripotency transcription factor (the same one Yamanaka had used as a reporter for full reprogramming), and Lin28 is an RNA-binding protein that affects which mRNAs get translated in the cell. The fact that two completely different sets of four factors could both reprogram human fibroblasts told the field something important: there isn't just one recipe. There's a network of pluripotency-supporting factors, and you can get into that network from multiple entry points. As long as you turn on enough of the right master regulators, the network self-assembles and pulls the cell up into the pluripotent state.

\[Pause.\]

Now, the human iPSC papers had immediate practical impact, but they also had a problem. The problem was c-Myc.

Remember from the cancer lecture: c-Myc is one of the most potent oncogenes in human biology. It's overexpressed in a huge number of cancers — Burkitt lymphoma, neuroblastoma, many breast and colon cancers. Forcing c-Myc into a cell is, biologically, a dangerous thing to do. And Yamanaka's reprogramming method did exactly that. The retrovirus integrated the c-Myc gene into the cell's chromosomes, where it stayed permanently, even after reprogramming was complete. The iPSC, in its final state, was a cell that had been forced to carry a viral integration of a powerful oncogene.

This was not a hypothetical concern. When Yamanaka generated chimeric mice from his early iPSCs — by injecting them into embryos and letting them contribute to all the tissues of the developing mouse — about 20% of those mice developed tumors. The tumors traced back to reactivation of the c-Myc transgene in iPSC-derived tissue. Reprogramming was working, but it was also seeding cancer.

This was, obviously, a problem for any clinical aspiration. If you wanted to make patient-specific iPSCs and then differentiate them into, say, dopamine neurons and inject them into a Parkinson's patient's brain, you absolutely did not want those cells to carry an integrated oncogene that might reactivate at any point and turn the implanted tissue into a tumor.

The field responded in two ways. First, people showed that you could actually leave c-Myc out and still get reprogramming. It just took longer and the efficiency dropped. Three factors — Oct4, Sox2, Klf4 — could do it, slowly and inefficiently. Better than nothing. Second, and more important, the entire delivery mechanism — the retroviral integration of the factors — had to be replaced. If the four genes were never integrated into the iPSC's chromosomes in the first place, then there would be no permanent oncogene risk. The cells could be made truly safe.

That sent the field into a multi-year sprint to develop **integration-free reprogramming**. Several different approaches emerged, and they're worth a segment of their own.

## SEGMENT 8 — Integration-free reprogramming: getting the virus out of the picture

The fundamental problem is this: you need to express OSKM transiently inside a cell for a few weeks, long enough to flip it into pluripotency. You don't need those factors there forever — once the cell has reset into the pluripotent state, its own endogenous Oct4, Sox2, and so on come on and maintain pluripotency for free. You only need the exogenous factors as a kick-start. Once the engine catches, you can turn off the starter motor.

The original retroviral approach was a hammer. It permanently integrated the four factor genes into the cell's chromosomes. Permanent expression. Permanent risk of insertional mutagenesis. Permanent presence of integrated viral DNA in any cell you'd later try to use clinically. We needed something gentler.

The first attempt was **episomal plasmids**. Plasmids are pieces of circular DNA — you remember from the plasmids lecture — that can exist inside a cell without integrating into the chromosomes. Researchers built plasmids carrying the four reprogramming factors and transfected them into fibroblasts. The plasmids floated in the nucleus, the genes were expressed, the cell got reprogrammed, and then over time, as the iPSCs divided, the plasmids got diluted out and disappeared. The final iPSC contained no transgene. Clean. Safe. The downside: efficiency was low, even lower than retroviral reprogramming. But it worked.

Then came **Sendai virus**. Sendai is an RNA virus that replicates entirely in the cytoplasm of the cell — it never goes into the nucleus, never integrates into chromosomes. You can package the four reprogramming factors into a modified Sendai virus, infect fibroblasts with it, and the virus will produce the OSKM proteins from cytoplasmic RNA. The cell gets reprogrammed. Then, since Sendai is an RNA virus that doesn't integrate, the viral RNA gets degraded and diluted out over cell divisions, and a few weeks later the iPSC is completely free of any viral material. Sendai reprogramming is now one of the most widely used commercial approaches — kits are sold by major suppliers, and most academic labs that make iPSCs use Sendai. The analogy is: **deliver the factors with a virus that doesn't integrate, so the iPSC doesn't have any virus DNA left behind**.

\[Pause.\]

Other approaches followed. **Synthetic mRNA reprogramming**, where you literally make mRNA molecules encoding the four factors and deliver them directly into the cell, day after day, for a few weeks. The mRNA gets translated into protein, does its work, and then degrades within hours — there's no DNA involved at all, no possibility of integration. The downside is you have to redose the cells repeatedly, because mRNA is short-lived. The upside is the iPSC is completely synthetic-material-free.

**Protein delivery** — where you produce purified OSKM proteins and somehow get them into the cells, either using cell-penetrating peptide tags or special delivery techniques. This works in principle but is technically harder and has been less widely adopted.

**microRNA reprogramming** — where short RNA molecules that regulate gene expression are used to shift the cell toward pluripotency, sometimes alongside reduced numbers of the OSKM factors.

And then, in some of the most striking recent work, **chemical reprogramming** — where small-molecule drugs alone, with no transcription factors at all, can drive cells through pluripotency. The chemical cocktails were developed over a decade of careful screening, mostly out of labs in China, and they showed that the OSKM logic could be replaced entirely by a recipe of small molecules that hit the same chromatin and signaling targets. That work is, in some ways, even more remarkable than Yamanaka's original — it suggests that pluripotency is not even fundamentally about transcription factor identity; it's about the chromatin state those factors create, and you can recreate that state in other ways.

\[Pause.\]

Today, in 2026, the standard practice in a well-run iPSC lab is to use Sendai virus or episomal plasmids for reprogramming. The resulting iPSCs are integration-free, with no permanent transgenes, no viral DNA in the genome. They are, in principle, safe enough to imagine using in patients. The c-Myc cancer worry that hung over the field in 2007 has, by careful engineering of delivery methods, been substantially defused.

## SEGMENT 9 — Reprogramming efficiency: the rare-event problem

I've been talking as if you put OSKM into a fibroblast and it becomes an iPSC. That's true, on average, but it's important to understand the statistics, because the statistics tell you something deep about what reprogramming is actually doing.

When you transfect a million fibroblasts with the four factors, you don't get a million iPSCs three weeks later. You get, depending on the method, somewhere between **one in a thousand and one in ten thousand** cells successfully completing reprogramming. The rest either die, stay fibroblasts, or get partially reprogrammed but stuck in some intermediate state that isn't quite pluripotent and isn't quite fibroblast. The successful conversion rate is, frankly, terrible. Less than 1%, often far less.

Here's the analogy. Reprogramming is like **finding a four-leaf clover by stepping on a field of grass**. You walk into the field — you treat the cells with OSKM. Most of the plants are three-leaf clovers — most of the cells stay fibroblasts. But here and there, by some combination of luck and the right local conditions, you find a four-leaf. Those are your iPSCs. They are rare. They are not designed for. You're just searching the field with selection conditions that let you spot them.

\[Pause.\]

Why is the efficiency so low? Several reasons, and they tell you something about the biology.

First, **chromatin is hard to remodel**. The fibroblast genome is locked down in a particular configuration. Heterochromatin marks. DNA methylation. Specific histone modifications. To erase all of that and recreate the open chromatin state of an ES cell, the OSKM factors have to physically dismantle a lot of repressive machinery. That takes many cell divisions. Many opportunities to fail. Many places where the cell gets stuck partway and never completes the journey.

Second, **the cell has to divide many times**. Cell division is when chromatin gets relaxed enough to be remodeled. If a cell isn't actively dividing, the reprogramming factors can sit there indefinitely and nothing happens. That's part of why c-Myc helps — it forces the cell cycle forward. Cells that don't divide enough don't reprogram.

Third, **stochastic gene expression**. Even when the factors are inside the cell, whether or not they bind to a specific target gene at a particular moment is a stochastic event — driven by molecular noise, by which other proteins happen to be around, by random thermal motion. For a cell to fully reprogram, it has to thread a long series of these stochastic events successfully — get the right genes on at the right times, get the right genes off, navigate the chromatin state space, and arrive at the pluripotent attractor. The fraction of cells that thread the whole needle is small.

Fourth — and this is the deepest reason — **becoming pluripotent is, in some sense, a fundamentally improbable event**. The pluripotent state is one specific attractor among many possible cellular states. Most random walks away from the fibroblast state lead to bad outcomes — apoptosis, senescence, partial reprogramming. The walks that lead specifically to pluripotency are a thin subset. Reprogramming efficiency reflects how thin that subset is.

\[Pause.\]

What this means in practice is that iPSC generation is, by necessity, a **selection process**. You start with many cells, you let the rare successful events happen, and you isolate the colonies that successfully reprogrammed. Each colony is, in principle, the descendant of a single rare reprogrammed cell. You pick those colonies, expand them, and call them iPSC lines. Each line is, in effect, the lineage of one four-leaf clover that you happened to step on.

This has practical consequences. It means iPSC lines from the same starting cells aren't necessarily identical. Each one went through a different stochastic path through the reprogramming landscape. They might differ in subtle ways — in their epigenetic state, in their differentiation propensities, in their behavior under stress. Good iPSC practice involves making many lines from the same starting cells and characterizing each one carefully, so you know which line behaves how.

The low efficiency is also why partial reprogramming has become an interesting topic in its own right — which we'll come to in Segment 16. If most cells, when exposed to OSKM, don't go all the way to pluripotency but do shift their state somewhat, can we use that partial shift for something useful, like reversing aging in adult tissues without actually making them pluripotent? Spoiler: the answer is maybe yes.

## SEGMENT 10 — iPSC quality control: what makes a good iPSC

We're back. Hope you stretched your legs. Let's pick up with a question that sounds boring but is actually critical for everything that follows: how do you know if an iPSC line is any good?

This sounds like a stupid question — surely you can just look at the cells and tell? But it turns out reprogrammed cells exist on a spectrum. Some are fully pluripotent, indistinguishable from ES cells. Some are partially reprogrammed, expressing some pluripotency markers but not others, with chromatin states halfway between fibroblast and ES. Some have acquired random mutations during the reprogramming process — chromosomal abnormalities, copy number changes, point mutations. Some carry residual epigenetic memory of the cell they came from, which biases what they like to differentiate into. A real iPSC line, before you use it for anything, has to pass a suite of quality control tests.

Here are the main ones.

**Test one: morphology.** Under the microscope, true iPSC colonies look very distinctive. They're tight, dome-shaped, with crisp edges. Individual cells inside the colony are small with a high nucleus-to-cytoplasm ratio. If the colonies look ragged, or if cells are spilling out and flattening, that's a sign the line isn't fully pluripotent or has started differentiating. Experienced iPSC technicians can tell, at a glance, whether a colony looks right.

**Test two: pluripotency marker expression.** You stain the cells for proteins that should be on in pluripotent cells — Oct4 (the endogenous one, not the transgene), Sox2, Nanog, SSEA-4, TRA-1-60. Bona fide iPSCs express all of these robustly. Lines that have lost expression of one or more pluripotency markers are suspect.

**Test three: differentiation potential — the embryoid body assay.** You let the iPSCs aggregate in suspension culture without the factors that maintain pluripotency. They spontaneously form little balls called embryoid bodies, and within those balls they start differentiating into all three germ layers. You then stain the embryoid bodies for ectoderm markers, mesoderm markers, endoderm markers. A real iPSC line gives you all three. A line that's restricted — that only differentiates well into, say, ectoderm — is partially functional but not fully pluripotent.

**Test four: the teratoma assay.** This is the gold standard, and it's a little gruesome. You take a clump of iPSCs and inject them under the skin or into the testis of an immunocompromised mouse. Over the next six to eight weeks, the cells form a benign tumor — a teratoma — that grows into a chaotic mass containing tissues from all three germ layers. You section the tumor, stain it, and look for the diagnostic features: glandular structures (endoderm), muscle and cartilage (mesoderm), neural rosettes and pigmented retinal epithelium (ectoderm). If the teratoma contains all three germ layers represented by recognizable tissue, the cells were pluripotent. The analogy is: **the teratoma is the smoking gun for pluripotency — inject iPSCs into a mouse, and a benign tumor forms with bone, gut, hair, neurons, all three germ layers**. It's a chaotic mini-embryo that grew in the wrong place. Disturbing, but diagnostic.

\[Pause.\]

**Test five: transcriptome comparison.** You do RNA sequencing on the iPSCs and on reference ES cells. The two transcriptomes should be very similar — most genes expressed at similar levels, similar pluripotency signature. If the iPSC transcriptome shows hallmarks of the original fibroblast state lingering — fibroblast genes still on, ES genes incompletely activated — that's incomplete reprogramming.

**Test six: karyotype.** You do a chromosome spread and check that the iPSC has the normal complement of 46 chromosomes, in the right configuration. Reprogramming, and prolonged culture afterward, can introduce chromosomal abnormalities — trisomies, large deletions, translocations. Lines with abnormal karyotypes are not safe to use for downstream applications, especially clinical ones.

**Test seven: integration-free verification.** If you used Sendai virus or episomal plasmids, you check by PCR that the iPSC has actually lost the reprogramming vector. If the Sendai genome is still detectable, you let the cells grow for more passages until it's gone.

**Test eight: genomic integrity at higher resolution.** Modern iPSC lines, especially those intended for clinical use, get whole-genome sequenced to look for point mutations and copy number variations introduced during reprogramming. Some mutations are tolerable; mutations in cancer driver genes are not.

\[Pause.\]

A good iPSC line, by 2026 standards, has passed all of these tests. It has normal morphology, expresses all pluripotency markers, differentiates into all three germ layers in embryoid bodies, forms a clean three-germ-layer teratoma, has a transcriptome very close to ES cells, has a normal karyotype, has no detectable reprogramming vector, and has no mutations in cancer genes. Making such a line takes months and costs tens of thousands of dollars. For research use, you can be a little looser. For clinical use — for cells you're about to put into a patient — every test has to be done, with documentation, under regulatory standards. The bar is appropriately high.

The reason the bar matters: if you skip QC and just use whatever you get, you can end up with cells that behave strangely, differentiate poorly, contain dangerous mutations, or worst of all, contain a small fraction of incompletely reprogrammed cells that will form tumors when transplanted. The history of the field has several embarrassing episodes where promising clinical programs were derailed by inadequate QC on the starting cells. Don't be that lab.

## SEGMENT 11 — Directed differentiation: making neurons, hearts, livers, and beta cells

OK, so now you have a beautiful iPSC line. It's fully pluripotent. It can become anything. The question is: how do you make it become something _specific_? You don't want a chaotic teratoma in a dish. You want, say, dopamine neurons. Or cardiomyocytes. Or insulin-producing beta cells. How do you guide the iPSC down a particular branch of the developmental tree, while keeping it off all the other branches?

This is the problem of **directed differentiation**. And here's the elegant trick. Development, in an embryo, is a sequence of decisions. The early embryo has cells that decide ectoderm vs. mesoderm vs. endoderm. Then those cells make further decisions — endoderm becomes lung, liver, pancreas, gut. Each decision is mediated by particular signaling molecules — cytokines, growth factors, morphogens — that the embryo deploys at specific times and concentrations. Wnt signals. BMP signals. Activin/Nodal signals. FGF signals. The embryo is, in effect, running a program of "add this signal for two days, then switch to that signal for three days, then add this other signal for a week."

Directed differentiation works by **recapitulating that embryonic program in the dish**. You take the iPSCs, and you give them the same signaling cues that an embryo would give them, in the same sequence, at the same approximate timing. Day one, you add this cytokine. Day three, you switch to that one. Day seven, you add another. By following a recipe that mimics natural development, you guide the cells down the developmental tree to the lineage you want.

The analogy is: **the reverse trick — give the iPSC the right cytokine cocktail and timing, and it goes BACK down the developmental tree toward whatever cell type you want**. Reprogramming rewound the tape; directed differentiation plays it forward again, but along the branch you've chosen.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you some examples.

**Cardiomyocytes** — heart muscle cells. The recipe involves adding Activin A and BMP4 for a couple of days to push the cells toward mesoderm, then adding Wnt inhibitors to drive them toward cardiac mesoderm, then giving them time and the right metabolic environment to mature into beating cardiomyocytes. The remarkable thing about iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes is that they actually beat. You can look at the dish under a microscope and see the cells contracting rhythmically, dozens of times per minute, like tiny disorganized hearts. They have action potentials. They respond to drugs the way real cardiomyocytes do. They are heart cells.

**Dopamine neurons** — the neurons that die in Parkinson's disease. The protocol involves pushing cells toward neural ectoderm, then specifying them toward midbrain identity using sonic hedgehog and FGF8, then maturing them with neurotrophic factors. After several weeks, you get neurons that produce dopamine, express the markers of midbrain dopamine neurons, and fire action potentials. They are functionally similar to the neurons in your substantia nigra.

**Hepatocytes** — liver cells. The recipe pushes cells through endoderm using Activin A, then to hepatic endoderm with BMP and FGF, then to mature hepatocytes with HGF and oncostatin M. The resulting cells produce albumin, do drug metabolism, and store glycogen. They behave a lot like liver.

**Beta cells** — insulin-producing pancreatic islet cells. The recipe is one of the most complex in the field, taking several weeks and going through pancreatic endoderm, pancreatic progenitor, endocrine progenitor, immature beta cell, and finally mature glucose-responsive insulin-secreting beta cell. Each step uses a different cocktail. The final cells, in the best protocols, can sense glucose and release insulin in response — they are functional beta cells, derived from someone's skin.

\[Pause.\]

The protocols for these differentiations are mostly worked out by a combination of looking carefully at embryonic development, trying many cocktails, and slowly optimizing. Today, you can buy commercial differentiation kits for many of these cell types — order the kit, follow the protocol, and a few weeks later you have functional iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes or neurons in your dish.

But there's an important caveat. iPSC-derived cells are usually **immature**. They have the markers and many of the functions of the target cell type, but they're often more like fetal versions of the cell than fully adult ones. iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes don't quite have the metabolic profile or the structural organization of adult cardiomyocytes. iPSC-derived hepatocytes lack some adult drug-metabolizing enzymes. iPSC-derived beta cells are not quite as glucose-responsive as adult human beta cells. Maturing these cells fully — closing the gap between fetal-like and adult — is an active area of research. Tricks like three-dimensional culture, mechanical stimulation, longer maturation times, and co-culture with supporting cells are all helping, but it's not a fully solved problem.

Still, even immature, these cells are extraordinarily useful. They give you, for the first time, an essentially unlimited supply of patient-specific human cells of any type you want. That capability transforms what you can do in research, in drug screening, and — increasingly — in the clinic.

## SEGMENT 12 — Disease in a dish: modeling Parkinson's, ALS, and diabetes

One of the most powerful applications of iPSCs is something the field calls **disease modeling**, or more colorfully, "disease in a dish." Here's the idea.

You have a patient with a genetic disease. Parkinson's caused by a mutation in LRRK2. ALS caused by a mutation in SOD1. Type 1 diabetes — though that's not strictly genetic, the autoimmune aspect makes it harder, but bear with me. You want to study what's wrong with this patient's affected cells — the dopamine neurons, the motor neurons, the beta cells. But you can't biopsy a living person's brain or pancreas to get them. So historically, you've been limited to postmortem tissue (dead cells with all sorts of artifacts), animal models (mice are not humans), or cell lines that don't have the patient's genetic background.

With iPSCs, you can do something new. You take a skin biopsy from the patient — a few square millimeters of fibroblasts. You reprogram those fibroblasts to iPSCs. The iPSCs carry the patient's exact genetic background, including the disease-causing mutation. Then you differentiate the iPSCs into the cell type that's affected by the disease — dopamine neurons, motor neurons, beta cells, whatever. You now have, in a dish, a population of cells that share the patient's genetics and represent the affected cell type. You can study them. You can watch them die. You can compare them to cells from healthy controls. You can test drugs on them.

\[Pause.\]

The Parkinson's application has been particularly fruitful. Researchers take skin from patients with familial Parkinson's — patients carrying mutations in LRRK2, SNCA, GBA1, or PARK2 — reprogram to iPSCs, and differentiate to midbrain dopamine neurons. The resulting neurons show Parkinson's-like features: accumulation of misfolded alpha-synuclein, mitochondrial dysfunction, oxidative stress, eventually cell death. You can watch a Parkinson's neuron die in your dish, over weeks. You can then test compounds that might slow or prevent the death. That's an enormously powerful screening platform.

The ALS application is similar. Motor neurons derived from iPSCs of ALS patients — particularly those with mutations in SOD1, C9ORF72, or TDP-43 — show ALS-like features in the dish. Mislocalization of TDP-43. Aggregation of toxic proteins. Impaired axonal transport. Eventually, death. Researchers screen compounds that protect these motor neurons, hoping to find drugs that might slow the disease in patients. Several ALS clinical trials of drugs identified through iPSC screening are ongoing.

For type 1 diabetes — where the immune system destroys the patient's beta cells — iPSC-derived beta cells are useful both for studying the disease and as a potential source of replacement cells. You can derive iPSCs from a diabetic patient, differentiate to beta cells, and study why those beta cells are autoimmune targets. Or, more practically, you can derive beta cells from anyone and use them as transplantable replacement tissue, which is the territory we'll cover in the next segment.

\[Pause.\]

A particularly elegant variant of disease modeling is **CRISPR-corrected isogenic controls**. Here's the setup. You have iPSCs from a patient carrying a Parkinson's-causing mutation. You also want a control — cells from a healthy person — to compare them to. But any healthy control will differ from your patient in millions of other genetic variants, not just the disease mutation. How do you know which differences in the dish are caused by the disease mutation and which are caused by all the other genetic noise?

Answer: use CRISPR to fix the disease mutation in the patient's own iPSCs. Now you have two cell lines that are genetically identical _except_ at the one nucleotide of the disease mutation. The patient line, with the mutation, and the corrected line, without it. Any difference in behavior between the two has to be caused by that single nucleotide change. You've controlled for genetic background completely. This is called an **isogenic control**, and it's now standard practice in iPSC disease modeling. It dramatically sharpens what you can conclude from your experiments.

iPSC disease modeling, in some ways, has been the most quietly productive application of the technology. It doesn't make news the way clinical trials do. But it has produced thousands of papers, illuminated dozens of disease mechanisms, and contributed to drug development pipelines across the industry. Every major pharma company has an iPSC group. The technology has become infrastructure.

## SEGMENT 13 — Cell therapy: autologous dreams and allogeneic banks

Now we get to the part of the iPSC story that originally generated the most excitement, and that has, in some ways, been the slowest to deliver: cell therapy. Using iPSC-derived cells as transplanted tissue, to replace what a patient has lost.

The original dream was **autologous therapy**. You take a skin biopsy from a patient with Parkinson's disease. You reprogram the fibroblasts to iPSCs. You differentiate them to dopamine neurons. You inject those neurons into the patient's substantia nigra, where dopamine neurons are dying. The transplanted cells take up residence, integrate into the existing circuitry, and produce dopamine. The patient improves. And because the cells are genetically identical to the patient — they came from the patient's own skin — there is no immune rejection. The immune system sees self, not foreign. No need for lifelong immunosuppression. The analogy is: **make your own replacement parts — your skin cell becomes a beta cell goes back into your pancreas, no immune rejection**.

That's the dream. The reality has bumped into several practical problems.

First, **cost and time**. Making patient-specific iPSCs takes months and costs tens of thousands of dollars per patient. Differentiating those iPSCs to clinical-grade cells takes more months. By the time you have the cells ready, the patient may be much sicker. For a slow-progressing disease this might be acceptable; for an aggressive one, it's a real problem.

Second, **manufacturing complexity**. Every patient's iPSCs are slightly different. Differentiation protocols that work beautifully for one line might be less efficient for another. Quality control has to be done individually for each batch. It's the opposite of mass manufacturing — every dose is bespoke.

Third, **regulatory burden**. The FDA, sensibly, wants every batch of cells used in a patient to meet the same safety and identity standards. Doing that QC for individual patient batches is staggeringly expensive.

For these reasons, the field has increasingly pivoted toward **allogeneic therapy** — using iPSCs derived from a small set of universal donors, not from each individual patient. You generate a library of well-characterized iPSC lines, each from a different donor with a specific HLA type — the genes that determine which tissue types your immune system recognizes as self. If the bank is large enough, most patients can be matched to a line with a compatible HLA type, the way blood banks match donors to recipients. The cells aren't a perfect match to the patient, but they're close enough that immune rejection is manageable, often with mild immunosuppression rather than lifelong heavy doses. Some efforts go even further — engineering "universal" iPSC lines where the HLA genes have been edited out entirely, so the cells are invisible to the immune system and can be transplanted into anyone.

\[Pause.\]

There's a tradeoff here, and it's worth being clear about it. Autologous iPSCs avoid rejection entirely but cost a fortune per patient. Allogeneic iPSCs are mass-producible but require some immune management. The field's bet, increasingly, is that allogeneic therapies will be the route to widespread clinical use — because medicine cannot afford to do individual reprogramming for every patient. A few companies, particularly in Japan where Yamanaka's CiRA institute has built a national iPSC bank, are betting hard on this approach. Off-the-shelf iPSC-derived therapies, available like blood products, matched approximately to the patient's HLA type, is the operational vision.

Whether that vision pans out — whether the immune management really stays manageable, whether the cost economics work — is being tested right now, in dozens of ongoing clinical trials. Let's look at a few of them.

---

## SEGMENT 14 — Vertex VX-880: islet cells for type 1 diabetes

I want to spend some time on the diabetes story, because it illustrates both the promise and the complications of iPSC-style cell therapy beautifully, even though the most prominent clinical program here uses ES-derived rather than iPSC-derived cells.

Type 1 diabetes is, fundamentally, a problem of missing beta cells. The patient's immune system has destroyed the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas, and now the patient cannot make insulin. They have to inject it, multiple times a day, for the rest of their lives. Insulin therapy is life-saving but imperfect — patients still have huge fluctuations in blood glucose, devastating long-term complications, and the constant management burden.

The obvious therapeutic dream, for decades, has been: just give the patient new beta cells. Transplant healthy islets into their body and let them make their own insulin again. Pancreatic islet transplants from deceased donors have been done for a long time and they work — temporarily, for some patients. But cadaver islets are scarce, the quality varies, and the procedure is complex. The supply is fundamentally limited.

What if you could make beta cells in unlimited quantity from a stem cell line? You could treat millions of patients. The supply problem disappears.

\[Pause.\]

A biotech company called Semma Therapeutics, founded by a Harvard researcher named Doug Melton, spent over a decade working out how to differentiate stem cells into functional, glucose-responsive beta cells. Melton had two children with type 1 diabetes and the work was personal. Semma was acquired by Vertex Pharmaceuticals in 2019, and Vertex took the program into clinical trials. The drug is called **VX-880**.

Here's how VX-880 works. They take human embryonic stem cells — though similar work is being done with iPSCs — and differentiate them through the long multi-step protocol I described earlier, ending up with islet-like cell clusters that contain beta cells, alpha cells, and the other islet cell types, all functional in vitro. They infuse those cells into the patient's portal vein, the vein that carries blood to the liver. The cells take up residence in the liver, where they sense blood glucose and release insulin in response. The patient gets immunosuppression to prevent rejection — because these are ES-derived, not autologous.

The early results, reported in 2023 and 2024, were genuinely stunning. Patients on VX-880 saw their daily insulin requirements drop dramatically. Some came off insulin entirely. The first patient enrolled in the trial, a man named Brian Shelton, achieved insulin independence for the first time in decades. He has, by 2026, remained off insulin for several years. He died unrelated to the treatment a few years in, but during his life, his diabetes was — functionally — cured.

\[Pause.\]

Now, VX-880 uses ES-derived cells, not iPSC-derived ones, and the patients require immunosuppression. The next-generation effort, also from Vertex, is **VX-264** — encapsulated beta cells that don't require immunosuppression because they're protected inside a permeable device that lets insulin and glucose pass through but blocks immune cells. And further out, there are programs working on truly iPSC-based versions where the cells come from off-the-shelf allogeneic banks or — eventually — from the patient's own reprogrammed cells.

The reason I tell you this story is twofold. First, it's a real example of cell-replacement therapy actually working in patients — and that wouldn't have been imaginable without the differentiation protocols developed for iPSCs and ES cells together. The Yamanaka-era pluripotency revolution provided the foundation. Second, it shows you the trajectory the field is on. The first wave of clinical successes is using ES-derived cells with immunosuppression. The second wave, coming now, will use encapsulation or HLA-matched allogeneic iPSCs to reduce or eliminate immune issues. The third wave, eventually, will deliver patient-specific or universal-donor cells, off-the-shelf, for all kinds of cell loss diseases.

## SEGMENT 15 — The Takahashi macular degeneration trial: the first human iPSC

Now let's talk about the very first clinical use of iPSC-derived cells in a human patient. Because in 2014, before VX-880 was even close, before most clinical iPSC programs had started, a Japanese ophthalmologist named **Masayo Takahashi** — no relation to Kazutoshi Takahashi, Yamanaka's postdoc — did the experiment that put iPSCs into a human eye for the first time.

The disease was **age-related macular degeneration**, AMD. AMD is the leading cause of blindness in elderly people in the developed world. The disease involves dysfunction and death of the retinal pigment epithelium, or RPE — a single layer of pigmented cells that sit behind the photoreceptors and provide critical metabolic support. When the RPE dies, the photoreceptors above it die, and vision is lost in the center of the visual field.

Replacing dead RPE with new RPE seemed, in principle, a tractable problem. RPE is a single sheet of cells. You don't have to integrate them into a complex circuit; you just have to put them in the right anatomical location. And iPSC-derived RPE differentiation, by the early 2010s, was actually pretty well worked out. The cells are highly distinctive — pigmented, polygonal, polarized — and you can verify identity just by looking at them.

Takahashi, working at the Riken Center for Developmental Biology, designed a trial in which she would take a skin biopsy from a patient with AMD, reprogram those fibroblasts to iPSCs, differentiate the iPSCs to RPE, manufacture a small sheet of RPE cells, and surgically transplant that sheet under the patient's retina to replace dying RPE. Autologous, patient-specific iPSC therapy. The first ever in humans.

\[Pause.\]

The first patient was treated in September 2014. The surgery was done by a colleague, Yasuo Kurimoto. The patient was a 77-year-old woman from Hyogo prefecture. Her own skin was reprogrammed, her own RPE was made, her own cells were transplanted into her own eye. The procedure went well. The cells engrafted. Her vision stabilized — meaning, importantly, that the disease stopped progressing, even though the lost vision didn't return.

That was, in some ways, the more important result. iPSC therapy was shown to be safe — no tumors, no aberrant differentiation, no obvious adverse effects from the cells themselves over years of follow-up. The cells did their job. The principle of patient-specific reprogramming-to-therapy had been demonstrated, end to end, in a real human being. 

The trial then ran into a complication that illustrates one of the practical headaches of autologous iPSCs. The second patient, in the pipeline, had iPSCs that — on careful sequencing — turned out to contain some mutations that hadn't been present in the starting fibroblasts. The mutations were probably introduced during the reprogramming or culture process. They weren't in cancer driver genes, and there was no obvious safety concern, but the regulators sensibly demanded explanation. The trial was paused. The team had to re-evaluate protocols. Some of the wind went out of the autologous-iPSC sail, and the field began shifting more aggressively toward allogeneic approaches where you could thoroughly characterize one line and use it for many patients.

But the Takahashi trial remains historically momentous. It was the proof that iPSCs could go all the way from skin biopsy to human therapy and back out with a safety record. The pipeline existed. The technology was real. The regulatory framework, however cautiously, could accommodate it.

\[Pause.\]

Since then, dozens of iPSC-based clinical trials have been launched around the world — for Parkinson's, for spinal cord injury, for heart failure, for blood disorders. Some use autologous cells. Most, increasingly, use allogeneic. The Takahashi trial opened the door. The rest of the field is walking through it.

## SEGMENT 16 — Partial reprogramming: rewinding aging without losing identity

Now I want to take you to one of the most provocative and weird frontier ideas to come out of iPSC research. It's the idea of **partial reprogramming**, and it goes like this.

The OSKM factors don't just reset a cell's identity. They also, in the process, reset a lot of the cell's epigenetic markers — including the markers we associate with aging. Old cells have particular patterns of DNA methylation, particular chromatin marks, particular accumulated damage signatures. When OSKM is applied long enough to fully reprogram the cell to pluripotency, all of those age markers get wiped clean. The resulting iPSC, even if derived from a 90-year-old skin cell, looks epigenetically young. The cellular clock has been turned back to zero.

That's interesting. But it's also a clinical dead end, because making your skin cells pluripotent doesn't help you — pluripotent cells in your tissues form teratomas. You don't want to be a pluripotent ball of cells. You want to be an old skin cell that's young again.

So in 2016, a group at the Salk Institute led by Juan Carlos Izpisúa Belmonte did an experiment. What if you applied OSKM only briefly? What if you turned it on for a few days, then off, before the cells lost their original identity? Could you rewind the aging clock without rewinding all the way to pluripotency?

The answer, surprisingly, was yes. They engineered mice in which OSKM could be turned on by giving them a drug. The mice had progeria — a genetic condition causing premature aging. When the researchers gave the drug intermittently, in short pulses, the mice's cells showed reversal of aging markers — their skin looked younger, their organs functioned better, they lived longer. And the cells didn't form tumors, because the OSKM was off most of the time. Brief, cyclic application of the factors was enough to partially rewind the cells' epigenetic age without losing their identity.

The analogy is: **rewind a little, not all the way — cyclic OSKM expression in old mice restores youthful gene expression without making cancer**.

\[Pause.\]

This work, replicated and extended over the past decade, has launched a whole subfield of **partial reprogramming for rejuvenation**. The idea is that the developmental tape doesn't have to be rewound all the way — you can just back it up a few steps, refreshing the cell's epigenetic state without erasing its identity. The cell remains a skin cell, or a liver cell, or whatever, but a younger version of itself.

Companies have formed around this concept. Altos Labs, which launched in 2022 with several billion dollars in funding from various billionaires, has hired some of the leading reprogramming researchers — including Yamanaka himself as a senior scientific advisor — to develop partial reprogramming as a therapy. Calico, owned by Alphabet, is working in adjacent space. Many smaller startups are exploring different combinations of reprogramming factors, different delivery methods, different cyclic protocols.

Is this going to work as actual rejuvenation therapy for humans? The honest answer is we don't know yet. The mouse data is suggestive but mouse aging is not human aging. The safety profile is critical — you absolutely cannot have a few cells go all the way to pluripotency and form tumors. The dosing has to be precise, the duration controlled, the cell types responsive. And the deepest question — whether epigenetic rewinding actually translates to functional rejuvenation, whether old organs can be made to work like young ones — is open. But the conceptual seed is real and remarkable: Yamanaka's reprogramming factors don't just reset identity, they reset age. Whether we can use that for medicine, we will find out in the next decade or two.

## SEGMENT 17 — iPSCs for drug screening and toxicity testing

A more immediately practical use of iPSCs, and one that's quietly become a major industrial application, is **drug screening and toxicity testing**.

Here's the problem the pharma industry has always faced. You want to know if a new drug is going to be toxic to human hearts before you give it to humans. Historically, you tested it in animals — rats, dogs, monkeys — and inferred. But animals are different from humans in lots of ways, and some drugs that look fine in animals turn out to be cardiotoxic in humans, leading to expensive late-stage failures or, worse, patient deaths after approval. Vioxx. Terfenadine. Astemizole. A whole gallery of drugs that caused cardiac arrhythmias in humans which animals hadn't predicted.

With iPSCs, you can do something new. You differentiate iPSCs into cardiomyocytes — actual human heart cells, in a dish, beating like little hearts. You expose those cells to your candidate drug. You watch what happens to their action potentials, their calcium handling, their contraction patterns. If the drug causes arrhythmias in iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes, it probably will in humans. If it doesn't, that's a positive sign.

This kind of testing has been formalized into something called the **CiPA** initiative — Comprehensive In Vitro Proarrhythmia Assay — where iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes are used as part of a standardized panel to predict drug-induced arrhythmia risk. The FDA accepts CiPA data as part of drug submissions now. iPSC cardiotoxicity testing has become regulatory infrastructure.

\[Pause.\]

The same logic applies to other tissues. iPSC-derived hepatocytes are used to predict drug-induced liver toxicity, which is one of the most common reasons drugs fail in clinical trials. iPSC-derived neurons are used to predict neurotoxicity. iPSC-derived kidney cells can predict nephrotoxicity.

And here's where it gets really interesting. You can do this with **patient-specific iPSCs**. Suppose you have a patient who had a severe adverse reaction to a drug — say, sudden cardiac arrhythmia. Was it the drug, or was it the patient's particular genetics? Take a skin biopsy, make iPSCs, differentiate to cardiomyocytes, expose to the drug. If the patient's cells, specifically, show the arrhythmia phenotype while healthy controls don't, you've identified that patient's particular susceptibility. That's the seed of pharmacogenomics — using each patient's own cells to predict their drug response.

For very rare diseases, where you can't ethically run a large clinical trial, iPSC-based drug screening can be the primary way to identify candidate therapies. You take cells from the rare-disease patient, model the disease in a dish, screen thousands of compounds, find ones that fix the phenotype, and then take the best ones forward into the few patients who exist. This is approximately what was done for the Milasen story you may have heard about — a custom antisense oligonucleotide drug designed for a single child with a unique genetic disease. iPSCs were part of the workup. Personalized medicine, at its most extreme.

\[Pause.\]

Drug screening on iPSCs is, in some ways, the steadiest revenue stream the iPSC industry has. It's not the headline-grabbing cell therapy story. But it's reducing late-stage drug failures, sharpening pharmacovigilance, and quietly improving the safety of the entire pharmaceutical industry. The Yamanaka factors, applied to skin biopsies, scaled into industrial cardiotoxicity testing, are saving lives in a roundabout way every day.

## SEGMENT 18 — The frontier: organoids, synthetic embryos, and off-the-shelf banks

Let me end with where the field is going. There are three frontiers I want to sketch, each one extending the iPSC concept further.

**Frontier one: organoids.** If you take iPSCs and differentiate them in three-dimensional culture, allowing the cells to organize themselves spatially, they don't just become single cell types. They build small, organized structures called **organoids** that contain multiple cell types arranged the way they would be in a real organ. You can make brain organoids — small balls of neurons and glia organized into rudimentary cortical structures. Intestinal organoids with villi and crypts. Liver organoids. Kidney organoids. Lung organoids. Each one is a partial, miniaturized version of a real organ, derived from a single iPSC line, grown in a dish.

Organoids are not real organs — they're missing vasculature, they're small, they lack the complex spatial cues of an embryo. But they are far better tissue models than any single-cell-type culture. You can study how cells interact, how diseases progress in a multi-cellular context, how drugs affect entire mini-organs. Brain organoids have been used to model microcephaly caused by Zika virus infection, to study schizophrenia, to test psychiatric drugs. Intestinal organoids are being used to model inflammatory bowel disease and to develop personalized therapies. The organoid field has exploded in the past decade and is becoming its own major branch of biology.

**Frontier two: synthetic embryos.** Even more provocatively, researchers have started using pluripotent stem cells — both ES cells and iPSCs — to build structures that resemble early embryos, without ever using a fertilized egg. By combining cells representing the embryonic and the extra-embryonic lineages, in the right geometry and culture conditions, they can self-assemble into structures that look strikingly like early post-implantation embryos. These are sometimes called **embryoids**, **gastruloids**, or **stembryos**.

In 2022 and 2023, several labs reported mouse synthetic embryos that developed in vitro to the equivalent of mid-gestation — with a beating heart, the start of neural tube, recognizable body plan organization — without any sperm or egg involvement, starting only from cultured stem cells. The ethical and regulatory questions here are deep. If you can build something embryo-like from stem cells, what is the moral status of that structure? Human versions of this work are being pursued cautiously, with ongoing ethical debate about how long such structures should be allowed to develop.

\[Pause.\]

**Frontier three: off-the-shelf allogeneic iPSC banks.** I mentioned this earlier but I want to come back to it because it's likely to define the next decade of clinical iPSC use.

The vision is: build a bank of perhaps a few hundred well-characterized iPSC lines, each with a different HLA type, collectively covering most of the genetic diversity of the population. Differentiate each line, at scale, into the major clinically useful cell types — cardiomyocytes, dopamine neurons, beta cells, hematopoietic progenitors, RPE, others. Manufacture these cells under pharmaceutical-grade conditions, characterize them exhaustively, freeze them. When a patient needs a cell therapy, you match them by HLA type to the best line in the bank, thaw the appropriate cells, and infuse. Like a blood bank, but for tissue.

Japan, under Yamanaka's CiRA institute, has been building such a bank since the mid-2010s, with the explicit goal of covering most of the Japanese population's HLA diversity using a few dozen lines. Other countries are following. Companies like Fate Therapeutics, Century Therapeutics, and Cellino are building variations on this model — sometimes with HLA-engineered "universal" cells designed to be invisible to any patient's immune system.

If this works, iPSC therapy stops being a custom-manufactured boutique product and becomes a mass-producible pharmaceutical-grade tissue product. Off-the-shelf cells, available in any hospital, matched to the patient like blood type. That's the operational endpoint of everything Yamanaka started in 2006.

\[Pause.\]

So that's where we are. Twenty years from a single Cell paper to a field that spans developmental biology, regenerative medicine, drug screening, disease modeling, rejuvenation research, and synthetic embryology. Yamanaka pressed rewind on a skin cell, and the entire field of cell biology pressed rewind alongside him.

Let me give you a final summing-up, and then we'll be done.

## APPENDIX — The big ideas to walk out with

Let me close by collapsing the whole lecture into the handful of ideas that, if you remember nothing else, I want you to leave with.

**One: differentiation is reversible.** For a century, the consensus was that adult cells were stuck in their identity. Yamanaka showed they aren't. Four transcription factors — Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc — are enough to take an adult fibroblast back to a fully pluripotent state. The developmental tape can be rewound.

**Two: the foundation was laid by Gurdon and Wilmut.** Gurdon's 1962 frog showed that adult nuclei retain all the genetic information. Wilmut's 1996 Dolly the sheep showed the same was true in mammals. Yamanaka's contribution was to replace the messy biological reprogramming machinery of egg cytoplasm with a defined set of purified factors. Three Nobel-worthy steps on the same staircase.

**Three: iPSCs sidestep the ethical problem of ES cells.** No embryos. No destruction of human blastocysts. Just a skin biopsy and a few weeks of culture. The ethical morass that paralyzed stem cell research in the 2000s was, in one experiment, made obsolete.

**Four: the four factors do specific things.** Oct4 and Sox2 are the master regulators of pluripotency. Klf4 supports proliferation and chromatin opening. c-Myc drives the cell cycle and broad chromatin remodeling. Each one earns its place in the cocktail.

**Five: reprogramming is rare.** Less than 1% of cells make it all the way to pluripotency. It's a stochastic, multi-step journey through chromatin remodeling and gene expression changes, and most cells get stuck along the way. iPSC generation is fundamentally a selection process.

**Six: integration-free delivery has made iPSCs clinically credible.** Sendai virus, episomal plasmids, mRNA — these methods let you reprogram without leaving any transgene behind in the final cells. The c-Myc cancer worry of 2007 has been substantially defused.

**Seven: directed differentiation lets you make any cell type.** By recapitulating the signaling sequences of embryonic development, you can guide iPSCs down any developmental branch. Cardiomyocytes that beat. Neurons that fire. Beta cells that secrete insulin. Hepatocytes that metabolize drugs.

**Eight: disease in a dish has quietly transformed biomedical research.** Patient-specific iPSCs let researchers study affected cell types from real patients, including patients with diseases of organs you can't biopsy. CRISPR-corrected isogenic controls sharpen the experiments to the point of single-nucleotide resolution.

**Nine: clinical translation is real but cautious.** The Takahashi macular degeneration trial in 2014 was the first human use. Vertex VX-880 and its successors are curing some patients of type 1 diabetes. Dozens of trials are ongoing across many diseases. Most are now allogeneic rather than autologous, for reasons of cost and manufacturing.

**Ten: partial reprogramming may reset aging.** Brief, cyclic application of OSKM appears to rewind epigenetic age markers without making cells pluripotent. Whether this translates to functional rejuvenation in humans is the open question of the next decade.

**Eleven: organoids and synthetic embryos extend the technology into new territory.** iPSCs aren't just for making single cell types. They self-organize into miniaturized organs and embryo-like structures, opening new research and ethical frontiers.

**Twelve: off-the-shelf iPSC banks are the operational future.** Allogeneic cell therapies, drawn from HLA-matched donor lines, manufactured at scale, available like blood products. The endpoint of the technology Yamanaka started.

\[Pause.\]

When you read about a new iPSC-based therapy in the news — a Parkinson's trial in Kyoto, a heart failure trial in San Diego, a diabetes cure in Boston — what's happening underneath is, in every case, the same. Someone took an adult cell. They pressed rewind. They got it back to pluripotency. They guided it forward to a useful tissue. They put that tissue into a patient. The pipeline. The dream. The future of medicine, twenty years and counting from one paper.

And the deeper lesson, the one I want you to carry with you whether or not you ever work on iPSCs directly: the conventional wisdom that surrounds you, in any field, is often less locked-in than it looks. People believed differentiation was one-way for a century. It took one stubborn researcher, one good screen, four genes, and a willingness to do the experiment that everybody else thought wouldn't work. Twenty-four candidates. Six years of patient subtraction. One paper that rewrote the rules.

That's how science actually moves. Not by consensus. By somebody asking a question consensus said wasn't worth asking. And then getting it to work.

Thanks for sticking with me. Go read the original 2006 Cell paper if you've never read it — it's twelve pages, it's clear, it's history. Questions are welcome.
