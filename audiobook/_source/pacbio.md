## SEGMENT 1 — The long-read problem: what short reads can't do

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. Today and for the next two hours, we're going to talk about PacBio sequencing — or, to give it its full name, single-molecule real-time sequencing on SMRT cells, with HiFi reads as the modern output. And I want to begin not with the technology, but with the problem the technology solves — because PacBio only makes sense if you first understand what was missing from the sequencing world it walked into.

So let's set the scene. By the late 2000s, the genomics field had a winning machine: Illumina. Short, accurate reads, generated by the millions, dirt cheap per base. If you wanted to count RNA transcripts, find single-nucleotide variants, do a GWAS study on a hundred thousand people, Illumina was unbeatable and remains unbeatable today. Tens of billions of bases for the cost of a nice dinner. The short-read revolution had arrived, and it had transformed biology.

But there was a problem. The reads were short. A hundred and fifty bases, two hundred and fifty bases at the high end. Beautiful little postage stamps of sequence. And it turns out that when you try to assemble a genome out of postage stamps — when you try to reconstruct an entire chromosome from billions of tiny overlapping fragments — there are vast regions of the genome where this just doesn't work.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy. Imagine you've shredded a book — not the kind of shred that gives you long ribbons, but the confetti shred, the secure document destruction shred, where every piece is about the size of a fingernail. Now imagine I hand you a wheelbarrow of those confetti pieces and ask you to reconstruct the book. You can do it, in principle, by matching the edges of pieces against each other and finding overlaps. And if the book is written in ordinary prose, where most sentences are unique, you can probably reassemble most of it. But what if the book contains a passage where the same paragraph is repeated three hundred times in a row? What if there's a chapter that's just the word "the" printed ten thousand times? You'll find lots of confetti with "the" on it, but you'll have no way to know which "the" goes where in the sequence. The pieces aren't long enough to span the repetitive region. The book has, in effect, a hole in it that your confetti can't reconstruct.

That's exactly the situation short-read sequencing finds itself in when it hits the repetitive parts of a genome. And the human genome — and most genomes — are full of repetitive parts. Centromeres, those big chunks of repetitive DNA at the middle of every chromosome, are essentially un-assemblable from short reads. Telomeres at the chromosome ends, made of the same six bases repeated thousands of times — un-assemblable. Tandem repeats inside genes, the kind that cause Huntington's disease and fragile X syndrome when they expand — un-measurable beyond a certain length. Segmental duplications, where megabase-sized chunks of the genome have been copied to other locations — short reads can't tell which copy is which. Whole classes of structural variation — large inversions, large deletions, complex rearrangements — just slip through the cracks of short-read pipelines because the events are bigger than the reads.

The Human Genome Project, finished officially in 2003, was missing about eight percent of the genome — and that missing eight percent wasn't randomly distributed. It was concentrated exactly in those repetitive regions where short reads can't reach. For nearly two decades, the human reference genome had holes in it, and those holes were the most genetically interesting and dynamic parts of our chromosomes.

\[Pause.\]

The solution, in principle, is obvious. If short reads can't span a five-thousand-base repeat, use ten-thousand-base reads. If ten thousand isn't enough, use fifty thousand. If fifty thousand isn't enough, use a hundred thousand. Make the confetti longer than the repetitive passage, and the problem goes away. You can reassemble the book.

But making long reads turns out to be technically vicious. Every chemistry that researchers had used for sequencing — Sanger's chain termination, Illumina's bridge amplification, the various other short-read methods — was built around bulk biochemistry, where you take a population of identical DNA molecules and read them collectively. As reads get longer, the chemistry gets noisier; molecules in the population drift out of phase with each other, signal-to-noise collapses, and by the time you're trying to read a kilobase, you've lost the signal. Short reads weren't a stylistic choice; they were a consequence of bulk methods. Reading long required a fundamentally different approach.

And that's what PacBio brought to the table. Not an incremental improvement on existing chemistry, but a wholly different physical paradigm. Instead of reading many molecules in bulk, watch one molecule, one polymerase, in real time, and let it tell you the sequence as it copies. Drop the bulk averaging entirely. Single molecule. One at a time. Real time observation. That's the bet. And the bet, after about fifteen years of struggle, paid off.

\[Pause.\]

So here's our contract for the next two hours. If you walk out of here at the end of the lecture and you can do three things — one, sketch a zero-mode waveguide and explain why a hole smaller than a wavelength of light lets us observe one polymerase at a time; two, walk through what a SMRTbell template is and how it produces HiFi reads of ninety-nine point nine percent accuracy out of fifteen percent raw error; three, look at a modern genome assembly paper — telomere-to-telomere, structural variant catalog, full-length transcript characterization — and tell me why PacBio specifically was the right tool for the job — then we've succeeded. Everything else hangs off those three.

We'll meet the rough commercial history along the way. PacBio, the company, had a famously rocky start. The first instrument was expensive, the early data was disappointing, the financials were ugly for years, and there was a period in the mid-2010s when the company looked like it might not survive. Then in 2019 they released the Sequel II, in 2020 they released the HiFi workflow as standard, and within two years they had become the gold standard for serious genome assembly. The technology that was nearly abandoned became the technology that finished the human genome. We'll get to that story in segments ten and thirteen.

And throughout the lecture, I'll be comparing PacBio against its main long-read competitor, Oxford Nanopore. The two platforms have a deep rivalry, they have different physical principles, different strengths, different weaknesses, and you can't really understand one without the other. Think of them as the two parallel solutions to the same problem — both born in roughly the same era, both attacking single-molecule sequencing, both producing long reads. PacBio is the precision tool. Nanopore is the speed-and-length champion. We'll see why throughout.

Now let's go meet the idea itself.

## SEGMENT 2 — The PacBio idea: watch a polymerase work, in real time, on a single molecule

So before we look at the physics, let me give you the central idea of PacBio in one sentence, because the elegance of the idea is genuinely worth pausing over.

**The idea is this.** Take one single molecule of DNA polymerase. Stick it to the bottom of a tiny well. Give it a template strand to copy. Provide it with fluorescently labeled nucleotides — each base type carries a different color of dye. Then watch, under a microscope, as the polymerase incorporates one nucleotide at a time. Each incorporation produces a flash of color. Red flash means a T was added. Green flash means a C. And so on. Record the sequence of flashes, and you've read the sequence of the DNA.

That's it. That's the whole concept. **Sequencing by watching a single polymerase work in real time.** Stay with me.

\[Pause.\]

Now, every single piece of that sentence sounds straightforward and is, in practice, fiendishly hard. Let me walk through why.

First — you have to be able to see a single molecule of fluorescent dye. Not a million copies. One. Not a population. A single molecule, when it's flashing for a few milliseconds in solution. To see a single molecule of fluorescence is already, by itself, the kind of thing that would have won a Nobel Prize twenty years before PacBio existed. It requires extremely sensitive cameras, low backgrounds, and clever optics. But that's just the entry fee.

Second — the polymerase is constantly being bombarded by free nucleotides floating around in solution. To do its job, the polymerase needs a pool of all four nucleotides at fairly high concentration, micromolar at minimum, hovering around it ready to be grabbed. Now, every one of those free nucleotides is carrying a fluorescent dye. If you tried to image the polymerase under a normal microscope, the background fluorescence from millions of unincorporated nucleotides drifting through the field of view would completely swamp the signal from the one nucleotide actually being incorporated. It would be like trying to spot a candle in a stadium full of stadium lights. The signal-to-background problem is catastrophic.

Third — you need to know that the flash you just saw came from the correct molecule, not from some other reaction happening nearby. If you have thousands of polymerases all working at once and you want to read each one's output independently, you can't have them confusing each other's signals.

Fourth — the polymerase itself has to be fast enough to be practical but slow enough that you can see each step. If it incorporates a hundred bases a second, that's two milliseconds per base, which is doable with a fast camera, but tight. If it incorporates ten bases a second, your sequencing throughput collapses. There's a sweet spot.

Fifth — you need a polymerase that can keep going for a long, long time on a single template, without falling off, without getting tired, without losing accuracy. Most polymerases run for a few hundred bases and stop. You want one that runs for tens of thousands of bases, on the same molecule, without giving up.

Each of these problems, individually, was a research challenge. Solving all of them together, in one integrated instrument — that's what PacBio spent more than a decade and several hundred million dollars doing.

\[Pause.\]

The conceptual breakthrough that made the whole approach possible was the zero-mode waveguide, which we're going to spend the next segment on. The zero-mode waveguide solves the background problem — it lets you illuminate only a tiny volume right at the bottom of a well, the volume where the polymerase sits, so unincorporated nucleotides floating around above the polymerase never get hit by the laser and never fluoresce. The result is that essentially the only fluorescence you see is from the dye actually attached to the nucleotide currently being incorporated by the polymerase. Background problem solved.

The polymerase problem was solved by adapting an enzyme called the phi29 polymerase — pronounced "phi twenty-nine" — which is a real workhorse of an enzyme borrowed from a bacterial virus. Phi29 has the unusual properties of being extremely processive (meaning it stays on the template for a long time without falling off), and being able to displace any strand it bumps into as it moves along (which becomes crucial for circular templates). We'll meet phi29 in segment six. PacBio has further engineered it to slow it down a bit, make it more error-tolerant, and make it work with their specific chemistry.

The chemistry problem — how to attach a dye to a nucleotide in a way that the dye lights up briefly during incorporation and then disappears — was solved by attaching the dye to the phosphate group of the nucleotide. We'll see that in segment five. When the polymerase incorporates the nucleotide, the natural reaction cleaves off the phosphate group, taking the dye with it. So the dye is present, attached, fluorescing only during the moment of incorporation, and then it's gone. The result is a clean, time-resolved pulse of color, one per base. Beautiful chemistry.

\[Pause.\]

So the PacBio idea, packaged up, is: zero-mode waveguides plus phi29 polymerase plus phospholinked fluorescent nucleotides, all integrated on a chip with millions of independent wells, observed in parallel by a camera. That's the platform. The product is a real-time movie of polymerase activity, played back as a sequence of colored flashes, which the software translates into a base sequence.

Now, here's the thing I want you to feel about this approach, before we go into the details. **It's a fundamentally different paradigm from short-read sequencing.** Illumina, Sanger, and every other short-read method works by amplifying DNA — making many many many copies — and reading the average signal from that population. The signal is loud because it's redundant. PacBio doesn't amplify. It reads a single molecule, the original one, directly. There's no PCR step, no amplification bias, no copying errors introduced by polymerases other than the sequencing polymerase itself. What you're reading is the actual DNA molecule that came out of the cell. That's a profound difference. We'll come back to its implications throughout the lecture — especially when we get to methylation calling, where the lack of amplification turns out to be a huge advantage because PCR erases methylation marks.

So that's the idea. Now let's look at the physics that makes it possible.

## SEGMENT 3 — Zero-mode waveguides: the physics that makes single-molecule observation possible

We need to spend a real segment on zero-mode waveguides, because the zero-mode waveguide — abbreviated ZMW — is the single most important physical innovation in PacBio sequencing. Everything else in the platform is enabled by this one piece of physics. So let me try to give you the intuition for what it is and why it works.

The problem, again, is background fluorescence. You want to watch a single polymerase incorporating a single nucleotide, and that nucleotide is carrying a fluorescent dye that flashes when you shine the laser on it. But there are millions of other nucleotides in solution, all carrying the same dye, all of which will also flash if the laser hits them. If your laser illuminates a typical microscope field — a volume of, say, ten femtoliters — there might be a few thousand free dye molecules in that volume at any moment. Their combined fluorescence is enormous compared to your single incorporated nucleotide. You'd see a glowing soup with no signal.

What you really want is to illuminate only a tiny, tiny volume — small enough that, on average, there's much less than one free dye molecule in it at any time. If your observation volume is small enough, the background goes from "thousands of dye molecules" to "essentially zero dye molecules most of the time," and the single dye you actually care about — the one held in place by the polymerase, the one being incorporated — becomes visible against a black background.

The question is: how do you illuminate a volume that small? Standard light microscopy has a fundamental resolution limit — the so-called diffraction limit — that says you can't focus light to a spot much smaller than about half its wavelength. For visible light, that means you can't get an illumination spot smaller than roughly two hundred nanometers across. Two hundred nanometers cubed is still hundreds of attoliters, which still contains many free dye molecules. Conventional optics can't shrink the illumination volume small enough.

\[Pause.\]

Here is where the trick comes in, and it's one of the most beautiful applications of physics in biotechnology. **You make a hole in a metal sheet that's smaller than the wavelength of light.**

Let me build up the intuition. Imagine you have a metal plate — aluminum, say — and you drill a hole through it. If the hole is large compared to the wavelength of light, then light shining on one side of the plate will pass through the hole and emerge on the other side as a beam, just like light through a window. Standard optics.

Now shrink the hole. Make it smaller than the wavelength of light. For green light, that's smaller than about five hundred nanometers. What happens?

Counterintuitively, light still tries to enter the hole. But it can't propagate through. The hole is too small to support a propagating light wave at that wavelength. Instead, the light field inside the hole becomes what physicists call an **evanescent field** — a wave that doesn't travel but instead decays exponentially as you move into the hole. The deeper you go, the dimmer it gets, very fast. Within about twenty or thirty nanometers of the entrance, the light field has dropped by something like a factor of ten. By fifty nanometers in, the field is essentially zero. The hole, in effect, has a finite depth of illumination — only the bottom-most twenty or thirty nanometers see significant light. Everything above that is dark.

That's the zero-mode waveguide. **A hole in a metal sheet, smaller than the wavelength of visible light, illuminated from below.** Light tries to enter from below but only penetrates a tiny depth into the hole. The illuminated volume at the bottom of the hole is on the order of twenty zeptoliters — that's two times ten to the minus twenty liters. To give you a sense of scale, that volume is something like a millionth of a millionth of the volume of a small bacterial cell. It's absurdly small.

And here's why that matters. A typical concentration of fluorescent nucleotides in the sequencing reaction is in the micromolar range. At one micromolar concentration, you have roughly six hundred molecules per attoliter. In a twenty zeptoliter volume — twenty thousandths of an attoliter — you'd expect, on average, only about twelve molecules. But most of those are not lit up at any given moment, because the dyes are only emitting fluorescence when excited by light, and the dye has to find itself in the illuminated zone to fluoresce. Free dyes in solution diffuse very fast — microseconds — so even when one enters the zone, it's gone before the camera can integrate a single frame. The result is that on average, the background fluorescence from free nucleotides inside the ZMW is essentially negligible. The volume is so small, and free molecules pass through it so fast, that they contribute almost nothing to the signal.

\[Pause.\]

But the polymerase at the bottom of the well — that's a different story. The polymerase is stuck. It's attached to the bottom of the ZMW. It doesn't diffuse anywhere. And when it grabs a nucleotide and holds it during the incorporation reaction, that nucleotide is held still, in the illuminated zone, for the millisecond or so it takes to be added to the growing strand. During that millisecond, the dye on the nucleotide is bathed in laser light, fluorescing at full intensity. The camera, which is integrating its signal over those milliseconds, picks up a bright, clear pulse from a single molecule, against a black background.

You can see the elegance of it. The zero-mode waveguide doesn't make the dye brighter. It doesn't make the laser stronger. It just **suppresses the background**, by making the illuminated volume so small that essentially nothing other than the trapped polymerase's current nucleotide is ever lit up. Signal-to-noise goes from impossible to easy. Single-molecule fluorescence detection, which was a heroic feat in the 1990s, becomes routine on every well of every SMRT cell.

The analogy I love for this. **Imagine you're trying to listen to one person whisper in a stadium full of people whispering.** Hopeless. Now imagine you put a long narrow tube down to the person's mouth, and the tube's walls absorb sound. Now you can only hear sound from the very bottom of the tube — from that one person's whispers — and the surrounding hubbub is shut out. The zero-mode waveguide is the tube. It restricts your listening — your observation — to the very bottom, where the polymerase sits, and everything floating above is acoustically, or in this case optically, shut out.

\[Pause.\]

A few technical notes for completeness. The ZMW is typically a cylindrical hole, about seventy nanometers in diameter and a hundred nanometers deep, drilled or photolithographically patterned through a hundred-nanometer-thick layer of aluminum deposited on a transparent glass substrate. Laser light is delivered from below, through the glass, up into the bottom of each well. The polymerase is anchored to the glass at the bottom of the well by a biotin-streptavidin linkage. There's one polymerase per well, ideally — and getting one and only one polymerase per well, rather than zero or two, is itself a Poisson statistics problem that PacBio has spent a lot of engineering on. Modern SMRT cells have millions of these ZMWs packed onto a single chip, each one independently watched by the camera. We'll talk about the chip itself in the next segment.

The fundamental innovation, though, is the physics. **A hole smaller than the wavelength of light, illuminating only its bottom twenty or thirty nanometers, is the trick that makes single-molecule sequencing observable in real time.** Hold that picture. It's the heart of the platform.

## SEGMENT 4 — SMRT cell architecture: millions of wells, one polymerase per well

Alright. So we have the physical idea — the zero-mode waveguide, this exquisitely small illuminated volume at the bottom of a sub-wavelength hole. Now let's zoom out and look at how PacBio assembles millions of these wells into a sequencing chip, the **SMRT cell**.

SMRT stands for Single Molecule Real Time. The acronym is the company's whole pitch, compressed into four letters. A SMRT cell is the consumable chip you load into a PacBio instrument to run a single sequencing experiment. Physically, it looks like a small rectangular slide, maybe the size of a microscope cover slip. Inside, it's an extraordinary piece of nanofabrication.

\[Pause.\]

Picture it this way. **A SMRT cell is a metal plate with millions of microscopic holes — the zero-mode waveguides — punched through it, mounted on a transparent glass substrate, with a flow chamber on top to deliver the sequencing reagents.** The metal is aluminum. The plate is about a hundred nanometers thick. The holes are about seventy nanometers across. And there are, in the modern chips, eight million or more of them, arranged in a precise grid.

At the bottom of each hole sits — ideally — one DNA polymerase enzyme, anchored to the glass. Loaded onto that polymerase is one circular DNA template — the SMRTbell template, which we'll meet in segment seven. Above the polymerase, filling the flow chamber, is a solution containing all four nucleotide types, each one labeled with a different colored fluorescent dye. The instrument shines laser light up through the glass, into the bottom of every well, simultaneously. And the instrument's camera watches the array, recording the color and timing of fluorescent pulses from every well in parallel.

That parallelism is what makes the throughput possible. Each well is producing one read from one polymerase. By itself, that's slow — a single polymerase reads only a few bases per second, so even running for hours, one well only generates tens of thousands of bases. But you have millions of wells running simultaneously. Eight million wells times some tens of kilobases per well equals on the order of hundreds of gigabases of sequence per cell. That's the magic of massive parallelism, the same trick Illumina uses, but executed on a totally different substrate.

\[Pause.\]

Now here's a subtle but important engineering problem: how do you get one polymerase into each well? Not zero. Not two. Exactly one.

The wells are loaded by simply incubating the chip in a solution of polymerase-template complexes — the polymerases already bound to their templates — and letting them diffuse into the wells. They get caught at the bottom by the streptavidin anchor. But the loading is statistical. If you use a low concentration of complexes, most wells get zero, a few get one, almost none get two. If you use a high concentration, most wells get two or more, which is unusable because you can't tell which polymerase's signal is which.

This is Poisson loading. The math says that the maximum fraction of wells that can have exactly one polymerase, with random loading, is about thirty-seven percent — when the average loading is exactly one per well, the Poisson distribution gives about 37% empty, 37% single, and 26% with two or more. So under random loading, you can never use more than about a third of your wells productively. The rest are either empty (no signal) or multiply loaded (uninterpretable signal).

PacBio has worked very hard to push past this limit using what they call **magnetic bead loading** and other tricks that bias the loading away from purely random. With these methods, they can achieve much higher single-occupancy rates — sixty, seventy, even eighty percent of wells getting exactly one polymerase. That's enormous for throughput, because it directly multiplies the usable fraction of the chip.

\[Pause.\]

A few more architectural notes. The flow cell on top of the chip allows continuous delivery of fresh reagents during the sequencing run. The reactions take place at body temperature, roughly thirty degrees Celsius, in an aqueous buffer that mimics cellular conditions. The reactions can run for hours — a single SMRT cell run, on a modern instrument, takes about thirty hours.

The optics underneath are complex. Multiple laser lines, one for each dye color. A field-of-view large enough to see every well at once, focused through high-numerical-aperture objectives. High-speed cameras, capturing at hundreds of frames per second, generating terabytes of raw image data per run. The instrument also has heating, fluidics, and stabilization to keep the optical alignment constant over the long run.

And the chip is a single-use consumable. Once you've run it for thirty hours and harvested your data, you throw it away. The chip itself, with all its nanofabrication, is the bulk of the cost-per-run. Driving down the cost of the chip — which they do by making chips with more wells, so cost per gigabase drops even as cost per chip stays similar or grows — has been a major focus of PacBio's engineering for the past decade.

\[Pause.\]

The lineage of SMRT cells over time tracks the throughput growth of the platform. The original RS chips had around 75,000 wells per cell. The Sequel chips, launched in 2015, jumped to a million wells. The Sequel II chips, in 2019, jumped to eight million. The Revio chips, the current generation as of the early 2020s, are at twenty-five million per cell, and PacBio runs four of them simultaneously per instrument. Each generation has roughly an order of magnitude more throughput than the last. We'll cover the instrument lineage in detail in segment ten.

The conceptual point I want to leave you with here is: **the SMRT cell is a chip of independent single-molecule reactions, observed in parallel, made possible by integrating semiconductor-style nanofabrication with biological chemistry.** It's a piece of physics, a piece of chemistry, and a piece of microfabrication, all stacked into a single consumable. The marriage of those three disciplines is what PacBio is, at its core. Now let's look at the chemistry of the nucleotides themselves, because that's the next piece of the puzzle.

## SEGMENT 5 — Fluorescent nucleotides: pulses of color as each base is added

We've built the chip. We've got the zero-mode waveguides. We've got the polymerase anchored at the bottom of each well. Now we need to ask: what's actually flashing? Where does the colored signal come from, and how does it correctly tell us which base was added?

This is one of the prettiest pieces of chemistry in the whole platform, and it has a name that sounds intimidating but actually describes the chemistry exactly: **phospholinked fluorescent nucleotides**.

Let me unpack what that means. A normal nucleotide that gets incorporated into DNA looks like this: a base — A, T, G, or C — attached to a sugar, which is attached to a chain of three phosphate groups. When the polymerase incorporates the nucleotide into the growing DNA strand, it forms a chemical bond between the sugar and the previous nucleotide, releasing the last two phosphate groups — what's called pyrophosphate — as a byproduct. So the base and the first phosphate stay on the DNA; the second and third phosphates fly off as waste.

\[Pause.\]

PacBio's trick was to **attach the fluorescent dye to the terminal phosphate group of the nucleotide — the very phosphate that gets cleaved off and discarded during normal incorporation.**

Read that again, because it's the key. The dye is attached, not to the base, not to the sugar, but to the very part of the nucleotide that the polymerase chops off as part of doing its job. When the polymerase grabs a nucleotide, the dye is held in the active site for a fraction of a second — illuminated by the ZMW, fluorescing as the laser hits it. Then the polymerase forms the new phosphodiester bond, cleaves off the pyrophosphate group, and the dye floats away with the pyrophosphate. The dye is gone. The new nucleotide is now in the DNA strand, dye-free, as if it had been a normal nucleotide all along.

The result is a clean, time-resolved pulse of fluorescence — bright while the polymerase is holding the nucleotide for incorporation, gone the moment incorporation completes. One nucleotide, one pulse. The duration of the pulse is on the order of milliseconds — long enough for the camera to integrate the signal, short enough that the polymerase keeps moving along the template at a reasonable speed.

And here's why the four bases give four different signals. PacBio attaches a **different colored dye to each base type**. The A nucleotides carry one dye. The T nucleotides carry a second. The G nucleotides carry a third. The C nucleotides carry a fourth. The dyes have different emission spectra — different colors of fluorescence. The camera, with appropriate filters and split optical paths, can distinguish the four colors. So when you see a pulse of color X, you know an A was added; a pulse of color Y, a T was added; and so on.

The output of a single ZMW, over the course of a run, is a movie. A movie of colored flashes — pulse, pulse, pulse — each one tagged with a color and a timestamp. The software watches the movie, identifies each pulse, calls the color, and translates the sequence of pulses into a sequence of bases. That base sequence is the read.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy. **Imagine a typist at a typewriter, where each key, when pressed, briefly lights up with a different color depending on which letter it is.** A key flashes red — you record "A." Another key flashes green — you record "C." Another flashes blue — "G." The typist types out a whole sentence, and you can read off the sentence from the sequence of colored flashes, even though you can't see the typist directly. The colors plus the timing are enough.

That's PacBio sequencing. The polymerase is the typist. The dye-labeled nucleotides are the keys. Each incorporation is a keystroke. And the colored flashes, watched in sequence, spell out the DNA.

\[Pause.\]

Now, this scheme has a few practical wrinkles. First, the polymerase has to be able to use these modified nucleotides — nucleotides with a bulky fluorescent dye stuck on the terminal phosphate — at all. Most polymerases are picky about substrate shape and will refuse to incorporate dye-labeled nucleotides. PacBio's phi29 variant has been engineered specifically to tolerate this modification. That engineering is non-trivial.

Second, the nucleotides have to be delivered to the polymerase at a high enough concentration that the polymerase doesn't sit waiting for a substrate — but not so high that the background creeps up despite the ZMW's tiny illuminated volume. There's a Goldilocks zone.

Third, the kinetics matter. The amount of time the polymerase spends holding a nucleotide before incorporating it depends on the local sequence context, on the identity of the nucleotide, and importantly on whether the template base — the one being read — has chemical modifications, like methylation. Methylated bases slow the polymerase down. That's the basis for PacBio's methylation calling, which we'll cover in segment twelve.

Fourth, the chemistry isn't perfect. Sometimes the polymerase grabs a nucleotide, holds it briefly, and then releases it without incorporating — a so-called "branch" event. Sometimes it incorporates very fast and the pulse is missed by the camera — an undercall. Sometimes two pulses overlap. The result is that raw PacBio reads have an error rate on the order of ten to fifteen percent — high enough that you can't use a single raw read for serious analysis. But — and this is the whole point of HiFi — you can correct those errors by reading the same molecule many times. That's the topic of segment eight. For now, just know that the chemistry is noisy at the level of single passes, and the cleverness is in how PacBio gets accurate sequence out of noisy chemistry.

\[Pause.\]

The deep insight here, the thing I want you to feel, is that **PacBio's chemistry produces a real-time movie of polymerase activity**, not just a sequence. The pulse durations carry information beyond just identity — they carry kinetic information about what the polymerase is doing at each step. This is something Illumina chemistry simply doesn't have. Illumina reads are static snapshots; PacBio reads are videos. The video carries extra channels of data — kinetic data — that turn out to be useful for things like methylation calling, which we'll come to. That's an unexpected dividend of doing real-time single-molecule observation: you get the sequence, and you get the dynamics of how it was read, both for free.

## SEGMENT 6 — Phi29 polymerase: the strand-displacing, high-fidelity, very slow workhorse

We've talked about the chip, the wells, the chemistry. Now let's spend a segment on the enzyme that sits at the bottom of every well doing the actual work: the polymerase. Because the choice of polymerase is one of the most consequential decisions in PacBio's whole design, and the enzyme they ended up with has some distinctly weird and useful properties.

The polymerase PacBio uses is derived from one called **phi29**, often written as Φ29. Phi29 is the DNA polymerase of a bacteriophage — a virus that infects the bacterium _Bacillus subtilis_. Like many bacteriophages, phi29 carries its own DNA polymerase as part of its genome; the phage uses that polymerase to replicate its own genome inside the bacterial host. The enzyme was originally characterized in the 1980s by the Spanish biochemist Margarita Salas, and it became a workhorse of molecular biology long before PacBio existed, for reasons we'll see.

What makes phi29 special?

\[Pause.\]

First and most important: **processivity**. Most DNA polymerases, left to their own devices, will copy a few hundred bases of a template and then fall off. They're not particularly sticky to the DNA. To get the polymerase to copy a long stretch, you typically need a partner protein — a "sliding clamp" — that physically rings around the DNA and tethers the polymerase to it. Phi29 doesn't need that. It's intrinsically extremely processive. A phi29 molecule, once it grabs a template, can stay on for tens of thousands of bases — hours of copying — without falling off. That's exactly what you need for single-molecule sequencing, because there's no way to put a fallen-off polymerase back on the same template; if you lose the enzyme mid-read, the read just stops.

Second: **strand displacement activity**. When phi29 is moving along a template and it encounters a stretch of double-stranded DNA — a region where some other piece of DNA is already paired with the template — it doesn't stop and it doesn't fall off. Instead, it actively pries the other strand off and keeps going, displacing the double-stranded region as it advances. Imagine a snowplow rolling forward and pushing snow off the road as it moves. That's strand displacement. Most polymerases stop at a double-stranded block; phi29 just plows through it.

This property is critical for PacBio's circular templates, which we'll cover in the next segment. The SMRTbell template is a circular single-stranded DNA loop, and the polymerase reads around it again and again, going past the joint between the original and the copy it just made — which is now double-stranded. Without strand displacement, the polymerase would stop after one pass. With strand displacement, it just keeps going. The polymerase reads around the circle, displacing its own previous copy, around and around, accumulating multiple passes of the same sequence. This is what enables circular consensus sequencing — the entire HiFi paradigm depends on it.

Third: **high fidelity**. Phi29 has a built-in proofreading exonuclease — a "spell-checker" that catches misincorporations and removes them. So even at the single-molecule level, the per-base error rate of incorporation is fairly low — much lower than, say, a typical Klenow fragment polymerase used in older sequencing chemistry. The proofreading helps, although it's not perfect; the overall error rate of PacBio raw reads is still in the ten-to-fifteen percent range, but that error comes mostly from the optical detection side — missed pulses, merged pulses, wrong color calls — rather than from the polymerase making chemical mistakes.

Fourth: **slow**. Phi29, modified for PacBio chemistry, runs at something like one to ten bases per second — which is glacially slow compared to a normal replicative polymerase that can do hundreds of bases per second. That slowness is actually good for the platform: it gives the camera enough time per base to integrate a clean signal. If the polymerase ran at full speed, the pulses would blur together and the optical system couldn't resolve them. PacBio has tuned the polymerase to a speed where the pulses are well separated and the camera can read them cleanly.

\[Pause.\]

So phi29 ticks every box that PacBio needs. **Highly processive, strand-displacing, high-fidelity, and a workable speed for optical detection.** It's almost as if the enzyme had been designed for the platform — but of course it wasn't. It was discovered, in nature, in a tiny bacteriophage, doing its job for entirely different reasons. PacBio's engineers then spent years modifying it to tolerate phospholinked fluorescent nucleotides, to be stable for long runs at room temperature, and to bind well to the streptavidin anchors at the bottom of the ZMW. The final enzyme PacBio uses is heavily engineered, but its core properties come from the original phi29.

This is, by the way, a recurring pattern in biotechnology — find an enzyme that nature has already optimized for some niche purpose, then re-engineer it for an entirely different application. Taq polymerase, the enzyme that made PCR possible, came from a thermophilic bacterium living in hot springs. The Cas9 enzyme of CRISPR came from a bacterial immune system. Phi29 came from a bacteriophage. In each case, the discovery of the enzyme came first; the application came years or decades later. It's worth remembering that the foundational molecular tools of the biotech revolution were almost all gifts from microbial evolution — sitting in libraries of obscure enzymes, waiting to be repurposed.

\[Pause.\]

One last note about the polymerase. PacBio has developed several generations of polymerase chemistry, named by version — the C2, P4, P5, P6 chemistries on the original RS platform, the various Sequel chemistries, and the most recent versions on Revio. Each new chemistry has typically delivered some combination of: longer reads (because the polymerase stays on longer), higher accuracy (because of better fidelity or better dye discrimination), or higher throughput (because of better well loading or faster cycling). The chemistry versions are part of why you'll see PacBio papers from different years reporting very different read lengths and accuracies. Same instrument, same fundamental physics, different chemistry generation. The platform has been improving steadily for over a decade.

Now, with the polymerase in hand, let's talk about the template — the SMRTbell — because the SMRTbell is the design innovation that turns PacBio into a self-error-correcting sequencer.

## SEGMENT 7 — SMRTbell circular templates: the design that enables circular consensus

We're now ready for one of the genuinely elegant pieces of design in modern sequencing. The SMRTbell template. It is a small piece of clever DNA architecture that turns a single linear molecule into a sequencing substrate the polymerase can read again and again, without ever falling off, and which delivers the same sequence information from multiple independent passes. The SMRTbell is what makes HiFi sequencing possible.

Let me build the picture from the ground up.

\[Pause.\]

You start with a normal piece of double-stranded DNA — say, a fifteen-kilobase fragment of genomic DNA, the kind you'd get from breaking up a human genome into manageable chunks. It's a linear double helix, with two ends. Each end has the usual structure: the top strand stops, the bottom strand stops, both blunt or with short overhangs.

The trick is this: you take a small hairpin-shaped piece of DNA — a single-stranded DNA that folds back on itself to form a short double-stranded stem with a loop at the top — and you ligate one of these hairpins onto each end of your linear DNA fragment. The hairpin's stem matches up with the end of your fragment, and DNA ligase fuses them together.

What you now have, structurally, is no longer a linear molecule with two free ends. **You have a single closed loop**, in which one strand of the original double-stranded fragment connects through a hairpin loop at one end into the other strand, which connects back through a hairpin loop at the other end into the first strand. If you traced the molecule with your finger, you'd go along one strand of the fragment, around a hairpin loop, back along the other strand, around the other hairpin loop, and back to where you started. It's one continuous single-stranded loop of DNA, with internal base pairing forming the double-stranded segments.

This is the SMRTbell. The shape, if you sketched it, looks roughly like a dumbbell or a bell — two loops at the ends connected by a long double-stranded middle section, the two strands of the middle section continuous with each other through the end loops. Hence the name.

\[Pause.\]

Why is this useful? Here's the magic.

You load the SMRTbell into a ZMW, with a phi29 polymerase ready to copy it. The polymerase grabs the template at some starting point and begins copying around the loop. It copies one strand of the long middle section — let's call it the "top" strand. It reads through, all fifteen kilobases or so, until it hits a hairpin loop at the end. Now, in a normal polymerase, that hairpin would be a problem — it's a structure, it's double-stranded, the polymerase would stop. But phi29 has strand displacement activity. It plows right through the hairpin, displacing the loop structure as it goes, and finds itself reading along what used to be the "bottom" strand of the original fragment — fifteen more kilobases — until it reaches the other hairpin loop at the other end. It plows through that one too, and now it's back to reading the top strand again, going around the loop a second time.

**The polymerase reads around the SMRTbell loop, over and over.** Each pass through the loop reads the same sequence — the original fragment, both strands, joined by hairpins. After the first pass, the polymerase is reading copies of its own previous output, because the SMRTbell has now been partially double-stranded by what the polymerase just made. But strand displacement keeps the polymerase going. It just chugs around, copying, displacing, copying, displacing, around and around.

If the polymerase stays bound for a long time — say, an hour or two — and the SMRTbell is, say, fifteen kilobases per pass, the polymerase might make ten, twenty, even thirty complete passes around the loop, generating an enormous output read called the **polymerase read** — the raw output from a single ZMW for an entire run.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the magic. Each pass reads the same sequence. The errors made on each pass are independent — they happen at random positions, by random misincorporations or missed pulses. The signal — the actual underlying base — is consistent across all passes. So if you take the polymerase read, split it back into individual passes by locating the hairpin sequences, and align all the passes against each other — you get a multiple sequence alignment of, say, twenty different reads of the same molecule. **At each position, the consensus call across all twenty passes is overwhelmingly likely to be the correct base, even if individual passes are noisy.**

This is the **circular consensus sequence**, or CCS. And we'll spend the whole next segment on what it does for accuracy. But the structural innovation that enables it — that's the SMRTbell.

Let me give you the analogy. **Imagine you're trying to read a letter that's been handwritten in messy cursive. You read it once, and you get most of the words right but miss a few — maybe ten percent of the words you can't quite make out. Now imagine you photocopied the letter onto a piece of paper twenty times in a row, and you read it twenty times. The places where you misread are random — different words each time. So if you compile all twenty readings, at each word, the majority answer is almost certainly the right one. Twenty noisy reads collapse into one accurate consensus.**

The SMRTbell is the trick that turns one molecule into twenty noisy reads. Hairpins on each end, connecting the two strands into a single closed loop, that the polymerase reads around and around. Such a simple structural insight. Such an enormous payoff.

\[Pause.\]

A couple of practical notes. The size of the SMRTbell insert — the length of the original DNA fragment — controls how many passes you can get. Short inserts can be read many times because each lap is fast; long inserts can only be read a few times because each lap is slow. So there's a tradeoff. PacBio HiFi sequencing typically uses inserts of fifteen to twenty-five kilobases. That's the sweet spot where you get long reads, but the polymerase can still complete eight to twenty passes per molecule during a run. Shorter inserts give shorter HiFi reads with very high accuracy from many passes. Longer inserts — fifty kilobases, a hundred — give longer reads but fewer passes per read, and the accuracy drops.

The hairpin adapters themselves are specifically designed sequences that PacBio software recognizes and uses to delineate where one pass ends and the next begins. They're standardized — every SMRTbell prep uses the same hairpin sequence — so the software always knows what to look for. Library prep for PacBio HiFi is essentially: shear the DNA to the right size, repair the ends, ligate the hairpins, purify, load. It's a few hours of bench work. Same as any library prep, just slightly different chemistry.

The point I want to leave you with from this segment: **the SMRTbell takes the inherently noisy single-molecule signal and amplifies the signal-to-noise ratio in software, by reading the same molecule many times.** It does this without amplifying the DNA itself — no PCR — so all the molecule-level information about chemical modifications, methylation, and so on is preserved. The accuracy comes from multiple passes of the same molecule, not from copies of it. That's a key distinction. PacBio HiFi reads are the actual original molecule, read many times. That property is going to matter again and again as we go through the applications.

## SEGMENT 8 — The CCS / HiFi paradigm shift: multiple passes, error correction, 99.9% accuracy

So we've now arrived at the most important conceptual moment in the whole history of PacBio: the **circular consensus sequencing** workflow, or CCS, also called **HiFi** in the modern PacBio branding. This is the change that, in 2019 and 2020, took PacBio from being a niche specialty platform to being the gold standard for serious genome assembly. I want to spend this whole segment on it, because if you understand only one thing about PacBio sequencing in the modern era, it should be this.

Let me set the stage. For most of PacBio's history, the platform produced what were called **continuous long reads**, or CLR. The polymerase would read across the SMRTbell once or twice, generating a long, single-pass read of maybe ten or twenty kilobases. The accuracy of these CLR reads was poor — somewhere between eighty-five and ninety percent base accuracy. That's the raw error rate of single-molecule sequencing chemistry: about ten to fifteen percent of bases are wrong, either substitutions, insertions, or — most commonly — deletions, where the polymerase added a base but the camera missed the pulse.

Eighty-five percent accuracy sounds terrible. It is, by Illumina standards. Illumina reads are around ninety-nine point nine percent accurate per base, raw, no correction needed. Comparing on a per-base basis, PacBio CLR was a thousand times worse than Illumina. For many years, this was the standard rap against PacBio — "PacBio reads are too noisy to use directly."

But — and here's the original PacBio insight — even with a fifteen percent per-base error rate, the errors are essentially random. They happen at random positions, with random identities. If you can read the same molecule many times and align the reads, the errors cancel out and the true sequence emerges. You can either align across multiple molecules (which is what you do when assembling a genome — overlap many independent reads and consensus the result), or you can align across multiple passes of the same molecule (which is what CCS does using the SMRTbell). Either way, the consensus accuracy is way better than the raw accuracy.

\[Pause.\]

For years, PacBio's strategy was the "many independent molecules" approach. You'd run a sample on PacBio, get a bunch of long noisy reads, and use them to construct a genome assembly by finding overlaps. The assembly itself would be highly accurate even though individual reads were not, because each genomic position was covered by, say, fifty different reads, and the consensus was reliable. This worked, but it required heavy sequencing — you needed lots of coverage to overcome the per-read noise — and the noisy reads were hard to use for any application that didn't involve assembly. You couldn't really use a single PacBio CLR read for, say, accurate variant calling, because the read itself was too unreliable.

Then in 2019, PacBio rolled out the **CCS workflow** as a major product, and renamed the output **HiFi reads**. The idea was: instead of optimizing for read _length_ — which had been the obsession of the previous decade — optimize for the number of _passes_. Use a slightly shorter insert, say fifteen to twenty kilobases instead of fifty. Let the polymerase read around it many times, accumulating many passes per molecule. Then run a consensus algorithm on those passes, in software, to produce a single corrected read.

The math works out beautifully. With ten or more passes, the consensus accuracy reaches ninety-nine point nine percent — that's Q30 quality, equivalent to Illumina, but on a read that's fifteen to twenty kilobases long instead of one hundred fifty bases. With twenty or more passes, you can hit ninety-nine point ninety-nine percent — Q40, better than Illumina. **Long and accurate, in a single read, from a single molecule.**

\[Pause.\]

The headline here is worth saying as clearly as I can: **HiFi reads are long-read and high-accuracy at the same time, for the first time in sequencing history.**

Until HiFi, there was always a tradeoff. Illumina: short and accurate. Nanopore CLR-style: long and noisy. PacBio CLR: long and noisy too. You picked one or the other; you couldn't have both. With HiFi, that tradeoff collapses. You get fifteen to twenty kilobases of read length, and you get Q30 to Q40 quality at every base. It's the long-read holy grail.

This is genuinely a paradigm shift, and the field's reaction over the following few years was to reorganize itself around it. Major genome assembly projects rebuilt their pipelines for HiFi. The first true telomere-to-telomere human genome assembly, T2T-CHM13 — the one that filled in those missing eight percent of the human reference — was driven by PacBio HiFi data, along with Oxford Nanopore data. The Vertebrate Genomes Project, building reference-quality assemblies for thousands of species, standardized on HiFi. Clinical sequencing labs that had been Illumina-only started adopting HiFi for cases where structural variation mattered. Within about two years, HiFi had become the de facto standard for serious _de novo_ assembly.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy I want you to remember for this. **A raw PacBio read is like a single hand-copy of a manuscript by a tired scribe — about fifteen percent of the words are slightly wrong. A HiFi read is what you get when you ask twenty different tired scribes to each independently copy the same manuscript, and then you compare their copies side by side. The places where they all agree are almost certainly right. The places where they disagree, you use majority vote, and you're almost certainly right there too. Twenty noisy copies, one accurate consensus.**

The SMRTbell gives you the twenty copies. The CCS algorithm gives you the consensus. The output is a HiFi read. Long. Accurate. Single-molecule. No PCR. The product of a beautifully integrated chain of physics, chemistry, biology, and software, with the final accuracy coming from statistical aggregation over many passes of the same molecule.

\[Pause.\]

Two more things I want to mention about HiFi before we move on.

First — HiFi reads are sometimes mislabeled as "consensus reads" in a way that suggests they come from averaging many _different_ molecules. They don't. They come from averaging many passes of the _same_ molecule. The distinction is crucial for downstream applications. A consensus across molecules averages away true single-molecule properties — for instance, methylation, which can differ from one molecule to another. A consensus across passes of one molecule preserves those properties, because every pass is sampling the same physical molecule. PacBio HiFi data, therefore, carries methylation information for every read, which is something Illumina simply cannot do (because of PCR amplification) and which Nanopore can do but in a slightly different way.

Second — the move to HiFi was, internally at PacBio, a major bet. For years the platform's marketing had emphasized read _length_, and the engineering had been pushed in that direction. Switching to shorter inserts to get more passes per molecule was a strategic reversal. But it was the right call. The combination of long-and-accurate has turned out to be more valuable than long-and-noisy, for almost every application. The HiFi era of PacBio is, in essence, the company finally finding its market fit, fifteen years after founding.

Now let's look at what those reads actually look like in practice — the read length distribution, the throughput, what you do with them.

## SEGMENT 9 — Read length distribution: 10-25 kb is the sweet spot

Let's talk about what a HiFi read actually looks like. Concretely. How long are these reads, in practice, and how do they distribute across a sequencing run?

A typical HiFi library is built from DNA fragments sheared to about fifteen to twenty kilobases. After SMRTbell prep — ligating the hairpins — and size selection on a special bead-based system to enrich for the right size range, you end up with a population of SMRTbell molecules whose insert sizes are tightly distributed around the target.

You load that library onto a SMRT cell and run the instrument. The polymerase makes many passes around each SMRTbell, generating the multi-pass polymerase read. After the run, the CCS software takes those polymerase reads, identifies the passes by locating the hairpin sequences, aligns them, and outputs HiFi reads. The HiFi read length is the length of one pass — which is the insert size of the SMRTbell.

In practice, a typical HiFi run produces reads distributed roughly between ten and twenty-five kilobases, with a peak around fifteen to twenty. That's your sweet spot. Long enough to span essentially all small repeats, all gene regions, almost all individual structural variants. Short enough to get many passes per molecule, hence very high accuracy.

\[Pause.\]

To give you a sense of why fifteen to twenty kilobases is so useful, let me compare it to what fits in a read.

A typical human gene — including its exons and introns — is somewhere between ten and a hundred kilobases long. The median is maybe twenty kilobases. So a single HiFi read can span an entire small-to-medium gene from start to finish, with no assembly required. That's huge for things like phasing — figuring out which variants are on the same chromosome — because variants linked on a single read are by definition on the same parental chromosome.

A typical processed mRNA transcript is one to five kilobases. A single HiFi read can easily span an entire transcript, including the 5' UTR, all the exons stitched together, and the 3' UTR with its polyA tail. That's what makes PacBio Iso-Seq — full-length transcript sequencing — possible. We'll get to that in segment fifteen.

A typical structural variant — an insertion, deletion, or inversion of, say, a few hundred to a few thousand bases — fits comfortably within a single HiFi read. The breakpoints, the inserted sequence, the flanking context — all visible in one piece of data. Compare this to Illumina, where structural variants have to be inferred from indirect signals (split reads, discordant pairs, abnormal coverage) because no single read can span the event. HiFi makes structural variant calling direct and unambiguous.

A typical region of segmental duplication — where multi-kilobase chunks of the genome have been copied to multiple loci — is often spannable by a single HiFi read if the unique flanking sequence is captured. This is what allows HiFi to resolve segmental duplications that short reads cannot.

\[Pause.\]

PacBio has also experimented with longer inserts, in modes sometimes called "ultralong HiFi" or just "long-insert" runs. With inserts of thirty to fifty kilobases, you can get longer reads, but you sacrifice some accuracy because the polymerase completes fewer passes per molecule. With inserts of a hundred kilobases or more, you essentially fall back to CLR-style noisy reads with one or two passes — useful for some niche assembly problems but not the HiFi standard.

For comparison, Oxford Nanopore's reads are typically much longer — twenty to a hundred kilobases is routine, and with the right library prep you can get individual reads in the megabase range. Nanopore reads are longer than PacBio HiFi reads, often by a factor of five or ten. But they're noisier — typical Nanopore Q-scores for simplex reads are around Q15 to Q20, an order of magnitude worse than HiFi. So the two platforms occupy slightly different niches. Need maximum length, willing to tolerate some noise — Nanopore. Need maximum accuracy at moderate length — PacBio HiFi. We'll come back to this comparison in detail in segment sixteen.

\[Pause.\]

One last point. The read length distribution out of a HiFi run isn't uniform. There's a peak, but there are also tails — some shorter reads from molecules where the polymerase quit early, some longer reads from rare oversized inserts that got through size selection. When you're planning an experiment, you typically calculate your expected coverage in terms of bases sequenced, not reads sequenced. A typical Revio run produces something like one hundred and twenty gigabases of HiFi data per SMRT cell. Divided by an average read length of seventeen kilobases, that's roughly seven million reads per cell. For a thirty-x coverage human genome (which has three gigabases), you need about ninety gigabases of HiFi data, which fits on a single Revio cell with room to spare. That's a huge change from earlier generations — in 2019 it took a full Sequel II run to get thirty-x human coverage; now you can get it on a fraction of a Revio cell.

The economics, in other words, have shifted dramatically. We'll cover that in segment eleven.

For now, hold this picture. **HiFi reads cluster around fifteen to twenty kilobases. That length is the sweet spot for spanning genes, transcripts, structural variants, segmental duplications, and most repeats.** The combination of that length with Q30-plus accuracy is what makes HiFi the dominant platform for high-quality genome work in the modern era.

## SEGMENT 10 — The PacBio family: RS, Sequel, Sequel II, Sequel IIe, Revio

Welcome back. We're going to spend this segment on the commercial history of PacBio, the company — the lineage of instruments, the rocky early years, the turnaround in 2019, and the position of the platform today. The history is, frankly, one of the more dramatic stories in biotech, and it's instructive about how transformative technologies sometimes take years longer to land than their inventors expect.

\[Pause.\]

PacBio — Pacific Biosciences, the company — was founded in 2004, originally under the name Nanofluidics. The core technology, the zero-mode waveguide, came out of academic research at Cornell, in the labs of Watt Webb and Harold Craighead. Three graduate students from those labs — Jonas Korlach, Steve Turner, and Mathieu Foquet — became the technical founding team. The CEO from 2004 was Hugh Martin. The company raised something on the order of four hundred million dollars in venture capital across six rounds before going public in October 2010.

That's a lot of money to raise pre-product. PacBio's first commercial instrument, the **PacBio RS**, was released to limited customers in late 2010 and broadly in 2011. It was a six-figure machine. The chemistry generated reads averaging about a thousand bases — disappointingly short, given the long-read promise — with high error rates and modest throughput. Early customers were enthusiastic about the principle but frustrated by the practicalities. PacBio's stock, which had IPO'd at around fifteen dollars, drifted downward through the early 2010s.

The **PacBio RS II**, released in 2013, was an upgrade. Better chemistry, longer reads — averaging ten to fifteen kilobases on the late P6 chemistry — higher throughput. The RS II found a real if limited market in microbial genomics, where its ability to finish bacterial genomes — to close the gaps that short-read assemblers couldn't — was genuinely useful. But human genomics was still Illumina territory, and PacBio's cost per gigabase was orders of magnitude higher.

The **Sequel**, released in 2015, was a more radical redesign. New optics, new chip with a million ZMWs (up from 150,000 on the RS), much lower cost per cell. Sequel was supposed to be the breakthrough. It wasn't, quite. The original Sequel chemistry was buggy and underdelivered. Reads were shorter than promised, the chip didn't load as well as expected, and PacBio's financials deteriorated through 2016 and 2017. Stock dropped below five dollars. The company looked like it might fold.

\[Pause.\]

Then in 2018, in what was probably the most dramatic moment in PacBio's commercial history, **Illumina announced it was acquiring PacBio for one point two billion dollars**. The deal was framed as Illumina expanding into long reads. For PacBio shareholders, it was a lifeline.

But it didn't go through. The Federal Trade Commission, and antitrust authorities in the UK, sued to block the acquisition on competition grounds — basically arguing that Illumina was buying its main long-read competitor in order to neutralize them. The legal fight dragged on for over a year. In January 2020, the deal was abandoned, and Illumina paid PacBio a ninety-eight million dollar termination fee. PacBio was on its own again.

But by then, something had changed. In April 2019, between the Illumina deal being announced and being blocked, PacBio had released the **Sequel II**. The Sequel II had a chip with eight million ZMWs — eight times the Sequel — and it was paired with a much better chemistry. Crucially, the Sequel II was the instrument on which CCS / HiFi sequencing was first practical at scale. The combination of high throughput and the HiFi workflow turned PacBio, almost overnight, into the right tool for human genome assembly.

The 2019-2020 period is, in retrospect, the platform's inflection point. The Sequel II hardware plus the HiFi workflow plus the failed Illumina acquisition plus a new CEO (Christian Henry, formerly an Illumina executive, took over in 2020) — these things combined to relaunch PacBio as a serious competitor. The stock recovered, customer interest surged, and major genome projects — the T2T consortium, the human pangenome project, the Vertebrate Genomes Project — adopted HiFi as a standard.

\[Pause.\]

The **Sequel IIe**, released in 2020, was an incremental upgrade with onboard computing that ran CCS analysis in real time during the sequencing run, so customers received HiFi reads as the run finished rather than having to do CCS analysis in a separate offline step. Small change, big workflow improvement.

The **Revio**, announced in late 2022 and shipping in 2023, is the current flagship. Revio has chips with twenty-five million ZMWs per cell, and the instrument runs four cells simultaneously. The throughput per instrument is around three hundred sixty gigabases per day at the high end. Cost per human genome on Revio is in the low hundreds of dollars, which is a huge drop from where PacBio was just a few years ago — and competitive with what Illumina genomes cost when Illumina was at the same level of maturity. PacBio claims Revio can sequence well over a thousand human genomes per year on a single instrument, where the Sequel II could do less than a hundred.

\[Pause.\]

So the arc is roughly: rough start with the RS (2011), persistent struggle through Sequel (2015), near-death experience with the failed Illumina acquisition (2018-2020), turnaround with Sequel II plus HiFi (2019-2020), and now mature deployment with Revio (2023-onward). It took the platform about a decade longer than its founders had hoped to find its market. But it found it. PacBio today is a profitable-trajectory company with real customer adoption and a clear technical lead in high-accuracy long reads.

The deeper lesson, if you want to take one from this history: **transformative laboratory technologies often take fifteen to twenty years from first commercial product to broad market success.** The technology has to mature, the chemistry has to improve, the cost has to come down, and the applications have to catch up. PacBio's principle — single-molecule real-time sequencing on ZMWs — was right in 2004. The execution took until 2019 to fully deliver on it. That's not unusual. It's about average for a complex platform technology in the life sciences.

Now let's talk about what you do with the platform today.

## SEGMENT 11 — Throughput economics: fewer reads, more bases per read, when it matters

Let me spend a segment on the economics of PacBio versus its competitors, because the cost structure of long-read sequencing is unintuitive and matters a lot for figuring out when to use which platform.

The basic cost equation in sequencing is: you have a fixed cost per run (the consumables — chip, reagents — plus a fraction of the instrument's amortized cost), and you divide that by the number of bases you generate per run, to get a cost per gigabase. For Illumina NovaSeq, the cost per gigabase is, roughly, a few dollars at scale. For PacBio Revio HiFi, it's somewhere in the tens of dollars per gigabase — three to ten times more expensive per base than Illumina. For Oxford Nanopore on their high-end PromethION, it's in between, somewhere around five to fifteen dollars per gigabase depending on chemistry.

So the headline comparison is: **PacBio HiFi data is more expensive per base than Illumina data. Significantly so.** That's the eternal trade-off — paying for the long reads and the high accuracy.

\[Pause.\]

But the relevant question is not "how much per base" — it's "how much for the answer I'm trying to get." And there, the calculus changes depending on the application.

If you want to do a million-sample GWAS — genome-wide association study — where you genotype a few hundred thousand known variants, Illumina is irreplaceable. PacBio is overkill, and you can't afford it at that scale.

If you want to count RNA transcripts in ten thousand single cells, Illumina is the answer. PacBio doesn't have the right chemistry or throughput for that workload, although PacBio Iso-Seq has a niche for full-length transcript characterization in smaller numbers of samples.

If you want to do a clinical exome on a patient, Illumina is the answer. It's cheap, fast, accurate enough, and well-supported by clinical pipelines.

But — and this is the "when it matters" part — if you want to **assemble a new genome from scratch, to a reference-quality standard, including the previously unassemblable regions** — PacBio HiFi is the answer, not Illumina. The cost per gigabase is higher, but the cost per assembled genome is lower, because you need much less coverage (thirty-x HiFi is enough for a high-quality human assembly, where you'd need much more Illumina to even attempt the same job, and you still couldn't assemble the repeats). The number of reads needed is far fewer, but each read does more work.

If you want to **catalog structural variants in a tumor or a rare disease patient**, HiFi is the answer, because each HiFi read can directly span the event. Short reads only see structural variants through indirect signals that are unreliable for any but the largest events.

If you want to **resolve the two parental copies of a chromosome — phased, diploid assembly** — HiFi reads, being long enough to span most heterozygous variants, make this routine. Short reads collapse the two copies together, and reconstructing phase from short reads requires statistical inference that is often unreliable.

If you want to **detect DNA methylation directly, without bisulfite conversion**, HiFi (and Nanopore) can do it. Illumina cannot, fundamentally, because PCR amplification erases the modification. We'll spend the next segment on methylation.

If you want to **characterize a microbial genome to closure, including any plasmids, prophages, and mobile elements** — HiFi is the answer. A single PacBio run on a bacterial isolate gives you a complete, closed, polished assembly. Illumina can give you a draft, but the draft is fragmented and the repetitive elements are unresolved.

\[Pause.\]

The pattern is: **PacBio HiFi wins when accuracy at every position, including in difficult regions, matters more than raw throughput.** The cost per useful answer can be much lower than Illumina, even if the cost per base is higher. The applications where this is true — _de novo_ assembly, structural variants, phasing, methylation, complete microbial genomics — are exactly the applications where PacBio HiFi has become the standard.

The reverse pattern is: **Illumina wins when you're counting things — transcripts, variants at known positions, allele frequencies — and you can tolerate some loss of information in repetitive regions, because the regions you care about are well-behaved.** Most clinical and population-scale work falls in this category, which is why Illumina remains overwhelmingly dominant.

Then there's the niche where **PacBio Iso-Seq is unique**: full-length transcript sequencing for isoform discovery in a single sample. We'll cover Iso-Seq specifically in segment fifteen.

And there's the comparison with Nanopore, which we'll dedicate segment sixteen to in detail.

\[Pause.\]

One throughput note: as of the early-2020s, the Revio instrument has dramatically narrowed the cost gap with Illumina for whole-genome sequencing. A Revio human genome at thirty-x HiFi coverage runs in the low hundreds of dollars — not equal to Illumina, but close enough that for many clinical applications the extra accuracy and SV detection ability is worth it. PacBio's trajectory is to continue driving this cost down, and they've publicly targeted human genomes in the very-low-hundreds or even sub-hundred range over the next few generations. If that happens, the cost-per-gigabase argument against PacBio largely disappears, and the platform becomes a real contender for clinical genomics at scale. We'll come back to this in segment eighteen.

Now let's look at what HiFi can see that other platforms cannot. Starting with methylation.

## SEGMENT 12 — Methylation calling: 5mC, 6mA from polymerase kinetics

We mentioned in passing, earlier, that PacBio reads carry kinetic information — the timing of each pulse — as well as identity information. I want to spend this segment showing you what that kinetic information is good for. The headline application is **direct detection of DNA methylation**, without any chemical conversion of the sample, just from the polymerase's natural behavior as it reads modified bases.

Let me set up why this matters.

\[Pause.\]

DNA methylation is one of the most important kinds of epigenetic modification. In mammalian genomes, the most common modification is **5-methylcytosine** (5mC) — a methyl group added to the fifth carbon of cytosine, typically in the context of CpG dinucleotides. Methylated CpGs in promoters generally silence gene expression; methylated CpGs in gene bodies have more complex effects. Methylation patterns differ between cell types, between healthy and cancerous cells, between maternal and paternal alleles (genomic imprinting), and they change over the lifespan. Reading the methylome — the pattern of methylation across the genome — is essential for understanding gene regulation, development, cancer, and many other things.

In bacteria, the dominant modification is **6-methyladenine** (6mA) and **4-methylcytosine** (4mC) — these mark the bacterium's own DNA so that its restriction enzymes don't chop it up, and they also play roles in DNA repair and gene regulation. Bacterial methylome patterns differ between species and even between strains, and are useful for typing and tracking.

For decades, the standard way to read methylation was **bisulfite sequencing**: treat the DNA with sodium bisulfite, which chemically converts unmethylated cytosines to uracil (read as thymine by sequencers) while leaving methylated cytosines intact. Then sequence with Illumina, and methylated Cs show up as Cs while unmethylated ones show up as Ts. Clever, but it has problems: the bisulfite treatment is harsh and damages the DNA, only methylated cytosines are read (5mC) — not 6mA or other modifications — and the workflow is laborious.

\[Pause.\]

PacBio offers a totally different approach. **The polymerase, when it encounters a methylated base on the template, takes slightly longer to incorporate the next nucleotide.** The methyl group sterically interferes with the active site geometry, slowing the polymerase by a few milliseconds. This delay shows up directly in the timing of the fluorescent pulses, and PacBio's software can analyze that timing — what they call the **interpulse duration**, IPD — and infer the presence and identity of modifications.

The information is right there, in the kinetics of the polymerase, which the platform records anyway as part of every read. **No special sample prep. No bisulfite. No chemical conversion. The methylation calls fall out of the same data that gives you the sequence.** Run a HiFi sample, and you get both the sequence and the methylation pattern, on every read, for every CpG and every methylated adenine in the genome. Free of charge, more or less.

The accuracy of methylation calling depends on the number of passes per read and the depth of coverage. For high-accuracy 5mC calling at every CpG in a human genome, you typically want around twenty-x HiFi coverage, which is well within the normal sequencing depth for an assembly. PacBio has explicit tools — the most recent ones use deep learning models trained on known methylation patterns — for calling 5mC, 6mA, and 4mC from HiFi kinetics data.

\[Pause.\]

The applications are real. **Bacterial methylomes** can be completely characterized in a single PacBio run — you get the genome and the methylation map together, which has revealed that bacterial methylation patterns are far more diverse and complex than anyone realized before long-read sequencing. **Cancer methylomes** can be analyzed with single-molecule resolution, meaning you can see methylation heterogeneity between individual tumor cells reflected in individual reads. **Imprinted regions** of the human genome — where the methylation differs between maternal and paternal alleles — can be analyzed directly, with phasing, because HiFi reads are long enough to link methylation patterns to specific haplotypes.

The deep advantage here is that PacBio's methylation calling preserves the link between methylation and the underlying genetic variation, in the same molecule. You can say: "This particular CpG, on this particular haplotype, in this particular cell-of-origin, is methylated." That kind of three-way linkage — variant, haplotype, methylation — is essentially impossible with bisulfite-based methods, which destroy the original molecule and require statistical reconstruction.

\[Pause.\]

Nanopore can also detect methylation directly, by a similar principle — the current through the nanopore is slightly different when a methylated base passes through. Both long-read platforms therefore offer this capability, and both are widely used for methylation analysis today. The market has split: Illumina still dominates classical bulk methylation surveys (via bisulfite), while PacBio and Nanopore have taken over for single-molecule, phased, or comprehensive methylome work.

The key insight to leave the segment with: **PacBio reads carry epigenetic information as a free side-channel, because the polymerase's kinetics are sensitive to base modifications and the platform records those kinetics natively.** This is one of the unexpected dividends of real-time single-molecule observation. The same physical fact that lets you sequence — watching the polymerase work — also lets you read the chemical modifications on the template. One sample, one run, two layers of data. That's a profound capability, and it's one of the things that has cemented PacBio's role in modern genomics.

## SEGMENT 13 — PacBio for human genome assembly: telomere-to-telomere, T2T-CHM13

We need to spend a segment on what is probably PacBio HiFi's single most celebrated achievement: the completion of the human genome. Not the original Human Genome Project completion of 2003 — that was a draft, with about eight percent of the sequence missing. I mean the **truly complete genome**, gap-free, every base from telomere to telomere on every chromosome, which was published in 2022 by the Telomere-to-Telomere (T2T) consortium. That achievement was made possible by long-read sequencing — predominantly PacBio HiFi, with help from Oxford Nanopore for the longest repeats. It is the proof of concept that the long-read era can actually finish what the short-read era could not.

\[Pause.\]

Let me set the stakes. The 2003 reference human genome, refined over the next two decades into versions like GRCh38, was overwhelmingly an Illumina-and-Sanger product, mapped against original BAC-based clones. It contained gaps — regions where the assembly software simply could not figure out the sequence, marked with strings of N's, meaning "we know there's DNA here, but we don't know what it says." Those gaps were concentrated in centromeres (the big repetitive structures at chromosome middles), in heterochromatic regions on the short arms of acrocentric chromosomes (where the ribosomal RNA gene arrays live), in segmental duplication blocks, and in some hard-to-sequence GC-rich regions. About two hundred million bases were missing — a meaningful chunk of the genome, and the most genomically active chunk in many ways.

The T2T consortium decided to attack this head-on with long reads. They chose a sample called CHM13 — a hydatidiform mole cell line, which is essentially a haploid genome (only one copy of each chromosome, because of unusual reproductive biology) — to simplify the assembly by removing heterozygosity. They sequenced CHM13 deeply with PacBio HiFi (around thirty-x coverage) and Oxford Nanopore ultra-long reads, and they used both data types together: HiFi for accuracy, Nanopore for spanning the very longest repeat arrays where even HiFi reads weren't long enough.

The result, published in **Science in 2022 as the T2T-CHM13 assembly**, was a complete, gap-free human genome. Every centromere reconstructed. Every telomere assembled. The acrocentric short arms, including the ribosomal RNA gene arrays, all sequenced. About two hundred new megabases of sequence revealed, and within that, around two thousand new gene annotations. Whole satellite array structures, never seen before, characterized base by base. It was a landmark.

\[Pause.\]

The role of PacBio HiFi specifically in that work cannot be overstated. **HiFi reads were the backbone of the assembly.** The Nanopore ultralong reads provided extra reach into the longest repeat arrays, but for most of the genome — for the gene-rich euchromatic majority, for the segmental duplications, for the small-to-medium repeats — HiFi alone was sufficient. The accuracy of HiFi made it possible to align reads against themselves without confusing similar but non-identical repeat copies. The length of HiFi made it possible to span almost everything short reads couldn't. Without HiFi, the assembly would not have been possible at the accuracy and completeness achieved.

The T2T paper was followed by the **Human Pangenome Reference Consortium**, which is building HiFi-based diploid assemblies for hundreds of diverse human samples — a real pangenome, capturing the structural variation that the single reference genome cannot represent. That project, ongoing as of the time of this lecture, is also overwhelmingly powered by PacBio HiFi data, supplemented with other technologies.

\[Pause.\]

The conceptual lesson here is significant. **The "reference genome" of any species is only as complete as the sequencing technology that built it.** For two decades, our reference genome had holes shaped exactly like the limits of short-read sequencing. We told ourselves those holes weren't important, because we couldn't see what was in them. The HiFi era showed us what was in them — including thousands of genes, vast amounts of structural variation, key elements of chromosome biology. Long reads have not just completed the genome; they have revealed that the genome we thought we knew was substantively incomplete in important regions.

The same revolution is now underway for every other species in genomics. The Vertebrate Genomes Project, the Earth BioGenome Project, the Darwin Tree of Life — all of these are building reference-quality HiFi assemblies for thousands of species, replacing the fragmented short-read drafts of the previous decade with telomere-to-telomere assemblies for organism after organism. We're, essentially, redoing genomics across the entire tree of life, this time properly. That's a thirty-year project. PacBio HiFi is the engine of it.

## SEGMENT 14 — Structural variant calling: what only long reads can see

Let me spend a segment on a specific application where PacBio HiFi has made a clinical and scientific difference that's worth understanding in its own right: **structural variant calling**.

A structural variant — an SV — is a genomic change larger than fifty base pairs. They include large insertions, large deletions, inversions (where a chunk of chromosome gets flipped), duplications (where a chunk gets copied), translocations (where pieces from different chromosomes get joined), and complex rearrangements. SVs are biologically and clinically very important. They cause major developmental disorders, drive cancer, contribute to evolution, and account for far more total base-pair difference between any two human genomes than do single-nucleotide variants. The typical human carries something like twenty thousand SVs relative to the reference.

Short reads are bad at SVs. To detect an SV with short reads, you have to infer the event from indirect signals: split reads (one end maps to one location, the other end to a distant location), discordant read pairs (paired ends map at unexpected distances or orientations), abnormal coverage (a region with twice or zero coverage suggests duplication or deletion), and so on. These signals are noisy, and they fail completely for SVs that lie within repetitive regions — which is most SVs, because repeats are where most structural change happens.

\[Pause.\]

HiFi reads change the game. **A HiFi read can directly span an SV from its left flanking unique sequence, through the breakpoint, into the variant sequence, through the second breakpoint, into the right flanking unique sequence — all in a single read.** No inference needed. You see the event directly. You can sequence the insertion. You can read the breakpoints with single-base resolution. You can characterize complex events that involve multiple breakpoints in a small region.

The result is that HiFi-based SV calling is dramatically more sensitive and dramatically more accurate than short-read SV calling. Studies comparing the two have consistently found that short reads miss roughly half of all SVs that long reads can find, and the SVs they miss are skewed toward larger events and events in repetitive regions — exactly the events that are most often clinically important.

\[Pause.\]

For clinical genomics, this matters enormously. Many rare disease cases have a structural variant as the underlying cause, and these have historically gone undiagnosed by exome or even short-read whole-genome sequencing because the SV was invisible. With HiFi, those cases now have a real shot at diagnosis. PacBio has been actively pursuing rare disease clinical applications precisely on this basis — there are now reports of patients receiving long-awaited molecular diagnoses, after years of failed short-read workups, when long-read sequencing finally revealed an SV.

For cancer genomics, SV detection is also transforming. Cancer genomes are full of complex rearrangements — chromothripsis, breakage-fusion-bridge cycles, oncogene amplifications — that short reads can only partially characterize. Long reads see these directly and reveal their structure. This is starting to influence cancer diagnostics and the choice of targeted therapies.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want to leave you with. **Short reads detecting structural variants are like trying to identify a car wreck by looking at scattered debris.** You can sometimes infer that there was a wreck, you might guess what kind of vehicles were involved, but you can't see the wreck itself. **Long reads are like having an aerial photograph of the wreck.** You can see exactly what happened, where each car ended up, what's missing, what's misplaced. The event is directly observable rather than inferred. That difference, for the things SVs cause — diseases, cancers, evolution — is enormous, and it's part of why HiFi is rapidly becoming the standard for any application where structural variation matters.

## SEGMENT 15 — PacBio Iso-Seq: full-length transcript sequencing, isoform discovery

Let's spend a segment on a PacBio application that doesn't get as much press as genome assembly, but which is uniquely powerful and uniquely PacBio: **Iso-Seq**, the platform's full-length transcript sequencing workflow.

Here's the motivation. RNA, in eukaryotic cells, is processed from primary transcripts into mature mRNAs through splicing — the cutting out of introns and the joining of exons. Most genes produce multiple distinct mRNAs through alternative splicing — different combinations of exons joined together in different ways, producing protein isoforms with subtly different functions. A typical human gene has, on average, four to ten major isoforms, and many genes have dozens. Knowing which isoforms a gene produces, in which tissues, at which developmental stages, is fundamental for understanding what the gene actually does.

Short-read RNA-seq, the standard tool, doesn't see isoforms directly. It sees short fragments of transcripts — 150-base reads from random positions along the mRNA — and the isoform structure has to be inferred from which exon junctions are observed in the reads. This inference works for simple cases but fails for genes with many isoforms, especially when isoforms differ only at distant points along the transcript. You see lots of evidence for various splice junctions, but you can't tell which combinations of junctions actually co-occur on the same molecule. The full isoform structure is a guess.

\[Pause.\]

Iso-Seq solves this directly. **A single HiFi read can span a complete transcript from 5' cap to polyA tail.** You see exactly which exons are joined together on that molecule. No inference. The isoform is observed as a single piece of data.

The Iso-Seq workflow takes total RNA, converts it to full-length cDNA using a clever oligo-dT-and-template-switching method that captures complete transcripts, builds these into SMRTbells, sequences with HiFi chemistry, and outputs full-length transcript reads. Each read is a single mature mRNA, in full, from one end to the other. Tens of thousands of distinct transcripts can be characterized in a single run.

The applications are powerful. **Isoform discovery in new species** — for any organism whose transcriptome hasn't been thoroughly characterized, Iso-Seq is the tool of choice. **Cancer-specific isoforms** — tumors often produce aberrant transcripts (gene fusions, exon skipping events) that Iso-Seq can directly characterize. **Tissue-specific isoforms** — Iso-Seq across tissue types reveals which isoforms are expressed where. **Developmental dynamics** — Iso-Seq across developmental stages reveals isoform switching that drives differentiation.

\[Pause.\]

A specific contrast with short-read RNA-seq. Suppose a gene has three isoforms: ABCD, ABD, and BCD (where each letter represents an exon). Short reads can see junctions A-B, B-C, C-D, B-D, A-C — but it cannot tell whether a read crossing A-B and a read crossing C-D came from the same molecule. The assignment of junctions to isoforms is statistical, and for genes with many junctions, the answer can be ambiguous. Iso-Seq reads, by contrast, directly observe each molecule from A through D, and the isoform identity is read off in one piece. ABCD is ABCD; you don't have to infer it.

Iso-Seq is therefore the gold standard for any work that requires accurate isoform-level resolution. It's been used to build comprehensive isoform catalogs for human tissues, to discover novel isoforms in disease contexts, to characterize complex transcriptomes in non-model organisms, and to validate isoform predictions from short-read pipelines.

Nanopore offers a similar capability, sometimes called direct RNA sequencing in their version, which has its own appeal (no cDNA conversion). But for accurate, full-length, quantitative isoform sequencing in the standard mode, PacBio Iso-Seq is the dominant tool today.

## SEGMENT 16 — PacBio vs Nanopore: the long-read head-to-head

It's time to do the head-to-head comparison with Oxford Nanopore that I've been promising throughout the lecture. The two long-read platforms are the two parallel solutions to the same problem, and you really cannot understand the long-read field without understanding how they differ.

\[Pause.\]

The fundamental difference is in the physical principle.

**PacBio** watches a polymerase incorporating nucleotides, by looking at fluorescent flashes. It's an optical platform. Read length is bounded by how long the polymerase stays bound and by the SMRTbell insert size.

**Oxford Nanopore** threads a single DNA molecule through a tiny protein pore, and reads the sequence by measuring the change in electrical current as each base passes through. It's an electrical platform. Read length is bounded essentially only by the size of the input DNA molecule — if you can prep a megabase-long molecule intact, you can read it.

So the platforms have totally different physics. They also have totally different form factors. PacBio instruments are large, expensive, lab-installed — a Revio is the size of a small fridge, costs hundreds of thousands of dollars. Nanopore instruments range from the **MinION**, a USB-stick-sized device that costs about a thousand dollars and runs on any laptop, up through the **PromethION**, a larger benchtop instrument competitive in throughput with a PacBio Sequel II. This portability is one of Nanopore's biggest selling points — Nanopore has been used in the field, in epidemics, in clinics, even on the International Space Station.

\[Pause.\]

Now the technical comparison.

**Read length.** Nanopore wins, often by a lot. Nanopore reads can routinely exceed a hundred kilobases, and with careful prep can reach the megabase range. PacBio HiFi reads peak around fifteen to twenty kilobases. So if you need the longest possible reads — to span the longest repeats, or to do contiguous assembly of repetitive arrays — Nanopore is your tool. The T2T project used Nanopore ultralong reads, alongside HiFi, specifically for spanning the very longest centromeric repeats.

**Read accuracy.** PacBio wins, clearly. HiFi reads are Q30-Q40 quality, with sub-percent per-base error rates. Nanopore simplex (single-pass) reads are typically Q15-Q20, which is roughly one to two percent error — better than they used to be but still meaningfully worse than HiFi. Nanopore has introduced duplex sequencing, where both strands of the same molecule are read sequentially and consensused, which gets to roughly Q30, comparable to HiFi but harder to achieve consistently and at lower yield. So for applications where every base matters — variant calling at the per-base level, accurate genome assembly without polishing, methylation calling with high specificity — PacBio HiFi tends to be preferred.

**Throughput and cost.** Both platforms have similar throughput on their flagship instruments, in the hundreds of gigabases per run. Cost per gigabase is similar, with the exact comparison depending on chemistry version and time of measurement. Both are several times more expensive than Illumina.

**Methylation calling.** Both can do it directly. PacBio uses kinetics; Nanopore uses raw signal. The results are largely comparable for 5mC; PacBio has historically been stronger for 4mC and 6mA, but Nanopore has been catching up. Both are widely used.

**Workflow.** PacBio is more like a traditional lab instrument — big, fixed, requires trained operators, runs are tens of hours. Nanopore is much more flexible — runs can be as short as fifteen minutes, the user can read data in real time as it comes off the instrument, libraries are simpler. For rapid turnaround or unusual settings, Nanopore wins.

\[Pause.\]

The summary I want to leave you with. **PacBio is the precision tool, Nanopore is the speed-and-length champion.** If you need maximum accuracy at moderate length, PacBio. If you need maximum length or rapid turnaround, Nanopore. Many large-scale projects, including T2T-CHM13, use both — HiFi as the backbone for accuracy, Nanopore ultralong for spanning the longest repeats. The two platforms are complementary as often as they're competitive.

The market has, at this point, settled into a duopoly within long reads. PacBio dominates _de novo_ assembly of complex genomes at reference quality, Iso-Seq for isoform discovery, and clinical applications where accuracy matters. Nanopore dominates field deployment, portable sequencing, ultralong reads, and applications where flexibility and rapid turnaround matter. Both are growing. Both have huge installed bases. Both have clear roadmaps. The era when "long reads" meant one of these companies and you had to pick is essentially over; the field has matured to where you pick based on the specific application.

## SEGMENT 17 — PacBio + Illumina hybrid assemblies: historical workflow, less common now

A brief segment on a workflow that was the dominant long-read approach for several years and has become less common in the HiFi era: **hybrid assembly** combining PacBio long reads with Illumina short reads.

The motivation was straightforward. In the CLR era — when PacBio raw reads were at 85-90% accuracy — you couldn't assemble a genome from PacBio alone, because the per-base errors would propagate into the assembly. The standard fix was to use PacBio long reads to determine the overall structure of the assembly — the contigs, the scaffolds, the spanning of repeats — and then use Illumina short reads, with their per-base accuracy, to **polish** the assembly: correct individual base errors in the PacBio-derived sequence.

The workflow looked like: sequence the genome with PacBio CLR to about thirty- or fifty-x coverage, assemble using one of the long-read assemblers (Canu, Flye, Falcon, hifiasm in later versions), then sequence the same genome with Illumina to about thirty-x coverage and use the Illumina reads to polish the PacBio assembly with tools like Pilon, racon, or medaka. The output was a genome assembly with PacBio-derived structure and Illumina-derived per-base accuracy. The cost: two separate sequencing runs on two platforms.

\[Pause.\]

In the HiFi era, this is largely obsolete. HiFi reads are already Q30-Q40 accurate, so the Illumina polishing step is no longer needed. A standard HiFi assembly today uses HiFi reads only — assembled with a tool like hifiasm — and the resulting assembly is both highly contiguous and highly accurate without any short-read polishing. The workflow has simplified, and the per-genome cost has dropped because you don't need a second platform.

Hybrid assembly persists in a few niches. For very large or very complex genomes, where adding any extra data source improves the result, some projects still incorporate Illumina data. For metagenomics, where you're assembling many species at once from mixed samples, hybrid approaches sometimes help. For some specialized applications where exquisite per-base accuracy is required, Illumina polishing of HiFi assemblies can still squeeze out marginal improvements. But the era when hybrid was the standard, default approach is over.

The historical note matters because if you read papers from the 2015-2018 period describing genome assembly methodology, you'll see "PacBio plus Illumina" as the standard recipe. That was right for that era. The HiFi paradigm shift, in 2019-2020, made the second platform unnecessary for most cases. It's a small thing, but it's part of the simplification that HiFi brought — one platform, one library prep, one sequencing run, one assembly — that has made high-quality genome assembly accessible to a much wider range of labs.

## SEGMENT 18 — The future: Revio, the cost-down trajectory, PacBio in clinical genomics

Let me close with a segment on where PacBio is headed, and what to watch for over the next five to ten years.

\[Pause.\]

The dominant trend, as with every sequencing platform in history, is **cost reduction**. PacBio HiFi today, on a Revio, generates a human genome for somewhere in the low hundreds of dollars in reagent cost. That's already a huge drop from where the platform was just three years ago. PacBio has publicly stated targets to drive this cost down further over the next few instrument generations, with the goal of bringing HiFi human genomes into clinical-realistic price ranges — comparable to what Illumina charges today.

If that happens — and it's plausible based on the historical trajectory — then PacBio HiFi becomes a real candidate for first-line clinical genome sequencing in cases where the extra information (structural variants, methylation, phasing) is clinically actionable. We're already seeing this in rare disease diagnostics, where HiFi has produced diagnoses in cases that Illumina missed. As the cost gap narrows, the calculus shifts further in PacBio's favor for clinical applications.

**Throughput growth** is the second trend. Each PacBio instrument generation has roughly an order-of-magnitude jump in throughput. The Sequel II's eight million ZMWs were eight times the Sequel; the Revio's twenty-five million ZMWs (per cell, with four cells per run) are several times the Sequel II. If this pace continues, the next-generation instrument should be in the hundreds-of-millions-of-ZMWs range, with throughput per instrument approaching what current Illumina NovaSeq instruments deliver. PacBio is investing heavily in nanofabrication to make this happen.

**New acquisitions and product expansions.** PacBio acquired Omniome in 2021 — Omniome had developed a different sequencing chemistry, based on the concept of "sequencing by binding," which they were pursuing for short-read applications. PacBio also acquired Apton Biosystems in 2023, which had developed proteomics-related single-molecule imaging. The pattern suggests PacBio is positioning to be a multi-modality single-molecule company — long-read DNA, short-read DNA, perhaps eventually direct protein or RNA detection — rather than purely a long-read DNA sequencer.

\[Pause.\]

**Clinical genomics adoption** is the most important strategic battle ahead. PacBio is actively pursuing clinical lab partnerships, regulatory approvals (their Revio has been validated in clinical settings), and integration with clinical pipelines. The pitch to clinical genomics is essentially: "For rare disease, for cancer, for any case where structural variation or comprehensive variant detection matters, you need long reads — and HiFi gives you the accuracy you need for clinical decision making." Whether this pitch lands at scale depends on cost reduction, on payer reimbursement, and on accumulating clinical evidence that HiFi adds real value over standard Illumina workflows.

The competitive dynamic with Nanopore remains live. Nanopore is also chasing clinical genomics, with somewhat different angles — portability, rapid turnaround, in-situ deployment for things like pathogen identification. Both platforms will probably find clinical niches. The two-platform duopoly within long reads seems stable for the foreseeable future.

The competitive dynamic with **Illumina** is fascinating. Illumina has its own short-read platforms running at massive scale and continues to dominate the sequencing market by revenue. Illumina has also been developing its own long-read capability, called **Complete Long Reads**, which uses a clever short-read-based proxy to derive long-range information. The success of that approach versus true single-molecule long reads remains to be seen. And of course, the FTC blocked Illumina from buying PacBio outright, so the two are head-to-head competitors in the long-read space.

\[Pause.\]

The deeper conclusion, looking at the platform's full trajectory. **PacBio was the first credible attempt at single-molecule sequencing, and it succeeded after a long and difficult execution.** The technology was sound from the beginning, but the chemistry, the polymerase, the optics, the chip, and the workflow all had to mature for the platform to deliver on its promise. The HiFi paradigm shift in 2019-2020 was the moment everything clicked into place. Today, PacBio HiFi is the gold standard for high-accuracy long-read sequencing, the platform that finished the human genome, the platform behind the Vertebrate Genomes Project, and the platform increasingly used in clinical rare disease work.

If you walk out of here remembering one big idea, let it be this. **PacBio solved the long-read accuracy problem by reading the same single molecule many times — using the SMRTbell to fold the molecule into a loop, the phi29 polymerase to chug around the loop without falling off, and the CCS algorithm to average across passes — turning fifteen percent raw error into Q30-plus consensus quality without ever amplifying the DNA.** That's the platform. That's why it matters. That's why the genome got finished, finally, twenty years after the draft.

## APPENDIX

A few topics that didn't fit neatly into the main lecture flow but are worth knowing.

**The "CCS" versus "HiFi" naming.** These two terms are used somewhat interchangeably. CCS — circular consensus sequencing — refers to the underlying algorithm (averaging across multiple passes of the same SMRTbell). HiFi refers to the product (reads with Q20-plus accuracy generated by CCS). In practice, HiFi has become the dominant marketing term, but if you read older papers or technical documentation, CCS often appears. They refer to essentially the same thing in modern usage. PacBio sometimes distinguishes "CCS reads" (any consensus from multiple passes) from "HiFi reads" (CCS reads passing the Q20 quality filter), but for most purposes you can treat them as the same.

\[Pause.\]

**Sample input requirements.** PacBio HiFi typically requires microgram-quantities of input DNA, and the DNA needs to be high-molecular-weight (typically tens to hundreds of kilobases of average size in the input) to support the fifteen-to-twenty-kilobase library prep. This is a meaningful constraint — for samples with limited or fragmented DNA, PacBio HiFi may not be feasible without amplification (which defeats the methylation calling) or pooling. PacBio has been working on lower-input workflows, and the current Revio chemistry can run from lower inputs than older platforms, but the high-molecular-weight requirement remains a practical consideration.

**Library prep options.** Beyond the standard HiFi workflow with size-selected inserts around 15-20 kb, PacBio supports several specialized library types: Iso-Seq for transcripts, MAS-Seq for ultra-multiplexed single-cell transcript reads, ultralong libraries for spanning extreme repeats, targeted enrichment libraries for specific genomic regions, and amplicon libraries for studying short, defined regions in high depth. Each has slightly different workflows but all use the SMRTbell architecture.

\[Pause.\]

**hifiasm and modern assemblers.** The assembler hifiasm, developed by Heng Li's group, has become the de facto standard tool for assembling genomes from HiFi data. It handles diploid (phased) assembly natively, producing two haplotype-resolved contigs per region of heterozygosity, and it scales to large genomes efficiently. Other long-read assemblers like Flye and Canu also work with HiFi data. The contiguity routinely achieved by these assemblers — contigs spanning entire chromosome arms in many cases — would have been unimaginable in the short-read era.

**The pangenome era.** As HiFi assemblies become routine for diverse human samples and for many species, the field is shifting from "single reference genome" thinking to "pangenome" thinking — representing the genetic diversity of a species as a graph or collection of assemblies rather than a single linear sequence. The Human Pangenome Reference Consortium, building on the T2T work, is constructing the first true human pangenome. PacBio HiFi is the underlying technology making this practical, because reference-quality assemblies are now affordable and reproducible.

\[Pause.\]

**Methylation calling tools.** PacBio's official tools for methylation calling include the **primrose** and **jasmine** software packages, which call 5mC from HiFi kinetics. Open-source tools like **pb-CpG-tools** also exist. The Nanopore community has its own ecosystem (megalodon, modkit, and others). The tools are evolving rapidly; if you're considering methylation work, check the current state-of-the-art before designing your experiment.

**Clinical and regulatory status.** As of the early-2020s, PacBio Revio has received CE-IVD marking in Europe for clinical use, and PacBio is pursuing FDA pathways for clinical applications in the US. Several clinical labs have adopted HiFi for rare disease work, and a number of published case reports describe HiFi-enabled diagnoses in patients who had remained undiagnosed with Illumina-only workups. The clinical footprint is growing but still small compared to Illumina; that gap is expected to narrow over the next several years.

\[Pause.\]

**A note on Sanger sequencing and where it still fits.** Sanger sequencing, the original sequencing method from the 1970s, is still in use today for validating individual variants, sequencing small numbers of plasmid clones, and confirming results from larger experiments. Sanger reads are about a kilobase long, very accurate, and inexpensive on a per-sample basis. HiFi has not replaced Sanger for these targeted applications; HiFi competes with Illumina for whole-genome and whole-transcriptome work, and with Nanopore for long-read applications, but the ecological niche of Sanger — single-sample, targeted, gold-standard validation — remains intact.

**A note on the politics of long-read sequencing.** The PacBio-Nanopore rivalry has been intense, often crossing into public marketing battles, with each company strenuously emphasizing the strengths of its own platform and the weaknesses of the other. As a student or working scientist, you should evaluate platforms by application rather than by marketing — the right tool depends on what you're trying to do, and both platforms have legitimate strengths for specific tasks. Pay attention to peer-reviewed benchmark studies rather than to vendor materials, especially when the question is which platform to use for a new project.

**Recommended reading and resources.** For the underlying technology, the original 2009 Science paper by Eid et al. introducing single-molecule real-time sequencing is the canonical reference, and Korlach's papers on ZMW physics are accessible. For HiFi and CCS specifically, the 2019 paper by Wenger et al. on accurate circular consensus sequencing is the entry point. For T2T human genome work, the 2022 Science papers from the consortium are mandatory reading. For Iso-Seq, see the various reviews from PacBio and the Tilgner group. For comparison with Nanopore, look at recent benchmark studies from independent academic groups — the Genome in a Bottle consortium has done useful work here.

**Final summary.** PacBio HiFi sequencing is single-molecule, real-time, optical sequencing on chips with millions of zero-mode waveguides, using engineered phi29 polymerase and phospholinked fluorescent nucleotides, with circular SMRTbell templates enabling multiple-pass reading of the same molecule and consensus-based error correction. The result is long-and-accurate reads — fifteen to twenty kilobases at Q30-plus quality — that have transformed genome assembly, structural variant calling, full-length transcriptomics, and direct methylation analysis. The platform completed the human genome, drives the modern pangenome era, and is steadily moving into clinical genomics. Its rivalry with Nanopore defines the modern long-read field, with PacBio as the precision tool and Nanopore as the length-and-flexibility champion. After fifteen years of difficult execution, the platform has fulfilled its founding promise. The accuracy problem is solved.
