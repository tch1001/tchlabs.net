## SEGMENT 1 — Opening and the central problem

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. Today and for the next two hours, we're going to talk about antibiotics — and specifically, we're going to talk about _how they work_. The mechanisms. Because this is a pharmacology course, not just a microbiology course, our central question isn't only "which bug does which drug kill." Our central question is: what is the molecular target, what does the drug do to that target, and why does interfering with that target either stop the bacterium from growing or kill it outright?

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can look at any antibiotic and tell me, one, what cellular process it attacks, two, the specific molecule it binds, and three, whether it's likely to be bacteriostatic or bactericidal and why — then we've succeeded. Everything else, the clinical details, the dosing, the side effects, those hang off the mechanism. Get the mechanism and the rest has somewhere to live in your memory.

So let me start with the single most important idea in this entire field. It's a concept called **selective toxicity**. Here's the problem we're trying to solve. A bacterial infection is, by definition, bacterial cells growing inside a patient who is made of human cells. We want to poison the bacteria. We do _not_ want to poison the patient. And that's genuinely hard, because at the biochemical level, bacteria and human cells run on a lot of the same chemistry. Both make proteins. Both copy DNA. Both run metabolism. So if I just hand you a generic cell poison, it'll kill the bacteria, sure — and it'll kill the patient too. That's not a drug. That's a disinfectant. Bleach is a wonderful antibacterial; we don't inject it.

The entire art of antibiotic pharmacology is finding the _differences_ between a bacterial cell and a human cell, and then designing a molecule that exploits one of those differences. A drug that hits something the bacterium has and absolutely needs, but that the human host either doesn't have at all, or has in a sufficiently different form that the drug ignores it. That gap — the difference between the bacterial target and anything in us — is the source of the drug's safety margin. When you hear later in this lecture that a particular antibiotic is unusually toxic, the underlying reason is almost always that its target in the bacterium is uncomfortably similar to something in our own cells. When you hear that a drug is remarkably safe, it's usually because its target is something bacteria have that we simply do not.

\[Pause.\]

So keep that frame in your head the whole time: every drug we discuss is an answer to the question, "what does a bacterium have that I don't, and how do I attack it?"

## SEGMENT 2 — A short history: how we stumbled into the antibiotic era

Before we get into mechanisms, I want to spend a few minutes on where these drugs came from, because the history isn't just decoration — it actually teaches you something about how the field thinks, and it sets up the resistance crisis we'll end on.

Try to imagine medicine before antibiotics, because it's genuinely hard to imagine from where we sit. For essentially all of human history, up until less than a century ago, a bacterial infection was a coin flip with death. A scratch from a rose thorn could kill you. A strep throat could turn into rheumatic fever and wreck your heart. Childbirth routinely killed mothers through infection. Pneumonia was called "the old man's friend" because it was how the elderly so often died. Surgeons could perform an operation flawlessly and still lose the patient days later to an infection they had no way to treat. There was no treatment. You supported the patient, you kept them comfortable, and you waited to see whether their immune system would win or lose. That was the entirety of what medicine could offer against bacteria.

The turning point — the discovery everyone knows — came in 1928, with Alexander Fleming, and it's worth telling because it's a story about _noticing_. Fleming was a bacteriologist in London, and by his own admission a somewhat untidy one. He'd been growing colonies of _Staphylococcus_ on culture plates, and he went off on holiday and left his plates out. When he came back, one plate had been contaminated by a mold — a fungus, _Penicillium_. Now, a contaminated plate is normally rubbish; you throw it out. But Fleming looked at it before discarding it, and he noticed something: right around the spot where the mold was growing, there was a clear zone — a halo where the bacteria had been killed off. Something the mold was producing was diffusing out into the agar and killing the _Staphylococcus_. He called that something penicillin.

Here's the part students often don't realize: Fleming couldn't turn it into a drug. He recognized its significance, he published, but purifying penicillin and producing it in usable quantities defeated him, and the work languished for over a decade. It took a second team — Howard Florey, Ernst Chain, and their colleagues at Oxford around 1940 — to figure out how to purify and concentrate penicillin and demonstrate that it could cure infections in animals and then in people. And then it took the industrial muscle of wartime, with the demand of treating wounded soldiers in the Second World War, to scale up production from a few milligrams to mass quantities. So the antibiotic era wasn't one eureka moment; it was a discovery, a long gap, a rescue, and an industrial mobilization. Fleming, Florey, and Chain shared the Nobel Prize in 1945.

And I want you to hear one thing Fleming said in that Nobel context, because it's eerily prophetic and it's the reason this lecture ends where it does. He warned, essentially, that it would be easy to underdose penicillin, to expose bacteria to non-lethal amounts of the drug, and thereby to _educate_ them — to select for resistant organisms. He saw the resistance problem at the very moment of triumph. He was completely right. Within a few years of penicillin's widespread use, penicillin-resistant _Staphylococcus_ was already appearing. The arms race began almost immediately.

Now, one more historical thread, because it changes how you should think about what an antibiotic _is_. Notice that penicillin came from a mold. That's not a coincidence. The overwhelming majority of our antibiotic classes were not invented by chemists from scratch — they were _discovered_, harvested from microorganisms, mostly soil fungi and soil bacteria, that had been making these molecules for hundreds of millions of years. Think about why. Soil is a fierce, crowded battlefield of microorganisms all competing for resources, and over evolutionary time they evolved chemical weapons to suppress their neighbors — and those chemical weapons are, in many cases, our antibiotics. The aminoglycosides, the tetracyclines, the macrolides, the very name "-mycin" — that suffix signals an origin in a soil bacterium of the genus _Streptomyces_. We didn't invent these weapons. We found an ancient microbial war already in progress and picked up the weapons lying on the field.

And that fact has a sobering corollary, which is the hinge of the whole resistance story: if these antibiotics have existed in nature for millions of years, then _resistance to them has also existed in nature for millions of years_. The bacteria that made these weapons had to protect themselves from their own products, and their neighbors evolved countermeasures. So the resistance genes were out there in the environment long before we ever showed up. We didn't create resistance. What we did, by deploying these drugs on a massive global scale, was apply an enormous selective pressure that took rare, pre-existing resistance genes and made them common — gave them a reason to spread. Hold onto that idea. It reframes resistance not as something we accidentally invented, but as something ancient that we summoned and amplified.

So that's the arc: a desperate pre-antibiotic world, a lucky observation by a man who bothered to look, a decade of neglect, a wartime rescue, a golden age of discovery from the soil — and resistance shadowing it the entire way, predicted at the moment of victory. Keep that arc in mind. Now let's get into how the weapons actually work.

## SEGMENT 3 — The five differences and the five drug targets

Let me make that concrete, because it organizes the whole lecture. There are really only a handful of major differences between a bacterial cell and one of your cells, and essentially every antibiotic class we have exploits one of them. If you learn these five, you have the skeleton onto which we'll hang every drug today.

Difference number one, and historically the most important: **the cell wall**. Bacteria are wrapped in a rigid mesh called peptidoglycan. Your cells have no cell wall at all — you have a flexible lipid membrane and nothing rigid outside it. This is a gift. Anything that interferes with building peptidoglycan attacks a structure the human body simply does not possess. So cell-wall-active drugs tend to be among our safest. This is target category one.

Difference number two: **the ribosome**. Both bacteria and humans make protein on ribosomes, so at first glance this looks useless — same machine, right? But it's not the same machine. The bacterial ribosome is what we call a 70S ribosome, built from a 30S small subunit and a 50S large subunit. Your ribosomes, in the cytoplasm, are 80S, built from 40S and 60S subunits. Different sizes, different RNA sequences, different proteins. Different enough that we can design drugs that jam the bacterial 70S ribosome and largely leave your 80S ribosome alone. I say _largely_ — hold that thought, because there's a complication we'll come back to involving your mitochondria. This is target category two.

Difference number three: **bacterial enzymes for copying and reading DNA**. Bacteria use enzymes called DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV to manage the twisting of their DNA, and these particular enzymes don't exist in your cells in that form. Bacteria also have their own RNA polymerase, distinct from yours. Attack those and again you've found something selective. Target category three.

Difference number four: **folate metabolism**. This one is subtle and beautiful. Both bacteria and humans need folate — it's an essential cofactor for making the building blocks of DNA. But here's the difference: _you_ can't make folate. You get it from your diet; it's a vitamin for us. Bacteria, most of them, _can't_ take up folate from the environment — they have to synthesize it from scratch. So bacteria carry an entire biochemical assembly line for making folate that your cells don't have, because you don't need it. Block that assembly line and you starve the bacterium of folate while leaving you untouched, because you got your folate from your salad. Target category four.

Difference number five, and this is the smallest category: **the cell membrane itself**. Now, both bacteria and humans have lipid membranes, so this is risky territory — it's the difference that's _least_ different. But bacterial membranes have a distinct lipid composition, and there are a couple of drugs that exploit that. Because the difference is so slim, these drugs tend to be our more toxic, last-resort agents. Target category five.

\[Pause. Let it land.\]

So — five differences, five target categories. Cell wall. Ribosome. Nucleic acids. Folate. Membrane. Write those down the side of your page, because for the rest of this lecture, every single drug is going to slot into one of those five boxes, and the toxicity of each drug is going to track how big or small the underlying difference is. Cell wall: huge difference, very safe drugs. Membrane: tiny difference, toxic drugs. That pattern will hold up remarkably well.

## SEGMENT 4 — The bacterial envelope: gram-positive versus gram-negative, and why it decides everything

I need to spend real time on the architecture of the bacterial cell surface, because I am going to invoke it in almost every drug section that follows, and if you don't have this picture solid, half of what I say later will sound arbitrary. The single most important structural distinction in all of antibacterial therapy is the difference between gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria, so let's build both, layer by layer.

The terms come from a staining technique developed by Hans Christian Gram in the 1880s — long before anyone understood why it worked. You apply a purple dye, then a treatment, then a wash, and some bacteria hold the purple stain — those are gram-positive — and some lose it and take up a pink counterstain — those are gram-negative. For a century that was just a useful classification. We now understand it reflects a fundamental difference in the construction of the cell envelope, and that difference turns out to govern which antibiotics can even reach their targets.

Let's build a **gram-positive** cell from the inside out. Innermost, you have the cytoplasm. Then a single lipid membrane — the cytoplasmic membrane, also called the inner membrane — wrapping the cell. And then, on the outside of that membrane, an enormously thick layer of peptidoglycan — that cell-wall mesh we discussed — many, many layers deep. So the gram-positive picture is simple: membrane, then a thick fortress wall of peptidoglycan, and that's essentially the outer surface of the cell. The peptidoglycan is exposed to the world. That thick peptidoglycan layer is what traps the purple dye.

Now build a **gram-negative** cell, and here's where it gets more complicated, and more clinically consequential. Innermost, cytoplasm. Then the inner cytoplasmic membrane, same as before. Then a _thin_ layer of peptidoglycan — just a few layers, much skimpier than the gram-positive wall. But then — and this is the crucial part — gram-negative bacteria have a _second_ membrane, an **outer membrane**, sitting outside the peptidoglycan. So the gram-negative envelope is a sandwich: inner membrane, thin peptidoglycan in the middle, outer membrane on the outside. That outer membrane is the thing that loses the purple dye in the Gram stain, but far more importantly, it is a formidable barrier to drugs.

Let me dwell on that outer membrane because it is the hero — or villain — of so many later stories. Its outer face is built largely of a molecule called lipopolysaccharide, LPS, which is densely packed and creates a greasy, tightly sealed barrier that many molecules simply cannot cross. So how does anything useful — nutrients, ions — get into a gram-negative cell at all? Through specific protein channels embedded in the outer membrane called **porins**. Porins are essentially gated doorways, and they're selective — they tend to admit small, water-loving molecules and exclude large or greasy ones. So for an antibiotic to reach a target inside a gram-negative bacterium, it usually has to fit through a porin. If it's too big, or the wrong chemistry, it's locked out at the front door, no matter how perfect its target inside.

Now you can immediately understand a whole set of facts I'll be throwing at you later, and I want you to see them as consequences of this one picture rather than as things to memorize separately:

Why is vancomycin useless against gram-negative bacteria? Because vancomycin is a big, bulky molecule, and it cannot fit through gram-negative porins to reach the peptidoglycan underneath the outer membrane. The target — the cell wall — is there, but the drug can't get to it. Gram-positives have no outer membrane, so their peptidoglycan is right there on the surface, freely accessible, and vancomycin works.

Why is plain penicillin G great against many gram-positives but weak against many gram-negatives? Same reason — difficulty crossing that outer membrane — which is exactly why chemists developed broader penicillins like ampicillin and amoxicillin that can thread through gram-negative porins.

Why is one major mechanism of gram-negative resistance simply "lose a porin"? Because if the bacterium reduces the number of porin channels, it narrows the doorways and the drug concentration inside falls — the door is partly bricked up. Gram-negatives can do this; gram-positives, lacking the outer membrane and its porins, generally can't use that particular trick.

And why are the last-resort polymyxins targeted at gram-negatives specifically? Because they go after that very outer membrane — they bind the LPS and tear the outer membrane apart. The structure that protects the gram-negative cell is also its specific vulnerability to that one drug class.

\[Pause.\]

So I want you to leave this segment with a mental model: a gram-positive bacterium is like a building with thick exposed walls but no perimeter fence — the wall is right there to attack. A gram-negative bacterium is like a building with a thinner inner wall but surrounded by a tough, sealed perimeter fence with only a few guarded gates — the porins — so even a good attack on the inner structures requires getting through the gate first. Gram-negatives are, as a rule, harder to treat, because that outer membrane keeps a lot of drugs out entirely. When you hear that some serious hospital infection is a "resistant gram-negative," part of why those are so feared is this very architecture working against us.

One more term while we're here, because it'll come up: some bacteria, like the one that causes tuberculosis, have yet another kind of envelope — an unusually waxy, lipid-rich wall built around a molecule called mycolic acid. That waxy coat makes them extraordinarily impervious to many drugs and is a big reason TB requires such long, multi-drug treatment. So the envelope isn't just a two-way classification; it's a spectrum of barriers, and the barrier largely sets the difficulty of treatment.

Alright — with the envelope firmly in mind, now we can talk about how we measure what a drug does.

## SEGMENT 5 — Pharmacological vocabulary: how we measure killing

Before we touch a single drug, I need to give you the vocabulary pharmacologists use to describe what an antibiotic _does_, quantitatively. These terms will come up over and over, and they're genuinely the language of the field.

First distinction: **bacteriostatic versus bactericidal**. A bacteriostatic drug stops bacteria from growing and dividing — it freezes the population. It doesn't actively kill them; it holds them in check, and then the patient's own immune system comes in and mops up the now-helpless, non-replicating bacteria. A bactericidal drug, by contrast, actively kills the bacteria itself. The population count goes down because the drug is doing the killing.

Now, why does this matter clinically? For most ordinary infections in a person with a working immune system, bacteriostatic is perfectly fine — you just need to freeze the bugs and let the immune system finish the job. But in certain situations, you really want bactericidal activity: in a patient whose immune system is compromised, or in infections where the immune system can't easily reach, like endocarditis — an infection on a heart valve — or meningitis, or osteomyelitis in bone. In those settings, you can't rely on the host to do the killing, so you want a drug that does it for you.

Here's a subtlety I want you to hold loosely, though: bacteriostatic versus bactericidal is not a fixed, intrinsic label stamped on a drug forever. It depends on the organism, the drug concentration, and the conditions. A drug can be static against one bug and cidal against another. So treat these as useful tendencies, not iron laws. But as tendencies, they correlate beautifully with mechanism, and I'll point that out as we go. As a preview: drugs that break the cell wall or shatter the membrane tend to be cidal, because they cause physical catastrophe. Drugs that gently inhibit protein synthesis often tend to be static, because if you remove the drug, the ribosome just starts working again. There are exceptions — there are always exceptions — but the logic holds often enough to be worth knowing.

\[Pause.\]

Second concept: the **MIC**, the minimum inhibitory concentration. This is probably the single most important number in clinical antibiotic use. The MIC is the lowest concentration of a drug that prevents visible growth of a particular bacterial strain. You take the bug, you expose it to a series of drug concentrations, and you find the lowest one that stops it growing. That's your MIC. A low MIC means the drug is potent against that organism — you don't need much. A high MIC means you need a lot, or that the drug is barely working. When a lab reports that an organism is "susceptible" or "resistant" to a drug, underneath that report is an MIC being compared to a threshold.

There's a sister measurement, the **MBC**, the minimum bactericidal concentration — the lowest concentration that actually kills, rather than just inhibits, 99.9% of the organisms. For a strongly bactericidal drug, the MBC is close to the MIC. For a bacteriostatic drug, the MBC is way higher than the MIC — you can inhibit it easily but you'd need enormous concentrations to actually kill it. So the gap between MIC and MBC is itself a clue about whether a drug is static or cidal against that organism.

\[Pause.\]

Now the third and most pharmacologically interesting concept, and this is where it gets elegant: there are two fundamentally different _patterns_ by which antibiotics kill, and they have completely opposite dosing strategies. This is one of those ideas that separates someone who's memorized drugs from someone who actually understands them.

Some drugs are **concentration-dependent** killers. For these, the higher the peak concentration you achieve relative to the MIC, the more effectively and the faster they kill. More is better. The relevant pharmacodynamic parameter is the ratio of the peak concentration to the MIC — we write it Cmax over MIC — or the total drug exposure over the day relative to MIC, the AUC over MIC. For these drugs, the dosing logic is: give a big, high dose, get a huge peak, kill aggressively, and then you can actually let the level fall quite low before the next dose. We'll see that aminoglycosides and fluoroquinolones behave this way, and it's why aminoglycosides are now usually given as one big dose once a day rather than split into small doses.

The other group are **time-dependent** killers. For these, cranking the concentration higher does _not_ kill faster — once you're a few times above the MIC, you've saturated the effect, and pushing higher buys you nothing. What matters for these drugs is _time_: the fraction of the dosing interval during which the drug concentration stays above the MIC. We write that as T greater than MIC, "time above MIC." For these drugs, the dosing logic is the opposite: you don't want a giant peak and a long trough; you want to keep the level above the MIC for as much of the day as possible. That means frequent dosing, or even continuous infusion. The beta-lactams — penicillins and cephalosporins — are the classic time-dependent killers, which is exactly why penicillin is dosed several times a day rather than once.

So notice what just happened: two drugs, two opposite dosing philosophies, and the difference comes straight out of the killing mechanism. That's the pharmacology talking.

\[Pause.\]

One more term and then we start on real drugs: the **post-antibiotic effect**, or PAE. This is the observation that for some drugs, bacterial growth stays suppressed for a while _even after the drug concentration has dropped below the MIC_. The drug is essentially gone, but the bugs are still stunned and don't resume growing for hours. Aminoglycosides have a long post-antibiotic effect, and this is the second reason you can give them once a day — the killing is done by the big peak, and the prolonged post-antibiotic effect covers the long trough while the level is low. Mechanism driving dosing, again.

And finally, the word **spectrum**. A _broad-spectrum_ antibiotic works against a wide range of bacterial types — gram-positives, gram-negatives, maybe more. A _narrow-spectrum_ antibiotic targets a limited range. Broad sounds better, but it isn't always — broad-spectrum drugs do more collateral damage to your normal, helpful bacteria, which is how you end up with complications like _C. difficile_ colitis. The ideal is to use the narrowest drug that covers the actual pathogen. Hold that thought; it'll matter when we talk about resistance at the end.

Okay. Vocabulary done. You now have the measuring tools. Let's go build some drugs — or rather, let's go break some bacteria. We start with the cell wall, because it's the biggest difference and the most famous drugs live there.

## SEGMENT 6 — From the bench to the bedside: how susceptibility is actually measured

I introduced the MIC a moment ago as the central number in antibiotic use, but I gave it to you as an abstraction. Let me make it concrete, because understanding how we actually _measure_ whether a drug works against a given patient's bug ties the pharmacology to what happens in a real hospital lab, and it'll make the resistance discussion at the end much more tangible.

When a patient has a serious infection, the lab doesn't just guess which antibiotic to use. They culture the organism — grow it from a blood sample, a urine sample, a wound swab — and then they test that specific organism against a panel of antibiotics to see which ones inhibit it and at what concentration. This is **antibiotic susceptibility testing**, and there are a few classic methods worth knowing.

The oldest and most visual is the **disk diffusion method**, often called Kirby-Bauer. You spread the bacteria evenly across the surface of an agar plate so they'd grow into a uniform lawn. Then you place small paper disks on the surface, each impregnated with a known antibiotic. As the plate incubates, each antibiotic diffuses outward from its disk into the surrounding agar, creating a concentration gradient — high right next to the disk, fading to nothing further away. Where the drug concentration is high enough to inhibit growth, no bacteria grow; further out, where the drug has thinned to ineffective levels, the bacteria grow normally. The result is a clear circle — a **zone of inhibition** — around each effective disk. Measure the diameter of that clear zone: a big zone means the drug is potent against this organism, a small zone or no zone means it isn't working. It's a beautifully simple readout — you can literally see susceptibility with your eyes — though by itself it gives you a category, susceptible or resistant, more than a precise number.

To get an actual MIC number, the classic method is **broth dilution**. You set up a row of tubes or wells, each containing the same bacteria but a different concentration of the antibiotic — typically doubling each step: one unit, two, four, eight, sixteen, and so on. You incubate them, and then you look at which tubes turned cloudy. Cloudy means the bacteria grew; clear means they didn't. You scan up the series until you find the lowest concentration that kept the tube clear — that's the lowest concentration that inhibited visible growth, and that, by definition, is the MIC. Then, if you want the MBC — the bactericidal concentration — you take samples from the clear tubes, where nothing grew, and try to subculture them onto fresh drug-free plates. If bacteria grow back, they were only inhibited, not killed — merely stunned. The lowest concentration from which essentially nothing grows back is the MBC, because at that concentration the bugs weren't just frozen, they were dead. And there it is, made physical: the gap between MIC and MBC is the gap between freezing and killing, which we can literally measure in a row of tubes.

There's a hybrid method many labs love called the **gradient strip** — a plastic strip laid on an agar lawn that carries a pre-formed gradient of antibiotic, marked with a concentration scale printed right on it. The zone of inhibition forms an elliptical shape around the strip, and you simply read off the MIC at the point where the edge of the bacterial growth intersects the strip's scale. It combines the visual simplicity of disk diffusion with an actual numeric MIC.

\[Pause.\]

Now, here's the part that connects all this back to the pharmacology and stops it from being mere lab trivia. Once you have an MIC, the question is: does it matter? An MIC is a number from a test tube. The patient is not a test tube. So the lab compares the MIC against a published threshold called a **breakpoint** — and reports the organism as susceptible, intermediate, or resistant. But where do those breakpoints come from? They come from exactly the pharmacodynamic concepts we built earlier: they're set with reference to the drug concentrations we can _actually achieve_ in a patient's blood and tissues at standard doses, and to the killing pattern of the drug. For a time-dependent killer like a beta-lactam, the breakpoint reflects whether we can keep the level above that MIC for enough of the dosing interval. For a concentration-dependent killer like an aminoglycoside, it reflects whether we can get the peak high enough above the MIC. So the breakpoint is where the in-the-tube number, the MIC, meets the in-the-patient reality of pharmacokinetics and the mechanism of killing. That's the whole chain: mechanism dictates the killing pattern, the killing pattern dictates the dosing strategy, the dosing strategy determines achievable concentrations, and those concentrations set the breakpoint that tells the clinician whether the MIC is good enough.

I'm laboring this because it's the moment where the molecular biology we're about to do becomes clinical medicine. Every time you'll later hear "this organism is resistant," what that means underneath is: its MIC has climbed past the concentration we can safely achieve in the patient. And _why_ the MIC climbed will always be one of the four resistance mechanisms we'll cover at the end — the bug changed its target, or learned to destroy the drug, or stopped letting it in, or started pumping it out. The lab report is the visible tip; the mechanism is the iceberg beneath. Keep that linkage in mind, and now — finally — let's open up the drugs themselves, starting with the cell wall.

## SEGMENT 7 — Target one, the cell wall: peptidoglycan and the beta-lactams

So, the cell wall. To understand the drugs, you have to understand the structure they attack, so give me three minutes on peptidoglycan and then the drugs will make complete sense.

A bacterium lives under enormous internal osmotic pressure. The inside of the cell is crowded with proteins, ions, metabolites — it's a concentrated solution — and it sits in a more dilute environment. Water wants to rush in. If you had nothing but a flexible membrane holding that in, the cell would swell and burst, like overfilling a water balloon. What stops that is the cell wall: a single, giant, mesh-like molecule called **peptidoglycan** — also called murein — that wraps the entire cell like a rigid exoskeleton, a net strong enough to resist the osmotic pressure pushing out. So the cell wall is, quite literally, what keeps the bacterium from exploding.

Now, how is that mesh built? Picture a chain-link fence. There are two directions of connection. The "horizontal" strands are long chains of sugars — alternating units of two amino sugars, N-acetylglucosamine and N-acetylmuramic acid. That's the glycan part — the sugar backbone. But sugar chains running parallel don't make a rigid net by themselves; you need to tie them together. So hanging off each muramic acid sugar is a short string of amino acids — a peptide — and these peptides reach across and link one sugar chain to the neighboring one. The peptide cross-links are the "vertical" wires that turn loose parallel strands into a strong two-dimensional and three-dimensional mesh. Peptido- for the peptide cross-links, -glycan for the sugar chains. The name tells you the structure.

Here is the critical detail, the one the most important antibiotics in history exploit. The peptide side chains, when they're first made, end in a specific two-amino-acid sequence: **D-alanine–D-alanine**. D-Ala-D-Ala, on the end. Now an enzyme comes along — and this enzyme is the star of our show — called a **transpeptidase**. The transpeptidase grabs that terminal D-Ala-D-Ala, snips off the last D-alanine, and uses the energy of that bond to forge a new cross-link to the peptide on the neighboring strand. That cross-linking reaction — transpeptidation — is the final, load-bearing step of cell wall assembly. It's the step that ties the whole net together and makes it strong. These transpeptidase enzymes have another name you must know, because it's the name used clinically: they're called **penicillin-binding proteins**, PBPs. And that name is a giant spoiler for what comes next.

\[Pause.\]

Enter the **beta-lactams**. This is the single most important antibiotic family in medicine — the penicillins, the cephalosporins, the carbapenems, and the monobactams. They all share one chemical feature that gives the family its name: a four-membered ring called a beta-lactam ring. And here is the mechanism, and it is gorgeous.

That beta-lactam ring is a structural mimic — a molecular impersonator — of the D-Ala-D-Ala terminus. It looks, to the transpeptidase enzyme, like its natural substrate. So the penicillin-binding protein, the transpeptidase, reaches out to grab what it thinks is D-Ala-D-Ala — and instead it grabs the beta-lactam. There's a serine amino acid in the active site of that enzyme, and it attacks the beta-lactam ring, the strained four-membered ring pops open, and the enzyme becomes **covalently, essentially permanently, stuck to the drug**. The active site is acylated. The enzyme is dead. It can't let go and it can't do its job.

So now play it forward. The transpeptidases are inactivated. Cross-linking stops. But here's a subtlety that explains why beta-lactams are bactericidal rather than merely static: the cell wall isn't a static object. It's constantly being remodeled. The bacterium has its own enzymes, called autolysins, that chew holes in the existing wall so the cell can grow and divide — and normally those holes are immediately patched by new cross-linking. With the transpeptidases knocked out, the autolysins keep chewing holes, but nothing patches them. The wall degrades, the mesh fails, and now that osmotic pressure we talked about wins. Water rushes in, the cell swells, and it bursts. Lysis. The cell physically explodes. That's why beta-lactams are bactericidal — they cause a physical catastrophe — and it's also why they only kill _actively growing, dividing_ bacteria. A dormant bacterium isn't remodeling its wall, so there's nothing for the drug to disrupt. Beta-lactams need the bug to be busy building.

Now connect this back to the pharmacodynamics from earlier. Beta-lactams are **time-dependent** killers. Once you're a few times above the MIC, all the available transpeptidase enzymes are getting hit; pushing the concentration higher doesn't kill faster because there are no more targets to engage. What matters is keeping the level above the MIC long enough for the autolysins to do their slow destructive work. That's the molecular reason penicillin is dosed frequently and not as one giant daily dose. The mechanism predicts the dosing.

And the safety: beta-lactams target an enzyme — the transpeptidase — and a structure — peptidoglycan — that your cells flatly do not have. There is no human transpeptidase, no human D-Ala-D-Ala, no human cell wall. So the selective toxicity is enormous, which is why penicillins are among the safest drugs we have. The major adverse effect isn't toxicity to your cells at all — it's _allergy_. The beta-lactam ring can haptenize, bind to your proteins and provoke an immune response, and that's where the penicillin allergy story comes from. But intrinsic toxicity? Minimal. That's the cell-wall pattern I promised you: biggest difference, safest drugs.

## SEGMENT 8 — The beta-lactam subclasses and the beta-lactamase war

Quick tour of the beta-lactam family, because they differ in ways that follow directly from the mechanism. They all do the same fundamental thing — kill the transpeptidase — but they differ in their chemistry, which changes which bacteria they reach and how well they survive bacterial counterattack.

The **penicillins** are the original. The narrow-spectrum ones, like penicillin G, are great against many gram-positives but struggle to cross the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria — and that outer membrane is going to be a recurring theme, so file it away. Chemists modified the side chains to make broader penicillins, like ampicillin and amoxicillin, that can get through gram-negative porins, and others like the anti-staphylococcal penicillins designed to resist enzymatic attack — more on that in a moment.

The **cephalosporins** are a huge group organized into "generations." Without memorizing the list, the trend you should know is that as you move from first generation up through later generations, the spectrum generally shifts: you tend to lose some gram-positive coverage and gain gram-negative coverage, and the later generations are more resistant to the bacterial enzymes that destroy beta-lactams. Mechanism's identical — same attack on the transpeptidase — but the molecular tweaks change reach and durability.

The **carbapenems** — drugs like meropenem and imipenem — are the heavy artillery. Extremely broad spectrum, and very resistant to most of the enzymes bacteria use to fight back. We hold these in reserve for serious or resistant infections, precisely because overusing our best weapon is how you train bacteria to defeat it.

The **monobactams**, really just aztreonam, are an interesting niche: they hit gram-negatives only, and because they're structurally distinct, they're often safe to give to patients with penicillin allergy.

\[Pause.\]

Now — the counterattack, and this is where mechanism and resistance collide beautifully. The most important way bacteria defend themselves against beta-lactams is an enzyme called **beta-lactamase**. Think about what these drugs are: they're molecules with a reactive four-membered ring. Bacteria evolved an enzyme that simply cleaves that ring open _before_ it can reach the transpeptidase. A beta-lactamase chops the beta-lactam ring, the drug is now chemically inert, and it sails harmlessly past the target. The penicillin-binding proteins are never touched. This is the single most common mechanism of beta-lactam resistance in the world, and it's why plain penicillin no longer reliably works against _Staphylococcus aureus_ — most strains now carry a beta-lactamase, in that context called penicillinase.

So how do we fight back against the enzyme? Two strategies, both elegant. Strategy one: chemically modify the drug so the enzyme can't grab it — put a bulky group near the ring that physically blocks the beta-lactamase from getting access. That's what the anti-staphylococcal penicillins, like methicillin and its cousins, do. Strategy two — and this is clever — is to give a **beta-lactamase inhibitor** alongside the antibiotic. These are molecules — clavulanic acid is the classic — that are themselves beta-lactams, but they're sacrificial. They have little antibacterial activity of their own; instead, they rush in and bind up the beta-lactamase enzyme irreversibly, acting as a decoy. The enzyme wastes itself destroying the inhibitor, and meanwhile the real antibiotic gets through to the transpeptidase unharmed. That's the logic behind a combination like amoxicillin plus clavulanic acid — you may know it by a brand name. The amoxicillin does the killing; the clavulanic acid is the bodyguard that neutralizes the enemy's enzyme. A drug and its dedicated decoy, working as a team. I find that genuinely beautiful pharmacology.

There's one more beta-lactam resistance mechanism I'll mention now and return to at the end, because it's clinically enormous: instead of destroying the drug, the bacterium can change the _target_. _Staph aureus_ can acquire a gene — _mecA_ — that produces a modified transpeptidase, called PBP2a, whose shape the beta-lactam can no longer bind effectively. The drug arrives intact, but the lock has been changed. That's the molecular definition of MRSA — methicillin-resistant _Staph aureus_. Note the difference: beta-lactamase destroys the drug; PBP2a hides the target. Same goal, two completely different molecular tactics. Keep that distinction sharp; we'll build on it.

## SEGMENT 9 — Cell wall, continued: vancomycin and the others

Beta-lactams aren't the only cell-wall-active drugs, and the most important of the others is **vancomycin** — a member of the glycopeptide class, and a drug you'll hear about constantly because it's a workhorse against resistant gram-positives, including MRSA.

Here's what's clever about vancomycin, and why I'm covering it right after beta-lactams: it attacks the _same construction project_ — peptidoglycan cross-linking — but at a completely different point, using a completely different mechanism. The beta-lactams bind the _enzyme_, the transpeptidase. Vancomycin ignores the enzyme entirely. Instead, vancomycin binds the _substrate_ — it grabs that D-Ala-D-Ala terminus directly, the very tail end of the peptide chain, before the enzyme can get to it. Vancomycin is a big, bulky molecule, and once it caps the D-Ala-D-Ala, it physically gets in the way. The transpeptidase can't access its substrate because there's a huge drug molecule sitting on it, and even the sugar-chain-extending step is blocked. So cross-linking fails, the wall can't be assembled, and again you get a fatally weakened cell wall. Bactericidal, same end result, but by steric obstruction of the substrate rather than inactivation of the enzyme.

Now here's a consequence of vancomycin's size that I want you to reason through, because it illustrates a principle. Vancomycin is a large molecule. Gram-negative bacteria have that outer membrane I keep flagging, studded with narrow channels called porins, and large molecules simply can't get through. So vancomycin is essentially useless against gram-negatives — not because their transpeptidase is different, but because the drug physically can't reach it. It's locked out at the front door. Gram-positives have no such outer membrane, so vancomycin reaches their wall freely. This is your first clean example of a recurring theme: sometimes a drug fails against an organism not because the target is wrong, but because the drug can't physically get to the target. Access is half the battle.

And how do bacteria resist vancomycin? Think about it mechanistically — the drug binds D-Ala-D-Ala, so the resistance is to _change the target it binds_. Vancomycin-resistant organisms swap that terminal D-alanine for a D-lactate: D-Ala-D-Lac instead of D-Ala-D-Ala. It's a tiny chemical change — one atom swapped, an oxygen for an NH — but it dramatically weakens vancomycin's grip while still allowing cross-linking to proceed. That single substitution drops vancomycin's binding affinity roughly a thousand-fold. That's the basis of VRE, vancomycin-resistant enterococci. Notice the theme repeating: target modification as a resistance strategy, just like PBP2a for the beta-lactams. Same logic, different molecule.

Let me round out the cell-wall category briefly with a few more agents, because they show the wall can be attacked at _every_ stage of its construction. **Fosfomycin** blocks one of the earliest steps — the synthesis of the muramic acid building block, before the sugar chain is even assembled. **Bacitracin** blocks the recycling of the lipid carrier molecule that ferries the building blocks across the membrane to the construction site — knock out the delivery truck and the materials never arrive; bacitracin's mostly topical because it's too toxic for systemic use. And **cycloserine**, used in tuberculosis, blocks the enzymes that make the D-Ala-D-Ala unit in the first place. So you can see the whole picture: early synthesis, transport, assembly, and final cross-linking — and we have a drug that hits essentially every step. The cell wall is such a good target, with such a big selective-toxicity advantage, that medicinal chemistry has attacked it from every conceivable angle.

\[Pause.\]

That's target category one, the cell wall — the biggest difference between bacteria and us, and home to our safest and most beloved drugs. Let's take a break here. We've done the cell wall and all the pharmacodynamic framing; when we come back, we'll do the ribosome, which is a richer and more crowded target, and then nucleic acids, folate, the membrane, and finally we'll pull the whole resistance story together. Stretch, and I'll see you in a few minutes.

## SEGMENT 10 — Getting the drug to the bug: pharmacokinetics in brief

Welcome back. Before we dive into the ribosome, I want to spend a little time on something that, since this is a _pharmacology_ course, I really can't ignore: it doesn't matter how brilliant a drug's mechanism is at its target if the drug can't physically get to where the bacteria are living, at a high enough concentration, for long enough. A perfect mechanism delivered to the wrong place is a failure. So let me give you the pharmacokinetic frame — how the drug moves through the body — and tie each piece back to mechanism and clinical decisions.

Pharmacokinetics is often summarized by four processes, sometimes abbreviated ADME: absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion. Let's walk them with antibiotics in mind.

**Absorption** — getting the drug into the bloodstream. The big practical question is oral versus intravenous. Some antibiotics are absorbed beautifully from the gut and achieve blood levels nearly as high as if you'd injected them — these have high oral bioavailability, and that's wonderful because the patient can take a pill at home. Many fluoroquinolones are like this; their oral and IV levels are so similar that you can often switch a hospitalized patient from the IV to the pill as soon as they're improving, and send them home. Other drugs are barely absorbed orally and must be given by injection to treat a systemic infection — vancomycin is the classic example: give it by mouth and it essentially stays in the gut. Now, here's a lovely mechanism-meets-pharmacokinetics twist: because oral vancomycin stays in the gut and isn't absorbed, that "limitation" becomes the _treatment_ for an infection _of_ the gut — _C. difficile_ colitis — where you actually _want_ the drug to sit in the intestine and not go anywhere else. A pharmacokinetic weakness, repurposed as a strength by matching it to the right target location.

**Distribution** — where the drug goes once it's in the blood. This is where antibiotic therapy gets genuinely hard, because some of the most important infection sites are places the body deliberately walls off, and the drug has to be able to reach them. The brain and spinal fluid are guarded by the blood-brain barrier, which keeps many drugs out — so to treat meningitis, you specifically need an antibiotic that _penetrates_ into the cerebrospinal fluid, and not all of them do. Bone is poorly perfused and hard to reach, which is part of why osteomyelitis — bone infection — requires prolonged therapy with drugs known to get into bone. The prostate, the eye, the interior of an abscess — all are sanctuary sites with their own access problems. So choosing an antibiotic isn't only "what's the right mechanism for this bug" — it's "what drug with the right mechanism will also actually _reach_ the place this bug is hiding." And remember daptomycin from later in our list: a fine anti-gram-positive mechanism, but it gets inactivated in the lung, so distribution to the wrong chemical environment kills its usefulness for pneumonia. Mechanism and distribution must both line up.

There's also the matter of getting inside _cells_. Some bacteria live _inside_ our own cells — the organisms behind diseases like the atypical pneumonias and chlamydia hide intracellularly. To kill those, the antibiotic has to penetrate into human cells and accumulate there. Macrolides and tetracyclines are good at this, which is exactly why they're favored for those intracellular pathogens. A drug that stays in the bloodstream will never reach a bug that's hiding inside a cell.

**Metabolism and excretion** — how the body clears the drug, which determines the dose and the dosing interval, and which can dictate whether a drug is even safe in a given patient. Many antibiotics are cleared by the kidneys, so in a patient with poor kidney function the drug accumulates and you must reduce the dose to avoid toxicity — this matters enormously for the aminoglycosides and vancomycin, which are themselves hard on the kidneys, so the very organ that clears them is the one they damage. Other drugs are cleared by the liver. And we already met two clinically huge metabolic interactions, both involving the liver's cytochrome P450 enzyme system: macrolides _inhibit_ P450, slowing the clearance of many other drugs and raising their levels, while rifampin _induces_ P450, speeding clearance and dropping the levels of other drugs, sometimes to the point of making them fail — including, famously, oral contraceptives and certain transplant drugs. So the antibiotic doesn't just act on the bacterium; it can reshape how the patient handles everything else in their medicine cabinet.

\[Pause.\]

Now let me tie this back to the killing patterns, because pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics meet here, and this is the synthesis. Recall: beta-lactams are time-dependent — you want the level above the MIC for as much of the interval as possible. The pharmacokinetic consequence is frequent dosing or continuous infusion, and a preference for drugs with a longer half-life so the level doesn't crash between doses. Aminoglycosides are concentration-dependent with a long post-antibiotic effect — the pharmacokinetic consequence is one big daily dose to drive a towering peak, then let the level fall, relying on the post-antibiotic effect to cover the long trough, which conveniently also gives the kidneys a daily rest from the drug, reducing toxicity. So once-daily aminoglycoside dosing simultaneously maximizes killing _and_ minimizes toxicity — a beautiful case where understanding both the mechanism and the pharmacokinetics lets you design a dosing scheme that's better on both axes at once. That is pharmacology doing exactly what it's supposed to do.

So as we go through the rest of the drug classes, keep a second question running alongside "what's the mechanism?" — namely, "can this drug get to where it needs to be, at the right level, for the right amount of time?" Because the bacteria don't care how elegant the mechanism is if the drug never shows up. Now — the ribosome.

## SEGMENT 11 — Target two, the ribosome: setup and the 30S inhibitors

We're moving now to the second great target: the ribosome, the protein-synthesis machine. This is the busiest category — more drug classes attack the ribosome than any other single target — so I want to set up the machinery carefully first, and then the drugs will sort themselves.

Recall the selective-toxicity logic. Bacterial ribosomes are 70S, made of a 30S small subunit and a 50S large subunit. Yours are 80S, made of 40S and 60S. The numbers, by the way, are sedimentation coefficients — how fast the piece sinks in a centrifuge — which is why 30 plus 50 doesn't equal 70; you can't just add them, because the value depends on shape and density, not just mass. Don't let the arithmetic bother you. The point is: the bacterial ribosome is different enough from yours that we can drug it selectively.

Let me remind you what the ribosome actually does, because the drugs map onto specific steps. The ribosome reads messenger RNA and builds a protein, one amino acid at a time. Amino acids are delivered by transfer RNAs. There are key sites on the ribosome: the A site, where a new incoming amino-acid-carrying tRNA docks; the P site, which holds the growing protein chain; and then the chain gets handed from the P-site tRNA to the A-site amino acid, forming a new peptide bond — that bond-forming reaction is catalyzed by an activity called peptidyl transferase, which lives in the large 50S subunit. After the bond forms, the whole assembly shifts over by one codon — that's translocation — and the cycle repeats. Docking, bond formation, translocation; docking, bond formation, translocation. Every antibiotic in this category jams one of those steps, and which step tells you a lot about whether the drug ends up static or cidal.

Now, before we go further, the complication I promised earlier. Your cytoplasmic ribosomes are 80S — safe. But you have a second population of ribosomes hiding inside your **mitochondria**, and for deep evolutionary reasons — mitochondria descend from ancient bacteria — your mitochondrial ribosomes resemble bacterial 70S ribosomes more than they resemble your own 80S ribosomes. So a drug that targets the bacterial 70S ribosome can sometimes also poke at your mitochondrial ribosomes, and _that_ is the source of a lot of the toxicity we'll see in this category. So unlike the cell-wall drugs, where the target was utterly absent from humans, here the target has a faint echo inside our own cells. Bigger overlap, more toxicity. The selective-toxicity pattern I set up at the start is already paying off.

Okay. Let's split the drugs by which subunit they hit. We'll do the 30S small-subunit drugs first. There are two classes that matter: the aminoglycosides and the tetracyclines, and — handy mnemonic — they're both "30S" drugs, so think of the two families that bind the small subunit.

\[Pause.\]

The **aminoglycosides** — gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, streptomycin. These are fascinating because they're the great exception to a rule I gave you earlier. Most protein-synthesis inhibitors are bacteriostatic — and you'd expect that, because if you just gently stop the ribosome and then the drug washes out, the ribosome resumes and the bug recovers. But aminoglycosides are strongly **bactericidal**. Why? Their mechanism isn't gentle. They bind to the 30S subunit, specifically to the 16S ribosomal RNA right at the decoding region, and they do two destructive things: they block proper initiation of protein synthesis, and — crucially — they cause the ribosome to _misread_ the genetic code. The ribosome starts inserting the wrong amino acids. So the bacterium isn't just making less protein; it's churning out garbled, nonfunctional, misfolded proteins. Some of those defective proteins jam into the cell membrane and punch holes in it, which lets more drug flood in, which causes more misreading — a vicious cycle that ends in cell death. It's not a gentle freeze; it's a corruption of the machine that cascades into catastrophe. That's why they kill.

And remember, aminoglycosides are **concentration-dependent** killers with a long post-antibiotic effect — that's the pair of properties I flagged earlier — which is exactly why we now give them as one big once-daily dose: a huge peak drives aggressive killing and misreading, and the prolonged post-antibiotic effect covers the trough.

Two more mechanistic points about aminoglycosides that students always find satisfying once they see the logic. First: aminoglycosides require **oxygen** to get into the bacterial cell. Their uptake across the membrane is an active, oxygen-dependent process. So they do _not_ work against strict anaerobes — bacteria that live without oxygen — because the drug can't get inside. Again: not a target problem, an access problem. Second: their toxicity profile. Aminoglycosides are notorious for damaging the kidneys and the inner ear — nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity, the latter sometimes permanent. And where does that come from? At least partly from those mitochondrial ribosomes we just discussed; the cells of the inner ear are particularly vulnerable. So the toxicity traces straight back to the imperfect selectivity of a 70S-targeting drug in a body that hides 70S-like ribosomes in its mitochondria. Mechanism explains the side effect.

\[Pause.\]

The other 30S class: the **tetracyclines** — tetracycline itself, doxycycline, minocycline. Their mechanism is cleaner and gentler. They also bind the 30S subunit, but they sit in the A site — the docking bay — and physically block the incoming aminoacyl-tRNA from binding. No tRNA can dock, so no new amino acid gets added, so the protein chain can't grow. But notice: this is reversible blockade. Remove the drug, the A site clears, and synthesis resumes. So tetracyclines are **bacteriostatic** — they freeze rather than kill — exactly as the gentle-inhibition logic predicts.

Tetracyclines are broad-spectrum and especially useful against unusual intracellular organisms — the bugs that cause things like Lyme disease, Rocky Mountain spotted fever, chlamydia, atypical pneumonias. A couple of mechanistically-flavored clinical pearls: tetracyclines bind calcium and other divalent metal ions avidly — they chelate them — which is why you shouldn't take them with milk or antacids, because the drug binds the calcium and you don't absorb it, and it's also why they deposit in growing teeth and bone and can stain children's teeth, so we avoid them in young kids and pregnancy. That side effect is a direct readout of the molecule's chemistry. The newer drug tigecycline is a tetracycline derivative engineered to dodge some common resistance mechanisms — a nice example of medicinal chemistry chasing resistance, which is our theme for the end of the lecture.

## SEGMENT 12 — The 50S large-subunit inhibitors

Now to the 50S large subunit, where there are more classes and a recurring shared real estate problem that creates a specific kind of resistance. Stay with me, because three of these drugs bind near the same spot, and that fact has clinical consequences.

First, the **macrolides** — erythromycin, azithromycin, clarithromycin. The "-mycin" or "-thromycin" ending is your tag. Macrolides bind the 50S subunit, specifically the 23S ribosomal RNA, near the exit tunnel — the channel through which the growing protein chain threads its way out of the ribosome. By parking near that tunnel, the macrolide blocks the elongating peptide from getting out, and it interferes with translocation, the shifting step. Protein synthesis stalls. Generally **bacteriostatic**. Macrolides are mainstays for respiratory infections, atypical pneumonia, and they're a common go-to for patients with penicillin allergy. A mechanism-adjacent clinical note: macrolides, especially erythromycin and clarithromycin, inhibit a liver enzyme system, cytochrome P450, which means they interfere with the metabolism of many other drugs — so they're famous for drug-drug interactions. That's not a ribosome effect; it's an off-target effect on your liver enzymes, but it's clinically huge, so I flag it.

Second, **chloramphenicol**. This one binds the 50S subunit and directly inhibits the peptidyl transferase activity — it blocks the actual peptide-bond-forming reaction itself, the catalytic heart of the large subunit. Very broad spectrum, bacteriostatic. So why don't we use this wonderful broad-spectrum drug all the time? Because of toxicity, and the toxicity is, once again, a selectivity story. Chloramphenicol can suppress the bone marrow. There's a dose-related, reversible marrow suppression that traces back to those mitochondrial ribosomes — the marrow cells are metabolically demanding and vulnerable. And there's a rarer, idiosyncratic, catastrophic aplastic anemia. Because of that toxicity, in wealthy healthcare systems chloramphenicol is now a rarely-used reserve agent — though it remains valuable globally because it's cheap and broad. The lesson stands: target the 70S ribosome, risk the mitochondria, pay in toxicity.

Third, **clindamycin**, a lincosamide. It binds the 50S subunit very close to where the macrolides bind and also interferes with peptide bond formation and the early elongation steps. Clindamycin is especially useful against anaerobes and certain gram-positive infections, and it has a particular trick: because it shuts down protein synthesis, it shuts down production of the _toxins_ that some bacteria secrete — so it's valuable in toxin-driven diseases even beyond simple killing. Its signature adverse effect is that, as a fairly broad agent, it disrupts the normal gut flora and is classically associated with _C. difficile_ colitis — that overgrowth complication I mentioned when we discussed spectrum.

Now here's the payoff for grouping these three together. Macrolides, clindamycin, and a third class called streptogramins all bind the _same general region_ of the 50S subunit. That shared address creates a single resistance mechanism that knocks out all of them at once. Bacteria can produce an enzyme — encoded by _erm_ genes — that methylates the 23S ribosomal RNA right at the binding site. One little methyl group added to the ribosomal RNA changes the shape of the shared binding pocket, and now the macrolide, the clindamycin, and the streptogramin all lose their grip simultaneously. This is called MLS-B resistance — for Macrolide, Lincosamide, Streptogramin-B — and it's a beautiful, slightly scary example of how a single small modification to the _target_, costing the bacterium almost nothing, can defeat three entire drug classes that happen to share a parking spot. Target modification again, but now with collateral breadth.

Fourth, a newer and important class: the **oxazolidinones**, the prototype being **linezolid**. These also bind the 50S subunit, on the 23S RNA, but at a distinct site, and their mechanism is to block the very _first_ step — they prevent assembly of the functional initiation complex, the joining of the subunits and the first tRNA that has to happen before synthesis can even begin. Because they hit a unique site by a unique mechanism, there's no cross-resistance with the older classes — that's exactly why linezolid is valuable: it works against resistant gram-positives including MRSA and VRE. Generally bacteriostatic. And the toxicities, predictably, trace to the mitochondrial ribosome again with longer use — marrow suppression and nerve effects — plus linezolid happens to inhibit an enzyme called monoamine oxidase, which creates some drug and food interactions. New drug, same underlying selectivity tradeoff.

\[Pause.\]

So step back and look at the whole ribosome category. Two subunits, a handful of classes, each jamming a specific step: aminoglycosides cause misreading and are cidal; tetracyclines block A-site docking and are static; macrolides and clindamycin and chloramphenicol jam elongation and bond formation on the 50S and are mostly static; linezolid blocks initiation. And running underneath all of them is the same toxicity logic — the closer the bacterial 70S resembles your mitochondrial ribosomes, the more you pay. That's the unifying story of target two.

## SEGMENT 13 — Target three, nucleic acids: quinolones, rifamycins, metronidazole

Third target category: the machinery for handling DNA and RNA. Three classes here, three distinct mechanisms.

Start with the **fluoroquinolones** — ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin, the "-floxacin" family. To understand them you need one piece of biology: when a bacterium copies its DNA, the double helix has to unwind, and that unwinding creates torsional strain ahead of the replication fork — imagine twisting a rubber band tighter and tighter. Bacteria have enzymes that relieve that strain by cutting the DNA, passing strands through, and resealing it. The key one is **DNA gyrase**, also called topoisomerase II, and a related enzyme, topoisomerase IV, that helps separate the daughter chromosomes after replication. These enzymes are essential, and — critically — they are bacterial enzymes; your cells use different topoisomerases, which gives us our selectivity.

Now here's the mechanism, and it's a nasty one for the bacterium. You might think the fluoroquinolone simply inhibits the enzyme so it can't cut the DNA. But it's worse than that. The fluoroquinolone binds the enzyme _while the enzyme has the DNA cut open_ — it traps the complex at the moment the DNA is broken and stabilizes it there, so the enzyme can never reseal the cut. So instead of just stopping the enzyme, the drug converts the enzyme into a machine that _generates lethal double-stranded breaks_ in the chromosome and then holds them open. The bacterial genome gets shredded. That's why fluoroquinolones are strongly **bactericidal**, and **concentration-dependent** killers — push the peak high and you trap more complexes and shred faster. Broad-spectrum, well absorbed orally, very useful drugs.

Their toxicities are mechanistically interesting and clinically topical. Fluoroquinolones have a black-box warning for tendon damage and rupture, can affect the central nervous system, can prolong the cardiac QT interval, and — note the developmental warning — they're generally avoided in children and pregnancy because of effects on developing cartilage. Regulators have increasingly urged that they be reserved for situations where there's no better option, precisely because the toxicity profile is broader than was once appreciated. So: powerful mechanism, real costs.

Second class: the **rifamycins**, mainly **rifampin** — a cornerstone of tuberculosis therapy. Rifampin inhibits the bacterial enzyme that transcribes DNA into RNA: the **DNA-dependent RNA polymerase**. It binds to the beta subunit of that enzyme — encoded by a gene called _rpoB_ — and physically blocks the channel through which the growing RNA strand would emerge, so transcription stalls right at the start. No RNA, no proteins, dead bacterium. Bactericidal. And again, selectivity: the bacterial RNA polymerase is structurally distinct from yours, so the drug ignores your enzyme.

But rifampin teaches a crucial resistance lesson that I want you to really absorb. Because the drug binds one specific spot on one specific protein, a _single point mutation_ in that _rpoB_ gene can change the shape of the binding site just enough that rifampin no longer sticks — and that single mutation confers high-level resistance in one step. And because bacterial populations are enormous, a resistant mutant is almost always lurking. So if you treat an active tuberculosis infection with rifampin _alone_, you will rapidly select out the resistant mutants and fail. That's why TB is _always_ treated with multiple drugs at once for months, and why we never, ever use rifampin alone for an active infection. The combination protects each drug from the bacterium's evolution. Two quick clinical tags: rifampin is a potent _inducer_ of liver P450 enzymes — the mirror image of the macrolide interaction — so it speeds up the metabolism of many other drugs, and it famously turns body fluids — urine, tears, sweat — a harmless orange-red, which is great for confirming whether your patient is actually taking it.

Third, **metronidazole** — and this one has the most distinctive mechanism in the whole lecture. Metronidazole is a _prodrug_: it arrives inert and has to be chemically activated to work. The activation requires a strongly reducing, oxygen-free environment — the kind found inside anaerobic bacteria and certain protozoa. Inside those organisms, the drug gets reduced, generating reactive radical intermediates that attack and fragment the organism's DNA. The genius is the selectivity: only anaerobes and certain protozoa have the reducing machinery to activate the drug, so it's essentially inert in your well-oxygenated human cells and in aerobic bacteria. It only "switches on" inside the very organisms you're trying to kill. So metronidazole is the go-to for anaerobic infections and for protozoal infections like giardia and trichomonas. Its famous clinical pearl: it produces a severe reaction with alcohol — flushing, nausea, vomiting — so patients must avoid alcohol while taking it.

## SEGMENT 14 — Target four, folate metabolism: the sulfonamides and trimethoprim

Fourth target: folate synthesis, and this is, to my taste, the most elegant selective-toxicity story in all of antimicrobial pharmacology, so I've saved it for a moment when you can appreciate it.

Recall the setup from the beginning: both bacteria and humans need folate to build the nucleotide bases for DNA. But you get your folate ready-made from your diet — it's a vitamin for you. Most bacteria cannot import folate; they must synthesize it from scratch, starting from a precursor called PABA, para-aminobenzoic acid. So bacteria run a folate factory that you simply don't possess. Attack the factory, and you've found a target that has no equivalent in the host at all. This is target category four, and it's the basis for one of medicine's oldest antibiotic strategies.

The **sulfonamides** — the "sulfa" drugs, like sulfamethoxazole — were among the very first antibacterials. Here's the mechanism, and it's almost poetically simple: the sulfonamide molecule is a structural mimic of PABA, the natural starting material for folate synthesis. The first enzyme in the bacterial folate pathway, dihydropteroate synthase, grabs the sulfonamide thinking it's PABA — a case of mistaken identity, just like the beta-lactam fooling the transpeptidase — and the enzyme is either blocked or it builds a useless dead-end product instead of folate. Either way, folate production grinds down. It's competitive inhibition by molecular impersonation. And because your cells don't have this enzyme or this pathway at all, the selectivity is excellent.

Now, on its own, blocking that one step is only bacteriostatic — it slows folate production but bacteria can limp along on reserves. The masterstroke is **combination**. There's a second drug, **trimethoprim**, that blocks a _later_ enzyme in the very same pathway — dihydrofolate reductase, which converts dihydrofolate to the active tetrahydrofolate. So now picture the folate assembly line with two drugs hitting two sequential steps: the sulfonamide blocks an early step, trimethoprim blocks a later one. This is called **sequential blockade**, and the effect is _synergistic_ — the two drugs together are far more powerful than the sum of their separate effects, and the combination becomes **bactericidal**. That combination — trimethoprim plus sulfamethoxazole, which you'll see abbreviated everywhere — is used widely, classically for urinary tract infections and for a particular pneumonia in immunocompromised patients.

And here's the selectivity capstone that makes trimethoprim so clever. You might object: wait, humans also have a dihydrofolate reductase enzyme — don't we risk blocking our own? Yes, we do have that enzyme — but trimethoprim binds the _bacterial_ version of the enzyme tens of thousands of times more tightly than it binds the human version. The bacterial and human enzymes are different enough in shape that the drug discriminates between them. So at therapeutic doses, trimethoprim shuts down the bacterial enzyme while barely touching yours. That selective affinity for the bacterial form of an enzyme that both species share — that is selective toxicity achieved not by attacking a unique target, but by exploiting subtle structural differences in a shared one. It's the same principle as the 70S-versus-80S ribosome, expressed in a single enzyme's binding pocket. A lovely note to close the target categories on.

## SEGMENT 15 — Why we combine drugs: synergy, antagonism, and the logic of using more than one

Before the last target category, I want to pause on a question that's been lurking in several places already: why do we sometimes give two or more antibiotics at once? We saw it with trimethoprim plus sulfamethoxazole. I mentioned it for tuberculosis. It seems to violate the principle I gave you earlier — use the narrowest drug that does the job — so when and why do we deliberately combine? There are good reasons, and there are also ways combinations can backfire, and the logic is all mechanistic, which makes it a perfect topic for this course.

There are four main reasons to combine antibiotics. Let me give you each with its rationale.

**Reason one: to prevent resistance from emerging.** This is the tuberculosis logic, and it's the most important one to understand deeply. Remember rifampin — a single point mutation in one gene confers high-level resistance in one step, and in a large bacterial population a resistant mutant is almost certainly already present before you start. So if you treat with rifampin alone, you kill all the susceptible organisms and hand the entire infection over to the resistant mutant, which then multiplies freely. You've selected for your own defeat. But now imagine you give two drugs at once, each with a different target. For any single bacterium to survive, it would need to _simultaneously_ carry resistance mutations to both drugs — and the probability of two independent rare mutations occurring in the same cell is the product of two small numbers, which is vanishingly tiny. A mutant resistant to drug A is killed by drug B, and vice versa. So combination therapy doesn't just hit harder; it mathematically slams shut the door on resistance emerging during treatment. That's why TB is treated with three or four drugs at once for months, and why we never, ever use rifampin alone for an active infection. The combination protects each drug from the bacterium's evolution.

**Reason two: to broaden coverage when you don't yet know the enemy.** When a patient is critically ill — septic, deteriorating — you often can't wait the day or two it takes the lab to identify the organism and its susceptibilities. So you start _empiric_ therapy: you make an educated guess and you cover broadly, sometimes with more than one drug, to make sure that whatever the bug turns out to be, you've got it covered. Then, crucially, once the lab tells you the organism and its MICs, you _de-escalate_ — you narrow down to the single best drug. So the combination here is a temporary insurance policy during uncertainty, deliberately walked back once you have knowledge. Starting broad and narrowing down is one of the central disciplines of good antibiotic use, and it directly serves the resistance-stewardship principle from earlier.

**Reason three: to achieve synergy — to make the combination more powerful than the sum of its parts.** This is the most mechanistically interesting reason, and we've already seen the cleanest example. Trimethoprim and sulfamethoxazole each block a different step in the same folate pathway — sequential blockade — and together they're synergistic and bactericidal where each alone is merely bacteriostatic. Here's a second classic example whose mechanism you can now reason out completely: combining a cell-wall agent, like a penicillin, with an aminoglycoside. On its own, an aminoglycoside has trouble getting into certain gram-positive bacteria — its uptake is poor. But if you also give a penicillin, the penicillin damages the cell wall, and that damage makes the membrane more permeable, which lets the aminoglycoside flood in and reach its ribosomal target far more effectively. So the penicillin opens the door for the aminoglycoside. The two together kill organisms — like the bacteria causing certain cases of endocarditis — that neither reliably kills alone. Notice that the synergy is entirely explained by mechanism: one drug breaches the barrier so the other can reach its target. That's not a coincidence you memorize; it's a consequence you can derive.

**Reason four: to treat polymicrobial infections** — infections genuinely caused by several different organisms at once, like some abdominal infections involving both aerobic and anaerobic bacteria — where no single drug covers everything, so you combine to cover the full cast of pathogens.

\[Pause.\]

But — and this is the cautionary half — combinations can also _backfire_. They can be **antagonistic**, where one drug actively undermines another, and the classic teaching example is mechanistically elegant once you see it. Recall that beta-lactams only work on _actively growing, dividing_ bacteria, because they need the cell to be busy remodeling its wall — the autolysins have to be chewing for the drug's effect to matter. Now suppose you also give a bacteriostatic drug — say one that halts protein synthesis and freezes the bacteria. You've just stopped the bacteria from growing. And a beta-lactam can't kill a bacterium that isn't growing. So in theory, the static drug, by freezing the cell, can _protect_ it from the cidal beta-lactam — the two drugs work at cross purposes. That's the textbook caution about combining certain bactericidal and bacteriostatic agents. In practice it's more nuanced than the simple rule suggests, and many such combinations are used successfully, but the underlying logic — that you can't shatter a wall the bug has stopped building — is real, and it's a clean illustration of why you have to think about _mechanisms_ when you combine, not just throw drugs together and hope.

So the takeaway on combinations: more is not automatically better. We combine for specific, mechanistically-grounded reasons — to prevent resistance, to cover uncertainty, to achieve synergy, or to cover multiple bugs — and we stay alert to antagonism, where mechanisms collide. And we always remember the cost of broad combinations: more collateral damage to the patient's normal flora, more toxicity, more expense, and more selective pressure driving resistance. The art is using exactly as much firepower as the situation demands and not one drug more. With that, let's finish the targets — the membrane.

## SEGMENT 16 — Target five, the membrane: daptomycin and polymyxins

The fifth and final target category is the cell membrane itself — and remember my prediction from the beginning: this is the _smallest_ difference between bacteria and us, because both of us have lipid membranes, so these drugs should be our more toxic, last-resort agents. Let's see if that holds.

**Daptomycin** is a lipopeptide that works against gram-positive bacteria, including resistant ones like MRSA and VRE. Its mechanism: it has a fatty tail that inserts into the bacterial cell membrane — a process that requires calcium — and once embedded, daptomycin molecules aggregate and disrupt the membrane, causing it to leak ions. The membrane potential collapses, the cell can no longer maintain the electrochemical gradients it needs to live, and it dies. Rapidly bactericidal. Now here's a mechanism-driven clinical fact that students love because it's so logical: daptomycin **cannot be used to treat pneumonia**. Why? Because in the lungs, daptomycin is bound up and inactivated by pulmonary surfactant — the lipid material that coats your alveoli. The drug gets sequestered before it can reach the bacteria. So a perfectly good anti-gram-positive drug is simply off the table for lung infections, purely because of where it ends up. Mechanism and even tissue chemistry dictating clinical use, one more time.

**The polymyxins** — polymyxin B and colistin — target gram-negative bacteria. They're cationic, positively charged molecules that are attracted to the negatively charged lipopolysaccharide of the gram-negative outer membrane; they bind there, disrupt both the outer and inner membranes like a detergent tearing apart a lipid layer, and the cell's contents leak out. Bactericidal. And true to the prediction, polymyxins are quite **toxic** — significantly nephrotoxic, damaging to the kidneys, and neurotoxic. For decades they were shelved precisely because of that toxicity. But here's the sobering part, and it sets up our final topic: colistin has come _roaring back_ into use in recent years — not because it got safer, but because we've run out of better options against certain extremely resistant gram-negative bacteria. We are now reaching for a toxic, sixty-year-old drug as a last resort because the modern, safer drugs no longer work against these organisms. That is a clinical alarm bell, and it's the perfect transition into where we finish: resistance.

So the prediction held. Membrane: smallest difference, most toxic drugs, drugs of last resort. The pattern I gave you early on has now held across all five categories. Cell wall — biggest difference — safest. Membrane — smallest difference — most toxic. Mechanism, all the way down.

## SEGMENT 17 — Pulling it together: the four ways bacteria fight back

We've now met every major target, and along the way I kept dropping in resistance mechanisms — beta-lactamases, PBP2a, D-Ala-D-Lac, _erm_ methylation, _rpoB_ mutations. Let me now gather them into a single framework, because here's the beautiful thing: there are really only **four fundamental strategies** a bacterium can use to defeat _any_ antibiotic, and once you see them as a set, every specific example we discussed slots neatly into one of the four. This is the synthesis I want you to walk out with.

Strategy one: **destroy or modify the drug**. If you can chemically wreck the molecule before it reaches its target, you win. The supreme example is the beta-lactamase enzyme, which cleaves the beta-lactam ring and renders the drug inert. There are also aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes that bolt chemical groups onto aminoglycosides so they can no longer bind the ribosome. The drug arrives, the bacterium chemically disarms it. Enzymatic inactivation.

Strategy two: **modify the target**. Change the lock so the key no longer fits, while keeping the lock functional enough to do its job. We saw this one again and again — it might be the most important strategy of all. PBP2a in MRSA: a modified transpeptidase the beta-lactam can't bind. The D-Ala-D-Lac swap in VRE: a modified cell-wall terminus vancomycin can't grip. The _erm_ methylation of ribosomal RNA: a modified ribosome that defeats macrolides, clindamycin, and streptogramins all at once. The _rpoB_ point mutation: a modified RNA polymerase rifampin can't hold. In every case, the drug arrives perfectly intact — but the target has changed shape, and the binding fails. Target modification.

Strategy three: **stop the drug from getting in**. If the drug can never reach its target, the target's vulnerability is irrelevant. Gram-negative bacteria are masters of this because of that outer membrane — they can reduce the number of porin channels, narrowing the doors through which drugs enter, and just like that the intracellular drug concentration drops below what's needed. This is a major mechanism of resistance to beta-lactams and fluoroquinolones in gram-negatives. Reduced permeability — locking the door.

Strategy four: **pump the drug back out**. Even if the drug gets in, the bacterium can install molecular pumps in its membrane — **efflux pumps** — that grab the antibiotic and actively eject it, keeping the internal concentration too low to be effective. Some efflux pumps are specific to one drug; others are alarmingly promiscuous and pump out many different antibiotic classes, conferring multi-drug resistance from a single mechanism. This is important for tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, macrolides, and more. Active efflux — bailing out the boat as fast as the water comes in.

\[Pause.\]

Four strategies. Destroy the drug, change the target, keep it out, pump it out. Now here's the unifying insight I most want to leave you with. Look back across the entire lecture and you'll notice that for every drug, the _resistance mechanism is logically predictable from the mechanism of action_. A drug that depends on binding a specific enzyme can be defeated by mutating that enzyme. A drug with a reactive chemical ring invites an enzyme that cleaves the ring. A large drug that struggles to enter can be locked out further. A drug that must accumulate inside can be pumped out. If you understand how a drug works, you can predict — before you ever see it in a clinic — how bacteria will likely escape it. Mechanism predicts resistance. That is the deepest payoff of approaching this subject through mechanism rather than memorization.

And one more layer, because it matters for how you'll practice or do research: where do these resistance genes come from, and why are they spreading so fast? Bacteria don't only inherit resistance from their parents — they trade resistance genes _horizontally_, swapping mobile chunks of DNA called plasmids directly between cells, even between different species. A resistance gene that arises in one bug can hop to another overnight. Layer on top of that the relentless _selection pressure_ we apply by using antibiotics — every dose kills the susceptible bacteria and leaves the resistant ones to flourish and multiply — and you have a recipe for the resistance crisis that brought colistin back from retirement. We are, in a real sense, the evolutionary force selecting for our own obsolescence every time we use these drugs carelessly.

## SEGMENT 18 — Synthesis and close

Let me bring this home. We've covered a lot of molecules in two hours, and if you try to hold them in your head as a flat list of names, you'll lose them by next week. So don't. Instead, hold the _architecture_, and let the individual drugs hang off it.

Here's the architecture in one breath. Every antibiotic works by exploiting a difference between a bacterial cell and your cells, and there are five such differences. The cell wall — attacked by beta-lactams, which inactivate the cross-linking transpeptidase, and by vancomycin, which caps the D-Ala-D-Ala substrate; biggest difference, safest drugs. The ribosome — attacked at the 30S by aminoglycosides, which cause lethal misreading, and tetracyclines, which block tRNA docking, and at the 50S by macrolides, clindamycin, chloramphenicol, and linezolid, which jam various steps of elongation and initiation; toxicity tracking the resemblance to your mitochondrial ribosomes. The nucleic acid machinery — fluoroquinolones trapping DNA gyrase mid-cut and shredding the chromosome, rifampin blocking RNA polymerase, metronidazole activated only inside anaerobes to fragment their DNA. Folate synthesis — sulfonamides and trimethoprim hitting two sequential steps of a pathway you don't even possess, synergizing into a bactericidal combination. And the membrane — daptomycin and the polymyxins tearing it open; smallest difference, most toxic, last resort.

And wrapped around all of it: four resistance strategies — destroy the drug, change the target, keep it out, pump it out — each one predictable from the drug's own mechanism.

\[Pause.\]

If you remember nothing else, remember the two organizing principles, because they're the ones that turn this from memorization into understanding. First: **the size of the selective-toxicity gap predicts the safety of the drug**. When the bacterial target has no human counterpart — the cell wall, the folate pathway — the drugs are beautifully safe. When the target faintly echoes something in our own cells — the ribosome shadowed by our mitochondria, or the membrane we share in lipid form — the toxicity creeps in. You can almost read a drug's side-effect profile straight off its mechanism. Second: **the mechanism of action predicts the mechanism of resistance**. Tell me precisely how a drug binds its target, and I can tell you how bacteria will most likely evade it — by altering that target, by destroying the drug, by refusing it entry, or by ejecting it.

Those two principles are the difference between a list and a framework. The list you'll forget. The framework will let you place a drug you've never seen before — and you _will_ meet new ones, because we keep inventing them in the arms race — and reason out roughly how it must work, how toxic it's likely to be, and how it'll eventually be defeated.

That arms race is the real reason this matters beyond the exam. Every mechanism we covered today is, simultaneously, a target the bacteria are working to neutralize. The drugs that felt invincible a generation ago are failing now, which is exactly why a toxic relic like colistin is back in our hands. Understanding these mechanisms isn't academic housekeeping — it's the foundation for using these drugs wisely enough to keep them working, and for designing the next generation when these ones fall. The bacteria are very, very good at evolution. Our advantage is that we can _understand_ — and understanding the mechanism is where it all starts.

That's where we'll stop. Next session we'll take these mechanisms into the clinic and talk about choosing among them for specific infections. Thanks, everyone — good work today.

## APPENDIX — Quick-reference summary (not for reading aloud)

**Target 1 — Cell wall (largest selective-toxicity gap → safest drugs)**

- Beta-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems, monobactams): inactivate transpeptidases (PBPs); beta-lactam ring mimics D-Ala-D-Ala; bactericidal; time-dependent. Resistance: beta-lactamase (destroys drug), PBP2a/mecA = MRSA (target change).
- Vancomycin (glycopeptide): binds D-Ala-D-Ala substrate; bactericidal; gram-positive only (too large for gram-negative outer membrane). Resistance: D-Ala-D-Lac = VRE.
- Others: fosfomycin (early synthesis), bacitracin (lipid carrier recycling), cycloserine (D-Ala-D-Ala synthesis).

**Target 2 — Ribosome (overlap with mitochondrial 70S-like ribosomes → toxicity)**

- 30S: aminoglycosides (misreading + initiation block; bactericidal; concentration-dependent; long PAE; O2-dependent uptake so no anaerobes; nephro/ototoxic). Tetracyclines (block A-site tRNA docking; static; chelate cations).
- 50S: macrolides (exit tunnel/translocation; static; P450 inhibitors). Chloramphenicol (peptidyl transferase; static; marrow suppression). Clindamycin (elongation; anaerobes; C. diff). Linezolid (blocks initiation complex; unique site, no cross-resistance). MLS-B resistance via erm methylation hits macrolides + lincosamides + streptogramins together.

**Target 3 — Nucleic acids**

- Fluoroquinolones: trap DNA gyrase/topo IV mid-cleavage → double-strand breaks; bactericidal; concentration-dependent; tendon/QT/cartilage cautions.
- Rifampin: inhibits RNA polymerase (rpoB); bactericidal; single-mutation resistance → never monotherapy; P450 inducer; orange fluids.
- Metronidazole: prodrug activated only in anaerobes/protozoa → DNA damage; alcohol reaction.

**Target 4 — Folate synthesis (pathway absent in humans → very selective)**

- Sulfonamides: PABA mimic, inhibit dihydropteroate synthase. Trimethoprim: inhibits bacterial dihydrofolate reductase (selective affinity vs human enzyme). Combination = sequential blockade = synergistic, bactericidal.

**Target 5 — Membrane (smallest gap → most toxic, last-resort)**

- Daptomycin: inserts into gram-positive membrane (Ca-dependent), depolarizes; bactericidal; inactivated by lung surfactant (no pneumonia use).
- Polymyxins/colistin: disrupt gram-negative membranes; bactericidal; nephro/neurotoxic; last resort.

**Four resistance strategies:** (1) inactivate/destroy drug; (2) modify target; (3) reduce entry (porin loss); (4) efflux pumps. Spread by plasmids (horizontal transfer) under antibiotic selection pressure.

**Two organizing principles:** (1) selective-toxicity gap predicts safety; (2) mechanism of action predicts mechanism of resistance.
