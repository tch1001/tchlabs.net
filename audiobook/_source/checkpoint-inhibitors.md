# Cancer Immunotherapy: Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors — A Deep Dive

**A 2-hour undergraduate immuno-oncology lecture — full spoken script**

## SEGMENT 1 — The brakes the cancer installed on your immune system

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. For the next two hours we are going to talk about what is, without exaggeration, the most important development in cancer therapy of the last fifty years — possibly of the last hundred. We are going to talk about immune checkpoint inhibitors. And I want to begin not with a molecule, not with a drug, not even with a patient, but with one mental picture that I want you to carry through the whole lecture.

Picture your immune system as an enormous standing army. Trillions of cells, organized into divisions and patrols and reconnaissance units, walking through every tissue of your body, every minute of every day, looking for trouble. The most lethal soldiers in that army are the T cells — specifically the killer T cells, the CD8 T cells, the ones that, when they identify a sick cell, walk up to it and inject it with toxins and tell it to die. Your T cells kill virally infected cells. They kill aberrant cells. And, importantly, they kill — or could kill — many cancer cells, every day, before those cancer cells ever grow large enough for you to notice.

That last fact is going to anchor this entire lecture, so let me say it again. Cancers — most cancers — are arising and being eliminated by your immune system constantly, throughout your life. The cancers that you eventually get diagnosed with are not the cancers that arose; they are the cancers that _escaped_. They are the cells that somehow managed to grow in the presence of a vast immune army that should, by all rights, have killed them years ago.

So the question that immunology and oncology spent the better part of a century trying to answer is: how do they escape? What is the trick? How does a cell carrying mutated, abnormal proteins — a cell that looks, biochemically, like a freak — manage to grow into a tumor the size of a walnut in an immune-competent host?

\[Pause.\]

And the answer, the answer that won the 2018 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, the answer that has rewritten the standard of care for melanoma and lung cancer and kidney cancer and bladder cancer and Hodgkin lymphoma and a dozen others, the answer is this. **Cancers learn to install brakes on your immune system.**

Your T cells, like any well-designed killing machine, have safety features. They have built-in brakes. There are molecules on the surface of your T cells whose job is to dampen, slow, or shut down the killing program when the killing has gone on too long, or when the target turns out to be a normal cell, or when the inflammation is reaching dangerous levels. These molecules are called **immune checkpoints**. They exist because, without them, your T cells would run amok — they'd attack everything, they'd cause catastrophic autoimmunity, they'd kill you faster than any infection ever could. The checkpoints are the safety mechanisms that keep the lethal army from turning on the city it is supposed to protect.

And cancers — and here is the punchline of the entire field — cancers have figured out how to _press those brakes_. They have figured out how to display, on their own surfaces or to recruit other cells to display, the exact molecular signals that tell an approaching T cell: _stand down. Don't kill me. I am not a threat._ The T cell, doing what it was designed to do, obeys. The cancer survives. The cancer grows.

So immune checkpoint inhibitors are drugs that do one thing, conceptually. **They remove the brakes that the cancer has installed on your immune system.** They block the brake-pressing molecules, the T cells stop receiving the stand-down signal, and the army that was already there, that had already found the cancer, that had already started its assault and then been told to halt — that army gets unleashed. It finishes the job.

That is the entire field, in one sentence. Everything we are going to discuss for the next two hours is built on that idea.

\[Pause.\]

Now, there are two specific brakes — two specific checkpoint molecules — that matter more than any of the others, at least so far. They were discovered, independently, by two scientists working on opposite sides of the world, who shared the 2018 Nobel Prize for their parallel and complementary work. The first brake is called **CTLA-4**, and it was the discovery of James Allison, an American immunologist who realized in the mid-1990s that this molecule was acting as a brake on T-cell activation and that an antibody blocking it could let mice's immune systems destroy their own tumors. The second brake is called **PD-1**, and it was the discovery of Tasuku Honjo, a Japanese immunologist who cloned the molecule in 1992 not even knowing what it did, and then spent the next decade working out that it, too, was a brake — but a different brake, acting in a different place at a different stage.

Together, CTLA-4 and PD-1 — and PD-1's partner molecule, PD-L1 — form the molecular core of modern cancer immunotherapy. The drugs you will hear about in the news — ipilimumab, pembrolizumab, nivolumab, atezolizumab, durvalumab, the whole "-mab" parade — every single one of them is, at its heart, an antibody that blocks one of these two brakes. That's it. That's the trick.

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, explain to a friend without jargon what a checkpoint inhibitor is and why it works; two, distinguish CTLA-4 from PD-1 from PD-L1 and roughly remember which drugs hit which target; three, look at a cancer-news headline about a checkpoint inhibitor and have a real sense of what it means, what the biomarkers are, who is likely to respond, and what the toxicities are — then we will have done our job. Everything else is detail hung onto that scaffold.

Let's build the scaffold.

## SEGMENT 2 — The tumor escape problem, in detail

Before we get to Allison and Honjo, we need to spend a little time on the problem they were solving. Because if you don't really feel, in your bones, why tumors are so hard for the immune system to clear, the elegance of what these two men figured out won't land.

So here we go. Your T cells, particularly the CD8 killer T cells, recognize their targets through a system called **MHC class I**. Every cell in your body — every single one, with a few exceptions — displays, on its surface, a constant slideshow of tiny peptide fragments. These fragments are chopped-up samples of whatever proteins the cell is currently making, inside. They're loaded onto a molecule called MHC class I, which carries them out to the cell surface and holds them up for inspection. T cells walk past, sniff the displays, and ask: do any of these peptides look foreign? Do any of them look mutated? Do any of them look like something I have been trained to attack?

If the answer is yes — if a virus is replicating inside the cell, or a protein has been mutated, or a foreign sequence has appeared — then a T cell that recognizes that peptide locks on, and within minutes the cell is dead. This is the central surveillance loop of cellular immunity, and it runs continuously, in every tissue, all the time.

Now think about cancer in this context. A cancer cell, by definition, carries mutations. Many of those mutations land in proteins. Those mutated proteins get chopped up like any other protein and displayed on MHC class I. The peptides that result are called **neoantigens** — new antigens, sequences that don't exist in any normal tissue, sequences your immune system has never seen and was never trained to ignore. A neoantigen, in principle, is a flashing red light. It is exactly the kind of signal a T cell should pounce on.

\[Pause.\]

And yet the cancer grows. Why?

There are, broadly, four answers. And these four answers map onto four ways tumors evade immunity, and onto four reasons immunotherapy sometimes works miraculously and sometimes doesn't work at all. I want you to hold them in mind.

**One.** The tumor doesn't make enough neoantigens to be visible. Some cancers — pediatric leukemias, some sarcomas — have very few mutations. They look, biochemically, almost normal. Their MHC slideshow is boring. T cells walk past and find nothing to attack.

**Two.** The tumor stops displaying MHC class I altogether. Some cancers mutate or downregulate the MHC machinery, so the slideshow goes dark. The cell becomes invisible to T cells. (Now, your body has a backup system for this — natural killer cells specifically attack cells that have stopped showing MHC — but tumors find ways around that too.)

**Three.** The tumor surrounds itself with suppressive cells. It recruits regulatory T cells, myeloid-derived suppressor cells, tumor-associated macrophages — a whole ecosystem of immune-dampening neighbors that turn the tumor microenvironment into a place where killer T cells get sluggish and demoralized. It is, in effect, building a fortress out of corrupted immune cells.

**Four — and this is the one that the Nobel-winning therapies attack.** The tumor activates the immune system's _own_ brakes. It displays, on its surface, ligands — protein molecules — that bind to the checkpoint receptors on T cells and tell those T cells: stand down. PD-L1, the ligand for PD-1, is the famous example. Many tumors learn to plaster themselves with PD-L1, in effect waving a white flag at every approaching T cell. The T cell sees the flag, the PD-1 receptor on its surface engages, the killing program shuts off, and the cancer cell survives.

That fourth strategy is what we are going to spend the rest of this lecture dismantling. Because once you understand that the cancer is _waving white flags_, the therapeutic strategy becomes obvious. _Take away the white flags. Or blindfold the T cell so it can't see them._ That's a checkpoint inhibitor. That's the whole game.

\[Pause.\]

One more piece of intuition before we move on. The reason this strategy works at all — the reason you can sometimes give a single drug, a single antibody, and watch a metastatic melanoma melt away over months — is that the T cells were _already there_. They had already infiltrated the tumor. They had already recognized the neoantigens. They had already started their assault. The cancer was suppressing them, but the army was on the field. All the drug has to do is lift the suppression. The killing was paused, not absent. Hit the unpause button, and the killing resumes.

This is also why checkpoint inhibitors don't work on every cancer. If the T cells never showed up to the tumor in the first place — if it's a so-called "cold" tumor, with no immune infiltrate — then there's nothing to unpause. Releasing the brake on an empty road does nothing. We'll come back to that problem in the second half.

For now, hold this picture. _The army is there. It has been told to stand down. The drug tells it to fight again._

## SEGMENT 3 — James Allison and the discovery of CTLA-4 as a brake

Let me tell you about James Allison.

It is the early 1990s. Allison is a Texan immunologist, born in Alice, Texas, country-music-loving, harmonica-playing, by all accounts not the typical buttoned-down Nobel laureate type. He's working on a molecule called CTLA-4, which stands for cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 — a name that, helpfully, tells you almost nothing useful. CTLA-4 had been cloned in 1987 by a French group, and it was known to appear on the surface of activated T cells, but its function was murky. The dominant view at the time was that CTLA-4 was probably an _accelerator_ — a second signal that revved T cells up, similar to a closely related molecule called CD28. CD28 was already known to be the gas pedal of T-cell activation. People thought CTLA-4 was a backup gas pedal.

\[Pause.\]

But Allison and a few others — notably Jeffrey Bluestone and Arlene Sharpe, who deserve more credit than the popular story usually gives them — started to suspect something stranger. Maybe CTLA-4 wasn't an accelerator at all. Maybe it was a brake.

Here's the logic. T cells, when they get activated, undergo a kind of two-key activation process. The first key is recognition of the antigen — the T cell receptor binds an MHC-peptide complex on an antigen-presenting cell. The second key is a co-stimulatory signal — CD28 on the T cell engages a molecule called B7 on the antigen-presenting cell. Both signals together, the T cell goes from zero to sixty and starts dividing, producing cytokines, mounting an attack. One signal alone, the T cell does nothing — or worse, becomes tolerant, lazy, anergic.

Now, CTLA-4 appears on the T cell's surface only _after_ the T cell has already been activated. It comes up late. And it binds the _same_ B7 molecule that CD28 binds — but with much higher affinity. Much, much higher. So as the T cell ramps up its response, CTLA-4 starts appearing and starts outcompeting CD28 for the B7 ligand. Whatever B7 is doing through CD28 to keep the T cell revved up, CTLA-4 starts stealing the substrate.

Allison's group did the experiment that mattered. They took mice with tumors. They made an antibody against CTLA-4 — an antibody that physically blocked CTLA-4 from binding B7. They injected it. And the tumors regressed.

Let me say that again. They injected an antibody that blocked a single T-cell surface molecule, and the mice's immune systems _destroyed their own tumors_. This was a 1996 paper in the journal Science. It was, at the time, almost unbelievable. The dogma had been that the immune system was, basically, irrelevant to cancer. You couldn't shrink tumors by tickling the immune system. People had tried. It hadn't worked. And here was a single antibody making colon tumors and fibrosarcomas vanish in mice.

The model Allison and his colleagues built from this was beautiful and clear. **CTLA-4 is a brake.** It is a molecule that emerges on activated T cells specifically to slow them down — to prevent runaway responses that would damage healthy tissue. In normal life, that brake is critical. Without it, you get fatal autoimmunity — and indeed, mice in which the CTLA-4 gene is deleted develop massive lymphoproliferative disease and die within weeks. The brake is essential.

But in the context of cancer, the brake is the enemy. The T cells are trying to attack a tumor. The brake is engaging and shutting them down. Block the brake — temporarily, with an antibody — and the T cells get to keep going. The brake-cutter, if you will, frees the immune system to do what it was already trying to do.

\[Pause.\]

Allison spent the rest of the 1990s trying to convince a pharmaceutical industry that did not want to listen. The standard view was still: immune therapy doesn't work, why are you wasting our time. He shopped his idea around. Eventually, a small biotech called Medarex — which had a platform for making fully human antibodies — agreed to take a chance. They developed an anti-human-CTLA-4 antibody, eventually called ipilimumab. Bristol-Myers Squibb bought Medarex. They put ipilimumab into trials for advanced metastatic melanoma — at the time, one of the most universally lethal cancers in adult medicine. Median survival, six to nine months. No drug had ever shown a survival benefit.

In 2010, the trial results came out. Patients given ipilimumab lived longer. Not all of them — only about twenty percent had durable responses — but those who did respond were, in some cases, still alive years later. In a few cases, decades later. People with metastatic melanoma simply did not survive ten years. With ipilimumab, some of them did.

In March 2011, the FDA approved ipilimumab — brand name Yervoy — for advanced melanoma. It was the first immune checkpoint inhibitor ever approved. The era began.

We'll come back to Yervoy specifically in Segment 5. For now, the takeaway is the principle. **Allison's insight: CTLA-4 is a brake on T cells. Block the brake, and the T cells fight cancer.**

That insight, eventually, won him half of the 2018 Nobel Prize. The other half went to a man who had been working, completely independently, on a second brake, on the other side of the Pacific.

## SEGMENT 4 — Tasuku Honjo and the discovery of PD-1

Tasuku Honjo is a quieter story. Where Allison is the cowboy, harmonica-in-hand showman, Honjo is the meticulous, courtly Kyoto professor. He spent his career working on immune-cell genetics, doing fundamental biology, and in 1992 his lab cloned a gene from a dying T cell. They named the gene **PD-1**, for "programmed death 1," because the cell they cloned it from was undergoing programmed death and Honjo's group thought, reasonably, that the gene might be involved in apoptosis.

It wasn't. That turned out to be a misnomer that stuck. PD-1 is not a death gene. It is a brake.

\[Pause.\]

For most of the 1990s, nobody knew what PD-1 did. It was just a gene Honjo's group had cloned — a sequence in a database, a molecule waiting for a function. Honjo, patient as he was, kept pulling on the thread. By 1999, his group had made PD-1 knockout mice — mice in which the PD-1 gene was deleted — and they discovered that those mice, like the CTLA-4 knockouts, developed autoimmunity. Not as severe; the PD-1 knockouts didn't die in weeks. But they developed lupus-like disease and arthritis. The signal was clear: PD-1, like CTLA-4, was holding the immune system back. It was a brake.

But it was, importantly, a _different_ brake. CTLA-4 acts early — during the priming phase, in the lymph node, when a T cell is first being activated by an antigen-presenting cell. PD-1 acts late — out in the tissues, in the tumor, after the T cell has already been activated, traveled to the site of infection or cancer, and is in the middle of trying to do its killing job.

The mechanism is different too. CTLA-4 competes with CD28 for the B7 ligand on antigen-presenting cells. PD-1, when activated, recruits a phosphatase enzyme called SHP-2 directly into the T-cell receptor complex, and SHP-2 dephosphorylates the signaling molecules that the T cell needs to keep going. PD-1 doesn't compete for a ligand. It hits the T-cell signaling machinery directly, like reaching into the engine and disconnecting the wires.

And the ligands — this is the crucial part — the ligands for PD-1 are called PD-L1 and PD-L2, and they appear, naturally, on a wide variety of tissues, especially inflamed tissues. Their job, normally, is to say to an arriving T cell: yes, there is inflammation here, but it's controlled, you can stand down. They're the off-switch for a tissue response that has gone on long enough.

Tumors, of course, figured this out. Many tumors — especially tumors that have already been infiltrated by T cells and are experiencing interferon-gamma signaling from those T cells — _upregulate PD-L1_. They paint themselves with the molecule that tells T cells to stand down. Every T cell that arrives sees the white flag, engages its PD-1 receptor, and gets the signal: don't kill this cell. It's fine. Stand down.

So Honjo's insight, parallel to Allison's: **PD-1 is a second brake, working at the tumor site, often pressed by the tumor itself via PD-L1.** Block PD-1 — or block PD-L1 — and the brake is released. The T cells, already present in the tumor, can kill.

\[Pause.\]

Honjo's group, like Allison's, had to fight to get this idea into clinical development. The story I've heard him tell — and you can find it in various Nobel interviews — is that pharmaceutical companies repeatedly turned him down. He partnered with a small Japanese company called Ono Pharmaceutical, who in turn partnered with Medarex (the same company Allison had worked with), and eventually with Bristol-Myers Squibb. The drug they developed, nivolumab, was an anti-PD-1 antibody. Around the same time, Merck developed pembrolizumab — also anti-PD-1, from a slightly different parent program originally acquired from Organon and Schering-Plough. Both drugs entered melanoma trials in the early 2010s, and both worked beautifully.

In September 2014, the FDA approved pembrolizumab for melanoma. Three months later, in December 2014, it approved nivolumab. The PD-1 era began.

And then the trickle became a flood. Lung cancer. Kidney cancer. Bladder cancer. Hodgkin lymphoma. Head and neck cancer. Hepatocellular carcinoma. Esophageal cancer. Gastric cancer. Microsatellite-instability-high anything. Triple-negative breast cancer. Cervical cancer. The list of indications has now exceeded forty cancer types and continues to grow. Pembrolizumab alone is approved in something like fifty distinct clinical settings.

Honjo and Allison shared the 2018 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. The committee cited "their discovery of cancer therapy by inhibition of negative immune regulation." A clunky phrase, but a fair one. They had discovered, independently, in different countries, on different molecules, the same principle: the immune system has brakes; cancer presses them; release the brakes, and the immune system can fight cancer.

That principle, alone, has changed the survival statistics for half a dozen major cancers. We are still living inside the consequences.

## SEGMENT 5 — Ipilimumab: the first brake-cutter

Let's spend a little time on ipilimumab — Yervoy — because it was the first, and the first is always interesting. Also because ipilimumab teaches you, in concentrated form, both the magic and the cost of checkpoint inhibition.

Ipilimumab is a fully human monoclonal antibody. Its target is CTLA-4 on T cells. Its mechanism: it binds to CTLA-4 and physically blocks CTLA-4 from binding B7 on antigen-presenting cells. Without that binding, CTLA-4 can't deliver its inhibitory signal. Cipla — sorry, CD28 — wins the competition for B7. The T cell stays activated. The brake is cut.

There may also be a second mechanism. Some recent work suggests that ipilimumab, by virtue of its particular antibody isotype, can actively recruit immune cells to kill regulatory T cells inside tumors. Regulatory T cells express CTLA-4 constitutively, and the antibody, bound to them, makes them targets for antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity. So ipilimumab may be both releasing the brake on conventional T cells _and_ depleting the suppressive T-reg population in the tumor. The full mechanism is still being worked out.

What's clear is what it does in patients.

\[Pause.\]

The pivotal trial for ipilimumab, called MDX010-20, was published in the New England Journal in 2010. It enrolled six hundred and seventy-six patients with advanced metastatic melanoma — patients who had already failed standard treatments, patients with a median survival measured in months. They got ipilimumab, with or without a vaccine, or the vaccine alone.

The headline result: median overall survival went from six and a half months in the control arm to ten months in the ipilimumab arm. Three and a half months of extra median survival.

If you stop there, you might say: that's a modest result. A few months. Not earth-shattering. And in fact, when the trial first reported, some oncologists were unimpressed.

But the median is the wrong number to look at. Here's the right number. In the ipilimumab arm, about twenty percent of patients were still alive at three years. About eighteen percent were still alive at four years. And then the curve went flat. The patients who responded — about one in five — appeared to be _cured_. Not improved, not extended, but cured. People with metastatic melanoma, who had every right to be dead in a year, were attending their children's weddings ten years later.

This is the pattern that ipilimumab introduced, and that all subsequent checkpoint inhibitors have, in various ways, reproduced. The median improvement is modest. But the _tail_ of the curve is what matters. A subset of patients — the lucky responders — get durable, sometimes permanent remissions. They don't relapse. The cancer doesn't come back. It is, functionally, gone.

Why this happens, biologically, is fascinating. When an immune system clears a tumor, it generates memory T cells. Those memory cells persist for years, decades, lifetimes. If even a single tumor cell tries to come back, the memory T cells recognize it and kill it before it can grow. The immune system has been, in some sense, vaccinated against the patient's own cancer. Once the cancer is cleared, it tends to stay cleared.

That is what ipilimumab demonstrated, for the first time, in 2010. That immunotherapy could produce durable cures in a cancer that was previously universally fatal.

\[Pause.\]

The cost, though. Let me tell you about the cost.

Ipilimumab is also, by some distance, the most toxic checkpoint inhibitor in widespread clinical use. Cutting the early brake on T-cell activation is a blunt instrument. The T cells that get released don't only attack the cancer; some of them attack normal tissue. About sixty percent of patients on ipilimumab develop some kind of immune-related adverse event — irAE in the clinical literature. Twenty percent have severe ones. Colitis, where T cells attack the gut lining and patients have bloody diarrhea. Hepatitis, where T cells attack the liver. Pneumonitis, where T cells inflame the lungs. Skin rashes. Endocrinopathies — the pituitary, the thyroid, the adrenals can all get hit, and the damage is often permanent. Some patients lose their thyroid function for life. Some have to take steroids for the rest of their lives.

A few patients die of these toxicities. It is a real risk.

So ipilimumab, in its monotherapy form, is now mostly used in carefully selected patients — patients for whom the potential upside of a durable cure is worth the risk of severe toxicity. In most front-line settings, it has been displaced by the gentler PD-1 inhibitors that came after. But it is often used in combination — ipi plus nivo, as we'll discuss — and it remains a workhorse for advanced melanoma.

Mostly, though, ipilimumab is important as the proof of concept. It showed that the principle worked. After Yervoy, every pharmaceutical company on Earth understood that checkpoint inhibition was real, and the race for the next target was on.

## SEGMENT 6 — Pembrolizumab and nivolumab: the PD-1 dynasty

If ipilimumab was the first brake-cutter, pembrolizumab and nivolumab are the brake-cutters that became blockbusters. They are, between them, the two best-selling oncology drugs in the world, and pembrolizumab — Merck's Keytruda — is now the single best-selling drug of any kind in the world. In 2024 it generated something north of twenty-nine billion dollars in revenue. That number is not a typo. One drug. Twenty-nine billion dollars.

Both drugs are anti-PD-1 antibodies. They are fully or nearly fully human IgG4 monoclonal antibodies that bind PD-1 on T cells and block it from interacting with its ligands PD-L1 and PD-L2. The IgG4 choice matters — IgG4 has weak effector function, so it doesn't kill the T cells it binds; it just sits on PD-1 and blocks the engagement. The drugs are, in essence, blindfolds — they cover the eyes of the T cell so it can't see the white flag the tumor is waving.

\[Pause.\]

Pembrolizumab and nivolumab were developed in parallel, by Merck and Bristol-Myers Squibb respectively. They have very similar mechanisms, similar efficacy, similar toxicity profiles. They were both approved in late 2014 for metastatic melanoma. Then, in 2015, both were approved for non-small-cell lung cancer — and that is the approval that made them giants. Lung cancer is the largest oncology market in the world. About a quarter of patients with non-small-cell lung cancer respond to anti-PD-1 therapy, and a subset of those responders — particularly those with high PD-L1 expression on their tumors — get durable remissions. For decades, advanced lung cancer had a five-year survival rate of less than five percent. With pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy as front-line therapy, that number is now closer to twenty-five percent in PD-L1-positive patients. We have, in less than ten years, quintupled five-year survival for one of the deadliest common cancers.

The clinical pattern with anti-PD-1 monotherapy is similar to ipilimumab but generally _better_: higher response rates — thirty to forty percent in melanoma, twenty to twenty-five percent in lung — fewer severe toxicities, and durable responses in those who respond. The tail of the survival curve goes flat just as it did with Yervoy, but a larger fraction of patients end up on the flat part.

The toxicity profile is also more forgiving. PD-1 inhibitors cause immune-related adverse events too — they have to, mechanistically, because PD-1 is also a brake on autoimmunity in normal tissues. But the rate of severe toxicity is lower than with ipilimumab. About ten to fifteen percent of patients get severe irAEs on PD-1 monotherapy, versus twenty-plus percent on ipilimumab. The thyroid, the lungs, the colon, and the skin remain the most commonly affected organs.

\[Pause.\]

Pembrolizumab has, over the past decade, accumulated an absolutely staggering set of approvals. As of 2025, the FDA-approved indications for Keytruda include melanoma, non-small-cell lung cancer, small-cell lung cancer, head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, classical Hodgkin lymphoma, primary mediastinal B-cell lymphoma, urothelial carcinoma, microsatellite-instability-high or mismatch-repair-deficient solid tumors of any origin, gastric cancer, esophageal cancer, cervical cancer, hepatocellular carcinoma, Merkel cell carcinoma, renal cell carcinoma, endometrial carcinoma, tumor-mutational-burden-high solid tumors, cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma, triple-negative breast cancer, biliary tract cancer — and the list keeps growing. There is, at this point, a serious case that pembrolizumab will eventually be tested in essentially every solid tumor on Earth.

Nivolumab — Opdivo — has a slightly smaller but still vast list. Both drugs have, between them, dozens of approved combination regimens with chemotherapy, with anti-angiogenic drugs, with other immunotherapies, with targeted therapies. The PD-1 inhibitors are not just drugs. They are platforms — building blocks that get combined with almost everything else.

And both drugs have transformed the commercial landscape of oncology. Keytruda revenue alone is greater than the entire revenue of most large pharmaceutical companies. Merck's whole modern business is, essentially, a Keytruda business with some other products attached. When the Keytruda patent expires in 2028 — assuming current extensions hold — there will be a cliff. Merck has spent much of the last five years frantically acquiring other companies and trying to develop next-generation immunotherapies to soften the fall. The whole industry is watching.

\[Pause.\]

The clinical lesson, for our purposes, is this. **Anti-PD-1 antibodies have become the most widely used cancer drugs in history.** They are the workhorses. They are the foundation onto which combination therapies are layered. Wherever there is a tumor that the immune system has tried to attack — wherever there are T cells in the tumor that have been turned off by PD-L1 — anti-PD-1 antibodies have a chance of working. That includes a startling range of cancers.

The principle is exactly what Honjo described. Block PD-1, the T cells already in the tumor stop accepting the stand-down signal, and they kill. Same idea as Yervoy, different brake, broader applicability, gentler toxicity. It is the second move in the chess game, and it turned out to be a better move than the first.

## SEGMENT 7 — Anti-PD-L1: hitting the ligand instead of the receptor

So far we have talked about three drugs that block T-cell receptors — ipilimumab blocking CTLA-4, pembrolizumab and nivolumab blocking PD-1. But there is another way to interrupt the same signaling axis. Instead of blocking the receptor on the T cell, you can block the _ligand_ on the tumor. You can hit the white flag instead of the eye that sees it.

That is the strategy of the anti-PD-L1 drugs — **atezolizumab** (brand name Tecentriq, from Genentech/Roche), **durvalumab** (Imfinzi, from AstraZeneca), and **avelumab** (Bavencio, from Pfizer/Merck KGaA). All three are antibodies against PD-L1. All three were approved between 2016 and 2017. All three are workhorses, particularly in lung cancer, bladder cancer, and Merkel cell carcinoma.

\[Pause.\]

Why bother with anti-PD-L1 if you already have anti-PD-1? Three reasons, and they're worth understanding.

First, **PD-1 has two ligands, PD-L1 and PD-L2.** Anti-PD-1 antibodies block both interactions, because they sit on the receptor. Anti-PD-L1 antibodies block only the PD-L1 axis. They leave the PD-L2 axis intact. Now, PD-L2 expression is more restricted than PD-L1 — it's mostly on antigen-presenting cells and some tissues like the lung — and there is a theoretical argument that leaving PD-L2 intact preserves some normal immune regulation. Whether this matters clinically is still debated. The toxicity profiles of anti-PD-L1 drugs are roughly similar to anti-PD-1 drugs, with maybe slightly less pneumonitis in some studies — possibly because PD-L2 signaling in the lung is preserved. The data are not airtight.

Second, **the antibodies hit different cell populations differently.** Anti-PD-1 sits on T cells. Anti-PD-L1 sits on tumor cells, and also on tumor-infiltrating myeloid cells, which also express PD-L1 and which are major sources of T-cell suppression. There's an argument that hitting the ligand provides a broader release of suppression in the tumor microenvironment. Again, whether this translates clinically is unclear.

Third, and perhaps most importantly, **patents and commercial competition.** When Merck and BMS got there first with anti-PD-1, the rest of the industry needed a way in. Anti-PD-L1 was the available alternative target. Roche, AstraZeneca, and Pfizer all built their immuno-oncology platforms around anti-PD-L1 drugs, and those drugs found niches.

\[Pause.\]

The most striking anti-PD-L1 result, in my view, is the PACIFIC trial of durvalumab in unresectable stage III non-small-cell lung cancer. These were patients with locally advanced lung cancer who had completed concurrent chemoradiation. Historically, after chemoradiation, you'd watch and wait, and most patients would relapse and die within a few years. PACIFIC added durvalumab as consolidation — given for a year after chemoradiation. The five-year overall survival went from about thirty-three percent in the placebo arm to forty-three percent in the durvalumab arm. A ten-percentage-point absolute improvement in five-year survival, in a setting where the standard of care had been stagnant for two decades. That kind of result rewrites a treatment paradigm.

Atezolizumab, meanwhile, has been the workhorse anti-PD-L1 in bladder cancer and in triple-negative breast cancer, although its trials in some of those settings have had a rockier history — withdrawal of some accelerated approvals, debate about which subgroups benefit. The history is messy. But the principle is sound.

The takeaway: anti-PD-L1 drugs work, they hit the same biological axis from the other side, and clinically they are largely interchangeable with anti-PD-1 drugs in many settings. The choice is often determined by which drug the company has and which combinations have been studied. The mechanism is the same in spirit. **Take away the white flag, the T cells stop accepting the stand-down signal, the killing resumes.**

## SEGMENT 8 — Combination ipi-nivo: the double brake-cutter

Let me tell you about a specific clinical idea that turned out to be far more powerful, and far more dangerous, than anyone initially expected. It is the idea of combining two checkpoint inhibitors at once.

The logic is straightforward. CTLA-4 and PD-1 are different brakes, acting in different places at different times. CTLA-4 brakes T cells early, during priming in the lymph node. PD-1 brakes T cells late, in the tumor. If you only release one brake, the other might still be holding the response back. If you release both — if you cut both brakes simultaneously — you should get a more complete unleashing of the T-cell response.

The first combination tested seriously was ipilimumab plus nivolumab — ipi-nivo, in clinical shorthand. The trial, CheckMate 067, enrolled patients with advanced melanoma and randomized them to nivo alone, ipi alone, or the combination. It was published in 2015 and has been updated repeatedly since.

\[Pause.\]

The headline result: in melanoma, the combination produces an objective response rate of around fifty-eight percent, versus forty-five percent for nivo alone and nineteen percent for ipi alone. Ten-year overall survival, in the most recent follow-up: forty-three percent on the combination, forty-three percent on nivo alone, and thirty-three percent on ipi alone. The combination is the most active regimen ever tested in advanced melanoma. Patients with brain metastases — historically a terrible prognosis — have particularly striking responses on ipi-nivo, with intracranial response rates over fifty percent.

So the upside is real. The combination is more efficacious than either monotherapy.

But the cost. The cost is enormous. Severe immune-related adverse events on ipi-nivo occur in about fifty-five percent of patients — fifty-five percent, not five — versus around twenty percent on ipi alone and ten to fifteen percent on nivo alone. Severe colitis, severe hepatitis, severe pneumonitis. Hospital admissions. Steroid courses lasting months. A small but non-trivial fraction of patients die of the toxicity. About a third of patients discontinue therapy because of side effects.

So ipi-nivo is a high-stakes regimen. It is used in settings where the cancer is aggressive enough to justify the risk: advanced melanoma, especially with brain mets; high-risk renal cell carcinoma; mesothelioma; some lung cancers; some hepatocellular carcinomas. It is given in tertiary cancer centers by oncologists who are experienced at managing the side effects. It is not a casual prescription.

\[Pause.\]

A more recent variation is **nivolumab plus relatlimab** — relatlimab being an anti-LAG-3 antibody, a third checkpoint we'll discuss in Segment 16. This combination, approved in 2022 for melanoma under the brand name Opdualag, has shown improved progression-free survival over nivo alone, with toxicity that is more like nivo-monotherapy than ipi-nivo. So it's a gentler combination, with smaller efficacy gains, and it's been positioned as an alternative when ipi-nivo is too toxic for a given patient.

The broader principle here is one we'll come back to. **Combination immunotherapy is the future, but the toxicity grows faster than the efficacy.** Every time you cut another brake, you get more T-cell activation against the tumor, _and_ more T-cell activation against the patient. The therapeutic window narrows. Finding combinations that improve efficacy without unmanageable toxicity is one of the central engineering problems of the field.

For now, on combination: ipi-nivo works, it's more toxic, it's reserved for high-stakes situations, and it teaches us that the brakes are additive — that releasing one is good, releasing two is better, but the patient pays the price.

## SEGMENT 9 — The biomarker problem: PD-L1, TMB, and MSI

We have arrived at the question that the field cannot stop arguing about. **Who responds to checkpoint inhibitors?**

Because here is the uncomfortable fact. For all the magic of anti-PD-1 therapy, only twenty to forty percent of patients with most cancers actually respond. The other sixty to eighty percent get nothing, or worse — they get toxicity without benefit. Given that these drugs cost over a hundred and fifty thousand dollars per patient per year, and given that patients with advanced cancer have limited time, the question of who to treat is not academic. It is life-and-death and it is billions of dollars and it is fundamentally unsolved.

What we have is a handful of imperfect biomarkers. Let me walk through the three main ones.

\[Pause.\]

**Biomarker one: PD-L1 expression on the tumor, measured by immunohistochemistry.** This is the most widely used. The pathologist takes a biopsy of the tumor, stains it with an antibody against PD-L1, and counts what fraction of tumor cells — or sometimes tumor cells plus immune cells — stain positive. The result is reported as a percentage, often using a metric called the Tumor Proportion Score (TPS) or the Combined Positive Score (CPS).

The logic is intuitive. If a tumor expresses lots of PD-L1, it's actively using the PD-1/PD-L1 brake. Blocking that brake should help. If a tumor doesn't express PD-L1, the brake isn't being pressed, and the drug has nothing to do.

In practice, PD-L1 is a flawed biomarker. It's useful — patients with high PD-L1 expression are more likely to respond on average — but the correlation is fuzzy. Some patients with PD-L1-negative tumors respond. Some patients with PD-L1-very-high tumors don't respond. The assays differ between manufacturers — there are at least four different antibody clones used clinically, each with different cutoffs, each with different scoring rules. PD-L1 expression is heterogeneous within a tumor, so a single biopsy might miss positive regions. And PD-L1 expression changes over time, especially under treatment. It's a snapshot biomarker for a moving target.

Still, in some indications — notably first-line lung cancer — PD-L1 expression is the gatekeeper. Pembrolizumab monotherapy is approved for advanced non-small-cell lung cancer only in patients with TPS greater than or equal to fifty percent. Below that threshold, you need to add chemotherapy. The biomarker drives the regimen.

**Biomarker two: tumor mutational burden, or TMB.** TMB is, basically, a count of how many mutations the tumor has. You sequence the tumor's exome and count the non-synonymous mutations per megabase of DNA. Cancers with more mutations have more neoantigens — more weird peptides for T cells to recognize — and so, in principle, more material for the immune system to attack.

TMB does correlate with response to checkpoint inhibitors. Melanoma and lung cancer, the two great success stories of immunotherapy, are among the most highly mutated cancers — because melanoma is driven by UV-induced DNA damage and lung cancer by tobacco-induced damage, both of which generate enormous mutational loads. Pancreatic cancer and prostate cancer, by contrast, have low TMB, and they respond poorly to immunotherapy.

In 2020, the FDA approved pembrolizumab for any solid tumor with high TMB — defined as ten or more mutations per megabase — based on the KEYNOTE-158 trial. It was the second tumor-agnostic approval (we'll get to the first in a moment). But TMB has been controversial as a biomarker. The cutoff is somewhat arbitrary. Different sequencing panels give different TMB numbers. And not every high-TMB tumor responds. It's a better-than-nothing biomarker, not a perfect one.

**Biomarker three: microsatellite instability, MSI, or its molecular cousin mismatch-repair deficiency, dMMR.** This is the one biomarker that approaches predictive perfection, in the cancers where it applies.

Microsatellites are short, repetitive DNA sequences scattered across the genome — things like "CACACACA..." or "GTGTGTGT..." When DNA replicates, these repetitive sequences are easy to misreplicate; the polymerase slips and adds or drops a repeat unit. Normally, a system called mismatch repair catches these slips and fixes them. If mismatch repair is broken — if the genes MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, or PMS2 are mutated or epigenetically silenced — then the slips accumulate, and you get microsatellite instability. The microsatellites in the tumor's DNA have different lengths than in the patient's normal DNA. You can detect this by PCR or by sequencing.

Tumors with MSI-high status — about fifteen percent of colorectal cancers, a smaller fraction of many other cancers — have absolutely enormous mutational burdens, often hundreds of mutations per megabase. They generate vast neoantigen repertoires. They are highly immunogenic. And — this is the punchline — they respond to PD-1 blockade at extraordinary rates. Forty to sixty percent objective response rates, with durable responses, in cancers that would otherwise be chemorefractory and lethal.

\[Pause.\]

In 2017, based primarily on the work of Dung Le and Luis Diaz at Johns Hopkins, the FDA approved pembrolizumab for any unresectable or metastatic MSI-high or dMMR solid tumor that had progressed on prior therapy. _Any_ such tumor. Colon, endometrial, gastric, small bowel, pancreatic, prostate, bladder, anything. It was the first time the FDA had ever approved a cancer drug based purely on a molecular biomarker, regardless of the organ of origin. The first **tumor-agnostic approval** in oncology.

This was a tectonic shift. For its entire history, oncology had organized itself by the organ of origin. There were breast cancer doctors and lung cancer doctors and colon cancer doctors. Drugs were approved for breast cancer or lung cancer or colon cancer. The new approval said: forget the organ. If the tumor has this molecular feature — MSI-high — give it pembro. The molecule, not the organ, defines the treatment.

We are going to spend the next segment on this idea, because it is one of the most important conceptual shifts in modern medicine.

For now: PD-L1, TMB, and MSI are the three main biomarkers for checkpoint inhibitor response. PD-L1 is widely used but imperfect. TMB is useful but messy. MSI is the cleanest signal we have. And the cleanest signal is the one that gave us the first tumor-agnostic drug approval, which is where we'll go after the break.

\[Pause.\]

## SEGMENT 10 — MSI-high and the first tumor-agnostic approval

Welcome back. Let me pick up where we left off, because the story of MSI-high cancers and the first tumor-agnostic approval deserves its own segment.

The tumor-agnostic approval — pembrolizumab for any MSI-high or dMMR solid tumor — happened in May 2017. To understand why this was such a big deal, you have to understand how oncology had organized itself for the previous hundred years.

Cancer drugs had always been approved organ-by-organ. Tamoxifen was a breast cancer drug. Imatinib was a chronic myeloid leukemia drug, then later a GIST drug — but those were separate approvals, separate trials, separate indications. Each new tumor type required its own pivotal trial. If you had a rare cancer, even one that was molecularly similar to a common cancer, you often had no approved therapy because nobody had done a trial in your tumor.

The MSI-high approval shattered that paradigm. The FDA said, in effect: we are approving this drug not for a tumor type but for a molecular feature that crosses tumor types. If your cancer is MSI-high, regardless of whether it's in your colon, your uterus, your pancreas, your stomach, or your prostate, you qualify for pembrolizumab.

\[Pause.\]

Why MSI? The answer goes back to the biology.

A tumor with broken mismatch repair accumulates mutations at an extraordinary rate. The genome becomes a snowstorm of small insertions, deletions, and substitutions. Many of those mutations alter protein sequences. Each altered protein gets chopped up and displayed on MHC. The tumor becomes covered in neoantigens — strange peptides the T cells have never seen before, and were never trained to tolerate.

In an immunocompetent host, those neoantigens are enormous red flags. T cells recognize them, infiltrate the tumor, attempt to destroy it. And the tumor, in classic fashion, defends itself by upregulating PD-L1 and other suppressive signals. The T cells are present, but suppressed.

This is the perfect setup for a PD-1 inhibitor. There is an enormous, already-primed T-cell army parked inside the tumor. All you have to do is take off the brakes, and the army does the rest.

The clinical results, when this was tested, were extraordinary. In Dung Le's first major report, in 2015, about half of MSI-high colorectal cancer patients had objective responses to pembrolizumab. In the broader basket trial that supported the 2017 approval, response rates across thirty-plus different tumor types were in the thirty-to-fifty percent range. Some patients had complete responses lasting years. In a population where the alternative was chemotherapy with a median survival of a year or two, this was transformative.

\[Pause.\]

A few practical points worth knowing.

First, **MSI-high is over-represented in certain hereditary cancer syndromes.** Lynch syndrome — the most common hereditary colon cancer predisposition — is caused by germline mutations in the mismatch repair genes. About three percent of all colorectal cancers and two percent of all endometrial cancers are Lynch syndrome cancers, all of them MSI-high. People with Lynch syndrome are now routinely tested, and their cancers are immunotherapy-responsive in a way that was unimaginable a decade ago.

Second, **the MSI-high signal extends to early-stage disease.** In 2022, a small but landmark study by Andrea Cercek and colleagues at Memorial Sloan Kettering treated patients with locally advanced MSI-high rectal cancer with dostarlimab — another anti-PD-1 antibody — for six months. All twelve initial patients had complete responses. _Every single one._ No surgery, no chemoradiation, just six months of an immune checkpoint inhibitor, and the tumors disappeared. The trial has been expanded and the results continue to hold. We are looking at the possibility that, for MSI-high rectal cancer, surgery and radiation may no longer be necessary at all. The immunotherapy is curative on its own.

Third, **the principle has been extended.** Following the MSI-high precedent, the FDA has approved several other tumor-agnostic indications — TMB-high tumors for pembrolizumab, NTRK fusions for larotrectinib and entrectinib, BRAF V600E for dabrafenib-trametinib, RET fusions for selpercatinib. Each of these approvals says: the genetic signature defines the treatment, not the tissue of origin. We are entering an era in which a patient's cancer is increasingly defined by its molecular fingerprint, and treatment follows the fingerprint wherever it leads.

The MSI-high story is the cleanest example of this shift, and it is one of the proudest moments of the modern era of cancer therapy. A biology insight — broken mismatch repair generates neoantigens, neoantigens prime T cells, T cells are released by PD-1 blockade — has produced cures in cancers that were previously incurable. And it has dragged the entire FDA regulatory framework into a new way of thinking.

## SEGMENT 11 — The economics of checkpoint inhibitors

Let's pause, briefly, on something less biological and more political. The money.

Pembrolizumab, in 2024, generated approximately twenty-nine and a half billion dollars in global revenue. Twenty-nine point five billion. That is more revenue than Coca-Cola or Pepsi makes from any single product line. It is the single most lucrative pharmaceutical product in human history. And it shows no sign of slowing — analysts project Keytruda revenue to crest at over thirty billion dollars before patent expiry in 2028.

How does one drug make twenty-nine billion dollars in a year? Volume and price.

\[Pause.\]

The price, in the United States, is approximately one hundred and seventy-five thousand dollars per patient per year for the standard dosing schedule. That figure varies by country — the same drug costs about half as much in Western Europe, less in Japan, and much less in low- and middle-income countries through tiered pricing arrangements — but in the U.S. it is, broadly, that range. And patients are now treated for one or two years routinely; some indications use it for life. So we are talking about three hundred and fifty thousand dollars in drug costs for a single course of therapy for a single patient.

The volume is also vast. As of 2024, more than three million patients worldwide have received pembrolizumab. It is approved for use across more than forty cancer types and in dozens of distinct settings — first-line, second-line, adjuvant, neoadjuvant, monotherapy, combinations. The drug has crept into so many corners of oncology that the average comprehensive cancer center now uses it daily.

The economic implications are enormous, and not all of them are good.

\[Pause.\]

The good first. The revenue has funded a colossal expansion of oncology research. Merck has plowed Keytruda profits into acquisitions, partnerships, and internal R&D. Every other major pharmaceutical company, seeing the success, has built or bought immuno-oncology platforms. The total annual R&D spend on cancer immunotherapy across the industry is now north of fifteen billion dollars. Hundreds of new combinations and novel targets are in clinical trials. Whatever you think about pharmaceutical industry margins, the revenue from checkpoint inhibitors has been re-invested into the science, and that re-investment has accelerated the field dramatically.

Now the less good. Health systems, particularly in the United States, are buckling under the cost. Medicare's drug spending on cancer immunotherapies is increasing by double-digit percentages annually. Private insurers are constraining access, requiring prior authorization, narrowing approved indications. Patients with high copays are facing six-figure out-of-pocket costs that drive them into bankruptcy. In countries with national health systems, like the UK's NICE, expensive checkpoint inhibitors are approved only for specific indications where cost-effectiveness can be demonstrated — and patients with off-label indications often cannot access them.

The cost is also distributed unequally. In high-income countries, pembrolizumab is widely available. In low- and middle-income countries, it remains largely inaccessible, even when the cancers it treats are common. A patient with MSI-high colorectal cancer in the Bronx and a patient with the same cancer in Lagos do not have the same options.

\[Pause.\]

And then there is the patent cliff. The composition-of-matter patent for pembrolizumab is currently expected to expire in 2028. After that — or after various extension strategies run their course — biosimilar versions of the drug will enter the market, and prices will fall, possibly by sixty to eighty percent. Merck's revenue from Keytruda will collapse. The whole company will need to absorb a multi-hundred-billion-dollar revenue shortfall over the subsequent decade.

That cliff is driving the most frantic dealmaking in pharmaceutical history. Merck has made several large acquisitions specifically to acquire next-generation immuno-oncology assets. They are paying enormous sums for late-stage assets, betting that the next wave — bispecifics, neoantigen vaccines, novel targets — will replace at least some of the lost Keytruda revenue.

Whether they succeed is one of the most-watched commercial questions in biotech. The science is racing the clock.

The takeaway for our purposes: checkpoint inhibitors are not only a scientific revolution but an economic one. They have made certain cancers more treatable and certain cancers more expensive than ever. They are paying for the science that will produce the next generation of therapies. And they are, all by themselves, reshaping the financial structure of the pharmaceutical industry.

## SEGMENT 12 — Immune-related adverse events: the dark side of brake-cutting

Now let me get serious about toxicity, because we have skimmed it and it deserves its own segment.

When you remove the brakes on T cells systemically, the T cells you unleash do not only attack the tumor. They attack, in some patients, more or less anything. The immune-related adverse events, the irAEs, can in principle hit any organ. They are protean — meaning they take many shapes, change between patients, surprise even experienced clinicians. They are, as a class, the dark side of checkpoint inhibition.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the catalog, organ by organ.

**Skin.** Most common. Rashes, pruritus, vitiligo, sometimes severe forms like Stevens-Johnson syndrome. Vitiligo, where T cells attack melanocytes and destroy pigment, is interesting because in melanoma patients it correlates with response — the T cells that are killing the cancer's melanocytes are also killing the patient's healthy ones. Some patients develop blotchy white patches across their face and trunk that are, in their own way, a sign that the drug is working.

**Colon.** Colitis. Diarrhea, abdominal pain, bloody stools. In severe cases, perforation. This is one of the most common reasons patients are hospitalized on these drugs. Treatment is high-dose steroids, sometimes infliximab — yes, the same anti-TNF antibody used for inflammatory bowel disease — and sometimes permanent discontinuation of the immunotherapy.

**Liver.** Hepatitis. Elevated liver enzymes, sometimes massive, requiring drug discontinuation and steroids. Most cases resolve, but some leave permanent injury.

**Lungs.** Pneumonitis. Inflammation of lung tissue. Cough, shortness of breath, hypoxia. CT scans show ground-glass infiltrates. Severe pneumonitis can be fatal, particularly in patients with underlying lung disease, which is a lot of lung-cancer patients. Pneumonitis is one of the most feared toxicities in lung cancer immunotherapy.

**Endocrine system.** Thyroiditis — leading often to permanent hypothyroidism. Hypophysitis — inflammation of the pituitary gland, sometimes causing permanent hormonal deficiencies. Adrenalitis — Type 1 diabetes, occasionally, particularly with combination therapy. The endocrinopathies are notable because they are often permanent. A patient who develops checkpoint-inhibitor-induced hypothyroidism takes thyroid replacement for the rest of their life. The brake was cut, the gland was destroyed, the gland does not come back.

**Heart.** Myocarditis. Rare — perhaps one to two percent of patients — but often catastrophic. T cells infiltrate the heart muscle, the heart fails, the patient dies. Mortality from checkpoint-inhibitor myocarditis, when it happens, is around fifty percent. It is the single most-feared rare toxicity.

**Nervous system.** Encephalitis, myasthenia gravis, Guillain-Barre-like syndromes, peripheral neuropathies. Rare but serious.

**Joints, muscles, kidneys, eyes, blood cells.** The list goes on. Pretty much any organ that has a tissue antigen that can be presented to T cells can become a target.

\[Pause.\]

A few important features of irAEs.

First, **they can occur late.** Most adverse drug reactions you learn about in pharmacology have a clear temporal relationship with the drug. You take the drug, the side effect happens, you stop the drug, the side effect resolves. Not so with checkpoint inhibitors. irAEs can develop months after the drug has been stopped. The immune memory persists. A patient who stopped pembrolizumab two years ago can still present, this week, with pembrolizumab-induced colitis. The drug is gone but the unleashed T cells remain.

Second, **the management requires immunosuppression.** Severe irAEs are treated with high-dose corticosteroids, often for weeks or months, and refractory cases are escalated to other immunosuppressants — infliximab, mycophenolate, sometimes more aggressive agents. There is a long-running concern that immunosuppression might also dampen the anti-tumor response. The data so far suggest that short-course steroids don't significantly reduce efficacy, but the question is real.

Third, **irAEs may correlate with efficacy.** Patients who develop irAEs tend, on average, to do better against their cancer than patients who don't. The same immune activation that's destroying the cancer is also destroying healthy tissue; if you don't see the collateral damage, maybe you're also not seeing the tumor damage. This is a population-level observation; individual patients vary enormously.

Fourth, **patients with pre-existing autoimmunity present a special problem.** People with rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, ulcerative colitis, multiple sclerosis — they have, in some sense, T cells already inclined to attack their own tissues. Giving them a checkpoint inhibitor risks catastrophic flares. These patients are often excluded from clinical trials, and in clinical practice they are treated cautiously, if at all. We do not yet have a great answer for how to extend the benefits of immunotherapy to patients with autoimmune comorbidities.

\[Pause.\]

The bottom line. Checkpoint inhibitors are powerful drugs, and the power comes with a price. The price is, in essence, the cost of cutting brakes that were installed for a reason. Most patients tolerate the drugs well. A meaningful minority do not. A small but real number die of the toxicity. Managing irAEs has become a clinical subspecialty of its own — there are now consensus guidelines, dedicated immunotoxicity clinics, specialized teams at every comprehensive cancer center.

This is the dark side of the revolution. It is real. It is manageable in most cases. It is occasionally devastating. And it is the price we pay for the durable remissions on the other side of the ledger.

## SEGMENT 13 — Primary versus acquired resistance

We talked about the patients who respond. Now let's talk about the patients who don't, and the patients who initially do and then stop. Resistance to checkpoint inhibitors is one of the central problems of the field, and like resistance to any cancer therapy, it comes in two flavors: primary and acquired.

**Primary resistance** is when a patient is given the drug and never responds. The tumor grows from the first dose. About sixty percent of patients across most indications have primary resistance — they get no benefit at all. We've discussed many of the reasons: low tumor mutational burden, no T-cell infiltrate, loss of MHC class I, an immunosuppressive microenvironment. Each of these is a way the cancer was never vulnerable to checkpoint blockade in the first place.

**Acquired resistance** is more poignant. A patient responds. The tumor shrinks. They feel better. Then, months or years later, the cancer comes back. Sometimes in the same place; sometimes in a new place. And often, when you biopsy the recurrent tumor, you find that it has changed. It is no longer the same tumor that responded.

\[Pause.\]

The known mechanisms of acquired resistance are a rogues' gallery of clever cellular tricks.

Some tumors **lose MHC class I expression.** They downregulate the antigen-presentation machinery so that even T cells whose brakes have been released can no longer see the cancer cells. The white flag is no longer up, but neither is the slideshow. The T cell walks past a blank wall.

Some tumors **mutate beta-2 microglobulin**, a key component of MHC class I. Same effect — no antigen presentation, no T-cell recognition.

Some tumors **lose interferon-gamma signaling.** Interferon-gamma is the cytokine that T cells secrete when they're killing, and it has many effects, including upregulating MHC on neighboring cells. Tumors that mutate the interferon-gamma receptor or its downstream signaling become unable to respond to T-cell signals, and they become invisible.

Some tumors **upregulate other checkpoints.** When you block PD-1, the tumor may compensate by displaying ligands for TIM-3, LAG-3, TIGIT, or other inhibitory receptors. The T cells are released from the PD-1 brake but immediately captured by another brake.

Some tumors **recruit more immunosuppressive cells.** Regulatory T cells, myeloid-derived suppressor cells, tumor-associated macrophages — all of these can be upregulated to suppress the responding T cells in new ways.

And some tumors simply **lose the neoantigens.** Tumors are heterogeneous; the cells that get killed are the ones that express the immunogenic peptides; the cells that survive and grow are the ones that, by mutation or epigenetic silencing, no longer express them. It is Darwinian selection inside the tumor, with the immune system as the selector. The cancer evolves to escape, exactly as it evolved to escape every other treatment we've thrown at it.

\[Pause.\]

The implication is that no single drug, no matter how powerful, is going to be enough. Even pembrolizumab, with its long, durable responses, eventually loses some patients to acquired resistance. We have to think in terms of combinations, sequencing, and rational selection.

This is also why understanding resistance mechanisms is one of the hottest research areas in immuno-oncology. If we can predict which resistance mechanism a given patient is going to develop, we can pre-empt it — combine a second drug from the start that addresses the predicted escape route. If we can detect acquired resistance early — through circulating tumor DNA, for example, or imaging biomarkers — we can switch therapies before clinical progression. The field is moving toward a more dynamic, longitudinal view of immune-tumor evolution.

Resistance is, in some ways, the central problem of the next decade. Checkpoint inhibitors got us into the room. They have not, by themselves, solved cancer. Most patients still progress. The question now is what we do about it.

## SEGMENT 14 — The cold-tumor problem

Now we come to a topic that haunts the field: the cold-tumor problem.

I've been talking, for most of this lecture, as if every tumor has T cells inside it that have been suppressed and are waiting to be unleashed. That is true for some tumors. It is decidedly not true for others.

The vocabulary of the field, somewhat unfortunately, divides tumors into **hot** and **cold**. A hot tumor is one that has been heavily infiltrated by T cells — the immune system has noticed it, marched in, and is engaged in a battle that the tumor is currently winning. Checkpoint inhibitors work in hot tumors because they tip the scales of an ongoing fight. A cold tumor is one that the immune system has, for whatever reason, never noticed or never reached. There are no T cells inside. The tumor is, immunologically, alone.

You can have all the checkpoint inhibitors in the world, you can release every brake in existence, but if there is no T cell present to release, the drug does nothing.

\[Pause.\]

Which cancers are hot? Melanoma, lung cancer, kidney cancer, bladder cancer, Hodgkin lymphoma, MSI-high tumors, head and neck cancer. Many of these are tumors driven by mutagenic exposures — UV light, tobacco smoke — that generate enormous mutational burdens and lots of neoantigens. Or they have other features, like the immune-rich microenvironment of Hodgkin lymphoma, that draw T cells in.

Which cancers are cold? Pancreatic adenocarcinoma. Prostate cancer. Most glioblastoma. Ovarian cancer. Microsatellite-stable colorectal cancer — which is most colorectal cancer. Many breast cancers, particularly hormone-receptor-positive breast cancers. These tumors have low mutational burdens. They sit in tissues that may have intrinsic immunosuppression. They recruit suppressive myeloid cells. They put up barriers — physical barriers, like the dense fibrotic stroma of pancreatic cancer — that exclude T cells from ever reaching the tumor cells.

The clinical consequence is brutal. Pancreatic cancer remains essentially incurable in the metastatic setting. Five-year survival for metastatic pancreatic cancer is single digits. Multiple large checkpoint-inhibitor trials in pancreatic cancer have failed. The drugs that have rewritten the survival curves for melanoma and lung cancer have done almost nothing for pancreatic cancer. Same for most glioblastoma. Same for hormone-receptor-positive breast cancer. The biology is the constraint; no amount of cleverness in drug design can rescue a tumor that has no T cells inside it.

\[Pause.\]

So the central question for cold tumors is: how do we make them hot? How do we get T cells to recognize and infiltrate a tumor that has been ignoring the immune system?

There are several strategies under active investigation.

One: **deliver antigen to the tumor directly.** Inject oncolytic viruses, which infect tumor cells and cause them to release their contents in a way the immune system can recognize. Talimogene laherparepvec — T-VEC — is an approved oncolytic herpes virus for melanoma, and it works in part by making cold lesions hot. Many similar viruses are in development.

Two: **prime T cells outside the tumor with vaccines.** If the immune system has never seen the tumor's antigens, give the patient a vaccine that contains those antigens — mRNA-encoded neoantigens, dendritic-cell vaccines, peptide vaccines. We'll come back to this in Segment 17.

Three: **destroy the physical barriers.** Pancreatic cancer's stroma can be partly broken down by drugs like hyaluronidase, which has been combined with chemotherapy in trials. Anti-angiogenic drugs can normalize the tumor vasculature and allow T-cell infiltration.

Four: **use radiation to spark an immune response.** Radiation kills tumor cells in a way that releases tumor antigens and inflammatory signals — the so-called "in situ vaccination" effect. Combining local radiation with systemic checkpoint inhibitors is an active area of investigation, with some striking case reports of the so-called "abscopal effect" — where radiation to one tumor lesion shrinks tumors elsewhere in the body, presumably through systemic immune activation.

Five: **adoptive cell transfer.** If the patient doesn't have T cells that can find the tumor, manufacture them. Take T cells out of the patient, engineer them to recognize tumor antigens — CAR-T cells, TCR-T cells, TIL therapy — and reinfuse them. This is a major topic in its own right, and one of the most active areas of cell therapy development.

\[Pause.\]

None of these strategies has yet cracked the cold-tumor problem. Pancreatic cancer remains the hardest to crack, and despite hundreds of trials testing checkpoint inhibitors plus this, plus that, plus the other thing, no combination has produced a meaningful survival improvement in the metastatic setting.

But the field is, methodically, working through every plausible angle. And every few years, a previously cold cancer turns out to have a hot subset — like MSI-high colorectal cancer turning out to be hot, while microsatellite-stable colorectal cancer remains cold. The boundaries are not static. Biology keeps surprising us.

The takeaway. Checkpoint inhibitors are not, by themselves, the answer to cold tumors. They work on tumors where T cells are already present. For cold tumors, we need to find ways to get T cells in first, and only then release the brakes. The next wave of immuno-oncology is in large part about that two-step problem: heat the tumor up, then release the brakes.

## SEGMENT 15 — Combinations: building on the checkpoint foundation

If checkpoint inhibitors are the foundation, what gets built on top? Almost everything.

Modern oncology, in cancers where checkpoint inhibitors work at all, is increasingly a combination game. Pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy. Pembrolizumab plus anti-angiogenic. Pembrolizumab plus radiation. Pembrolizumab plus another checkpoint inhibitor. Pembrolizumab plus a personalized vaccine. The drug has become a platform, and the field is exploring every possible thing to combine with it.

Let me walk through the major combination categories.

\[Pause.\]

**Chemo plus checkpoint.** This is, by volume, the most important combination in current clinical practice. For first-line non-small-cell lung cancer in patients with low or intermediate PD-L1, the standard of care is pembrolizumab plus platinum-based chemotherapy. The chemotherapy kills tumor cells in a way that releases antigen and stimulates immune recognition; the checkpoint inhibitor amplifies and prolongs the resulting T-cell response. The KEYNOTE-189 trial established this approach in non-squamous lung cancer in 2018, with median overall survival improvements of around eleven months over chemo alone. Similar combinations are now standard in triple-negative breast cancer, small-cell lung cancer, esophageal cancer, gastric cancer, biliary tract cancer, and others. Chemo-plus-checkpoint is, at this point, the workhorse regimen of modern oncology in many settings.

**Radiation plus checkpoint.** Radiation, as I mentioned, can spark immune activation. Combining local radiation with systemic checkpoint inhibitors has been tested extensively, with mixed but interesting results. The PACIFIC trial, already discussed, established consolidation durvalumab after chemoradiation in stage III lung cancer as a standard of care. In some smaller studies, radiation has produced abscopal effects when combined with checkpoint inhibitors — distant tumors regressing in addition to the irradiated one. The mechanism is presumably immune-mediated, but it remains rare and unpredictable. Active trials are exploring how to make abscopal effects more reliable.

**Anti-angiogenic plus checkpoint.** Many tumors recruit aberrant blood vessels that not only feed the tumor but also exclude T cells. Anti-angiogenic drugs — bevacizumab against VEGF, or VEGF-receptor tyrosine kinase inhibitors like axitinib, lenvatinib, cabozantinib — normalize the vasculature and improve T-cell access. Combinations of pembrolizumab plus axitinib or lenvatinib are now standard in advanced renal cell carcinoma and endometrial cancer. Atezolizumab plus bevacizumab is the standard front-line therapy for advanced hepatocellular carcinoma. The principle is consistent: dismantle the tumor's vascular barrier, then release the immune brake.

**Vaccine plus checkpoint.** This is a frontier I'll spend more time on in Segment 17. The idea is that a personalized vaccine generates new T cells against tumor neoantigens, and the checkpoint inhibitor lets those new T cells do their work. Preliminary data from melanoma trials of mRNA neoantigen vaccines plus pembrolizumab are extremely encouraging, and we are in the midst of large Phase 3 trials.

**Targeted therapy plus checkpoint.** Drugs that block specific cancer-driving mutations — BRAF inhibitors in melanoma, EGFR inhibitors in lung cancer — sometimes synergize with checkpoint inhibitors. Sometimes they don't. The interactions are complex, and the combinations are an area of active investigation.

\[Pause.\]

A few principles emerge from the combination story.

First, **combinations are usually more toxic.** Every drug brings its own side-effect profile, and combinations stack them. Combinations of chemo plus immunotherapy mean both chemo toxicities and immune toxicities; combinations of two checkpoint inhibitors mean more immune toxicities, sometimes catastrophically more. The therapeutic ratio narrows.

Second, **sequencing may matter as much as combination.** Giving drug A then drug B is not always the same as giving drug B then drug A, and the optimal sequence depends on biology that is often only partly understood. Clinical trials testing sequencing questions are slow and expensive, and many sequences remain unstudied.

Third, **the right combination is increasingly biomarker-driven.** A patient with high PD-L1 and high TMB might do well with checkpoint monotherapy; a patient with low PD-L1 might need chemotherapy added; a patient with specific resistance markers might benefit from a non-PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor in combination. The era of one-size-fits-all is fading. The era of biomarker-driven combination selection is upon us.

Pembrolizumab and nivolumab will, almost certainly, end up in more combinations over the next decade. The pipeline is enormous. The drug class is the foundation; everything else is, in some sense, scaffolding to extend and refine the immune response it unleashes.

## SEGMENT 16 — Novel checkpoints: LAG-3, TIGIT, TIM-3, and beyond

Beyond CTLA-4 and PD-1, the immune system has more brakes. Many more. The field has, for the last decade, been hunting for the next checkpoint molecule that, when blocked, will unleash anti-tumor immunity in a new way. Some hunts have succeeded. Many have failed. Let me walk through the major ones.

\[Pause.\]

**LAG-3.** Lymphocyte-activation gene 3. LAG-3 was identified in the 1990s as a marker on exhausted T cells, and it functions, broadly, as another brake. It binds MHC class II and other ligands, and when engaged it dampens T-cell activation.

The clinical development of anti-LAG-3 antibodies took a long time and was punctuated by frustration. Multiple early trials of LAG-3 inhibitors as monotherapy showed only modest activity. The breakthrough came with **relatlimab**, an anti-LAG-3 antibody from BMS, tested in combination with nivolumab in the RELATIVITY-047 trial. The combination, known as Opdualag, was approved by the FDA in March 2022 for advanced melanoma. It produced improved progression-free survival over nivo alone, with toxicity that was — as I mentioned earlier — closer to nivo monotherapy than to ipi-nivo. It is a gentler, less efficacious alternative to ipi-nivo for melanoma. Multiple other LAG-3 inhibitors are in development, but Opdualag is the first.

LAG-3, in some sense, is the third Nobel-worthy checkpoint after CTLA-4 and PD-1. Whether the eventual prizes get given is for the Karolinska Institute to decide, but the molecule is now firmly in the clinic.

**TIGIT.** T-cell immunoreceptor with immunoglobulin and ITIM domains. TIGIT is another inhibitory receptor on T cells and NK cells. For several years, anti-TIGIT antibodies were the most-hyped new checkpoint in oncology, with multiple companies racing to develop them.

The hype crashed in 2024. Roche's Phase 3 trial of its anti-TIGIT antibody **tiragolumab** plus atezolizumab in lung cancer failed to meet its overall survival endpoint. Multiple other TIGIT programs reported disappointing data around the same time. The TIGIT story became, briefly, a cautionary tale about expecting too much from preclinical data and early-phase results. Some TIGIT programs continue — there are reasonable arguments that specific patient subsets, or specific antibody-engineering choices, might still produce wins. But the field, as of 2025, is much more skeptical than it was three years ago.

The TIGIT story is a useful reminder that not every checkpoint inhibitor works. The cell-biology principle — block a brake, release T cells — is necessary but not sufficient. Whether a specific brake matters in a specific clinical context depends on the brake, the cancer, the patient's immune state, and the combination. Generalization from CTLA-4 and PD-1 has limits.

**TIM-3.** T-cell immunoglobulin and mucin-domain containing-3. Another exhaustion marker, another inhibitory receptor. Multiple anti-TIM-3 antibodies are in clinical development. Results so far have been modest. The field is still waiting to see whether TIM-3 will produce a clinical winner.

**Other checkpoints.** The list keeps growing — VISTA, BTLA, B7-H3, BTN3A1, and others. Many are targets of antibodies or fusion proteins in early-phase trials. Some will succeed; most will fail. The pattern is that, just as CTLA-4 and PD-1 emerged from a much larger pool of candidate molecules, the next clinically important checkpoint will likely emerge from the current crowded pipeline through a combination of biology, luck, and design.

\[Pause.\]

The broader strategic question is whether we should be hunting for more checkpoints at all, or whether the remaining problems in checkpoint inhibition are better addressed through combinations, smarter biomarkers, and getting T cells into cold tumors in the first place. Different parts of the field bet on different answers. The truth is probably that all of these strategies will, eventually, contribute.

For now, LAG-3 is in the clinic with Opdualag. TIGIT is in retreat. TIM-3 and others are coming up the pipeline. The brakes are many, and the field is, brake by brake, working through them.

## SEGMENT 17 — Neoantigen vaccines plus checkpoint inhibitors

I want to spend this segment on one specific combination strategy, because I think it represents one of the most exciting near-term frontiers in cancer therapy: the combination of personalized cancer vaccines with checkpoint inhibitors.

Remember the scaffold. Checkpoint inhibitors work when T cells are already present in the tumor. If you could _make_ T cells present — generate, on demand, new T cells specifically targeting the patient's own tumor antigens — and then release the checkpoint brakes — you'd have a one-two punch that might extend the benefits of immunotherapy to many more patients, and might convert cold tumors into hot ones.

That is exactly what personalized neoantigen vaccines are designed to do.

\[Pause.\]

The mechanism is beautiful. You take a patient's tumor and sequence it. You compare the tumor's DNA to the patient's normal DNA. You identify the mutations that are unique to the tumor. You then use prediction algorithms to figure out which of those mutated proteins are most likely to be presented on the patient's specific MHC molecules — because every patient has different MHC alleles, and different alleles bind different peptides. You pick, say, twenty to thirty of the most promising predicted neoantigens. You synthesize an mRNA molecule that, when injected into the patient, encodes those neoantigens. The mRNA gets taken up by the patient's own dendritic cells, translated into protein, processed, displayed on MHC. T cells against those neoantigens get primed. The patient now has a T-cell army specifically targeting peptides that exist only in their own cancer.

Then, you give the patient pembrolizumab. The newly primed T cells, equipped with neoantigen-specific receptors, traffic to the tumor. They encounter the same mutated peptides being presented by tumor cells. They engage their targets. And the PD-1 blockade prevents the tumor from shutting them down.

\[Pause.\]

The lead clinical candidate, as of 2025, is **mRNA-4157**, also called **V940**, from Moderna and Merck. It's a personalized mRNA vaccine encoding up to thirty-four neoantigens per patient, manufactured individually based on each patient's tumor sequencing.

In a Phase 2 trial in melanoma, published in 2023, patients with high-risk resected melanoma were randomized to mRNA-4157 plus pembrolizumab versus pembrolizumab alone. The combination reduced the risk of recurrence or death by about forty-four percent compared to pembrolizumab alone. In a cancer where pembrolizumab monotherapy had already set the bar high, the vaccine combination raised it further.

Phase 3 trials in melanoma and lung cancer are ongoing as of 2025. If they confirm the Phase 2 results, mRNA-4157 plus pembrolizumab will become a new standard of care in adjuvant melanoma, and almost certainly the template for similar combinations in many other cancers.

\[Pause.\]

There are competitors. **BNT122**, from BioNTech and Genentech, is a similar personalized mRNA vaccine being tested in pancreatic cancer, colorectal cancer, and other settings. Early data in pancreatic cancer — historically one of the hardest cancers to crack with immunotherapy — have been provocative, with vaccine-induced T-cell responses correlating with longer recurrence-free survival. A randomized Phase 2 trial is reading out in the next year or two.

The mRNA-vaccine plus checkpoint combination is, in my view, the most exciting near-term development in immuno-oncology. It rests on technology — mRNA manufacturing, neoantigen prediction algorithms, individualized therapy delivery — that did not exist a decade ago. It potentially extends immunotherapy benefit to settings where current drugs do not work. And it is being driven not by speculation but by Phase 2 randomized data showing real benefit.

If the Phase 3 results hold up, we are looking at a future where every patient with high-risk solid cancer gets, as part of standard care, a personalized vaccine manufactured from their own tumor's mutations, paired with a checkpoint inhibitor. The vocabulary of cancer therapy will shift from "what kind of cancer do you have" to "what are your tumor's specific mutations and what vaccine do we need to make for you."

That is, in some sense, the realization of the personalized-medicine promise that has been advertised for thirty years. It took mRNA technology, neoantigen prediction, and checkpoint inhibitors to come together. They are coming together now.

## SEGMENT 18 — Frontier: the post-checkpoint era

Let me close with a look forward. Checkpoint inhibitors are the foundation. What is being built on top of that foundation? Where is the field going in the next ten or twenty years?

\[Pause.\]

**Bispecific checkpoint inhibitors.** Antibodies engineered to bind two targets at once — for example, both PD-1 and CTLA-4, or PD-1 and LAG-3. The hope is that a single molecule targeting two checkpoints simultaneously can deliver combination efficacy with potentially better safety, by ensuring that brake-cutting only happens where both targets are present. Several bispecifics are in late-stage development. **Cadonilimab**, from Akeso, is a PD-1/CTLA-4 bispecific that has been approved in China and is being tested globally. Others are coming.

A particular subtype, **PD-1/VEGF bispecifics**, like **ivonescimab**, has produced striking early data — head-to-head against pembrolizumab in lung cancer, ivonescimab improved progression-free survival meaningfully in a Chinese Phase 3. Confirmatory global trials are ongoing. If those results hold, ivonescimab could disrupt the entire first-line lung cancer market.

**Cytokine-based therapy.** Before checkpoint inhibitors, the original cancer immunotherapies were cytokines — interleukin-2 (IL-2) and interferon-alpha. They worked, occasionally, in melanoma and renal cell carcinoma. They were also extraordinarily toxic — high-dose IL-2 was given in ICUs, with significant treatment-related mortality. The toxicity made them unfeasible in most settings.

But the cytokine concept is being revived, with engineering. **Engineered IL-2 variants** — molecules that preferentially activate effector T cells rather than regulatory T cells, that have longer half-lives, that have tumor-targeted delivery — are in active development. **IL-15** and **IL-12**, two other immune-activating cytokines, are being similarly engineered. The hope is that the next-generation cytokines will have the efficacy of the originals without the toxicity. Combinations with checkpoint inhibitors are an obvious starting point.

**STING agonists.** STING — stimulator of interferon genes — is a pathway in immune cells that, when activated, triggers a type-I interferon response and drives T-cell infiltration into tumors. Small molecules that activate STING are an attractive way to heat up cold tumors. The clinical development has been bumpy — early agents had pharmacokinetic problems and disappointing trial results — but newer molecules and intratumoral delivery approaches are reopening the field.

**TGF-beta blockade.** TGF-beta is one of the major immunosuppressive cytokines in the tumor microenvironment. Drugs that block TGF-beta in combination with checkpoint inhibitors are being explored, with the rationale that lifting two layers of suppression simultaneously may work where one is not enough.

**CAR-T cells for solid tumors.** CAR-T cells — engineered T cells expressing chimeric antigen receptors — have transformed treatment of certain blood cancers, with multiple FDA-approved products for leukemia, lymphoma, and multiple myeloma. Extending CAR-T to solid tumors has been much harder, in part because solid tumors are immunosuppressive environments that exhaust the CAR-T cells quickly. Combinations of CAR-T with checkpoint inhibitors — to keep the CAR-T cells fresh — are an obvious experimental direction.

**TIL therapy.** Tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte therapy involves harvesting T cells from a patient's own tumor, expanding them outside the body, and reinfusing huge numbers of tumor-specific T cells. **Lifileucel** was approved by the FDA in 2024 for advanced melanoma, the first TIL therapy approval. It works in some patients who have failed checkpoint inhibitors, suggesting that — at least in melanoma — adoptive transfer can rescue some checkpoint-resistant disease.

\[Pause.\]

If I had to bet on which of these directions will pay off biggest in the next decade, I'd put my money on three things.

First, **mRNA neoantigen vaccines plus checkpoint inhibitors** will become standard of care in multiple high-risk adjuvant settings, particularly melanoma and lung cancer, and possibly colorectal and pancreatic.

Second, **bispecific antibodies targeting checkpoint plus angiogenesis** — PD-1/VEGF combinations like ivonescimab — will displace some of the current chemo-plus-pembro regimens, particularly in lung cancer.

Third, **TIL therapy and other adoptive cell approaches** will become real options for checkpoint-refractory patients, though they will remain expensive and centralized.

And one more, the wild card: **single-cell biology and AI-driven biomarker discovery** will, over the next ten years, give us much better tools for predicting who responds to which therapy. We will move from PD-L1 and TMB to integrated multi-omic biomarkers that capture immune state, tumor genotype, and microenvironment characteristics simultaneously. Treatment selection will become substantially more personalized, and response rates will improve as we stop giving drugs to patients who can't benefit from them.

\[Pause.\]

What we are not going to have, in the foreseeable future, is a single drug that cures all cancers. There is no such drug. There never will be. Cancer is too heterogeneous, too cleverly evolved, too varied across tissues and patients to surrender to any single molecule. What we will have is a richer and richer combinatorial toolkit — checkpoint inhibitors, vaccines, cytokines, CAR-Ts, targeted therapies, chemotherapy, radiation — and increasingly accurate ways of matching the right combination to the right patient.

The Allison-Honjo discoveries unlocked the room. The next two decades will be about filling the room with furniture.

\[Pause.\]

So let me close where we started. Twenty-five years ago, James Allison made an antibody against a brake on T cells and watched mice cure themselves of tumors. Thirty years ago, Tasuku Honjo cloned a gene from a dying T cell and spent the next decade figuring out it was a second brake. Today, the drugs that came out of those discoveries are in the IV bags of millions of patients worldwide, and the cancers that used to kill in months now, for many of those patients, can be turned into chronic conditions or, in the best cases, into cures.

We are living, in cancer therapy, in the post-checkpoint-inhibitor era. The brakes have been cut. The immune army has been unleashed. The questions that remain — who responds, how to extend the benefit, how to manage the toxicity, how to combine with everything else — are the questions that will define the next generation of oncology.

You are walking out of this lecture into a world where, increasingly, cancer is being treated by tuning your own immune system rather than poisoning your tumor. That is a revolution. It is one of the great achievements of modern biology. And it is, even now, only getting started.

Thank you.

\[Pause.\]

## APPENDIX — A reference table and a few resources

For your reference, here are the major checkpoint inhibitors discussed in this lecture, with target, brand name, year of first approval, and the indication that launched them.

**Ipilimumab** — anti-CTLA-4 — Yervoy — Bristol-Myers Squibb — 2011 — advanced melanoma. The first checkpoint inhibitor. High toxicity, durable responses in about 20% of patients.

**Pembrolizumab** — anti-PD-1 — Keytruda — Merck — 2014 — advanced melanoma. Now the world's best-selling drug, approved across more than 40 cancer types.

**Nivolumab** — anti-PD-1 — Opdivo — Bristol-Myers Squibb — 2014 — advanced melanoma. The second anti-PD-1; broadly similar to pembrolizumab in efficacy and indications.

**Atezolizumab** — anti-PD-L1 — Tecentriq — Genentech/Roche — 2016 — urothelial carcinoma, then non-small-cell lung cancer.

**Durvalumab** — anti-PD-L1 — Imfinzi — AstraZeneca — 2017 — urothelial carcinoma; landmark approval in stage III NSCLC consolidation (PACIFIC trial).

**Avelumab** — anti-PD-L1 — Bavencio — Pfizer/Merck KGaA — 2017 — Merkel cell carcinoma. Smaller indication footprint than its anti-PD-L1 siblings.

**Cemiplimab** — anti-PD-1 — Libtayo — Regeneron/Sanofi — 2018 — cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma. The third major anti-PD-1.

**Dostarlimab** — anti-PD-1 — Jemperli — GSK — 2021 — endometrial cancer; notable for the rectal cancer complete-response trial.

**Relatlimab** — anti-LAG-3 — combined with nivolumab as Opdualag — Bristol-Myers Squibb — 2022 — advanced melanoma. The first non-CTLA-4 non-PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor to reach the clinic.

**Tislelizumab, toripalimab, sintilimab, and others** — anti-PD-1 antibodies developed primarily in China, with growing global approvals. Increasingly important in the international market and likely to compete on price as biosimilars emerge.

A few suggested further-reading sources for the curious.

Siddhartha Mukherjee's _The Emperor of All Maladies_ (2010) — for the broader cultural history of cancer therapy, though written before the immunotherapy revolution fully arrived.

Charles Graeber's _The Breakthrough_ (2018) — a popular-press account specifically of the checkpoint-inhibitor story, with substantial material on Allison and on the early melanoma trials.

The 2018 Nobel Prize lectures by James Allison and Tasuku Honjo, available free online at nobelprize.org, are excellent primary sources and convey both the science and the personalities behind the discoveries.

For technical depth, the periodic Hallmarks-of-Cancer review papers by Hanahan and Weinberg, particularly the 2022 update incorporating "unlocking phenotypic plasticity" and "polymorphic microbiomes," provide an integrated framework into which immunotherapy fits.

And for the current state of the field, NCCN guidelines, the major oncology society publications (ASCO, ESMO), and clinicaltrials.gov searches for specific cancer types will give you up-to-date information on standards of care and active investigations.

That brings us to the end. Thank you for your attention through what has been, by any measure, a dense couple of hours. If you remember nothing else from today, remember the scaffold:

The immune system has brakes. Cancer presses them. We can release them. That release sometimes cures cancers that were previously incurable. The brakes are CTLA-4 and PD-1, mostly. The drugs are the "-mab" antibodies. The biomarkers are PD-L1, TMB, and MSI. The dark side is the immune-related adverse events. The frontier is combinations, vaccines, and the cold-tumor problem.

That's the lecture, in five sentences. The rest is detail. But the detail — as you have hopefully come to feel — is where the magic, and the medicine, lives.
