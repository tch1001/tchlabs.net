## SEGMENT 1 — Why adenovirus matters: the vector with the biggest history and the second life

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. Today and for the next two hours, we are going to talk about adenovirus — and more specifically, about adenovirus as a gene delivery vehicle. As a **vector**. And I want to tell you up front that of all the viral vectors we have in the modern toolkit — AAV, lentivirus, retrovirus, herpes, adenovirus — this one has by far the most complicated history. It is the vector that almost killed the entire field of gene therapy in 1999. It is also the vector that, twenty-one years later, was running in the arms of hundreds of millions of people as a COVID-19 vaccine. The breakthrough, the tragedy, the comeback. All in one virus.

So before we get into the molecular details, I want to give you the emotional shape of this story, because it's going to color everything we discuss today.

In the early 1990s, adenovirus was the great hope of gene therapy. It could be made at enormous titers. It carried a relatively large payload. It infected almost every human cell type you could throw at it. Researchers were lining up to use it for cystic fibrosis, for liver diseases, for cancer. And then, in September 1999, an eighteen-year-old named Jesse Gelsinger volunteered for a Phase I safety trial at the University of Pennsylvania. He had a partial deficiency in an enzyme called ornithine transcarbamylase — a disease he was managing reasonably well with diet. They infused him with an adenoviral vector carrying a corrected gene. Four days later, he was dead. Multi-organ failure, brain death, the lot. The vector did exactly what adenovirus does — it triggered an enormous innate immune response — and his body could not cope.

That single death froze adenoviral gene therapy in human patients for nearly a decade. Trials were halted. The whole field of gene therapy, not just adenoviral gene therapy, went into a long winter. And the people who had been most enthusiastic about adenovirus had to confront the fact that the very property that made the virus a great delivery vehicle — its ability to enter and express in essentially any cell — was inseparable from the immune fury it provoked.

And then — and this is the part that's almost novelistic — in 2020 and 2021, four of the major COVID-19 vaccines deployed worldwide were built on adenoviral vectors. Sputnik V in Russia. Johnson and Johnson's Janssen vaccine. The AstraZeneca-Oxford vaccine. CanSino in China. Hundreds of millions of doses. And the immune response that killed Jesse Gelsinger — that same property — was now the feature, not the bug. The whole point of a vaccine is to provoke an immune response. Adenovirus was now being used precisely because it shouted at the immune system.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy I want you to hold in your head for the next two hours. **Adenovirus is a delivery truck.** A big delivery truck. Much bigger payload bay than AAV — which is more like a delivery drone — and unlike lentivirus, the truck doesn't tear up the road by integrating into the host genome. It just drives in, dumps its cargo in the front yard, and the body eventually tows the truck away. That sounds great until you remember: a big armored truck rolling into a quiet neighborhood is **noisy**. People notice. The neighborhood watch comes out with binoculars. The police get called. That noise — that immune visibility — is the central design challenge of adenovirus as a vector. Every generation of engineering since 1990 has been an attempt to make the truck quieter without losing its carrying capacity.

So that's the arc. Big truck. Loud arrival. Tragic accident when the noise overwhelmed a patient. Then a long, careful redesign — quieter trucks, smaller fleets, different paint jobs. And finally, a stunning second act when the noise itself turned out to be useful for a different job entirely. Adenovirus didn't fail gene therapy. Adenovirus taught gene therapy how to be honest about immunity. And then it came back as one of the most successful vaccine platforms in history.

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, sketch the structure of an adenoviral particle and explain why its biology makes it both a powerful delivery system and a powerful immunogen; two, tell the Jesse Gelsinger story in your own words and explain what the field learned from it about dose, route, and pre-existing immunity; three, look at any modern adenoviral product — vaccine, gene therapy, oncolytic cancer drug — and have a rough sense of which generation of vector it's built on and why — then we've succeeded. Everything else hangs off those three.

\[Pause.\]

One more thing before we open up the molecular biology. Adenovirus is, on its own, a pretty boring respiratory bug. You have almost certainly had at least one adenoviral infection in your life. It's one of the viruses lumped under "the common cold." It can also give you pinkeye, or a stomach bug, or — in immunocompromised people — much worse. It was first isolated in 1953 from human adenoid tissue — the lymphoid tissue at the back of your throat — which is where it got its name. So for the first forty years after its discovery, adenovirus was studied by virologists who wanted to understand respiratory disease. Nobody thought of it as a tool.

That changed in the late 1980s and early 1990s, when molecular biologists realized they could **gut the virus** — delete its essential genes, replace them with a therapeutic payload, and grow the resulting crippled virus in special helper cell lines that supplied the missing functions. Suddenly, adenovirus stopped being a pathogen and started being a programmable cargo container. And that's when our story really begins. Now let's go meet the wild virus first, before we start cutting it up.

## SEGMENT 2 — Wild adenovirus: what it is and how it makes you sick

Let's start, the way we always should, with the natural history. What is adenovirus actually doing out in the world, before we got our hands on it?

Adenovirus is a family of viruses — the Adenoviridae — that infect vertebrates broadly. Birds, fish, reptiles, mammals — every major vertebrate lineage has its own adenoviruses. The ones that infect humans are called the human adenoviruses, or **HAdV**, and there are something like eighty-eight distinct types known so far, organized into seven species labeled A through G. Each type has a different surface protein profile, a slightly different tissue preference, a slightly different disease pattern. Most of them cause mild illness. A few can be nastier, particularly in babies and in immunocompromised people.

The bread-and-butter adenoviral diseases are respiratory. Sore throat, runny nose, cough, fever — a typical adenoviral upper respiratory infection is indistinguishable from any other common cold. In children especially, adenovirus is a major cause of pharyngitis. It can also cause **pinkeye** — viral conjunctivitis — and outbreaks of pinkeye in schools and military barracks are often traceable to a specific adenoviral type. There's a type, Ad14, that has caused some genuinely dangerous pneumonia outbreaks, particularly in military trainees in the US in the 2000s. There's a cluster of types that cause gastroenteritis — adenoviral diarrhea, usually in infants. And there are types that cause hemorrhagic cystitis in bone marrow transplant patients. The list goes on.

But for the majority of healthy adults, an adenoviral infection is unmemorable. You feel lousy for a few days, your immune system clears it, you make antibodies against the viral capsid proteins, and you go on with your life. Those antibodies stay with you — and this is going to matter enormously when we get to vaccines — because pre-existing immunity to common adenoviral types is the central problem of using those types as vectors.

\[Pause.\]

Let me put a number on the pre-existing immunity issue, because it shocks people the first time they hear it. The most-studied adenoviral type, the one most early vectors were built on, is **adenovirus type 5** — Ad5. It's a perfectly ordinary respiratory adenovirus. In adult populations in the United States and Europe, somewhere between forty and seventy percent of people have detectable neutralizing antibodies against Ad5. In some sub-Saharan African populations, the prevalence is over ninety percent. That means if you take an Ad5-based vector — a souped-up, payload-loaded version of Ad5 — and infuse it into a random adult patient, there's a very good chance their immune system already has antibodies waiting to neutralize the virus before it does anything useful. The vector gets chewed up at the front door. We'll come back to this in Segment 11; for now just lock in the number — most adults have already met Ad5.

\[Pause.\]

Now, what does wild adenovirus actually _do_ when it infects a cell? Here's the cycle, briefly. The virion — that's the technical term for a single virus particle — lands on a cell's surface. It binds, via a long protruding protein called the **fiber**, to a specific receptor on the cell membrane. For most adenoviral types, including Ad5, that receptor is called **CAR** — coxsackie and adenovirus receptor. CAR is a cell-cell adhesion molecule, normally involved in holding epithelial cells together in tight junctions. Adenovirus hijacked it as an entry door. Once the fiber is bound to CAR, a second viral protein at the corners of the capsid — the **penton base** — engages integrins on the cell surface, and the virus gets pulled inside through a process called clathrin-mediated endocytosis. Imagine the cell forming a little pocket around the virus and pinching it off into an internal vesicle.

Inside the cell, the virus escapes from that vesicle into the cytoplasm — a clever bit of molecular acrobatics involving the partial uncoating of the capsid — and then trundles along microtubules toward the nucleus. At the nuclear pore, the virus essentially injects its DNA genome through the pore into the nucleus. The capsid stays outside; the DNA goes in. Once inside the nucleus, the viral genome stays as an **episome** — a free-floating piece of DNA, not integrated into the host chromosome — and starts using the cell's transcription and translation machinery to make viral proteins. New capsids are assembled, new genomes are packaged, and eventually the cell lyses and thousands of new virions are released.

That whole cycle takes about a day and a half in a tissue culture cell. It's fast. It's efficient. And it leaves behind a host cell that has dedicated essentially all its machinery to making virus.

\[Pause.\]

The reason I'm walking you through this cycle in detail is that **every step is a design choice we can intervene on** when we engineer the virus into a vector. We can change the fiber to change what cells the virus binds to. We can delete the early viral genes that hijack the host machinery, to make the virus crippled and unable to replicate. We can keep the part that gets the genome into the nucleus — the entry machinery — and gut everything else. The wild virus is a complete machine. The engineered vector is the same machine with most of the working parts taken out and replaced with cargo.

But before we start gutting, we need to look at the architecture of the virion itself, because that's the part we don't usually mess with. The capsid is what the immune system sees. The capsid is what binds the receptor. The capsid is, in a sense, the truck's chassis — and the chassis matters a lot.

## SEGMENT 3 — Anatomy of an adenoviral particle: non-enveloped dsDNA in an icosahedral capsid

Let's open up an adenoviral particle. Picture, if you can, a soccer ball. Or more precisely, an **icosahedron** — a twenty-sided shape with triangular faces. That's the basic geometry of the adenoviral capsid. It's about ninety to a hundred nanometers across, which makes it medium-sized as viruses go — about five times bigger than an AAV particle, about a third the size of a herpesvirus particle. Big enough to see clearly in an electron microscope; small enough that several thousand of them would fit comfortably across the diameter of a human hair.

The capsid is built almost entirely from three proteins. **Hexon** is the most abundant — there are 240 copies of it on each virion, tiling the twenty triangular faces. If you look at an electron micrograph of an adenoviral particle, the surface looks faceted and somewhat textured, and what you're seeing is mostly hexon. **Penton base** sits at each of the twelve corners — the vertices of the icosahedron — twelve copies per virion, one per corner. And sticking out from each penton base, like an antenna or a spike or a tiny stalked mushroom, is the **fiber** — a long, slender protein with a globular knob at the tip. That knob is what binds CAR. Twelve fibers on each virion, one at each vertex.

Hexon, penton, fiber. That's the visible structure. Underneath, there are several internal capsid proteins that hold things together — proteins called pIIIa, pVI, pVIII, pIX — and inside the capsid is the genome itself, packaged with two small DNA-binding proteins called pV and pVII that condense and stabilize the DNA. At the very ends of the genome, attached covalently, is a protein called the **terminal protein** — TP — that primes DNA replication and stays attached to the genome end like a little cap. You don't need to memorize the protein names. What I want you to remember is the architecture: a sturdy outer shell of hexons and pentons and fibers, with a tightly packed DNA core inside.

\[Pause.\]

Now here are two structural features that I want to call out specifically, because they shape everything about adenovirus's behavior as a vector and as an immunogen.

First: the genome inside is **double-stranded DNA**, about thirty-six thousand base pairs long. That's 36 kb. For comparison, AAV's single-stranded DNA genome is about 4.7 kb. So adenovirus's genome is nearly eight times bigger. That matters because it determines how much cargo we can fit when we use the virus as a vector. AAV can carry maybe 4.5 kb of payload before it physically can't be packaged. Adenovirus — depending on how much of its own genome you delete to make room — can carry anywhere from a few kilobases up to about 36 kb of total foreign DNA in the gutless form we'll discuss in Segment 8. That capacity advantage is enormous. It is the main structural reason you might pick adenovirus over AAV for a given job.

Second: adenovirus is **non-enveloped**. This is critical. Many viruses you know — flu, HIV, coronavirus, herpes — wrap themselves in a membrane stolen from the host cell as they bud out. That membrane envelope is studded with viral surface proteins, but underneath it is essentially a host-derived lipid bilayer. Enveloped viruses have a soft outer surface and they look, in some sense, partly like a piece of the body. Adenovirus has no envelope. It's just protein — a bare, hard, protein shell. There is no membrane, no stolen lipid layer, no host-derived camouflage. The capsid is what the immune system sees, undisguised.

The analogy I like: **a non-enveloped virus is an armored package that doesn't carry a membrane sticker on the outside.** An enveloped virus is a package wrapped in plain brown paper from the host's own mailroom — it has some host-derived material on the surface that gives it a little camouflage. Adenovirus arrives in raw armor plating. No paper. No sticker. Just hard, foreign, exposed protein. Pattern-recognition receptors in your innate immune system are tuned to notice exactly this — they see bare viral protein and they sound the alarm immediately.

That structural fact — non-enveloped, exposed protein capsid — is the deepest reason adenovirus is such a powerful immunogen. The hexons and pentons on the surface are loud signals. Antibodies bind them with high affinity. Complement proteins decorate them. Macrophages eat them. And inside the cell, the viral DNA itself — unmethylated bacterial-style DNA — triggers another whole branch of innate immunity through sensors like cGAS-STING. The body sees adenovirus from multiple angles and responds vigorously.

\[Pause.\]

One more architectural detail before we move on. The 36-kb genome of adenovirus is divided, organizationally, into a set of regions named for when their genes get expressed during the viral life cycle. There are **early regions** — E1, E2, E3, E4 — which are transcribed shortly after the virus enters the cell and which encode regulatory proteins and the viral DNA polymerase. And there are **late regions** — L1 through L5 — which kick on later and encode the structural proteins, the hexons and pentons and fibers I just described. The crucial early region, the one we're going to delete to make first-generation vectors, is **E1**. E1 is the master switch. It encodes proteins, E1A and E1B, that activate transcription of all the other viral genes and that push the host cell into a state hospitable to viral replication. Delete E1, and the virus can't turn itself on. It can still infect cells, it can still get its genome into the nucleus, it just can't replicate. Which is exactly what you want from a vector.

There's also E3, which encodes proteins that help the wild virus evade the host immune system — they down-regulate MHC class I on infected cells, for instance, so cytotoxic T cells can't see them. E3 is dispensable for replication in tissue culture, so you can delete it freely to make room for cargo. Deleting E1 and E3 is the original recipe for a first-generation adenoviral vector, and it gives you about eight kilobases of cargo capacity — which is, again, way more than AAV.

So now you have the parts. Icosahedral capsid, three main capsid proteins, non-enveloped, double-stranded DNA genome of 36 kb, organized into early and late regions, with E1 as the master switch we're going to remove. Now let's talk about why the immune system pays so much attention to all this protein hardware.

## SEGMENT 4 — Why the immune system notices adenovirus: capsid antigens, complement, neutralizing antibodies

I want to spend a full segment on the immunology of adenovirus, because if you don't internalize this, nothing else in the lecture will make sense. The Gelsinger tragedy makes no sense. The need for multiple generations of engineering makes no sense. The whole vaccine platform makes no sense. The story of adenovirus as a vector is, more than anything, the story of a virus that the human immune system has been honing its response to for at least tens of thousands of years, and that lights up essentially every alarm bell the body has.

Let's go through those alarm bells in order.

**Layer one: pre-existing neutralizing antibodies in the blood.** As we discussed, most adults have already been infected with multiple adenoviral types, including Ad5, and they carry serum IgG antibodies against the hexon protein on the surface of those types. When you inject an Ad5-based vector, those antibodies bind the hexon, opsonize the virus, and target it for clearance by macrophages in the liver and spleen before it can reach its intended cells. We call those antibodies **neutralizing antibodies**, NAbs, and they are the single biggest reason a given person might fail to respond to an adenoviral vector or vaccine. If you have high titers of anti-Ad5 NAbs and you get an Ad5-based vaccine, your immune system might mop up the vaccine before it ever expresses its payload. This is why the field eventually moved to rare serotypes and chimpanzee adenoviruses — to dodge the pre-existing antibody problem.

**Layer two: complement activation.** Complement is a cascade of about thirty soluble proteins in your blood plasma that constitutes one of the most ancient parts of the immune system. It exists specifically to coat foreign particles with markers — opsonins — and to drill holes in foreign membranes. Adenoviral capsids, with their dense array of repeating hexon trimers, are exactly the kind of surface complement was built to recognize. Within minutes of an intravenous adenoviral injection, complement components bind the capsid, decorate it, and accelerate clearance. Complement activation also has systemic effects — it releases anaphylatoxins like C3a and C5a that produce vasodilation, capillary leak, and the general flu-like syndrome you see with high-dose adenoviral delivery.

**Layer three: innate sensors recognizing viral patterns.** Even cells that don't have antibodies have molecular sensors that recognize viral patterns. The adenoviral capsid, once it enters a cell, can be detected by cytosolic protein sensors. The viral DNA, once it escapes the capsid and enters the cytoplasm or the nucleus, can be detected by DNA sensors like **cGAS**, which then activates a signaling adapter called **STING**, which then triggers the production of type I interferons. Type I interferons are the alarm horns of antiviral defense. They put surrounding cells into an antiviral state, they recruit more immune cells, they amplify the response.

**Layer four: cytokine release from cells that ate the virus.** Macrophages and dendritic cells that engulf adenoviral particles get activated through their Toll-like receptors, particularly TLR9, which recognizes unmethylated DNA. These activated cells release a flood of cytokines — IL-6, TNF-alpha, IL-1 — that drive the systemic inflammatory response. If the dose is high enough, this cytokine release can become uncontrolled, which is essentially what happened with Jesse Gelsinger.

**Layer five: adaptive responses against the vector itself.** Even if you somehow snuck a first dose past pre-existing immunity, your body would now develop, over the following two to three weeks, a strong adaptive response against the vector. Cytotoxic T cells would seek out and kill any cell still expressing viral proteins from the vector — and that includes, in early-generation vectors, the cells you were trying to treat, because leaky expression of residual viral genes flagged those cells for destruction. New antibodies would form, ensuring that a second dose of the same vector would be neutralized even more efficiently than the first. This is the **redosing problem**, and it's the reason adenoviral gene therapies are essentially one-shot affairs unless you can swap vectors between doses.

\[Pause.\]

So when I say adenovirus is a "loud" delivery vehicle, this is what I mean. It is loud at five distinct layers of immune defense. Pre-existing antibodies. Complement. Innate sensors of DNA. Cytokine release from antigen-presenting cells. And rapid adaptive priming against the vector itself. Compare this to AAV, which we'll contrast more carefully in Segment 14 — AAV is much smaller, carries less obvious surface antigens, doesn't activate complement nearly as much, and provokes a comparatively muted response. Adenovirus is by some margin the loudest commonly used viral vector.

And here's the design tension. Everything that makes adenovirus a good cargo carrier — its big robust capsid, its ability to infect almost any cell, its high transduction efficiency, its capacity to deliver large genomes — is structurally connected to its immune visibility. The capsid is sturdy because it's heavily proteinaceous, and that protein surface is what antibodies see. The transduction efficiency comes from the fact that the virus delivers its cargo to the nucleus in large amounts, which means a lot of viral protein gets made early, which means a lot of immune signal. You can't get the benefits without the costs, at least not without doing serious engineering to quiet the response down.

The history of adenoviral vector engineering, which we're about to walk through, is essentially the history of repeated attempts to **quiet the truck** without losing its carrying capacity. First generation deleted E1 and E3, which made the truck unable to drive itself, but the engine could still misfire and make a lot of noise. Second generation deleted more, quieter still. Third generation — the helper-dependent, gutless vector — stripped the truck to the chassis. Each step has been an attempt to find a quieter version that still works. We'll spend the next several segments walking through that history, and then we'll see how the same virus that was being engineered down for gene therapy ended up being deployed up for vaccines, where the noise was the whole point.

## SEGMENT 5 — First-generation adenoviral vectors: E1/E3 deletions and the original gene therapy hope

Let's go back to the late 1980s and early 1990s. The first generation of adenoviral vectors had just been developed, and the entire gene therapy community was electrified. Here was a virus that could infect almost any human cell. It could be grown to enormous titers — billions of particles per milliliter, easily. It could deliver large payloads. And it had a key structural advantage over the retroviruses that had been the early gene therapy vectors of choice — adenovirus doesn't integrate. It stays as an episome in the nucleus. So there was no insertional mutagenesis risk, no chance of accidentally activating an oncogene by inserting the vector next to it. Adenovirus seemed almost too good to be true. And, well — it almost was.

Here's the recipe for a first-generation adenoviral vector. You start with the genome of human adenovirus type 5 — the most-studied human adenovirus, the one we have the most molecular tools for. You delete the E1 region — that master-switch region that activates all the other viral genes. With E1 gone, the virus can't replicate. It can still infect, still deliver its DNA to the nucleus, still express genes that you put in under their own promoters. But it can't make new virus particles. You've crippled it.

But now you have a problem: how do you produce the vector in the first place? If the virus can't replicate, you can't grow it. The solution was elegant. A cell line called **HEK293** — human embryonic kidney 293 — had been created in the 1970s by Frank Graham, and HEK293 cells happen to have integrated, into their own genome, a piece of the adenoviral genome containing the E1 region. So HEK293 cells stably express E1 proteins from their own chromosome. Which means: if you put an E1-deleted adenoviral vector into HEK293 cells, the cells supply E1 in trans, the vector can replicate inside those cells, and you can harvest packaged vector particles. But if you put that same vector into any other human cell — a patient's liver cell, say — there's no E1 anywhere, and the vector just sits there as a one-shot delivery vehicle.

HEK293 cells are still, today, the workhorse producer line for first-generation adenoviral vectors. Every COVID-era adenoviral vaccine was produced in either HEK293 cells or a closely related line. The pairing of E1-deleted vector and E1-complementing cell line is the central engineering trick that makes the whole platform possible.

\[Pause.\]

Now, the typical first-generation vector also has the E3 region deleted. Recall from Segment 3 that E3 encodes proteins that help the wild virus evade host immunity — particularly cytotoxic T cells. The wild virus needs E3 to persist in an infected animal. But for vector production in tissue culture, E3 is dispensable. So we delete it, and we gain about 3.5 kb of extra cargo space. Together, deleting E1 and E3 frees up about 8 kb of cargo capacity in a first-generation vector. You can stuff in an 8 kb expression cassette — promoter, gene of interest, polyA signal, maybe a marker — and the vector will deliver and express it in patient cells.

In the early 1990s, this design was used in dozens of preclinical and early clinical studies. Cystic fibrosis was a major target — the idea was to deliver the CFTR gene to lung epithelial cells via inhaled adenoviral vector. Familial hypercholesterolemia was another target. Various cancers were targeted, by delivering tumor suppressor genes or genes that made cancer cells sensitive to particular drugs. And the OTC deficiency trial, which we'll discuss in detail in the next segment, was an attempt to deliver a functional copy of the ornithine transcarbamylase gene to liver cells via direct hepatic artery infusion.

\[Pause.\]

But there was a problem nobody had quite reckoned with, and in retrospect it should have been obvious. First-generation vectors have E1 deleted, yes, but they still have all the other viral genes — E2, E4, the late genes. Those genes are not expressed when E1 is missing, in theory. But in practice, in patient cells, those genes get expressed at low levels. There's leaky transcription. There are stray promoter activities. There are pieces of viral protein wandering around in the cell. And when patient T cells encounter a cell expressing fragments of viral protein, they recognize that cell as infected and they kill it.

So the very cells you were trying to treat — the liver cells expressing the corrective gene — were being slowly destroyed by the patient's own T cells, because they were also presenting bits of viral protein from the leaky residual viral genes. The therapeutic effect lasted maybe a few weeks at best, then faded as the transduced cells were killed off. And the patient was left with a strong immune response against adenovirus and against the corrective protein, making redosing essentially impossible.

This is what the analogy I gave you in Segment 1 was pointing at. **In a first-generation vector, the drug works, but the leftover viral genes still get expressed and the immune system mounts a response.** The truck arrived. The cargo was delivered. But the truck's engine kept rumbling, and the neighborhood watch arrested the homeowner who took delivery of the package. The therapy lasted briefly and then was extinguished.

The field's response to this realization, by the mid-1990s, was to start designing second-generation and third-generation vectors with deeper and deeper deletions, trying to eliminate every last viral gene that could trigger T cell killing. We'll get to those in Segments 7 and 8. But before we do, we need to sit with the moment when the first-generation problem became horrifyingly real. We need to talk about Jesse Gelsinger.

## SEGMENT 6 — The Jesse Gelsinger tragedy of 1999: what happened and what we learned

I am going to walk you through this case carefully and respectfully, because it is the single most important event in the history of gene therapy, and the field still operates in its long shadow. Twenty-six years later, every Phase I trial design, every dose-escalation protocol, every informed consent document for a viral vector study, carries some trace of what happened at the University of Pennsylvania between September 13 and September 17, 1999.

Jesse Gelsinger was eighteen years old. He had a partial deficiency in an enzyme called **ornithine transcarbamylase** — OTC. OTC is a liver enzyme; it's part of the urea cycle, the metabolic pathway that converts toxic ammonia, produced when your body breaks down protein, into urea, which is harmless and gets excreted in urine. If you lack OTC entirely, ammonia builds up and you typically die in infancy. Jesse had a partial form — a mosaic, with some functional OTC in some of his liver cells and none in others. He was managing the disease with a low-protein diet and medications. He was, in essentially every meaningful sense, a healthy and active eighteen-year-old.

He volunteered for a Phase I safety study at the Institute for Human Gene Therapy at the University of Pennsylvania, led by Dr. James Wilson. The study was testing a first-generation adenoviral vector carrying a functional OTC gene. The vector was infused directly into the hepatic artery, the blood vessel supplying the liver, so that it would concentrate in liver cells where OTC is needed. The dose escalation had been planned in advance — successive volunteers received progressively higher doses, to map out a safety profile.

Jesse was in the highest-dose cohort. He received approximately 38 trillion viral particles infused into his hepatic artery on September 13, 1999. Within hours, he developed a fever and signs of systemic inflammation. Within a day, he was jaundiced and had liver dysfunction. Within two days, he was in acute respiratory distress and was on a ventilator. Within four days, his lungs, kidneys, and liver had failed, and he was brain dead. He died on September 17, 1999.

\[Pause.\]

What actually killed him, biologically, was a massive uncontrolled innate immune response to the vector. The high dose of adenoviral particles in his bloodstream activated complement, triggered enormous cytokine release from macrophages and dendritic cells, drove disseminated intravascular coagulation, and caused acute respiratory distress syndrome — what we now sometimes call a cytokine storm. The viral payload was almost incidental. What killed him was the body's response to the delivery vehicle itself, in the quantity it had been given.

The subsequent FDA investigation and academic scrutiny revealed several layers of problem. First, the trial had departed from its own protocol in several ways. Jesse's ammonia levels on the morning of the infusion were elevated above the cutoff that should have excluded him from dosing, but he was dosed anyway. Second, previous patients in earlier cohorts had experienced significant adverse events — high fevers, liver enzyme elevations — that should have been reported to the FDA and disclosed to subsequent volunteers, and were not reported. Third, animal studies that had used similar adenoviral vector preparations in non-human primates had shown deaths from inflammatory toxicity at high doses, and those deaths had not been disclosed in the informed consent documents Jesse signed. Fourth, Dr. Wilson and the University of Pennsylvania had significant financial conflicts of interest — equity stakes in companies that stood to benefit from the trial's success — that had not been adequately disclosed.

The result was a near-total halt of adenoviral gene therapy in human patients in the United States. Trials were suspended. The Institute for Human Gene Therapy at UPenn was effectively shut down. Dr. Wilson was barred from leading clinical trials for years. Settlements were paid. And the broader gene therapy field — not just adenoviral but all gene therapy — went into a multi-year period of intense self-examination.

\[Pause.\]

What the field learned, technically, is several things. Let me list them, because they shape the entire subsequent history.

**Lesson one: dose matters enormously, and innate immunity scales nonlinearly with dose.** A dose that is well-tolerated at level X may produce a catastrophic cytokine response at level 3X. The Phase I dose-escalation paradigm, in which you slowly creep up from low doses to higher ones in successive small cohorts of patients, has to be done with extreme caution, and the upper levels need to be informed by primate toxicology, not just lower-dose human data.

**Lesson two: route of administration matters as much as dose.** Direct intravascular delivery — into a blood vessel — produces a much more violent systemic inflammatory response than local delivery into a specific tissue. Inhaled adenovirus into the lung, for example, is much better tolerated than intravascular adenovirus into the liver, even at comparable total doses. Subsequent adenoviral gene therapy strategies have largely moved away from intravascular delivery for exactly this reason.

**Lesson three: the leaky-expression problem of first-generation vectors is not just a durability problem; it's a safety problem.** Residual viral gene expression contributes to the inflammatory response. Deeper-deleted vectors — second and third generation — produce less leaky expression and therefore less of this contribution. This realization accelerated work on helper-dependent vectors.

**Lesson four: pre-existing immunity to common serotypes amplifies acute reactions.** Patients with high pre-existing anti-Ad5 antibodies experience worse infusion reactions. This pushed the field toward rare and animal serotypes, which we'll discuss in Segment 12.

**Lesson five — and this is the cultural lesson — the gene therapy community had been racing forward with insufficient attention to the basic principles of clinical trial safety, conflicts of interest, and informed consent.** The Gelsinger case forced a serious reckoning with research ethics in the field. Modern clinical trial oversight in gene therapy is substantially more cautious than what existed in 1999, and that caution is a direct legacy of Jesse Gelsinger's death.

\[Pause.\]

Jesse's father, Paul Gelsinger, became a passionate advocate for clinical trial transparency and patient safety in the years after his son's death. He has spoken extensively about the trial and about what families considering gene therapy should ask. His advocacy, alongside the FDA's regulatory response, has shaped the modern landscape of gene therapy clinical trials. When you read about modern adenoviral, AAV, or lentiviral trials and see the careful dose escalation, the extensive primate toxicology, the multi-layer institutional review — much of that infrastructure exists because of what happened in September of 1999.

The gene therapy field eventually recovered. AAV, in particular, became the workhorse for in vivo gene therapy, partly because it provokes a much smaller immune response. Adenovirus retreated from systemic gene therapy and found new homes — in localized delivery, in cancer therapy where the immune response could be useful, and ultimately in vaccines, where the immune response was the entire point. But the recovery took the better part of a decade, and the field that emerged was much more humble about the limits of its tools than the field that had existed before. Now let's see how the engineering responded — the deeper deletions of the second and third generations.

## SEGMENT 7 — Second-generation ad vectors: deeper deletions of E2, E4, and the polymerase

So the field, by the late 1990s and into the 2000s, knew that the leaky expression of residual viral genes from first-generation vectors was a problem. Both for durability — because T cells were killing the transduced cells — and, after Gelsinger, for safety. The natural engineering response was: delete more viral genes. Quiet the truck further.

The second generation of adenoviral vectors went after additional early regions, principally **E2** and **E4**.

E2 is the region that encodes the viral DNA polymerase and the DNA-binding protein and the precursor terminal protein — the proteins that the virus uses to replicate its own genome. In a first-generation E1-deleted vector, E2 is still present, and even though it's not strongly transcribed without E1, some leaky E2 expression occurs and contributes to the immune response. Deleting E2 — or specific genes within E2 like the polymerase, called E2b — silences a major source of leaky viral protein expression.

E4 is a more complex region that encodes proteins involved in viral mRNA export, in shutting down host gene expression, and in modulating various cellular pathways. E4 proteins, when leakily expressed, are also recognized by T cells. Deleting E4 — or specific subregions within E4 — further quiets the vector.

So a typical second-generation vector might be E1-deleted, E3-deleted, and additionally E2b-deleted, or E4-deleted, or both. Each additional deletion brings two benefits: it removes a source of immunogenic viral protein, and it frees up more cargo space. A vector with E1, E3, and E2b all deleted can carry maybe 12 kb of cargo, up from the 8 kb of first generation.

But each additional deletion brings a production challenge. If the vector lacks E2b, you need a producer cell line that supplies E2b in trans, on top of the E1 already supplied by HEK293. That means engineering new producer lines that express both E1 and E2b stably. Similarly for E4-deleted vectors — you need producer cells that supply E4. Each new deletion requires a new corresponding producer system, and getting those producer systems to work reliably and at scale is non-trivial.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy here is straightforward. **Second-generation vectors are first-generation vectors with more of the engine taken out, and a more elaborate factory required to build them.** The vectors themselves are quieter. But the factory got more complicated. There's a real-world trade-off — every deletion that improves the patient experience adds engineering burden on the producer side.

Did the second-generation strategy work? Partly. Studies in animals showed that second-generation E2b-deleted vectors produced less inflammation and longer transgene expression than first-generation vectors. They were better. But they didn't eliminate the problem. There was still some leaky expression. There was still some T cell killing of transduced cells. The fundamental issue — that as long as any viral genes remain, the immune system can find them and react — was not solved by partial deletions. To really solve it, you had to delete _all_ the viral genes. Which is what the third generation does.

## SEGMENT 8 — Helper-dependent (high-capacity) adenovirus: gutless vectors, the design and the production

The third generation of adenoviral vectors is variously called **helper-dependent adenovirus**, or **high-capacity adenovirus**, or **gutless adenovirus** — they all refer to the same thing. And it represents the logical endpoint of the deletion strategy: delete _every_ viral protein-coding gene. Keep only what's absolutely required for the virus to be packaged into a particle and to deliver its DNA into a target cell.

What's absolutely required? Just two things from the viral genome. First, the **inverted terminal repeats** — short sequences at each end of the linear adenoviral genome that serve as origins of DNA replication and that act as the priming sites for the terminal protein. Second, the **packaging signal**, called psi, which is a short sequence near one end of the genome that the viral assembly machinery uses to recognize "this DNA goes in the capsid." That's it. ITRs at the ends, packaging signal near one end. Everything else — E1, E2, E3, E4, all the late genes encoding the capsid proteins themselves — gets deleted from the vector genome.

Of course, the capsid proteins still need to be made, because the vector still needs a capsid. So where do those proteins come from? They come from a **helper virus** — a separate, intact (or nearly intact) adenoviral genome that you include in the producer cells alongside your gutless vector genome. The helper virus supplies all the viral proteins needed for replication and packaging. The gutless vector genome — which is just ITRs, packaging signal, and your therapeutic cargo — gets recognized and packaged by the helper-supplied machinery, producing capsid particles that contain only the cargo, with none of the viral genes.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy. **A gutless vector is the truck stripped to the metal — frame, axles, wheels, cargo bed. No engine, no transmission, no fuel system, nothing under the hood.** You can't drive this stripped truck under its own power. So in the factory, you put a complete second truck next to it — the helper truck — that has all its working parts. The helper truck's engine drives the assembly process; the helper truck's machinery loads the cargo into the stripped truck's bed. At the end, you ship just the stripped truck to the customer. The customer gets a truck with cargo and no working engine — which is exactly what you want, because that truck can't go anywhere on its own, can't replicate, can't make any viral proteins. It's purely a one-shot delivery container.

The cargo capacity of a gutless vector is enormous. You can carry up to about 36 kb of foreign DNA — the entire size of the adenoviral genome, because you've effectively replaced everything with your own sequence. That makes gutless adenovirus the largest-capacity viral vector available. Big enough to deliver an entire gene with its native promoter and regulatory regions, or to deliver multiple genes simultaneously, or to deliver a complete CRISPR system with multiple guides — we'll come back to that in Segment 16.

And the immunogenicity profile is dramatically improved over first-generation vectors. There are no viral genes to express, so T cells have nothing viral to recognize on transduced cells. The transduced cells, expressing only the therapeutic cargo, look like normal cells to the immune system. Transgene expression lasts much longer — months to years in animal models, rather than weeks.

The capsid is still adenoviral, of course, and the capsid still triggers innate immunity. So the acute phase of vector infusion is still risky — gutless vectors still produce cytokine responses, still activate complement, still get neutralized by pre-existing antibodies. The benefits of being gutless show up after the acute phase: longer expression, less T cell killing of transduced cells, less leaky inflammation. The truck is just as loud arriving as a first-generation truck. But once it's parked, it stays quiet.

\[Pause.\]

The production process for gutless adenovirus is more complicated than for first-generation vectors, and this has limited its clinical adoption. You need to grow the helper virus alongside the gutless vector in producer cells, then somehow separate the gutless particles from the helper particles in the final harvest. The classical strategy uses a helper virus with a packaging signal that can be cut out by a site-specific recombinase — the Cre recombinase from bacteriophage P1, recognizing sites called loxP. You put the helper virus's packaging signal between two loxP sites and grow everything in a producer cell line that stably expresses Cre. Inside the producer cell, Cre cuts out the helper's packaging signal — but the helper's genome itself is still there and still functional, so it still produces the capsid proteins needed to package the gutless vector. The gutless vector, whose packaging signal is _not_ flanked by loxP, retains its packaging signal and gets efficiently packaged. The helper virus, having lost its packaging signal, can replicate but can't get its own genome packaged into capsids. So most of the particles you harvest contain the gutless vector genome; only a few percent contain the helper genome.

You then purify by physical methods — cesium chloride density gradient centrifugation, ion exchange chromatography — to enrich for gutless vector particles. The final product still contains some helper contamination, typically less than one percent, which is acceptable for many applications but which has been a regulatory hurdle for clinical use. Newer helper-dependent systems use additional features to drive helper contamination even lower.

Gutless adenovirus has been used in preclinical and early clinical studies for diseases including hemophilia, muscular dystrophy, and inherited liver diseases. It has not yet had a major commercial approval — partly because of the production complexity, partly because AAV has captured most of the in vivo gene therapy market over the same period. But the gutless platform remains an important tool, especially for applications where the cargo size exceeds what AAV can carry.

\[Pause.\]

Let me make the engineering arc explicit one more time, because the three generations are easy to confuse if you don't have the framework. **First generation: delete E1 and E3, keep everything else. Loud, leaky, short-lived therapy, immunogenic over time. About 8 kb cargo.** **Second generation: delete E1, E3, and additionally parts of E2 or E4. Quieter, longer-lived, harder to produce. About 10–14 kb cargo.** **Third generation, gutless: delete every viral gene; keep only ITRs and packaging signal. Quietest in the long run, most cargo capacity, most complex to produce. Up to 36 kb cargo.** Three generations, three levels of engineering depth, three different trade-offs between simplicity and quietness.

Now let's switch from how we engineer the vector to how the vector actually finds and enters cells. Because the truck doesn't just stop at random houses — it has a delivery address coded into its fiber knob. That's CAR.

## SEGMENT 9 — CAR receptor binding: the coxsackie-adenovirus receptor as the primary entry route

Let's talk about how adenovirus actually finds its way into cells. The mechanism matters because it determines which cells get transduced when you deliver a vector — the **tropism** of the virus — and it constrains what kinds of therapies are practical and what kinds aren't.

Most human adenoviral types, including Ad5, bind a cell-surface protein called the **coxsackie and adenovirus receptor**, or **CAR**. The name is a historical accident — the protein was discovered as the entry receptor for coxsackie B virus and for several adenoviral types simultaneously, and the joint name stuck. CAR is a member of the immunoglobulin superfamily of cell adhesion molecules. Its natural job in the body has nothing to do with viruses; it's a component of tight junctions between epithelial cells, where it helps neighboring cells adhere to each other. Adenovirus and coxsackie B virus both evolved to use CAR as a doorway because epithelial cells — the cells lining the airway, the gut, and various ducts — express CAR abundantly on the surfaces facing the lumen.

When an adenoviral particle approaches a cell, the fiber knob at the tip of one of the twelve fibers protrudes from the capsid, and the knob's distal surface — the part farthest from the capsid — has a binding pocket that engages CAR's extracellular domain. The interaction is tight — submicromolar affinity — and once engaged, the virus is firmly tethered to the cell. Then a second event happens at the **penton base** at the corner of the capsid the fiber is anchored on. The penton base displays a short loop containing an RGD motif — arginine-glycine-aspartate, a classic integrin-binding sequence — and that RGD loop engages **integrins** on the cell surface, particularly alpha-V-beta-3 and alpha-V-beta-5 integrins. The integrin engagement triggers the formation of a clathrin-coated pit, the cell internalizes the virus into an endocytic vesicle, the vesicle acidifies, the capsid partially uncoats and escapes into the cytoplasm, the partially uncoated capsid trundles along microtubules to the nuclear pore, and the genome gets injected into the nucleus.

So the entry process has two key receptor engagements: **fiber knob to CAR for binding, penton base RGD to integrin for internalization.** Two handshakes, one for the lock-on, one for the pull-in. Both handshakes have to work for efficient entry.

\[Pause.\]

The tropism implications of this are important. CAR is highly expressed on differentiated epithelial cells — airway epithelium, intestinal epithelium, hepatocytes facing the bile canaliculi. CAR is moderately expressed on many other cell types. But CAR is **poorly expressed on a few cell types that you might really want to target**, including most cancer cells (which often have down-regulated CAR as part of their loss of epithelial character), hematopoietic stem cells, mature lymphocytes, and many neural cells. So an unmodified Ad5 vector is great at transducing liver hepatocytes from the bloodstream — too great, actually; the liver acts as a giant sponge for systemic adenovirus — but it's mediocre at transducing tumor cells, blood cells, or neurons.

There's also a quirk that turned out to matter a lot for liver targeting. When Ad5 is injected into the bloodstream, the hexon protein on the capsid surface binds, with surprisingly high affinity, to **coagulation factor X** in the blood. Factor X then bridges the virus to **heparan sulfate proteoglycans** on the surface of liver hepatocytes, and this factor-X-mediated route dominates over CAR-mediated entry for systemic delivery. So the liver tropism of Ad5 in vivo is driven less by CAR and more by this hexon-factor-X bridging. Knowing this has led to engineering strategies — point mutations in the hexon to disrupt factor X binding — that detarget the vector from the liver, which is useful when you want to deliver it elsewhere systemically without losing most of it to hepatic uptake.

\[Pause.\]

The big idea to leave this segment with is that adenoviral tropism is determined by capsid surface interactions — fiber knob, penton base, hexon — that engage specific cellular and serum molecules. If you want to change the tropism, you have to change those surface interactions. Which is what the next segment is about: re-targeting by modifying the fiber knob and other capsid features. The truck has a default delivery route, written into its bumper stickers and hood ornaments. Change the stickers, change the route.

## SEGMENT 10 — Tropism and re-targeting: fiber knob modifications

Welcome back. We left off talking about CAR as the natural entry receptor for most human adenoviral types, and we noted that CAR's tissue distribution doesn't perfectly match what we'd like a therapeutic vector to target. Cancer cells, blood cells, neurons — these are all cells you might want to deliver to, and they all express CAR poorly. So the obvious engineering question is: can we change what receptor the virus uses, by modifying the capsid surface?

The answer is yes, and there are several distinct strategies, all of which intervene on the fiber protein. Recall that the fiber is the long stalk-and-knob protein that sticks out from each of the twelve corners of the icosahedral capsid, and the knob at the tip is what binds CAR. If we modify the knob, we modify the binding specificity, and we modify the tropism.

**Strategy one: insert a targeting peptide into the knob.** The knob has several surface-exposed loops, particularly the so-called HI loop, that can tolerate the insertion of short peptide sequences without destroying the overall knob fold. If you insert, say, an RGD peptide into the HI loop, the modified knob now binds integrins via the inserted peptide in addition to whatever it was binding via CAR. The vector now has dual tropism — it can enter cells via CAR _or_ via integrins — and on cells that lack CAR but express integrins, it can now enter where it couldn't before. Different inserted peptides target different cells. There are peptides that target the asialoglycoprotein receptor on hepatocytes, peptides that target tumor-associated antigens, peptides that target endothelial cells.

**Strategy two: swap the entire fiber knob for a different serotype's knob.** Different adenoviral types use different primary receptors. Ad5 uses CAR, but Ad3 and Ad11 and Ad35 use a different receptor called CD46, which is upregulated on many tumor cells. So you can construct a chimeric vector — an Ad5 capsid with an Ad35 fiber knob, often called Ad5/35 — that has the manufacturing and genomic backbone of Ad5 but the receptor specificity of Ad35. Such chimeric vectors transduce tumor cells and hematopoietic stem cells far more efficiently than wild Ad5, because they bind CD46 rather than CAR.

**Strategy three: ablate the natural binding and add a new ligand.** This is more aggressive: you make point mutations in the knob that destroy CAR binding entirely, and you make mutations in the penton base that destroy RGD-integrin engagement. The resulting vector has no natural tropism. Then you fuse a new targeting ligand — a single-chain antibody, a designed binder, an affibody — to a different part of the capsid, often the C-terminus of the modified fiber. Now the only way the vector can bind a cell is through your engineered ligand, which gives you exquisite tropism specificity for whatever the ligand targets. The trade-off is that the engineered targeting often has lower affinity than the natural fiber-CAR interaction, so transduction efficiency drops.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy. **Re-targeting is changing the truck's destination label.** A default Ad5 truck delivers to every house with a CAR mailbox out front. You can paste a new label onto the bumper that says "also deliver to houses with integrins on the porch." You can rebuild the whole truck with a different company's logo so it gets routed to a different set of warehouses. Or you can strip off all the default labels and put on a single, very specific one — "deliver only to houses with this exact serial number on the door" — which gives you precision at the cost of speed.

The re-targeting field has been active for over twenty years and has produced a lot of beautiful engineering. But the practical clinical impact has been modest. In part because re-engineered tropism is only one piece of the puzzle — you still have to deal with pre-existing immunity, with capsid-driven innate response, with cargo capacity. In part because for many applications, you can achieve the targeting you want by changing the route of delivery rather than the vector — directly injecting into a tumor, for instance, mostly solves the targeting problem without any vector engineering at all. Re-targeting remains an active area, particularly for oncolytic adenoviruses (Segment 15), but it has not transformed the field the way some early enthusiasts hoped.

## SEGMENT 11 — Pre-existing immunity in humans: the seroprevalence problem

Now we have to confront the largest single barrier to adenoviral vector deployment in humans, and one of the central reasons the field moved in the directions it did over the last twenty years. The problem is **pre-existing immunity** — the fact that most adult humans have already met most common adenoviral types in childhood and carry serum antibodies against them.

Let me put the numbers back on the table from Segment 2, because they're the spine of this segment. For Ad5 specifically — the most commonly used vector backbone — anti-Ad5 neutralizing antibodies are detectable in roughly 40 to 70 percent of adults in the United States and Europe. In sub-Saharan Africa, the prevalence runs higher, often over 90 percent. For other common human adenoviral types — Ad2, Ad6, Ad26, Ad35 — the prevalence varies by type and by region, but it's never trivial.

What does pre-existing immunity actually do to a vector? Several things, all bad.

**One: it neutralizes the vector before it reaches target cells.** Anti-hexon antibodies bind the capsid, opsonize the particle, and target it for clearance by liver and spleen macrophages. A patient with high anti-Ad5 titers can clear a vector dose dramatically faster than a patient with low titers. The effective dose delivered to the intended tissue is correspondingly lower.

**Two: it amplifies the acute inflammatory response.** Antibody-coated capsids activate complement more efficiently than uncoated capsids, drive more cytokine release from Fc-receptor-bearing immune cells, and worsen the infusion reaction. Patients with high pre-existing immunity have worse acute side effects from adenoviral vectors.

**Three: it reduces the efficacy of the encoded therapy.** Whatever payload the vector was supposed to deliver gets delivered at lower levels because fewer vector particles reach target cells. For a gene therapy, this means less corrective protein. For a vaccine, this means less expressed antigen and a weaker immune response to the antigen.

**Four: it eliminates the possibility of redosing with the same vector.** Even if you sneak a first dose past pre-existing immunity, by the time you'd want to give a second dose — weeks later, for a vaccine boost, or months later, for a gene therapy top-up — the patient has now developed even higher antibody titers from their first exposure to your vector. A second dose of the same vector gets neutralized even more efficiently than the first. So adenoviral vectors are, in practice, one-shot tools unless you can switch to a different serotype for the boost.

\[Pause.\]

There are several strategies to deal with the pre-existing immunity problem.

**Strategy one: use rare human serotypes.** Instead of Ad5, use an adenovirus that most people have never been exposed to. The most prominent example is **Ad26**, a relatively rare human adenoviral type with much lower seroprevalence in most populations — typically 10 to 30 percent in adult populations. Ad26-based vectors have been developed for HIV, Ebola, Zika, and most famously for the Johnson and Johnson COVID-19 vaccine. Using a rare serotype doesn't eliminate the problem, but it shifts the proportion of patients who can effectively receive the vector from a minority to a majority.

**Strategy two: use animal adenoviruses, particularly chimpanzee adenoviruses.** Humans have never been infected with chimpanzee adenoviruses, so humans have no pre-existing immunity to them. The ChAdOx1 vector — chimpanzee adenovirus Oxford 1 — is the backbone for the AstraZeneca-Oxford COVID-19 vaccine. We'll spend the whole next segment on this strategy, because it's important enough to deserve its own treatment.

**Strategy three: chemically modify the capsid.** You can coat the capsid surface with polyethylene glycol — PEGylation — to physically shield the antigenic surface from neutralizing antibodies. PEGylated adenoviral vectors are partly protected from pre-existing antibodies, though the protection is incomplete and the chemistry adds production complexity. PEGylation also slows clearance, which can be a benefit.

**Strategy four: heterologous prime-boost.** Use one vector for the first dose and a different vector for the second dose. The pre-existing immunity from dose one doesn't carry over to dose two if the two vectors are antigenically distinct. The Sputnik V COVID-19 vaccine actually uses this strategy within a single course — the first dose is Ad26-based, the second dose is Ad5-based. The two adenoviral types are antigenically different enough that the anti-Ad26 antibodies from dose one don't substantially neutralize the Ad5 in dose two.

\[Pause.\]

The big idea here: **adenoviral vector engineering is, in significant part, an arms race against pre-existing human immunity.** Every strategy I just listed is an attempt to dodge or outflank the neutralizing antibodies that adult humans carry around against common adenoviral types. The field has been quite creative — rare serotypes, animal serotypes, capsid modifications, prime-boost regimens — but the fundamental problem is unsolvable in principle, because the same surface features that let you exploit the virus's tropism are the surface features that the immune system can learn to recognize. You can change the surface, and the immune system will eventually learn the new surface too, given enough exposure.

The strategy that has worked best in practice — that allowed AstraZeneca to dose hundreds of millions of people with their COVID vaccine — is to switch to a serotype the human immune system has essentially never seen. Chimpanzee adenovirus. Let's spend a full segment on that.

## SEGMENT 12 — Chimpanzee adenovirus vectors: ChAdOx1 and ChAd3 sidestepping pre-existing immunity

The idea of using non-human adenoviruses as vectors goes back to the early 2000s, when the seroprevalence problem with Ad5 was becoming clear. Researchers reasoned: if the problem is that humans have been exposed to human adenoviruses, then we should use an adenovirus from a species humans don't share viruses with. Chimpanzees were the obvious candidate. Chimpanzees, our closest evolutionary relatives, carry adenoviruses with overall structures very similar to human adenoviruses — so the molecular biology of vector construction would be familiar — but with surface antigens different enough that human immune systems have not seen them before.

The best-known chimpanzee adenoviral vector is **ChAdOx1** — chimpanzee adenovirus Oxford 1 — developed at the Jenner Institute at Oxford in the 2010s. It is based on a chimpanzee adenovirus called Y25, with the E1 region deleted and replaced with an expression cassette for whatever antigen you want to encode. The vector was originally developed for influenza and tuberculosis vaccines, and then in early 2020, when SARS-CoV-2 emerged, the Oxford team rapidly cloned the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein gene into ChAdOx1 and produced what became the AstraZeneca-Oxford COVID-19 vaccine, also called Vaxzevria or Covishield.

There is a related vector called **ChAd3**, based on a different chimpanzee adenovirus, that was developed earlier and was used in Ebola vaccines.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want to use here. **Switching from Ad5 to ChAdOx1 is switching from a delivery company everyone in town has dealt with before — and remembers — to a brand-new delivery company that no one has seen.** When the new company's truck pulls up, nobody recognizes the logo. No neighborhood watch is on alert. No barricades go up. The truck delivers its package without incident. That novelty is the entire selling point.

The pre-existing immunity to ChAdOx1 in human populations is essentially zero — typically less than 5 percent, often less than 1 percent. So you can dose almost anyone and expect the vector to evade neutralization. The vector reaches target cells, the encoded antigen is expressed in those cells, the antigen is presented to the immune system, and a robust adaptive response develops against the encoded antigen. Which is exactly what you want from a vaccine.

There is, of course, an asterisk. After the first dose, the patient _does_ develop anti-ChAdOx1 antibodies. So a second dose of ChAdOx1 — a homologous boost — is partially neutralized by the antibodies the patient has already generated against the first dose. The AstraZeneca vaccine showed reduced efficiency on boost compared to prime, and considerable optimization went into the dosing interval to maximize the overall response. Heterologous boosting — for example, ChAdOx1 prime followed by an mRNA vaccine boost — was widely studied during the pandemic and turned out to produce excellent immune responses, often better than two doses of either vaccine alone.

\[Pause.\]

The construction of ChAdOx1 follows the same first-generation logic as Ad5-based vectors: delete E1, delete most of E3, leave E2 and E4 intact, and insert your transgene expression cassette in place of the deleted E1. The vector is produced in HEK293 cells or T-REx-293 cells modified to express the chimpanzee adenoviral E1 proteins (which differ slightly from the Ad5 E1 proteins that HEK293 endogenously expresses). The producer cells supply E1 in trans, the vector replicates and packages, and you harvest by the same purification protocols used for Ad5 — cesium chloride centrifugation or anion exchange chromatography (Segment 17).

One nice property of ChAdOx1 is that, like Ad5, it is non-replicating in patient cells, it doesn't integrate, and expression is transient — peaking at a few days post-administration and declining over a few weeks. For a vaccine, transient expression is fine; you just need long enough for the antigen to be presented and the adaptive immune response to be primed. For a gene therapy, transient expression is a limitation, but vaccines are where ChAdOx1 has had its most dramatic impact.

\[Pause.\]

Now I want to flag two safety issues that emerged specifically with adenoviral COVID-19 vaccines, because the story has to be told honestly. Both ChAdOx1-based vaccines (AstraZeneca) and Ad26-based vaccines (Johnson and Johnson) were associated with a rare but serious side effect called **vaccine-induced immune thrombotic thrombocytopenia**, or VITT. VITT involves the formation of antibodies against a platelet protein called PF4, which can drive abnormal blood clotting in unusual locations — cerebral venous sinuses, splanchnic veins — sometimes with fatal consequences. The mechanism is not fully understood, but it appears to involve a complex interaction between the adenoviral capsid, plasma proteins including PF4, and the patient's immune system. VITT is rare — incidence around one in 100,000 doses or less, depending on the population — but it was severe enough to lead to changes in vaccination guidance in many countries, particularly for younger patients with available alternative vaccines.

This is a sobering footnote. Adenoviral vaccines saved a great many lives during the COVID-19 pandemic — particularly in low and middle income countries where AstraZeneca was the most widely deployed vaccine. But they also carried a small but real risk of severe side effects that we did not fully appreciate before deployment. The field is still working to understand VITT mechanistically, and that understanding will inform the next generation of adenoviral vaccines.

So that's chimpanzee adenoviral vectors. Engineered to dodge the pre-existing immunity problem. Powerful as vaccine vehicles. With known limitations and a small set of rare but serious adverse events. The next segment will put this in the broader context of how the COVID era reshaped adenoviral vector deployment globally.

## SEGMENT 13 — Adenoviral vaccines during COVID: Sputnik V, J&J/Janssen, AstraZeneca/Oxford, and the lessons learned

Let's stitch together the COVID-era story, because it is the moment adenoviral vectors went from a niche tool to a global public health technology, and it deserves to be told as a single arc.

When SARS-CoV-2 emerged in late 2019 and early 2020, the scientific community had a remarkably broad menu of vaccine platforms to draw on. mRNA vaccines, which had been in development for over a decade but had never been licensed before, would famously become two of the dominant products — Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna. Inactivated whole-virus vaccines, the classical platform, would be deployed by several Chinese and Indian manufacturers. And **adenoviral vector vaccines** would constitute a major share of doses delivered worldwide, particularly outside the wealthiest countries.

The major adenoviral COVID vaccines, in roughly the order they entered widespread deployment, were:

**Sputnik V** — developed by the Gamaleya Institute in Russia. It uses a heterologous prime-boost: first dose is **Ad26**-vectored spike, second dose is **Ad5**-vectored spike. The two serotypes are antigenically distinct enough that anti-Ad26 antibodies from dose one don't substantially neutralize the Ad5 in dose two. Sputnik V was widely deployed in Russia, parts of Latin America, parts of the Middle East, and parts of Asia.

**The Johnson and Johnson / Janssen vaccine** — developed by Janssen Pharmaceutical, a J&J subsidiary in the Netherlands. Single dose, **Ad26**-vectored spike. The original Phase III trial demonstrated meaningful efficacy with one shot, which was an enormous logistical advantage compared to two-dose regimens in resource-limited settings.

**AstraZeneca-Oxford / Vaxzevria / Covishield** — developed at Oxford and manufactured at scale by AstraZeneca and, importantly, by the Serum Institute of India under the Covishield name. **ChAdOx1**-vectored spike. The Serum Institute supplied vast quantities to India and to COVAX, the WHO-led global vaccine equity initiative. AstraZeneca/Covishield was the single most widely deployed COVID vaccine in many low and middle income countries.

**CanSino Convidecia** — developed in China, **Ad5**-vectored spike. Used in China and in several countries that received it through bilateral arrangements.

\[Pause.\]

A few patterns emerge from the COVID adenoviral vaccine experience that are worth pulling out.

**Pattern one: adenoviral vaccines were generally cheaper, more stable, and easier to distribute than mRNA vaccines.** AstraZeneca specifically did not require ultra-cold storage; standard refrigeration would do. This made adenoviral vaccines especially well-suited to deployment in countries without robust cold-chain infrastructure, and it is one of the main reasons adenoviral vaccines were so important in the global response, particularly in 2021 before mRNA supply scaled up.

**Pattern two: adenoviral vaccines produced strong cellular immune responses, sometimes stronger than mRNA vaccines.** The encoded spike is expressed inside transduced cells and is partially processed and presented on MHC class I, eliciting CD8 T cell responses. mRNA vaccines also produce cellular responses, but the relative balance of T cell versus antibody response differs between platforms. For long-term protection against severe disease, a strong T cell response is valuable, because T cells can recognize multiple peptide fragments of the spike even when the surface antibody-binding sites have mutated. This contributed to the durability of adenoviral vaccine protection against severe disease through successive variants.

**Pattern three: adenoviral vaccines showed reduced efficacy against early variants compared to mRNA vaccines, but reasonable protection against severe outcomes.** Head-to-head efficacy comparisons against symptomatic infection generally favored mRNA platforms, but for the most important clinical outcome — preventing hospitalization and death — adenoviral vaccines performed well.

**Pattern four: pre-existing immunity to the vector did affect responses, especially for the Ad5-based vaccines.** Studies of CanSino in populations with high Ad5 seroprevalence showed reduced antibody responses compared to populations with lower Ad5 seroprevalence. This was a vindication of decades of warnings from the vector community about the seroprevalence problem.

**Pattern five: the VITT side effect, discussed in the previous segment, was a real and serious phenomenon — rare, but real.** It changed risk-benefit calculations for younger patients with access to alternative vaccines, and led to use restrictions for AstraZeneca and J&J in several countries by mid-2021. The mechanism is still being studied. It appears specific to the adenoviral platform, not to the spike antigen itself; mRNA vaccines did not produce VITT.

\[Pause.\]

What were the broader lessons? Several, and they're going to shape the next decade of adenoviral vaccine development.

**Lesson one: adenoviral vectors are deployable at vast scale.** Hundreds of millions of doses, with manufacturing distributed across multiple continents, demonstrated that the platform can be scaled up urgently for pandemic response. The cell culture infrastructure, the purification workflows, the fill-finish capacity all proved adequate to global emergency demand.

**Lesson two: rare and chimpanzee serotypes are the way forward.** Ad26 and ChAdOx1 outperformed Ad5 in populations with high Ad5 seroprevalence, exactly as the vector community had predicted. Future adenoviral vaccines will overwhelmingly use rare or non-human serotypes for primary doses.

**Lesson three: heterologous prime-boost is powerful.** Sputnik V's two-serotype regimen, and later mix-and-match studies with adenoviral prime followed by mRNA boost, consistently showed strong immune responses. The future of vaccination, particularly for pathogens that require sustained immunity, may involve deliberate platform mixing across doses.

**Lesson four: even rare serious side effects can shape deployment.** VITT was rare enough that the overall risk-benefit ratio of adenoviral COVID vaccines clearly favored vaccination during the acute pandemic. But understanding rare adverse events better, ideally before deployment rather than after, is a critical area for future development. Better preclinical models for VITT-like syndromes are an active research priority.

**Lesson five: adenoviral vectors are a real, durable part of the vaccine toolkit going forward.** They were not a stopgap that mRNA technology displaced. They have unique advantages — strong cellular immunity, room-temperature stability, cheaper production — that make them the right choice for some indications and some settings even when mRNA is available. The next decade will likely see adenoviral vaccines for pathogens including influenza, RSV, HIV, malaria, tuberculosis, Lassa, Marburg, and Ebola, alongside the continued evolution of mRNA platforms.

That's the COVID story. The same property — immune visibility — that killed Jesse Gelsinger in 1999 was deliberately exploited at planetary scale in 2020 and 2021 to teach hundreds of millions of immune systems about SARS-CoV-2 spike. The truck didn't change. The use case changed. And in changing the use case, the field finally found a job where the truck's noisiness was exactly what was wanted.

## SEGMENT 14 — Adenovirus vs AAV: when to use which

You can't really understand adenovirus as a vector without understanding its main competitor for in vivo gene delivery, which is AAV. We'll spend a segment doing a direct comparison, because in practice the choice between these two vectors is one of the most consequential decisions in any in vivo gene therapy program, and the trade-offs matter.

Let me lay out the key axes of comparison.

**Cargo capacity.** AAV: about 4.7 kb of single-stranded DNA, and that's the hard limit — bigger inserts simply don't package efficiently. Adenovirus: 8 kb for first-generation, up to 36 kb for gutless. If your gene of interest plus regulatory elements exceeds 4.5 kb, AAV is out and adenovirus or some other vector is your only option. For a small gene like factor IX (used in hemophilia B gene therapy), AAV is fine. For a huge gene like dystrophin — which encodes the protein deficient in Duchenne muscular dystrophy and is one of the longest human genes — AAV simply cannot fit the full-length gene, and you have to deliver truncated mini-dystrophin or use a larger vector.

**Genome behavior in target cells.** AAV: predominantly episomal, like adenovirus, with a small fraction of insertional integration that has become a subject of intense study. Adenovirus: episomal, no integration. Both vectors are non-integrating in practice, which is a safety advantage over lentiviral and retroviral vectors that integrate by design.

**Duration of expression.** AAV in non-dividing cells: expression can be sustained for years. AAV in dividing cells: expression is gradually diluted as cells divide and lose episomal copies. First-generation adenovirus: a few weeks at most, due to immune clearance of transduced cells. Gutless adenovirus: months to years, comparable to AAV in many tissues.

**Immune response.** AAV: low to moderate; pre-existing antibodies are a problem for some serotypes (AAV2 is common; AAV9 less so), and capsid-specific T cell responses are real but generally manageable. Adenovirus: high, as we have discussed at length. AAV is the quieter, lower-acuity vector.

**Tropism.** Both have natural tropisms determined by their capsid surface, and both have been extensively engineered to alter those tropisms. AAV has a much larger range of natural serotypes with diverse tropisms — AAV2 for many tissues, AAV9 for muscle and CNS, AAV8 for liver, AAVrh10 for various — and the AAV community has been particularly active in directed evolution of capsids to find novel tropisms. Adenovirus has fewer commonly used serotypes for vector use, though the chimeric and re-targeted variants discussed earlier expand the repertoire.

**Manufacturing.** AAV: traditionally challenging and expensive at scale, though manufacturing capacity has expanded significantly in the last decade. Adenovirus: easier and cheaper at scale, with well-established producer cell lines and purification protocols.

**Cost per dose.** AAV doses for in vivo gene therapy are extraordinarily expensive — multi-million-dollar treatments are common for AAV-based therapies. Adenovirus doses for vaccines are cents to a few dollars at scale, partly because manufacturing is cheaper and partly because doses are much smaller per recipient.

\[Pause.\]

So when do you pick which? Here are some heuristics.

**Pick AAV when:** the cargo is small (under ~4.5 kb); you want sustained expression in a non-dividing tissue (liver, muscle, retina, CNS); the patient population doesn't have prohibitive pre-existing anti-capsid immunity; you can afford the manufacturing and dosing costs; you only need to deliver once.

**Pick adenovirus when:** the cargo is large (over 4.5 kb); you want transient expression of an antigen (vaccines); you want to drive a strong T cell response (vaccines, some cancer therapies); you need cheaper manufacturing at scale; you want to deliver something that actively benefits from triggering inflammation (oncolytics, certain vaccines).

**Pick neither — pick lentivirus — when:** you need permanent integration into the host genome (CAR-T manufacturing, some HSC gene therapies); you want stable transduction of dividing cells.

The vector choice is not a fashion judgment. Each tool has a real envelope of use cases, and the engineering history of the last forty years has clarified those envelopes considerably. AAV captured most of in vivo gene therapy in the 2010s; adenovirus captured most of viral-vector vaccines in the 2020s; lentivirus captured most of ex vivo cell engineering. They coexist in the toolkit. Which one you pick is determined by the job, not the brand.

## SEGMENT 15 — Oncolytic adenovirus: ONYX-015, T-VEC's cousins, and the engineering tricks

We have spent a lot of time on adenovirus as a delivery vehicle for therapeutic genes and as a vaccine platform. There is a third major use case I want to spend a segment on, because it represents a fundamentally different conceptual approach to using viruses in medicine. **Oncolytic adenoviruses** are vectors engineered to **replicate selectively in cancer cells and kill them.** Not to deliver a cargo. To be the cargo, in effect — to use the viral life cycle itself, in cancer cells specifically, as the therapy.

Let me build up the logic. Adenovirus normally replicates in any cell it can infect, lyses the cell, and spreads. If we could constrain that replication so that it happens only in cancer cells, we'd have a self-amplifying, tumor-targeted therapy: inject some virus into a tumor, the virus replicates in the cancer cells, kills them, releases more virus, infects neighboring cancer cells, repeats. A wave of viral replication spreads through the tumor, leaving healthy tissue alone. The truck doesn't just deliver a package — the truck _is_ the package, and it makes copies of itself.

The trick is constraining the replication to cancer cells specifically. There are several approaches.

**Approach one: exploit deficiencies in tumor cell defenses.** The classical example is **ONYX-015**, an oncolytic adenovirus developed in the 1990s that has a deletion in the E1B-55K gene. The wild adenoviral E1B-55K protein normally inactivates the tumor suppressor p53, allowing the virus to replicate in normal cells that would otherwise mount a p53-driven antiviral response. The reasoning behind ONYX-015 was: if we delete E1B-55K, the virus can no longer inactivate p53, so the virus can only replicate in cells where p53 is _already_ inactivated — that is, in cancer cells that have lost p53 function. And p53 loss is one of the most common cancer mutations, occurring in perhaps half of all human cancers. So ONYX-015 should replicate in p53-deficient tumor cells and be blocked in normal cells.

The reality turned out to be more complicated. ONYX-015 did show selective replication in some tumor cells, but the selectivity wasn't strictly p53-dependent — other cellular factors influenced replication too — and clinical trials in the 1990s and 2000s showed modest activity. ONYX-015 itself was not approved in Western markets, but a closely related construct called **H101** was approved in China in 2005 for treatment of head and neck cancers, making it one of the first approved oncolytic virus therapies anywhere in the world.

**Approach two: use tumor-specific promoters to drive viral genes.** You can put the essential viral E1A gene under control of a promoter that is active only in cancer cells — for example, the telomerase promoter, which is active in most cancer cells (which need telomerase to keep dividing) and largely silent in differentiated normal cells. In normal cells, the modified vector cannot express E1A, cannot turn itself on, cannot replicate. In cancer cells, the telomerase promoter drives E1A expression, the vector replicates, the cell lyses. This is tumor-selective replication driven by cancer-specific transcriptional activity.

**Approach three: combine oncolytic replication with immune stimulation.** The most successful oncolytic virus to reach the clinic in the United States is actually not an adenovirus — it's **talimogene laherparepvec**, or T-VEC, an oncolytic herpesvirus modified to express the immune-stimulating cytokine GM-CSF. T-VEC is approved by the FDA for advanced melanoma. The principle, though, applies to adenoviruses too: arm the oncolytic virus with a transgene encoding an immune-stimulating molecule, so that the lysed tumor cells release not just viral particles but also a cytokine that recruits immune cells to the tumor. The lysis releases tumor antigens; the cytokine recruits cells that can present and respond to those antigens; a systemic anti-tumor immune response can develop. The oncolytic virus, in effect, becomes an in situ vaccine — turning a tumor into its own antigen factory.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I like for oncolytics. **An oncolytic adenovirus is the truck engineered to become a weapon, but only in enemy territory.** In a normal cell — friendly territory — the engineered truck can't start its engine. In a cancer cell — enemy territory — the engineered truck starts up, drives around making copies of itself, demolishes the building it's in, and the copies drive off to demolish the next building. The selectivity is built into the truck's wiring, so that it only goes active where you want it to.

Oncolytic adenoviruses have had a long and somewhat frustrating clinical history. The basic concept is appealing; the practical implementation has been hard. Tumors are heterogeneous — different cells in the same tumor have different mutations, different receptor profiles, different replicative states. Getting an oncolytic virus to spread efficiently through a solid tumor and reach all the relevant cells has proven difficult. Intratumoral injection limits delivery to accessible lesions; systemic delivery brings back all the immune barriers we discussed earlier. The field remains active — there are dozens of oncolytic adenoviruses in clinical trials worldwide — but the dramatic approval-and-deployment story we saw with COVID vaccines has not yet happened in oncolytics. It may. We'll see.

## SEGMENT 16 — Adenovirus for CRISPR delivery: fitting Cas9 plus guide plus donor template

One of the unexpectedly important applications of adenoviral vectors in the last decade has been the delivery of CRISPR-Cas9 components for genome editing. And this application leverages exactly the property that distinguishes adenovirus from AAV — the cargo capacity.

CRISPR-Cas9, as you may know, has three main components for most editing applications. **Cas9 itself**, the DNA-cutting enzyme, which is a fairly large protein — the most-used variant, from _Streptococcus pyogenes_, has a coding sequence of about 4.1 kb. The **guide RNA**, which targets Cas9 to a specific DNA sequence, only about 100 base pairs. And, for editing applications that involve repair templates — homology-directed repair or knockin of new sequences — a **donor template**, which can range from a few hundred base pairs for small edits to several kilobases for inserting full new genes.

Add it all up. Cas9 plus guide plus a substantial donor template easily exceeds AAV's 4.7 kb limit. You can squeeze a Cas9 expression cassette and a guide cassette into a single AAV vector — barely — by using a compact promoter and removing all non-essential elements. But there's essentially no room left for a donor template. So if you want to deliver a full edit-and-replace CRISPR system in a single vector, AAV is too small.

Adenovirus solves this directly. First-generation adenoviral vectors can carry Cas9, guide, and a meaningful donor template — say, 6 to 7 kb total — with room to spare. Gutless adenovirus can carry vastly more, including entire genes as donor templates for large knock-ins. The cargo capacity advantage of adenovirus, which is mostly a footnote when comparing vaccine platforms, becomes the deciding factor when comparing CRISPR delivery options.

\[Pause.\]

There are some specific advantages of adenovirus for CRISPR delivery beyond just cargo capacity.

**One: transient expression is actually a feature for CRISPR.** You want Cas9 active long enough to make the edit, then gone. Sustained Cas9 expression in cells you have already edited is a recipe for off-target editing — Cas9 keeps cutting at other places in the genome, with low probability per unit time, and over months that adds up. Adenovirus's natural transient expression profile, often described as a disadvantage for gene therapy, is exactly right for CRISPR.

**Two: high expression levels.** Adenovirus drives strong expression of its payload in transduced cells. Strong Cas9 expression maximizes editing efficiency. Combined with the transient profile, you get a pulse of high Cas9 activity followed by clearance — almost ideal kinetics for editing.

**Three: ability to deliver multiple components in a single vector.** Some CRISPR strategies require multiple guides, or guide-plus-Cas9-plus-donor, or even base editor or prime editor systems that are larger than ordinary Cas9. The cargo capacity of adenovirus accommodates all of these.

The trade-off is the immune response. Adenoviral delivery of CRISPR components, especially in vivo, brings all the immunology we have already discussed. This has limited adenoviral CRISPR delivery mostly to ex vivo applications — where you transduce cells in a dish, edit them, and transplant the edited cells back into the patient — or to localized in vivo applications where the immune response is tolerable. For applications like CAR-T cell editing, where cells are manipulated in a controlled ex vivo setting, adenoviral CRISPR delivery has been quite useful.

\[Pause.\]

The big idea here: when you have a delivery problem that's bottlenecked on cargo size, adenovirus is your friend, and CRISPR has been one of the most important examples of that. The same large carrying capacity that justified gutless vectors for delivering huge dystrophin transgenes also justifies first-generation adenoviral vectors for delivering complete CRISPR editing systems. The vector finds its niche where the alternative is too small.

## SEGMENT 17 — Modern adenovirus production and purification: CsCl, AEX, and GMP scale

Let me walk you through how adenoviral vectors are actually made at scale, because the manufacturing reality has shaped the field's deployment as much as any biology. The Gelsinger trial used vector produced in academic facilities. The AstraZeneca COVID vaccine campaign produced billions of doses across multiple commercial facilities. Bridging that gap involved enormous engineering work in production and purification.

**Producer cells.** As we've discussed, first-generation adenoviral vectors are grown in HEK293 cells or close derivatives like HEK293T or PER.C6. The producer line must supply, in trans, whatever viral genes are deleted from the vector. HEK293 stably expresses E1, so it complements E1-deleted vectors. For chimpanzee adenoviral vectors like ChAdOx1, modified producer lines that express the chimpanzee E1 are used. For higher-deletion second-generation vectors, more elaborate producer lines are required.

The producer cells are grown in **bioreactors** — large stirred tanks of culture medium, typically from a few liters at lab scale up to thousands of liters at commercial scale. Historically, producer cells were grown adherent on plastic surfaces, which was a scaling nightmare — you can only put so much surface area in a given volume. Modern adenoviral production has largely moved to **suspension culture**, where the cells grow free-floating in stirred medium, allowing direct scaling by simply using a larger tank. The shift from adherent to suspension culture in the 2000s and 2010s was one of the major engineering enablers of pandemic-scale adenoviral vaccine production.

The producer cells are infected with the vector at low multiplicity, allowed to expand for a few days, and then the culture is harvested when most cells are producing vector at maximum yield. The cells are lysed — chemically, with detergents — to release the vector particles from inside the cells, since adenovirus largely accumulates intracellularly before lysing. The crude lysate, after some clarification, contains the vector along with cellular debris, host cell proteins, host cell DNA, and a great deal of other contamination that needs to be removed.

\[Pause.\]

**Purification, classical method: cesium chloride gradients.** The original purification method for adenovirus, used since the 1960s, exploits the density of the viral particle. Adenovirus has a buoyant density of about 1.34 grams per milliliter, distinct from cellular debris and most other contaminants. You make a tube of concentrated cesium chloride solution, layer the crude lysate on top, and centrifuge at very high speed for many hours. The CsCl forms a density gradient — denser at the bottom, less dense at the top — and the viral particles migrate to the position in the gradient where the local density matches their buoyant density. A sharp band of pure virus forms at that position; everything else bands elsewhere or sediments to the bottom. You puncture the tube with a needle, collect the viral band, dialyze out the CsCl, and you have purified vector.

CsCl gradient centrifugation produces very pure vector — pure enough for research use and for many early clinical trials. But it has serious limitations for large-scale production. The centrifuges are expensive, the throughput is low (you can only process so many tubes at once), the CsCl is toxic and has to be carefully removed, and the process is hard to validate to GMP — good manufacturing practice — standards. CsCl gradients are still used in research labs, but commercial production has moved to chromatography.

**Purification, modern method: chromatography.** The dominant modern method is **anion exchange chromatography** — AEX. Adenoviral particles have a net negative surface charge at physiological pH, so they bind to a column packed with positively charged beads. You load the crude lysate onto the column, the virus binds while most other proteins and cellular contaminants wash through, and then you elute the virus by raising the salt concentration. The bound virus is displaced from the column at a specific salt threshold and comes off as a relatively pure peak. The eluate is then often run over a second column — size exclusion chromatography, or hydrophobic interaction chromatography — to remove residual contaminants and to put the virus into the final formulation buffer.

AEX-based purification scales beautifully. You can run columns of any size, processing thousands of liters of bioreactor harvest in continuous workflows. The process is amenable to GMP validation, with well-defined inputs and outputs and quantitative quality controls at each step. The yields are reasonable — typically 30 to 70 percent of input virus recovered as pure vector. And the resulting material meets stringent purity standards for clinical use.

\[Pause.\]

**Quality control.** Once purified, the vector preparation has to be characterized extensively before it can be released for clinical use. Key parameters include:

**Particle count** — typically measured by HPLC or by spectroscopic methods that exploit the absorption of viral DNA at 260 nm. The particle count tells you the total number of viral particles per milliliter, including both infectious and defective particles.

**Infectious titer** — typically measured by infecting indicator cells with serial dilutions of vector and counting plaques or transduced cells. The infectious titer tells you how many functional vector particles you have.

The ratio of particles to infectious units (the **P:I ratio**) is a key quality measure. Good adenoviral preparations have ratios of 10 to 50; ratios above 100 suggest a lot of defective particles and reduced clinical performance.

**Identity and integrity** — sequencing or restriction analysis to confirm the vector genome is correct. Adenoviral vectors can recombine or rearrange during production, particularly during multiple passages in producer cells, so genome integrity has to be confirmed.

**Replication competent adenovirus (RCA) contamination** — this is critical for E1-deleted vectors produced in E1-complementing cells. Through homologous recombination between the vector and the E1 sequence in the producer cell, occasional revertant viruses can arise that have re-acquired E1 and can replicate autonomously. Such revertants are dangerous and have to be screened out. Modern producer cell lines like PER.C6 were specifically designed to minimize the sequence overlap that allows RCA formation, but every clinical lot is still tested for RCA contamination.

**Endotoxin** — bacterial lipopolysaccharide contamination from the production process must be below clinical thresholds. Even small amounts of endotoxin cause severe febrile reactions in patients.

\[Pause.\]

The overall picture: adenoviral vector production at clinical scale is a mature industrial process, capable of producing billions of doses per year worldwide. The COVID-19 vaccine campaigns demonstrated this conclusively. The technology stack — suspension culture in bioreactors, anion exchange chromatography, robust QC analytics — is well established and well validated. For future pandemic responses or for new adenoviral therapies, the manufacturing bottleneck is not technical capability; it's deployment of capacity. The how-to of making adenoviral vectors at scale is now a solved problem.

## SEGMENT 18 — Frontier: immune-evading capsids, re-targeted serotypes, and next-gen oncolytics

We come, in our last segment, to the frontier. Where is adenoviral vector engineering going in the next ten years? Let me sketch the main directions.

**Immune-evading capsids.** The pre-existing immunity problem and the acute innate response are still the central limitations of adenoviral vectors. Several groups are working on capsids engineered to be invisible to common neutralizing antibodies. One approach: replace immunodominant epitopes on the hexon surface with sequences from rare or non-human serotypes, producing a chimeric hexon that retains the structural function of the original but is not recognized by antibodies against the parent serotype. Another approach: directed evolution of capsids in the presence of human serum, selecting for variants that escape neutralization. Another: chemical modification (PEGylation and beyond) to physically shield the antigenic surface. None of these has yet produced a fully invisible vector, but the field is making steady progress, and a vector that could be effectively redosed without neutralization would be transformative — it would unlock multi-dose gene therapy regimens that are currently impossible.

**Re-targeted serotypes for new tissues.** Specific tissues remain hard to target with current vectors — the central nervous system from the bloodstream is the canonical example, because of the blood-brain barrier. New capsid engineering is exploring vectors that can cross the blood-brain barrier or target specific cell populations within the brain. Similar work is happening on muscle, on hematopoietic stem cells, on specific tumor populations. The combination of natural serotype mining (looking at rare human and animal adenoviruses for novel tropisms) and engineered re-targeting (modifying fibers and other capsid components) is expanding the menu.

**Next-generation oncolytics.** Oncolytic adenoviruses with multiple armaments — combinations of immune-stimulating cytokines, checkpoint inhibitor payloads, bispecific T cell engagers — are moving through clinical trials. The vision is a tumor-targeted virus that not only kills cancer cells directly but also recruits and activates the patient's own immune system to attack the tumor systemically. Combinations of oncolytic adenovirus with systemic checkpoint inhibitor antibodies (which we discussed in the antibody lecture) are showing meaningful clinical activity in melanoma and other tumors.

**New vaccine targets.** Following the COVID success, adenoviral vaccines are being developed against many other pathogens — HIV (a major historical target, with mixed clinical results so far), tuberculosis, malaria, RSV, multiple hemorrhagic fever viruses. ChAdOx1 and Ad26 are both being deployed across multiple programs simultaneously. The challenge for the next generation of vaccines will be to maintain efficacy with repeated use across pathogens — patients who get an Ad26-based COVID vaccine now have anti-Ad26 antibodies that may interfere with future Ad26-based vaccines for other diseases. The vector immunology that constrains gene therapy now constrains vaccine deployment too, and engineering around it will require continued serotype diversification.

**Manufacturing innovation.** Continuous bioprocessing, single-use bioreactors, intensified upstream processes — the manufacturing technology continues to evolve toward higher productivity and lower cost. Production costs of a few cents per vaccine dose are achievable at scale, which is what made adenoviral vaccines so important for low-income countries during COVID. Further cost reductions could open up vaccine deployment for diseases that are currently economically marginal.

**Combination platforms.** The future may involve adenoviral vectors used in combination with other platforms — adenoviral prime followed by mRNA boost, or adenoviral delivery combined with protein subunit boosters, or even multiple adenoviral serotypes used in sequence. The mix-and-match studies done during COVID showed that combining platforms can produce broader and more durable responses than any single platform alone.

\[Pause.\]

Let me close with the arc one more time, because the arc is what I want you to leave with.

Adenovirus was discovered in 1953 as a respiratory pathogen. It was engineered into a vector in the late 1980s and early 1990s. It became the great hope of gene therapy in the mid-1990s. It became the great tragedy of gene therapy in 1999, with Jesse Gelsinger's death. It was redesigned across three generations of engineering through the 2000s. It found new life as a vaccine platform in the 2010s, with chimpanzee and rare-serotype vectors. And it was deployed at planetary scale in 2020 and 2021, with hundreds of millions of people vaccinated against COVID-19 using adenoviral vector vaccines. The same virus, the same fundamental biology, played all those roles. The truck was the same truck. We just kept learning how to use it.

If you remember nothing else, remember this: **the immune response to adenovirus is the central feature of the platform, and the history of adenoviral vectors is the history of figuring out which jobs to use it for and which to avoid.** For gene therapy in tissues where you need quiet, sustained expression — pick AAV. For vaccines, where you want a strong immune response to a delivered antigen — adenovirus shines. For cancer therapy, where you want immune recruitment to a tumor — oncolytic adenovirus may be the right tool. The vector hasn't changed. Our understanding of where to deploy it has.

And remember Jesse Gelsinger. The field that built the COVID vaccines is the same field that asked a healthy eighteen-year-old to volunteer for a Phase I trial that killed him. The lessons learned from his death — about dose, about route, about disclosure, about humility — are baked into the design of every vector trial that runs today. He didn't volunteer in vain. The infrastructure of clinical responsibility that grew up after 1999 is part of why we could deploy hundreds of millions of doses of adenoviral vaccines two decades later without catastrophe. The tragedy and the comeback are connected.

That's the lecture. Thanks for sticking with me through two hours of dense material. Take a break, talk to each other, and bring me your questions next time. We're done.

## APPENDIX — Key concepts, vocabulary, and study questions

**Core concepts to lock in:**

1. **Adenovirus is a non-enveloped, double-stranded DNA virus with an icosahedral capsid about 90 nm across and a genome of about 36 kb.** The capsid is built from hexon (240 copies), penton base (12 copies), and fiber (12 copies), with several minor proteins.

2. **The viral genome is organized into early regions (E1, E2, E3, E4) and late regions (L1–L5).** E1 is the master switch; deleting E1 cripples the virus and is the basis of first-generation vectors.

3. **Three generations of adenoviral vectors:**
   - **First generation:** E1 and E3 deleted. ~8 kb cargo. Leaky residual viral gene expression, T-cell-mediated clearance of transduced cells, short-term expression.
   - **Second generation:** additional deletions of E2 or E4. ~10–14 kb cargo. Less leaky, longer expression, more complex production.
   - **Third generation (helper-dependent, gutless, high-capacity):** all viral genes deleted, only ITRs and packaging signal retained. Up to 36 kb cargo. Requires helper virus during production.

4. **Producer cell lines complement the deleted viral functions.** HEK293 stably expresses E1, allowing E1-deleted vectors to replicate during production but not in patient cells.

5. **Entry receptor for most types (including Ad5) is CAR — coxsackie-adenovirus receptor.** Penton-base RGD engages integrins for internalization. Systemic Ad5 also uses hexon-factor X bridging to enter hepatocytes.

6. **The immune response to adenovirus operates at five layers:** pre-existing neutralizing antibodies, complement activation, innate DNA sensors (cGAS-STING, TLR9), cytokine release from antigen-presenting cells, and adaptive T cell responses against vector-derived antigens.

7. **Pre-existing immunity to Ad5 is a major barrier in adult human populations** (40–70% seropositive in US/Europe, often >90% in sub-Saharan Africa). Strategies to address: rare serotypes (Ad26), chimpanzee adenoviruses (ChAdOx1, ChAd3), capsid PEGylation, heterologous prime-boost.

8. **Jesse Gelsinger died on September 17, 1999**, four days after receiving a first-generation adenoviral vector intravascularly for OTC deficiency. Cause of death: massive innate immune response — cytokine storm, complement activation, multi-organ failure. Key lessons: dose-response nonlinearity, route matters, leaky expression is a safety issue, pre-existing immunity amplifies acute reactions, conflicts of interest and protocol deviations are unacceptable.

9. **Adenoviral COVID-19 vaccines** included Sputnik V (Ad26 prime, Ad5 boost), Johnson and Johnson Janssen (Ad26), AstraZeneca-Oxford / Covishield (ChAdOx1), and CanSino (Ad5). Collectively deployed at scales of hundreds of millions of doses. Demonstrated room-temperature stable formulations and strong cellular immunity. Associated rare adverse event: vaccine-induced immune thrombotic thrombocytopenia (VITT).

10. **Adenovirus vs AAV trade-offs:** Adenovirus carries much more cargo (up to 36 kb vs ~4.7 kb), produces stronger and faster immune response, has shorter duration of expression in patient cells, and is cheaper to manufacture at scale. AAV is quieter, longer-expressing, and more suitable for chronic in vivo gene therapy.

11. **Oncolytic adenoviruses** are engineered to replicate selectively in cancer cells. ONYX-015 (deletion of E1B-55K, intended to require p53-deficient host cells) was the prototype; its Chinese cousin H101 was approved in 2005. The field continues to evolve toward "armed" oncolytics that deliver immune-stimulating payloads alongside selective lysis.

12. **Adenoviral CRISPR delivery** leverages the large cargo capacity to deliver Cas9, guide RNAs, and donor templates in a single vector — capability that AAV cannot match for full edit-and-replace systems.

13. **Manufacturing has moved from CsCl gradient centrifugation (research scale) to anion exchange chromatography (GMP commercial scale).** Suspension cell culture in bioreactors has replaced adherent culture as the dominant upstream process.

**Vocabulary checklist:**

ITR — inverted terminal repeat. Sequences at each end of the linear adenoviral genome; required for replication and packaging. Retained in all vector generations.

Psi — packaging signal. Short sequence near one end of the genome that the assembly machinery uses to recognize the genome for packaging. Retained in all vector generations.

E1, E1A, E1B — early region 1 and its sub-regions. The master switch for viral transcription. Deleted in all standard vector generations to cripple the virus.

E2, E2b — early region 2; encodes viral DNA polymerase. Sometimes deleted in second-generation vectors.

E3 — early region 3; encodes immune-evasion proteins. Dispensable in tissue culture; usually deleted in vectors to free cargo space.

E4 — early region 4; encodes multiple regulatory proteins. Sometimes deleted in second-generation vectors.

CAR — coxsackie and adenovirus receptor. Primary entry receptor for most human adenoviral types.

CsCl — cesium chloride. Used in density gradient centrifugation for classical adenoviral purification.

AEX — anion exchange chromatography. Modern method for commercial-scale adenoviral purification.

HEK293 — human embryonic kidney 293 cell line. Stably expresses adenoviral E1; standard producer line for E1-deleted vectors.

PER.C6 — alternative E1-complementing producer cell line, designed to minimize RCA formation.

RCA — replication-competent adenovirus. Contaminating revertant virus that has re-acquired E1 by homologous recombination with producer cell sequences. Must be screened out of clinical lots.

ChAdOx1 — chimpanzee adenovirus Oxford 1. Backbone for the AstraZeneca-Oxford COVID-19 vaccine and several other vaccine programs.

Ad26 — human adenovirus type 26. Rare serotype with relatively low seroprevalence; backbone for Johnson and Johnson COVID vaccine and several Janssen products.

VITT — vaccine-induced immune thrombotic thrombocytopenia. Rare but serious adverse event associated with adenoviral COVID vaccines, involving antibodies against PF4.

OTC — ornithine transcarbamylase. Urea-cycle liver enzyme; deficient in the disease Jesse Gelsinger had.
