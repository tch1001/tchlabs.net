## SEGMENT 1 — Embryos without conception

Alright, settle in. Today, for the next two hours, we're going to talk about one of the most genuinely disorienting developments in modern biology — a line of work that, depending on who you ask, is either the most important advance in developmental biology since the discovery of induced pluripotent stem cells, or a slow-motion ethics crisis that the field is only beginning to grapple with. Probably both. The topic is **synthetic embryos** — structures grown in dishes, from stem cells alone, without an egg, without a sperm, without fertilization, that nonetheless look like embryos and, to a remarkable extent, behave like embryos. They're called by different names depending on who's doing the work and what the construct contains: **blastoids**, **embryoids**, **ETX embryos**, **stembryos**, **synthetic embryo models**. Underneath all those names is one central, astonishing observation, and it's the observation I want you to walk out of here remembering.

If you walk out of here today and remember nothing else, I want you to remember this single sentence. **You take embryonic stem cells, or induced pluripotent stem cells, or trophoblast stem cells — you plate them together in the right gel with the right signaling cocktail — and they self-organize into structures that look like, and partially behave like, real early embryos.** You don't sculpt them. You don't print them. You don't direct individual cells with tweezers. You provide the substrate and the cues, and the cells self-organize the embryo themselves. The architectural program for the earliest stages of mammalian development is in the cells. We just turn them loose and let them rebuild it.

\[Pause.\]

Let me unpack that, because every word matters, and because the implications run pretty deep.

**Embryonic stem cells.** These are the cells that, in a real embryo, form the inner cell mass of the blastocyst — the little ball of cells inside the early hollow embryo that goes on to make the body itself. They're pluripotent: each one can become any tissue in your body, given the right cues. In a dish, we can grow them essentially forever, and we can push them toward any developmental endpoint we choose.

**Induced pluripotent stem cells.** These are adult cells — skin, blood, whatever — that have been reprogrammed back to a pluripotent state by forcing them to express a few key transcription factors. Yamanaka's work in 2006. We talked about these in detail in another lecture. The crucial point for today: iPSCs behave, for most purposes, like embryonic stem cells. They can also build embryo-like structures, and that fact is important ethically because iPSC-derived synthetic embryos don't trace back to a destroyed embryo.

**Trophoblast stem cells.** This one is newer, and it's the missing piece that made the synthetic-embryo field really take off. Trophoblast is the outer layer of the blastocyst — the cells that go on to form the placenta and the extraembryonic membranes, not the body itself. For a long time, we could grow embryonic stem cells beautifully in dishes, but we couldn't grow stable trophoblast stem cells, especially from humans. That meant we could model the part of the embryo that becomes the body, but we couldn't model the part that becomes the placenta. We were missing half the picture. The breakthroughs in trophoblast stem cell derivation — first in mouse, then more recently in human — gave us the second ingredient we needed.

**The right signaling cocktail.** This is the same idea you've encountered in the organoid lectures. The cells in an early embryo are talking to each other constantly through a defined set of signaling pathways — Wnt, BMP, Nodal, FGF, Hippo, and a handful of others. The synthetic-embryo cocktail is, essentially, a reconstitution of those embryonic conversations, mixed up from purified factors and added to the dish. We're not inventing new signals; we're replaying the signals the embryo uses, in vitro, so the cells think they're inside an embryo.

**They self-organize.** This is the word that does the most work. The cells aren't told where to go. There's no scaffold pre-shaped like a blastocyst that the cells fill in. There's no microscale architect arranging the trophoblasts on the outside and the pluripotent cells on the inside. The cells, given the right starting conditions, work it out themselves. Trophoblast cells, in the right environment, segregate to the outside of the aggregate. Pluripotent cells cluster on the inside. A cavity forms. A polarized inner cell mass emerges. The whole thing organizes itself into something that, under the microscope, looks like a blastocyst — sometimes indistinguishably so.

\[Pause.\]

Now, the obvious question. **Why?** Why have we spent the last decade figuring out how to grow these things?

Three answers.

First: early-embryo research is ethically and technically limited. Real human embryos, in the first two weeks of development, are exquisitely difficult to work with. They exist for a few days after fertilization and then implant into the uterine wall, where they become inaccessible. Researchers have, in some jurisdictions, been allowed to study embryos donated from IVF clinics — but only up to a strict time limit, with strict oversight, and only in tiny numbers. The biology of the first two weeks of human development — the period when most miscarriages happen, when most early developmental abnormalities arise, when the basic body plan is being laid down — is, despite a century of work, still mysterious in important ways. Synthetic embryos, derived from stem cells, give us a system to study this period without using real embryos.

Second: synthetic embryos offer experimental access that real embryos simply don't allow. You can make hundreds of them. You can knock out genes in them. You can dose them with drugs. You can image them around the clock. You can vary the conditions. You can run experiments at a scale and rigor that's simply impossible with the precious, rare, ethically constrained real human embryos that occasionally make their way into research labs from IVF donation.

Third: synthetic embryos blur the definition of "embryo," and that, for better or worse, is reshaping bioethics in real time. If a structure has trophoblast on the outside, pluripotent cells on the inside, can implant into the uterine wall of a mouse, can develop heart and brain — at what point does it count as an embryo for ethical purposes? Different countries, different ethics committees, different scientific societies are giving different answers, and we're going to walk through the landscape carefully.

\[Pause.\]

So here's our trajectory for today. We'll start with the central idea — what a synthetic embryo is and how the field thinks about it. We'll work through the missing piece that held the field back for years: the absence of stable trophoblast stem cells, and the breakthroughs by Kathy Niakan in 2014 and Hiroaki Okae in 2018 that finally gave us those cells. We'll walk through Nicolas Rivron's 2018 mouse blastoid paper — the first proof that the system could work. Then the Magdalena Zernicka-Goetz ETX embryoids, the Polo and Hanna lab human blastoids, the Hanna lab synthetic mouse embryos that develop to day 8.5 in vitro with hearts and brains. Then we pivot into bioethics — the 14-day rule, the 2021 ISSCR update, the philosophical question of what "embryo" even means. And we'll end on the frontier: extending the time window, organogenesis-in-a-dish, the eventual question of in-vitro gestation.

By the end, you should be able to explain to a friend at dinner what a synthetic embryo is, why it's scientifically interesting, why it's ethically fraught, and why the existence of embryo-like structures grown from skin cells is going to be one of the defining bioethical questions of the next decade. Let's begin.

## SEGMENT 2 — Why early-embryo research is so hard

Before we get to synthetic embryos, I want to spend a few minutes on why early-embryo research is hard in the first place, because the difficulty is the reason synthetic embryos exist as a field. If real embryos were easy to work with, we wouldn't need synthetic ones.

Think about what an early mammalian embryo actually is, and where it lives. After fertilization, a single-cell zygote starts dividing — two cells, four cells, eight cells, sixteen cells. Around the morula stage, the cells start to differentiate into an outer layer and an inner cluster. By about day five in humans, day four and a half in mice, you have a structure called the **blastocyst**: a hollow sphere of cells, roughly a hundred to two hundred microns across, with two clear lineages. The outer layer — the **trophectoderm** — will become the placenta and the extraembryonic membranes. The inner cluster — the **inner cell mass** — will become the embryo proper, plus the yolk sac. The hollow space in the middle is called the blastocoel.

The blastocyst is what implants. Around day six or seven in humans, the blastocyst hatches out of its zona pellucida and burrows into the uterine wall. From that moment on, the embryo is inside the mother. It's no longer accessible. Whatever happens during implantation, whatever happens during gastrulation a week later — the establishment of the three germ layers, the formation of the body axes, the laying down of the primitive streak — happens deep inside maternal tissue, with the embryo only millimeters across, the surrounding tissue covered in blood and decidua. You cannot watch it. You cannot biopsy it. You cannot easily image it. For most of human history, what was going on inside that window — roughly weeks two through four of pregnancy — was almost completely hidden.

\[Pause.\]

This is what's sometimes called the **black box of early development**. We know what goes in — a blastocyst. We know what comes out — a recognizable embryo with a heart, a head, the beginnings of limbs. What happens in between, the most crucial choreographic moves of mammalian development, has been visible only in pieces: the rare miscarriage tissue, the rare ectopic pregnancy that can be studied, the occasional embryo donated from IVF and cultured for as long as the law allows.

In humans, the law allows two weeks — fourteen days from fertilization, no further. That rule, which we'll come back to in detail in Segment 8, was established in the 1970s and 1980s and has, until very recently, been the universal ethical limit for human embryo research. Beyond fourteen days, you cannot grow a human embryo in a lab, even from a donated source. And in practice, until 2016, nobody had managed to grow a donated human embryo even close to fourteen days. The technical difficulty of keeping an early embryo alive outside its natural environment was simply too great. Most embryo research, by necessity, stopped around day five or six — the blastocyst stage — because the cells just wouldn't survive past implantation in a dish.

So here's the situation, as it stood for most of the last few decades. The first two weeks of human development, the period when most things go wrong — most miscarriages, most early genetic disorders, most failed implantations during IVF — was essentially inaccessible. We knew anatomy from rare historical specimens like the Carnegie collection of human embryos, fixed and stained in jars. We knew genetics from working backwards from later defects. We could study mouse embryos in detail because we could control the timing of pregnancies and dissect mothers at known time points. But the live cellular and molecular biology of human implantation, gastrulation, early neural development — most of it was guesswork, extrapolation from mouse, and inference from indirect evidence.

\[Pause.\]

Now think about what you'd want to do if you could. You'd want to ask, for example: what genes does an inner cell mass express on day six versus day seven versus day eight? Which signaling molecules are flowing between which cell types? When does the primitive streak form, exactly, and which transcription factors mark its emergence? If you treat the embryo with a candidate drug, does development proceed normally or are there subtle defects? If a particular gene is mutated, does the embryo fail at the morula stage, the blastocyst stage, or implantation? These questions matter clinically. They matter for IVF, where most implantation failures happen in this window. They matter for understanding birth defects. They matter for understanding why human embryos so often fail — by some estimates, more than half of all conceptions never make it to a live birth, with most losses happening in these first few weeks.

You can't answer those questions in real human embryos. Not because nobody wants to, but because the embryos aren't available, the time window is forbidden, the ethics are contested, and the technical tools just aren't there.

So you go around the problem. You build a model. You take stem cells, which we can grow in essentially unlimited quantities, and you try to get them to do, in a dish, what a real embryo does in the uterus. If the model is good enough, it gives you a window into the black box.

That's the project. That's why the field exists. The technical and ethical limits on real embryo research created a vacuum, and synthetic embryos are what's filling it.

\[Pause.\]

There's a counterargument worth taking seriously, and we'll come back to it: if your synthetic embryos work too well, they become indistinguishable from real embryos, and now you have the same ethical questions you started with, just transposed onto a different physical substrate. That's the central tension in the field. The better the model is, the more useful it is, but also the more it starts to look like the very thing the ethics were originally protecting. We'll spend a lot of time on this in the second half of the lecture.

For now, hold this in mind: synthetic embryos exist because real embryos are inaccessible, technically and ethically, during the most crucial period of development. The question of whether synthetic embryos solve that problem, or merely relocate it, is the question the field is wrestling with right now.

## SEGMENT 3 — The trophoblast missing piece: Niakan, Okae, and the second stem cell

Let me back up to a piece of history that, until quite recently, was the rate-limiting step for the whole field.

Embryonic stem cells were derived from mouse blastocysts in 1981 — Martin Evans and Matthew Kaufman in Cambridge, and independently Gail Martin at UCSF. They cracked the recipe for taking the inner cell mass out of a blastocyst, plating it in the right conditions, and getting cells that would self-renew indefinitely while retaining the ability to become any tissue. That was a Nobel-prize-winning discovery and it transformed biology. Human embryonic stem cells followed in 1998 — Jamie Thomson at Wisconsin. iPSCs followed in 2006 — Yamanaka at Kyoto. By the late 2000s, we had a robust toolkit for working with the **inner cell mass** lineage. We could grow it, manipulate it, freeze it, differentiate it down any number of developmental paths.

But here's what we couldn't do. We couldn't reliably grow **trophoblast stem cells** — at least not from humans. Trophoblast, remember, is the other lineage — the outer layer of the blastocyst that becomes the placenta. If you want to build a structure that looks like a complete blastocyst, you need both lineages: pluripotent cells for the inner part, trophoblast cells for the outer part. With only embryonic stem cells, you could make pluripotent aggregates — embryoid bodies — that did a lot of interesting differentiation but never organized into anything resembling a real blastocyst, because they didn't have the outer-layer cells.

In 1998, the same year Thomson derived human embryonic stem cells, a research group led by Janet Rossant in Toronto derived **mouse trophoblast stem cells**. They took cells from a particular region of the mouse blastocyst — the polar trophectoderm — and showed that with the right culture conditions, including high levels of FGF4 and serum, those cells could be grown as stable, self-renewing trophoblast cells. They retained the ability to differentiate into the various trophoblast lineages that make up the placenta. Mouse trophoblast stem cells became, from then on, a standard reagent.

But humans were different. For nearly two decades after the mouse work, nobody could derive stable human trophoblast stem cells. People tried, repeatedly. The mouse recipe didn't work on human cells. Human trophoblast, in culture, would either die, or stop being trophoblast, or terminally differentiate. You couldn't get a stable line.

\[Pause.\]

That changed in two stages, both quite recent.

**Kathy Niakan**, working at the Francis Crick Institute in London, and her colleagues, around 2014, started doing careful single-cell transcriptomics on human blastocysts. They were trying to understand which signaling pathways were actually active in human trophectoderm, and how those differed from what was active in mouse trophectoderm. They found important differences. The signaling logic of human early development isn't a perfect copy of mouse. Human trophoblast, for instance, depends much less on FGF4 than mouse trophoblast does. Other pathways — particularly TGF-beta family signaling, Wnt signaling, hippo pathway components — turned out to be more important than people had assumed.

This was the conceptual groundwork. It told us why the mouse recipe didn't translate, and it pointed at what the human recipe should contain.

**Hiroaki Okae**, working in Takahiro Arima's group at Tohoku University in Japan, took that conceptual groundwork and turned it into a working protocol. In 2018, Okae and colleagues published in _Cell Stem Cell_ a recipe for deriving **human trophoblast stem cells** from blastocysts and from first-trimester placental tissue. The key ingredients turned out to be a Wnt activator (CHIR99021), an EGF source, several inhibitors of TGF-beta and other differentiation-pushing signals (A83-01, SB431542, Y-27632), and the right combination of substrate and basal medium. With this cocktail, human trophoblast cells finally became cultivable as a stable, self-renewing stem cell population. They could be grown indefinitely. They could be differentiated into the major trophoblast subtypes — extravillous trophoblast, syncytiotrophoblast, and so on — that you find in a real placenta.

That's the breakthrough. For a long time we had embryonic stem cells but couldn't model the trophoblast. Niakan and Okae finally derived stable trophoblast stem cells. We now had the second ingredient. The recipe was complete.

\[Pause.\]

I want you to feel why this matters. With only embryonic or pluripotent stem cells, the best you could do was an embryoid body — a clump of cells that differentiates randomly into pieces of various tissues, in no particular order or arrangement. That's useful, but it's not an embryo. A blastocyst has two cooperating populations. Trophoblast on the outside, signaling inward. Inner cell mass on the inside, signaling outward. The two are talking to each other constantly through Wnt, BMP, Nodal — the embryo is a conversation. Without trophoblast stem cells, you have only half of that conversation. With trophoblast stem cells, suddenly you can recreate the conversation, in a dish, between two real, separately derived, propagatable cell populations.

This is what made blastoids possible. Within months of Okae's paper, multiple labs were racing to combine human pluripotent cells with human trophoblast cells and see what happened. And what happened, as we'll see in a minute, was remarkable.

There's a third population worth mentioning here too: **extraembryonic endoderm**, sometimes called primitive endoderm or hypoblast. In the real embryo, this is a thin layer of cells that emerges from the inner cell mass and goes on to form the yolk sac. For a complete model of post-implantation development, you also need this lineage. Recipes for primitive endoderm-like stem cells were developed in parallel — in mice, by various groups in the 2010s; in humans, more recently. These cells are often called **XEN cells** (for extraembryonic endoderm). They're the third ingredient. With embryonic, trophoblast, and XEN cells in hand, you have all the major lineages of the early embryo as separately culturable stem cell populations. And that, as we'll see in the next segment, is what enabled the ETX embryoids.

## SEGMENT 4 — Mouse blastoids: Rivron's 2018 proof of concept

In May 2018, **Nicolas Rivron**, then at MERLN Institute in Maastricht and now at the Institute of Molecular Biotechnology in Vienna, published a paper in _Nature_ that, in retrospect, marks the formal beginning of the synthetic embryo era. The paper was titled "Blastocyst-like structures generated solely from stem cells," and what it showed was simple, striking, and consequential.

Rivron took **mouse embryonic stem cells** and mixed them with **mouse trophoblast stem cells** — both of which, recall, had been derivable as stable lines since the 1980s and 1990s respectively. He put them together in small microwells, in a defined medium, with a specific signaling cocktail. The cocktail included a Wnt activator and a TGF-beta inhibitor and a couple of other ingredients, fine-tuned to push the two cell types into the right conversation. And he watched what happened.

What happened was that the two cell types **self-organized**. The trophoblast cells migrated to the outside of the aggregate. The embryonic stem cells clustered on the inside. A central cavity opened up, mimicking the blastocoel. The whole structure, within a couple of days, looked under the microscope like a mouse blastocyst — same size, same general organization, same proportions of cells in each lineage. The structures even expressed the molecular markers you'd expect: Cdx2 in the outer trophoblast cells, Oct4 and Nanog in the inner cell mass, and so on.

He called them **blastoids**. The blastocyst-like structure made from stem cells alone.

\[Pause.\]

Now, the easy version of this experiment would have been to make a structure that just _looked_ like a blastocyst. Rivron went further. He asked whether the blastoid could actually do what a blastocyst does. The defining functional test of a blastocyst is **implantation** — it has to be able to attach to the wall of a uterus and start the process of forming a placenta. So Rivron transferred blastoids into the uteruses of pseudopregnant mice. And in a fraction of cases, the blastoids implanted. They attached to the uterine wall. They began to induce decidualization — the maternal tissue response to an implanting embryo. They started to form the early structures of a developing pregnancy.

They didn't go all the way. Most of them aborted within a few days, never developing into a recognizable embryo. The efficiency was low and the developmental fidelity was imperfect. But the fact that they implanted at all, that they could trigger the maternal response, was the proof of concept. **A structure made from stem cells alone, in a dish, can behave like a real blastocyst — well enough to engage with a uterus.**

That's the core demonstration. Take iPSCs (or in this case mouse ESCs) and trophoblast stem cells, gel them together with the right cocktail, get a structure that looks like and partially behaves like a blastocyst. The recipe is reproducible. The yields are decent. And the resulting structures are, for many practical purposes, models of the real thing.

\[Pause.\]

Rivron's paper opened the floodgates. The recipe was clear, the principle was demonstrated, and labs around the world started adapting it. In the next couple of years, multiple groups produced mouse blastoid variants — using different cell sources, different timings, different cocktails — and the field rapidly converged on what worked and what didn't.

A few key technical points are worth noting, because they recur throughout the field.

**Microwells matter.** You don't just throw the cells in a dish and hope. You typically aggregate them in defined micro-cavities — tiny wells just big enough for one blastoid to form. This standardizes the geometry, gets the cell numbers right, and dramatically improves reproducibility. Without microwells, you get a mess of variable aggregates. With them, you get something close to one blastoid per well, all roughly the same size.

**Cell ratios matter.** A real blastocyst has, roughly, more trophoblast cells than inner cell mass cells. The synthetic recipes get the ratio right by starting with a defined number of each cell type per microwell. Get the ratio wrong, and the structure fails to organize properly.

**The signaling cocktail is critical.** The cells, on their own, are not always smart enough to figure out the right organization. You have to push them with the right signals — Wnt to maintain pluripotency in some cells, TGF-beta inhibition to drive trophoblast identity in others, and various other small molecules to coordinate the timing.

**Quality control matters.** Not every aggregate becomes a proper blastoid. Many fail. The successful ones look like real blastocysts; the failed ones look like disorganized clumps. Labs typically score blastoid morphology under the microscope and only count the well-formed ones as "blastoids" for downstream experiments.

\[Pause.\]

Rivron's paper, and the wave of follow-ups it inspired, established the synthetic-embryo field as a legitimate area of research. It also raised, immediately, the questions that the field has been wrestling with ever since. **If a structure made from stem cells can implant into a uterus and start a pregnancy, even briefly, what is it?** Is it an embryo? Is it a model of an embryo? Is it a research tool? Is it a moral subject?

The mouse work pushed these questions to the surface without yet making them urgent — because mouse work, however striking, doesn't engage human embryo ethics directly. But everyone in the field knew, in 2018, that the same recipe was going to be tried with human cells the moment human trophoblast stem cells were robustly available. And human trophoblast stem cells, courtesy of Okae's 2018 paper, became available exactly then.

So the next phase, which we'll get to in Segment 6, was inevitable. The technical pieces were in place. The race to make a human blastoid was on.

## SEGMENT 5 — Mouse ETX embryoids: Zernicka-Goetz pushes past the blastocyst

While Rivron's blastoids were establishing the basic recipe at the blastocyst stage, another lab was doing something even more ambitious: trying to build embryo-like structures that pushed past implantation, through the early post-implantation stages, into what's called the **egg cylinder** or **gastrula-like** stage.

The lab was **Magdalena Zernicka-Goetz**'s, then at the University of Cambridge, now also at Caltech. Zernicka-Goetz had spent her career studying mouse pre- and peri-implantation development, and she had a deep intuition for how the three early lineages — epiblast (which becomes the body), trophoblast (placenta), and primitive endoderm (yolk sac) — talk to each other to coordinate the next steps of development. She thought that if you put all three lineages together as stem cells, in the right environment, you could get something that went beyond the blastocyst stage and into early post-implantation morphogenesis.

Her group called these constructs **ETX embryos** — E for embryonic stem cells, T for trophoblast stem cells, X for extraembryonic endoderm (XEN) cells. ETX. Three letters, three lineages, one structure.

In a series of papers starting around 2017 and culminating in 2022, Zernicka-Goetz's group showed that you could combine mouse ES cells, mouse TS cells, and mouse XEN cells in defined ratios, in a 3D matrix, and get them to self-organize into structures that recapitulated not just the blastocyst stage but the post-implantation **egg cylinder** stage — the elongated, cup-shaped early mouse embryo that forms after implantation, with epiblast in one half, extraembryonic tissue in the other, primitive endoderm wrapping around the outside, a pro-amniotic cavity opening up in the middle, and the beginnings of the anterior-posterior axis being established.

\[Pause.\]

This was a major step beyond Rivron's blastoids. A blastoid is a static snapshot of an early stage — it implants, but it doesn't develop in any sustained way. An ETX embryoid is a developing model. It changes shape over time. It establishes axes. It begins gastrulation-like processes. It expresses the molecular markers of an early post-implantation embryo. In the most advanced versions, ETX embryoids have shown signs of beginning the primitive streak, which is the morphological hallmark of gastrulation — the moment when the three definitive germ layers (endoderm, mesoderm, ectoderm) begin to form.

The recipe, like Rivron's, depended on getting cell ratios right, getting the gel right (typically Matrigel or a defined alternative), and getting the signaling cocktail right. Different versions of the protocol used different combinations of small molecules and culture conditions. Yields were low in early iterations — only a small fraction of starting aggregates would develop into a proper ETX embryoid — but they could be selected morphologically and analyzed in detail.

The biological insight was important. The three lineages aren't optional ornaments around the epiblast. They're necessary instructors. The trophoblast and the primitive endoderm send signals to the epiblast that tell it when and where to form the primitive streak, where the anterior is, where the posterior is. Without the extraembryonic lineages, the epiblast doesn't know what to do. ETX embryoids work because they restore the full conversation. Embryonic + trophoblast + extraembryonic endoderm, talking to each other, give you something that goes beyond a clump and starts to behave like a developing embryo.

\[Pause.\]

The Zernicka-Goetz work also raised the bar on what counted as a "synthetic embryo." Rivron's blastoids were impressive but limited — a snapshot at a single stage. ETX embryoids were a process — a stem cell-derived structure that develops over time, that changes, that begins to lay down the body plan. This is closer to what a real embryo does, and it raised, more acutely than blastoids did, the question: at what point of developmental progression does a stem cell aggregate become, ethically and scientifically, an embryo?

We'll come back to this question in detail. For now, hold the picture: ETX embryoids in mouse, by 2022, were getting through implantation-stage and into early gastrulation-like organization. They weren't going all the way to a beating heart and a brain — that was a different lab, which we'll meet in Segment 7 — but they were demonstrating that the recipe could carry you well past the blastocyst, into the early body plan stages.

The Zernicka-Goetz lab has also been important in another way. They've been outspoken about the ethical and policy implications of their work. Zernicka-Goetz has written and spoken extensively about the need for new ethical frameworks for synthetic embryos — frameworks that don't simply transplant the rules made for real embryos, because synthetic embryos are different in important ways, but also frameworks that don't pretend the ethical questions don't exist, because they clearly do. Her position has been roughly: this work is too important to stop, but too consequential to let proceed without scrutiny. We'll come back to that position when we discuss the 2021 ISSCR guidelines.

## SEGMENT 6 — Human blastoids: Polo, Hanna, and the 2021 papers

In March 2021, two papers appeared back-to-back in _Nature_ that, more than any other publications in the field, brought synthetic embryos out of the specialist literature and into the public conversation. One was from **Jose Polo**'s lab at Monash University in Australia. The other was from **Jacob Hanna**'s lab at the Weizmann Institute in Israel. Both papers reported the creation of **human blastoids** — blastocyst-like structures, made from human stem cells, that recapitulated the organization of a real human blastocyst.

The Polo paper used **human induced pluripotent stem cells** as the starting point. Through a careful sequence of culture conditions, the iPSCs were pushed to generate the three lineages of the early embryo — epiblast-like cells, trophectoderm-like cells, and hypoblast-like cells — in a single integrated process. The cells were aggregated in microwells, and within a few days they self-organized into structures that had the three lineages in the right positions, a central cavity, and the right overall size and shape. Human blastoids. From iPSCs. No real embryo involved at any step.

The Hanna paper, published in the same issue, took a different but converging approach. Hanna's group had been working for years on **naive human pluripotent stem cells** — a particular state of pluripotency that more closely resembles the inner cell mass of a blastocyst than the more conventional "primed" state of most human ES and iPSC lines. From naive cells, his group could derive both epiblast-like and trophoblast-like progeny in a single system, and combine them — along with hypoblast-like cells — into self-organizing blastocyst-like structures. Same general result, slightly different route. Human blastoids made from stem cells alone.

\[Pause.\]

Why did these papers cause a stir? Several reasons.

First, the structures were good. Under the microscope, a human blastoid from either lab is hard to distinguish from a real human blastocyst at first glance. The size is roughly right — about 200 microns. The cavity is right. The cell type distribution is roughly right — outer trophectoderm-like cells, inner cell mass-like cluster, hypoblast-like layer at the boundary. The molecular markers match. Single-cell sequencing showed that the three populations in the blastoid expressed roughly the right genes at roughly the right levels.

Second, the structures were testable. You could, in principle, ask whether a human blastoid could implant. Polo and Hanna both tested this with **in vitro implantation models** — taking the blastoid and putting it on a layer of endometrial cells, or in conditions that mimic the early uterine environment, and watching whether it attached and started to organize as an implantation event. To some degree, yes. The blastoids attached. They began to flatten and spread. They started behaving like implanting embryos. They were never transferred to actual human uteruses — that would have been ethically and legally impermissible — but the in vitro implantation experiments showed that the blastoids had functional, not just morphological, similarity to real blastocysts.

Third, the policy implications were immediate. Within weeks of the papers, ethics committees, professional societies, and regulatory bodies were grappling with what to do about human blastoids. Were they human embryos for legal purposes? The answer, in most jurisdictions, was unclear, because the laws had been written for embryos created by fertilization, and these structures were created from stem cells. The International Society for Stem Cell Research, the ISSCR, was finalizing its 2021 guidelines just as the papers came out, and the timing was not coincidental — the guidelines had to address synthetic embryos directly, and they did.

\[Pause.\]

The Hanna and Polo papers also illustrated something important about the state of the technology. **Efficiency is low.** Only a small fraction of starting aggregates — typically less than ten percent, sometimes much less — develop into properly organized blastoids. The rest are misshapen, missing one or more lineages, or just disorganized clumps. The papers reported their results by selecting the morphologically correct blastoids for analysis. This is honest practice — you have to specify what counts as a successful blastoid — but it also means that the headline numbers can obscure the noisiness of the actual experiments.

**Fidelity is imperfect.** Even the best human blastoids aren't perfect copies of real blastocysts. Single-cell sequencing has shown subtle differences in gene expression between blastoid cells and real blastocyst cells. The proportions of cell types can be off. Some markers are expressed too strongly, others not strongly enough. The blastoids are recognizable as models of blastocysts, but they're not indistinguishable. This matters scientifically — if you're using a blastoid to study a particular biological process, you need to know how well that process is recapitulated — and it matters ethically, because the imperfections are part of why many researchers argue blastoids should not be treated as full equivalents of real embryos.

**Variability is high between labs.** Different protocols give different blastoids. Some protocols give better trophoblast representation, others better epiblast, others better cavity formation. The field is still standardizing, still figuring out which differences in protocol matter and which don't. A blastoid from one lab in 2021 was not necessarily the same as a blastoid from another lab in 2022.

These caveats matter. The headline — "scientists make human embryos from stem cells" — is technically accurate but glosses over the imperfections that make blastoids more a useful model than a true equivalent. We'll come back to these limitations in Segment 13.

But the proof of concept is real. In 2021, two labs independently showed that you could take human iPSCs, run them through a defined protocol, and end up with structures that look and partly behave like human blastocysts. That fact, by itself, changed what was possible. The next question — and it was asked immediately — was: can we go further? Can we keep these structures developing past the blastocyst stage? Can we model implantation, gastrulation, early organogenesis? In humans, in vitro, from stem cells?

The answer, as we'll see in Segment 7 and Segment 11, was yes — at least partially. And the lab that pushed furthest was the same Hanna lab in Israel that had co-authored the 2021 human blastoid paper.

## SEGMENT 7 — Hanna's mouse synthetic embryos: heart and brain in a dish

In August 2022, **Jacob Hanna**'s group at the Weizmann Institute published a paper in _Cell_ titled "Post-gastrulation synthetic embryos generated ex utero from mouse naive ESCs." A follow-up paper in _Nature_ in 2023 extended the work. Taken together, these papers are, to my mind, the single most striking demonstrations the synthetic embryo field has produced. They are the demonstrations that make people gasp when they see the videos.

The setup was this. Hanna's group took **mouse pluripotent stem cells** — naive ES cells — and pushed them through a sequence of conditions designed to generate the three lineages of the early embryo. They combined the cells in a specialized **mechanical incubator** — a rotating bottle system that keeps the embryo-like structures gently agitated, with carefully controlled gas concentrations and continuous medium changes. This system had been developed in the same lab a couple of years earlier, and it had been shown to support development of real mouse embryos ex utero — outside the uterus — for substantially longer than any previous culture system.

Hanna's team applied this same system to stem-cell-derived aggregates. And what they saw, over the course of roughly eight days of culture, was extraordinary. The aggregates self-organized into structures that progressively looked more and more like real post-implantation mouse embryos. By day 8.5 of culture — equivalent to day 8.5 of real mouse development, which is around when the body plan is being laid down — the structures had: a recognizable head fold, with the beginnings of a forebrain. A neural tube extending along the body axis. **A beating heart.** Somites — the segmented blocks of mesoderm that will become vertebrae and ribs. A yolk sac. An allantois. Blood islands beginning to form. The early structures of essentially all the major organ systems of an early mammalian embryo.

\[Pause.\]

Let me let that sink in. **From stem cells alone — no egg, no sperm, no fertilization — they grew a mouse synthetic embryo that, by day 8.5 in vitro, had a beating heart and a forming brain.**

If you watch the videos that accompanied the papers, you see a translucent structure, a few millimeters across, with a clear head, a clear tail, segmented somites along the length of the body, and a tiny pulsating spot at the front where the early heart tube is contracting. The structure is suspended in liquid, in a glass rotating bottle, hooked up to nothing biological — no maternal blood supply, no uterus, no placenta beyond the synthetic embryo's own self-generated extraembryonic membranes. The whole thing is, as Hanna himself has put it, "a kind of self-contained mouse."

It's not a full embryo. The synthetic embryos have anatomical drift — proportions are sometimes off, particular structures are missing or extra. The efficiency is low — only a small fraction of starting aggregates make it to day 8.5 with normal-looking morphology. And the structures cannot be implanted into a real mouse and continue developing, at least not in any consistent way. The system has a ceiling, currently around day 8.5, beyond which the synthetic embryos fail. Real mouse development goes to day 19 or 20 to birth; the synthetic ones get a little less than halfway there.

But they get to the heart. They get to the beginnings of the brain. They get the somites and the early circulation. From stem cells. This is the most striking demonstration the field has produced.

\[Pause.\]

A few things to note about how this was done.

**The naive pluripotent state is key.** Hanna's lab works extensively with naive pluripotent cells — cells in a state that more closely resembles the inner cell mass of a real blastocyst than the more conventional "primed" pluripotent cells most labs use. Naive cells appear to be more capable of giving rise to both embryonic and extraembryonic lineages, which is part of why this approach works. Other labs working with primed cells have had a harder time getting the same range of lineage output.

**The mechanical incubator is critical.** Mouse embryos, in the uterus, develop in a rotating, well-perfused environment with constantly changing nutrient gradients. The rotating bottle system mimics aspects of this — keeping the embryo from settling, ensuring gas exchange, allowing waste removal. Without this kind of system, post-implantation development stops quickly. Hanna's lab spent years developing this incubator, and it's a key piece of why their work goes as far as it does.

**The signaling timing is crucial.** The cells need different cocktails at different stages. Pluripotency maintenance early on, then differentiation cues, then morphogenesis-supporting signals. Getting the timing right — when to switch from one medium to another, when to introduce particular factors, when to step back and let the structure self-organize — was the result of careful empirical work.

**The structures are mouse, not human.** Crucially. The Hanna 2022 and 2023 papers used mouse cells. The ethical landscape is much simpler for mouse work — mice are research animals, mouse embryo research is regulated but allowed in most jurisdictions, mouse synthetic embryos do not engage human embryo ethics directly. But everyone in the field knew, when these papers came out, that the obvious next step was to try the same approach with human cells. And as we'll see in Segment 11, that step was taken in 2023.

\[Pause.\]

The Hanna 2022 work crystallized, more than any prior paper, what was at stake in the field. The mouse synthetic embryos are not just blastocysts. They are post-implantation, gastrulated, organogenesis-stage structures. They have hearts. They have brains. They were made from stem cells. They were grown in a dish.

If the same recipe works with human cells — even partially — then the field has, in effect, created a way to study early human organogenesis without using human embryos. That's an enormous scientific opportunity. But it also means that the structures we're talking about are getting closer and closer to what most people, looking at them, would call embryos. The line between "model" and "thing being modeled" is getting fuzzy, and Hanna's mouse work was the moment when the fuzziness became impossible to ignore.

We're going to spend the next two segments stepping back from the science to talk about the ethics, because the ethical questions, by 2022, had become as central to the field as the science was.

## SEGMENT 8 — The 14-day rule: where the ethical limit came from

To understand the ethics of synthetic embryos, you need to understand the history of human embryo research ethics, which centers on something called **the 14-day rule**. This is the rule, agreed upon in most of the developed world for decades, that human embryos cannot be cultured in research labs beyond fourteen days after fertilization. Where did it come from? Why fourteen days? And why is it now under pressure?

The 14-day rule emerged in the late 1970s and early 1980s, in the wake of the first successful IVF birth — Louise Brown, born in 1978 — which suddenly made human embryos available for research in larger numbers than ever before. Before IVF, human embryos in research labs were extremely rare — occasional specimens from miscarriages, ectopic pregnancies, abortions. After IVF, there were spare embryos created during fertility treatment, some of which were donated for research. This created an urgent need for clear ethical guidelines: what kinds of research were permissible? Up to what point? Under what oversight?

In the UK, the **Warnock Committee** was convened in 1982 to address these questions. Chaired by the philosopher Mary Warnock, the committee published its report in 1984. It recommended, among other things, that human embryos could be used for research, with strict licensing, up to a specific developmental milestone: the appearance of the **primitive streak**. The primitive streak is a thickening on the embryonic disc that appears around day 14 to 15 of human development, and it marks the beginning of gastrulation — the moment when the three germ layers form and the body axes are definitively established. It's also, importantly, the last point at which **identical twinning** can occur. Before the primitive streak forms, an embryo can still split into two embryos. After the primitive streak, it cannot — the embryo becomes a single, individual, definitively-one-body developmental unit.

\[Pause.\]

The choice of the primitive streak was not random. It reflected a particular ethical reasoning. Before the primitive streak, the embryo is a cluster of cells that hasn't yet committed to being one individual. It can still twin. It can still fail. It is a precursor to individuality but isn't yet individual. After the primitive streak, the embryo is, in some morally relevant sense, a unique developing organism. The Warnock Committee argued that this transition was a defensible place to draw a line.

Fourteen days post-fertilization is when the primitive streak appears, in the typical case. So the recommendation became: research on human embryos is permitted up to day 14 or the appearance of the primitive streak, whichever comes first.

This recommendation was codified into UK law in the Human Fertilisation and Embryology Act of 1990. Similar rules were adopted in most other countries that permitted any embryo research at all — through legislation in some places, through professional society guidelines in others, through institutional review board policies elsewhere. By the 1990s, the 14-day rule was the global norm. The International Society for Stem Cell Research, the ISSCR — the main professional body for this work — included it as a strict prohibition in their guidelines.

\[Pause.\]

A few important nuances about the 14-day rule, because they bear on what comes next.

**It was always partly pragmatic.** For most of the history of the rule, you couldn't grow a human embryo in a lab to fourteen days even if you wanted to. The technology simply wasn't there. Embryos in IVF labs were typically transferred to a uterus or frozen by day five or six — the blastocyst stage. Beyond that, they died. So the 14-day rule was, for decades, a theoretical limit. Nobody was bumping up against it because nobody could get close.

**The line was always somewhat arbitrary.** The Warnock Committee acknowledged this. The primitive streak is a real biological milestone, but ethical reasoning about embryos doesn't reduce to "is the primitive streak present." You could have drawn the line earlier — at the blastocyst, say — or later — at the heartbeat, or at recognizable brain activity, or at viability outside the womb. The 14-day point was chosen because it was a clear, biologically grounded milestone that had defensible moral significance. But other defensible lines could have been drawn.

**It applied to embryos made by fertilization.** This is crucial. The 14-day rule was written in 1984, decades before synthetic embryos existed. The drafters were thinking about embryos created by sperm fertilizing egg. The question of whether the rule applied to embryos created some other way — by somatic cell nuclear transfer, for example, or by stem cell assembly — wasn't on the radar. The rule was about preimplantation human embryos as they had existed for thousands of years, just now in a laboratory dish.

That last nuance is what's been blown apart by synthetic embryos. If a structure is made from stem cells, with no fertilization event, does the 14-day rule apply at all? If it does, then the developmental clock has to be inferred — how do you measure "days post-fertilization" for an embryo that wasn't fertilized? If it doesn't, then synthetic embryos exist outside the entire ethical framework that was built for embryo research, and a new framework has to be constructed.

\[Pause.\]

The 14-day rule, then, is both a real ethical constraint and a historical artifact. It represents decades of careful moral reasoning about when an embryo becomes individuated, when it gains additional moral status, when research limitations should kick in. But it was built for a different kind of object than the one synthetic embryos represent. As long as the rule was a theoretical limit — as long as nobody could grow a real embryo close to fourteen days anyway — the question of how to apply it to synthetic embryos didn't matter much.

But starting around 2016, the technology to grow real human embryos in dishes started to improve. Magdalena Zernicka-Goetz's group and Ali Brivanlou's group, working in parallel, developed culture systems that allowed real human embryos donated from IVF to be cultured in dishes for substantially longer than before — close to the 14-day limit. And in the same window, synthetic embryos became real. Suddenly the 14-day rule, which had been a theoretical limit for thirty years, was both a practical limit (because real embryos could now reach it) and a deeply ambiguous limit (because synthetic embryos didn't clearly fall under it).

The field, by 2020 or so, needed an updated framework. That's where the 2021 ISSCR guidelines come in.

## SEGMENT 9 — The 2021 ISSCR update: the rule that moved

In May 2021, the International Society for Stem Cell Research published a major revision of its guidelines for stem cell research and clinical translation. This was the first comprehensive update since 2016, and it had been in preparation for years, by a committee of scientists, bioethicists, lawyers, and other stakeholders. The update was wide-ranging, but two pieces of it are directly relevant to today's lecture.

**First, the 14-day rule was removed from the list of prohibited research activities.** It wasn't abolished. Embryos beyond fourteen days were not green-lit. But the rule was moved from the "forbidden" category to a category requiring case-by-case review by a specialized research oversight committee. In effect, the ISSCR was saying: this is not an absolute prohibition anymore. If you want to extend the time window past fourteen days, you can apply for permission. You'll have to make a strong scientific case, you'll have to justify why the work can't be done in any other way, you'll have to commit to specific limits and oversight. But the door is open.

This was a substantial change. For thirty years, fourteen days had been a hard line — research society guidelines and national laws had treated it as essentially uncrossable. The 2021 update reframed it as a case-by-case judgment, on the grounds that the original justification — the impossibility of growing embryos that long — had been overtaken by technology, and that important scientific questions could be addressed by allowing carefully reviewed extensions.

**Second, the ISSCR explicitly addressed synthetic embryos for the first time.** The guidelines introduced terminology — they called them **stem-cell-based embryo models** or **integrated and non-integrated embryo models**, depending on whether they contained all the major lineages or only some. They distinguished synthetic embryo research from real-embryo research, but they explicitly subjected it to the same oversight committees and many of the same constraints. They recommended that human integrated embryo models — those containing all the early lineages and capable of recapitulating the architecture of a real embryo — be subject to specialized research oversight comparable to that for real human embryos.

In other words: the ISSCR took the position that synthetic embryos that closely resemble real embryos should be treated, for ethical purposes, more or less like real embryos. Not identically — there are differences — but with comparable oversight, comparable limits, comparable scrutiny.

\[Pause.\]

The 2021 ISSCR update is, I think, the single most important policy document in the synthetic embryo field. It changed the rules under which the field operates. It moved the 14-day line from "absolute" to "discretionary." It brought synthetic embryos formally into the regulatory conversation. And it explicitly acknowledged that the ethics here are evolving in real time, that the field needs ongoing scrutiny, that no one document can settle these questions definitively.

A few observations about the 2021 update.

**It was a society guideline, not law.** The ISSCR doesn't make law. Its guidelines are influential but advisory. Individual countries — through their laws and regulatory bodies — still set the legal boundaries. The UK, for instance, kept the 14-day rule in law, even as the ISSCR softened it in guidelines. We'll talk about the legal landscape in Segment 17.

**It explicitly anticipated synthetic embryos getting more sophisticated.** The 2021 guidelines were written knowing that the Hanna lab's mouse synthetic embryos were already showing remarkable development, and knowing that human synthetic embryos at similar stages were likely. The framework had to be flexible enough to accommodate not just where the field was but where it was clearly heading.

**It generated significant controversy.** Some ethicists welcomed the change as a long-overdue recognition that the 14-day rule had become outdated. Others viewed it as the field policing itself in a direction that suited its scientific interests, weakening protections without adequate public deliberation. The argument continues. Different jurisdictions have responded differently — some adopting the ISSCR framework wholesale, others holding to stricter limits, others remaining permissive without much oversight at all.

**It explicitly recognized that the ethics here is evolving.** The document itself notes that synthetic embryos blur the categories on which the previous ethical framework was built, and that further guidance will be needed as the field develops. This is, I think, intellectually honest — the field doesn't have a settled answer to what synthetic embryos are or how they should be treated. The ISSCR was articulating a starting framework, not a final answer.

\[Pause.\]

The change in the 14-day rule deserves a bit more attention, because it's been one of the most discussed pieces of the update. The 2021 guidelines did not say: research on embryos beyond fourteen days is now allowed. They said: the prohibition is no longer absolute, and proposals to extend the time window can be considered on a case-by-case basis by specialized research oversight committees. The change is real, but it's a procedural change — the substantive limit is now decided locally rather than dictated globally. Some committees, presumably, will approve extensions for compelling scientific cases. Others, presumably, will not. The result is a more flexible, but also more variable, regulatory landscape.

This matters because a few labs immediately started preparing proposals to extend the time window. The technical capability — to grow real human embryos in dishes past fourteen days — was emerging. With the ISSCR's procedural opening, those experiments became approvable, at least in principle. As of the time of this lecture, several have been approved, in several countries, under careful oversight.

Whether you think this is the right direction depends on your starting position. If you believe that early-embryo research yields important biomedical knowledge that can be obtained no other way, the ISSCR opening is welcome. If you believe that the moral status of the human embryo is foundational and that the 14-day rule was a hard-won ethical achievement that shouldn't be eroded, the change is troubling. Most working biologists I know are somewhere in between — they recognize the scientific value, they take the ethical concerns seriously, they want oversight to be real rather than nominal. The field is, in good faith, trying to navigate this.

We'll come back to the legal landscape in Segment 17. For now, hold the picture: the 14-day rule still exists, but it's no longer absolute. Synthetic embryos are formally inside the regulatory framework, treated comparably to real embryos when they closely resemble them. The ethics is evolving, and the 2021 ISSCR update was the inflection point.

## SEGMENT 10 — Defining "embryo": the question that won't go away

Welcome back. We spent the first half of the lecture mostly on what synthetic embryos are and how they're made, with one detour into the policy history. The second half is going to be more conceptual and forward-looking. We're going to start with what may be the deepest question in the field: **what counts as an embryo?**

This sounds like a definitional quibble, but it isn't. It's actually the question that drives most of the ethical debate, most of the policy work, and a fair amount of the scientific debate too. If we don't know what an embryo is — or rather, if we don't agree on what an embryo is — then we can't say which structures are subject to embryo-research rules, which are not, which require which kinds of oversight, which can be made freely and which need authorization. The definition is doing real work.

For most of history, the definition of "embryo" was simple in practice if complex in philosophy. An embryo was what you got when an egg was fertilized by a sperm. The product of conception. The early developmental stages of an organism, before the fetal period. There were edge cases — twinning, molar pregnancies, parthenogenesis in some species — but the central concept was clear. Egg plus sperm, divisions begin, embryo exists.

Synthetic embryos blow this up. No egg. No sperm. No fertilization event. But you still get a structure with trophoblast on the outside and pluripotent cells on the inside, with the right cavity, with the molecular markers of an early embryo, capable of triggering implantation responses in maternal tissue. Is that an embryo?

\[Pause.\]

The field has roughly three positions on this question, and I want to walk you through them so you can have your own opinion.

**Position one: an embryo is defined by its origin.** On this view, an embryo is what you get when egg and sperm combine and start developing. Synthetic embryos, no matter how realistic, are not embryos in this strict sense, because they don't come from a fertilization event. They're models of embryos. They may be very good models, but they're still models. They should be regulated as research tools, with appropriate oversight, but not as embryos per se. The ethical concerns about destroying embryos don't apply, because you're not destroying anything that was ever an embryo.

This position has the virtue of clarity. It draws a bright line, and it's hard to argue with on definitional grounds — if the word "embryo" means "the product of fertilization," then synthetic embryos aren't embryos. But it has the weakness that it can lead to counterintuitive conclusions. If a synthetic embryo is morphologically and functionally indistinguishable from a real embryo, can really implant in a uterus, can really develop into a fetus given the right environment — does it really make moral sense to treat it as a totally different kind of object, just because its origin is different?

**Position two: an embryo is defined by its developmental potential.** On this view, an embryo is anything that, given the right conditions, can develop into a full organism. This focuses not on origin but on what the structure is capable of becoming. By this definition, a real embryo is an embryo because it can become a baby; a synthetic embryo is an embryo if and only if it can also become a baby. Under this view, the question of whether a particular synthetic structure is an embryo depends on its developmental potential, which depends on the structure's completeness, fidelity, and capacity to implant and continue developing.

This position aligns well with traditional ethical reasoning about embryos — the special status of embryos has typically been linked to their potential to develop into people. If synthetic embryos really have that potential, they share that special status. If they don't, they don't. The weakness is that "potential" is hard to measure. Many synthetic embryos can't develop very far. Some real embryos can't develop very far either — they have genetic defects that doom them. Where do you draw the line?

**Position three: an embryo is defined by morphological and molecular similarity.** On this view, what matters isn't origin or potential but resemblance. If a structure looks like an embryo and expresses the same molecular markers, then it should be treated like an embryo, regardless of how it was made. This is essentially the position the 2021 ISSCR guidelines move toward — they call the most embryo-like synthetic structures "integrated embryo models" and recommend they be treated comparably to real embryos.

The strength of this position is that it's pragmatic. You can examine a structure, look at its morphology, sequence its cells, decide how embryo-like it is, and apply rules accordingly. The weakness is that "similar enough" is itself a judgment, and the line is fuzzy. Synthetic embryos vary in how well they recapitulate real embryos. Where do you set the threshold?

\[Pause.\]

In practice, the field uses a mix of these definitions. Regulatory bodies tend to use a combination of origin and similarity — a synthetic structure is treated more like an embryo the more closely it resembles one and the more developmentally complete it is. Scientific descriptions of these structures often use careful language — "blastocyst-like structure," "embryo model," "stem-cell-derived embryo-like structure" — to avoid implicitly committing to any one definition.

But the underlying disagreement is real, and it surfaces in different ways. It surfaces when journalists ask whether scientists have "created human embryos in the lab" — the answer depends on which definition you use. It surfaces when ethicists debate whether destroying a synthetic embryo is morally problematic — depends on the definition. It surfaces when regulators decide whether a synthetic-embryo experiment needs IVF-clinic-grade oversight or just regular biological research review — depends on the definition.

My own view, for what it's worth, is that the definition question doesn't have a clean answer because the underlying ethical concept is doing too many jobs at once. The concept of "embryo" was developed in a world where the only embryos were the products of conception, and where their moral status was tied to their potential to become babies. In that world, the definition didn't need to be precise — every embryo was both originated by fertilization and possessed of developmental potential, so you didn't have to choose. Synthetic embryos pull these criteria apart. Now you can have something with embryonic potential but non-embryonic origin, or with embryonic origin but no real developmental potential. The old concept doesn't slice cleanly through these new objects.

The honest answer, I think, is that "embryo" is becoming a family of related concepts rather than a single concept, and that ethical and legal frameworks will have to track that pluralization. Different kinds of embryo-like objects will need different rules, and the rules will depend on what features the object has — origin, completeness, developmental progress, capacity to implant — rather than on a binary "embryo or not."

The field is working this out, slowly, in real time. There's no clean answer yet. And the next two segments — about the most recent human synthetic-embryo work — are going to make the question even more urgent.

## SEGMENT 11 — The 2023 human ETX embryos: gastrulation in a dish

Now we come to the most recent — and possibly most consequential — developments in the field. In 2023, multiple labs published papers showing that the recipe for mouse synthetic embryos could be extended to humans, with human cells, producing structures that developed past the blastocyst stage and into the early post-implantation period — through the start of gastrulation, with recognizable early body axes and beginnings of organogenesis-stage events.

Two papers stand out, both published in June and September 2023. The first was from **Magdalena Zernicka-Goetz**'s group at the University of Cambridge and Caltech. The second, again, was from **Jacob Hanna**'s group at the Weizmann Institute. The two groups had been working in parallel on the same problem, with somewhat different approaches, and they reported converging results within a few months of each other.

The Hanna paper, published in _Nature_ in September 2023, used **naive human pluripotent stem cells** as the starting point. Through a sequence of culture conditions analogous to the mouse synthetic embryo protocol, the cells were pushed to generate all three early lineages — epiblast, trophoblast, and hypoblast — in a coordinated way. They were then allowed to self-organize in a defined gel and culture environment. By the equivalent of day 14 of human development — the same time when the primitive streak first appears in real embryos, the same time when the original 14-day rule says you have to stop — the synthetic structures had:

- A recognizable epiblast disc
- A surrounding hypoblast layer
- Trophoblast outer cells with appropriate subtypes
- A pro-amniotic cavity
- Early signs of axis polarization
- The beginnings of primitive-streak-like organization in some specimens

These are not full embryos at this stage. They're early post-implantation models — at roughly the same developmental level as a real human embryo around day 12 to 14. They don't have hearts. They don't have brains. The mouse synthetic embryos at day 8.5 are much more advanced than the human synthetic embryos at day 14, because the developmental timing differs — humans are slower, and the technical hurdles for pushing human cultures further are still being worked out.

\[Pause.\]

The Zernicka-Goetz paper, posted as a preprint in June 2023 and ultimately published in late 2023, reported a similar achievement. Human stem cells, combined in the right ratios with appropriate signaling, formed structures with the three lineages, with appropriate axis specification, with early gastrulation-like organization. The protocols differed in details, the structures had different efficiencies and slightly different morphological features, but the underlying message was the same: **the recipe works in humans, at least up to the early post-implantation period, at least at proof-of-concept levels.**

A few things to note.

**This was done under careful oversight.** Both labs operated under their institutional ethics frameworks, which by 2023 had been updated in light of the 2021 ISSCR guidelines. The work was reviewed by specialized embryo research oversight committees. The constructs were not transferred to any human uterus and were not intended to be — that would be prohibited in essentially every jurisdiction and is firmly excluded by ISSCR guidelines. The work was bench science, with carefully specified developmental endpoints.

**The structures vary in fidelity.** As with all synthetic embryo work, the human ETX models are imperfect copies of real embryos. Some lineages are over- or underrepresented. The proportions can be off. Single-cell sequencing has shown subtle differences from real embryos at comparable stages. The work clearly demonstrates the principle — humans can be modeled this way — but it doesn't claim to produce indistinguishable copies of real embryos.

**Efficiency remains low.** Many starting aggregates fail to develop into proper structures. The papers report yields in the percent or low-tens-of-percent range, depending on protocol details and criteria. Most starting material doesn't become a proper model. The successful ones are selected morphologically for downstream analysis.

**The structures cannot continue developing indefinitely.** As of 2023, no human synthetic embryo had been pushed to anything close to the heart-and-brain stage that mouse work reaches at day 8.5. The technical challenges for extending human cultures are significant, both in terms of culture conditions and in terms of ethics — the further you push, the more the ethical scrutiny intensifies, and the more uncertain the regulatory status becomes.

\[Pause.\]

The 2023 human ETX work is, in my view, the most consequential development since the 2021 blastoid papers. It demonstrates that the synthetic-embryo project — the project of modeling early human development in dishes, from stem cells — is technically achievable, not just for the blastocyst stage but for the post-implantation stages that have historically been inaccessible. The window into the black box is opening.

It also brings the ethical questions into very sharp focus. If a synthetic structure recapitulates the events of the second and third weeks of human development — implantation, gastrulation, the early body plan — then we're at exactly the developmental window that the 14-day rule was designed to limit. The fact that the structure was made from stem cells, not from fertilization, doesn't make the developmental events themselves less morally significant. Or, depending on your view, it makes them somewhat less morally significant — because the structure wasn't initiated by a conception event, because it isn't traceable to any potential person — but how much less is exactly the question the field is debating.

The 2023 papers were generally well-received in the scientific community as careful, rigorous, important work. They were generally received with concern in the bioethics community as a development that pushes the field into territory where the ethical guidelines haven't fully caught up. Both reactions are appropriate. The work is good science. The implications are unsettled.

We're now at the point where the technical and the ethical have to be discussed together — you can't have a sensible conversation about one without the other. That's where the field is. That's where we'll spend the rest of the lecture.

## SEGMENT 12 — Applications: what synthetic embryos are actually for

We've spent a lot of time on the structures themselves and on the ethical landscape. Let me step back and remind us why anyone wants to do this work in the first place. What are synthetic embryos actually for, scientifically and medically?

**Application one: understanding early human development.** This is the foundational scientific motivation. The first two to four weeks of human development are when most things go wrong. Most miscarriages happen here. Most birth defects originate here. Most failed IVF cycles fail here. And it's the period we know least about, because real embryos are inaccessible during this window. Synthetic embryos give us, for the first time, a system to study human implantation, gastrulation, and early body-plan formation at scale, with full experimental control. What signals does the epiblast send to the trophoblast? When does the primitive streak emerge molecularly? How is left-right symmetry broken? What goes wrong when these events fail? Synthetic embryos let us ask these questions in ways we couldn't before.

**Application two: modeling genetic diseases of early development.** Many genetic disorders manifest at or before birth — neural tube defects, congenital heart disease, congenital kidney disease, and so on. Studying the early molecular pathology of these conditions in humans has been nearly impossible. With synthetic embryos derived from iPSCs of patients carrying disease mutations, you can recreate the early developmental events of that patient's biology and watch where things go wrong. This is potentially transformative for disease models. A patient with a known mutation in a developmental gene can, in principle, donate skin cells; those cells can be reprogrammed to iPSCs; those iPSCs can be assembled into synthetic embryos; and the developmental defect specific to that patient's mutation can be studied at the cellular and molecular level.

**Application three: drug screening on early development.** Many drugs cause birth defects when taken during pregnancy — thalidomide is the historical example, but the list is long, and pregnancy categories on drug labels are based on often-incomplete animal data. Synthetic embryos could, in principle, allow systematic screening of drugs for effects on early human development. Take a panel of synthetic embryos, expose them to a candidate drug at various doses and developmental stages, and see what happens. This is still aspirational — the throughput isn't there yet, the readouts aren't standardized, and the regulatory status is uncertain — but in principle it's a major application.

**Application four: improving IVF.** Roughly half of all IVF cycles fail, and most failures happen in the first two weeks after embryo transfer — implantation failures and early miscarriages. We don't know, in any detail, why these failures happen. Synthetic embryos allow us to study implantation directly, model it in vitro, and ask which factors — embryo factors, endometrial factors, signaling factors — affect implantation success. Improved understanding could translate into better IVF outcomes. Several groups are now using blastoid models to screen factors that affect implantation, with the explicit goal of improving fertility treatment.

\[Pause.\]

**Application five: understanding why most conceptions fail.** This connects to IVF but is broader. The estimated rate of conceptions that never make it to a live birth — including very early losses that happen before a pregnancy is even recognized — is high, perhaps 50% or more. We don't know why. Some fraction is genetic — chromosomal abnormalities that doom early embryos. But a large fraction isn't, and those failures involve subtle issues with implantation, signaling, or early developmental coordination. Synthetic embryos provide a system to study these failure modes systematically, which we couldn't do before.

**Application six: studying placentation.** The placenta is one of the most remarkable organs in mammalian biology. It's also one of the least understood, particularly in humans. It develops from trophoblast, it interacts intimately with maternal tissue, it secretes hormones, it filters nutrients and waste, it can fail in subtle ways that cause pregnancy complications. Now that we have human trophoblast stem cells and synthetic embryo models, we can study placental development in vitro, model placental disorders like preeclampsia and intrauterine growth restriction, and potentially develop interventions.

**Application seven: regenerative medicine, indirectly.** Synthetic embryos themselves are not going to be transplanted into people for regenerative medicine — that's not the point. But the cell types you can isolate from them, and the developmental biology you can learn from them, feed back into regenerative medicine. Understanding how, for instance, the heart first forms in a synthetic embryo could improve protocols for differentiating iPSCs into heart cells for cell therapy. The synthetic embryo is a developmental biology lab in a dish, and the lessons it teaches propagate outward into many other applications.

\[Pause.\]

There's a temptation — common in popular coverage of this field — to focus on the most speculative or dramatic applications. People talk about "growing humans in artificial wombs" or "designer babies from stem cells" and so on. We'll come back to the long-term frontier in Segment 18, and some of those scenarios are worth taking seriously. But for now, in 2024 and 2025, the bread-and-butter applications are the ones I just listed. Understanding early development. Modeling diseases. Screening drugs. Improving IVF. Studying placentation. These are not speculative. These are happening now, in labs around the world, with synthetic embryo models as the experimental substrate.

The reason the field is exciting is not that it might someday let us do something dramatic. It's that, in the present, it's giving us experimental access to a period of human development that we've never had access to before. The first two to four weeks of human life have been, until now, a black box. Synthetic embryos are starting to open that box.

The question is how far it's appropriate to open it, and what we do with what we see. That's the conversation we'll continue in the remaining segments.

## SEGMENT 13 — Limitations: what synthetic embryos don't do

Let me spend a segment on what synthetic embryos can't do, because the limitations are real and important and they bear directly on both the scientific applications and the ethical assessment.

**Limitation one: low efficiency.** I've mentioned this several times. Across essentially every synthetic embryo protocol, only a minority of starting aggregates develops into well-formed structures. Reported efficiencies vary wildly depending on what you count — sometimes a few percent, sometimes tens of percent for the simpler structures like blastoids. For the most advanced post-implantation models, the proportion of starting aggregates that develop into anything resembling a proper mid-development structure is small. The rest fail — they're disorganized, they're missing lineages, they don't form the right cavities, they don't acquire the right axes. This means a lot of starting material goes to waste, and it means that the structures used for downstream experiments are a selected, possibly atypical subset of all the things you could have made. This is not a flaw per se — many biological experiments work this way — but it's an important caveat.

**Limitation two: anatomical drift.** Even the well-formed synthetic embryos aren't perfect copies of real embryos. They have characteristic drifts — cell type proportions that are slightly off, particular structures that are over- or under-represented, axes that don't always align with the conventions of real development. A mouse synthetic embryo at day 8.5, for example, might have the right structures but with slightly distorted proportions, or with particular cell types in slightly wrong positions, or with axis orientations that vary from specimen to specimen. The structures are recognizable models, not photographic reproductions.

**Limitation three: incomplete cell type coverage.** Real embryos contain not just the major lineages but many specialized cell types within each lineage. Real trophoblast, by mid-development, has differentiated into syncytiotrophoblast and extravillous trophoblast and various other subtypes. Real epiblast gives rise to highly specialized cells of each germ layer. Synthetic embryos capture the major populations but often miss the specialized subtypes, or get them in wrong proportions, or get them on wrong timing. This matters when you're trying to model a particular developmental event that depends on these subtypes.

**Limitation four: developmental time ceiling.** The most striking demonstration we have, Hanna's mouse synthetic embryos, gets to day 8.5. Real mouse development goes to day 19 to 20. So even at the cutting edge, synthetic embryos cover less than half of mouse development. Human synthetic embryos cover even less — currently into the early post-implantation period, but not yet into anything close to organogenesis. Pushing the time ceiling further requires solving multiple technical problems simultaneously — better culture systems, vascular support, the right signaling at each stage. Progress is happening, but slowly.

**Limitation five: no real placenta, no maternal interface.** A real pregnancy is a complex interaction between embryo and mother — the maternal blood supply, the maternal immune system, the uterine environment, the hormonal cross-talk. Synthetic embryos in dishes don't have any of this. The trophoblast cells are present, and they do some of the things real trophoblast does, but they're not attached to a real uterus. They don't get maternal blood. Many aspects of normal development that depend on the maternal interface — proper vascular development, immune tolerance establishment, certain hormonal feedback loops — are missing or distorted in the dish.

\[Pause.\]

**Limitation six: variability between labs.** I've mentioned this for blastoids, but it applies to all synthetic embryo work. Different protocols give different results. A "synthetic embryo" from one lab is not necessarily directly comparable to a "synthetic embryo" from another lab. This is normal for a young field — protocols haven't standardized, the field is still figuring out what matters and what doesn't — but it makes cross-comparison and meta-analysis harder.

**Limitation seven: incomplete recapitulation of human-specific biology.** Most of the most advanced synthetic embryo work has been done in mouse. Translating those successes to human cells is technically demanding, and even successful human work currently lags well behind what's possible in mouse. We have human blastoids; we have human early-post-implantation structures; we don't yet have human equivalents of Hanna's day-8.5 mouse synthetic embryos. The cell biology of humans differs from mouse in important ways — the timing of events, the signaling pathways involved, the relative importance of different lineages. So scaling up from mouse to human is not just a matter of changing the cells.

\[Pause.\]

These limitations matter for two reasons.

Scientifically, they bound what you can conclude from synthetic embryo experiments. If you treat a synthetic embryo with a drug and see a particular effect, you have to be careful about extrapolating to real embryos, especially across species. The model is a model. It tells you something, but it tells you something less than the thing being modeled.

Ethically, they bound how seriously the structures should be taken as moral analogs of real embryos. The position that synthetic embryos should be regulated like real embryos depends, at least in part, on how closely they resemble real embryos. If synthetic embryos have major developmental drifts, can't make it past a particular stage, can't really continue developing into a fetus, then the case for treating them like real embryos weakens. Conversely, as the technology improves and the limitations shrink, the case for stricter regulation strengthens.

This is the tension at the heart of the field. The better the model is, the more useful it is — but also the more it triggers the ethical concerns that the limitations had been holding at bay. As synthetic embryos get better, the ethics has to get more careful. As the limitations shrink, the regulatory questions intensify.

For now, in 2025, the limitations are substantial. Synthetic embryos are useful models, but they are clearly models. They are not, by any reasonable measure, indistinguishable from real embryos. That's why most regulatory frameworks treat them somewhat differently — typically with comparable oversight but not identical legal status. As the technology improves, the frameworks will have to update too. We're going to come back to this in Segment 14, with a slightly different framing.

## SEGMENT 14 — The two axes: developmental progress and morphological similarity

I want to give you a conceptual framework that's been useful, both in the literature and in my own thinking, for sorting out what kinds of synthetic embryos there are and how each kind should be treated. The framework involves two axes.

**Axis one: developmental progress.** How far along the developmental timeline does the structure go? An embryoid body that just sits there as a clump of differentiating cells is at one end. A blastoid that recapitulates the blastocyst stage is somewhat further along. An ETX-style early post-implantation model is further still. A Hanna-style synthetic embryo with a beating heart is much further. And at the far end, hypothetically, would be a fully gestated synthetic embryo carried to viability. The axis runs from "barely organized" to "complete development."

**Axis two: morphological similarity.** How closely does the structure resemble a real embryo at the developmental stage it occupies? You can have a blastoid that's a recognizable but somewhat off blastocyst, or a blastoid that's almost indistinguishable from a real blastocyst under the microscope. You can have a post-implantation structure that has roughly the right organization but with notable drift, or one that's a near-perfect copy. The axis runs from "loosely embryo-shaped" to "indistinguishable from a real embryo."

These two axes are independent. You can have a structure that covers a lot of developmental progress with relatively low morphological fidelity — a sort of "long and sloppy" model. You can have a structure that covers little developmental progress but with very high fidelity — a "short and sharp" model. Most synthetic embryos sit somewhere in the middle, and as the technology improves, the frontier moves toward the upper right of the diagram: more developmental progress, with higher fidelity.

\[Pause.\]

This framework helps because it gives us a vocabulary for talking about which structures should be subject to which kinds of oversight. Roughly, the further along both axes you go, the more the structure starts to look like the thing real-embryo ethics was originally designed to regulate.

A clump of differentiating iPSCs that doesn't organize into anything embryo-like is not subject to embryo research rules. It's just a cell culture experiment. A blastoid that recapitulates blastocyst-stage organization with moderate fidelity is starting to engage embryo research rules, but with relatively limited concern because it's at an early stage that, even in real embryos, doesn't trigger the most stringent ethical limits. An ETX-style structure that's getting close to gastrulation, with high fidelity, is engaging the central ethical concerns of the 14-day rule directly. A hypothetical future synthetic embryo that reproduces organogenesis with high fidelity is engaging concerns that the 14-day rule didn't even contemplate.

This is, I think, the practical way the field is going to evolve regulatorily. Not "synthetic embryos are or aren't embryos" as a binary, but a graded set of categories based on where along these two axes a particular construct sits. The ISSCR guidelines are already moving in this direction — they distinguish between "integrated" embryo models (with all the major lineages) and "non-integrated" ones (missing some), and they emphasize developmental milestones over construct identity.

\[Pause.\]

Let me suggest a specific question to ask about any synthetic embryo construct, which captures both axes:

**At what real-embryo developmental stage does this construct sit, and how convincingly?**

If the answer is "at the morula stage, very loosely" — there's not much ethical concern, and not much practical use. If it's "at the blastocyst stage, very convincingly" — there's modest ethical concern (because blastocyst stage in real embryos has accepted research uses), and there are real applications. If it's "at the gastrulation stage, very convincingly" — there's substantial ethical concern (because this is the stage the 14-day rule was protecting), and there are very valuable applications. If it's "at organogenesis stage, very convincingly" — there's intense ethical concern, and the construct is approaching a developmental status that, in real embryos, would clearly be a moral patient by most ethical frameworks.

The framework also helps clarify a confusion I sometimes see in popular coverage. People sometimes argue that synthetic embryos can't be "real" embryos because they're imperfect, or because they can't be implanted into a uterus and gestated, or because they have anatomical drifts. These are all morphological-fidelity arguments — they're saying the structures are low on Axis Two. But Axis One — developmental progress — is independent. A synthetic embryo that reaches the gastrulation stage, even with significant morphological drift, is still recapitulating events that, in real embryos, occur after the traditional 14-day limit. The fidelity argument doesn't make the developmental-progress concern go away.

Conversely, people sometimes argue that any synthetic embryo is a moral problem because it might be an embryo. This is an Axis-Two argument that ignores Axis One. A construct that's morphologically embryo-like but doesn't recapitulate any significant developmental progress is, in most reasonable views, less ethically charged than one that pushes deep into gastrulation.

The two-axis framework forces both questions to be considered together. That's why I find it useful.

\[Pause.\]

One more observation. The frontier of the field is moving toward the upper right corner of the diagram. Better protocols, better culture systems, better cell sources, better understanding of the signaling are all pushing both fidelity and developmental progress upward. Five years ago, the cutting edge was blastoid-stage with moderate fidelity. Today, the cutting edge is post-implantation stage with moderate-to-high fidelity in humans, and well into organogenesis in mouse. In five more years, it's reasonable to expect human models will push further into post-implantation and possibly into early organogenesis stages, with higher fidelity.

This trajectory matters because it means the ethical concerns are not static. What's acceptable today, with today's limitations, may need to be reconsidered tomorrow as the constructs improve. The 2021 ISSCR guidelines were written with this in mind — they're designed to be updated as the technology evolves. The question of whether the updates will happen quickly enough, and whether they'll be stringent enough to match the technical capabilities, is part of what the field is wrestling with.

## SEGMENT 15 — Yamanaka, Knoblich, and the ethics from the inside

I want to spend a segment on what some of the most prominent scientists in this and adjacent fields have actually said about the ethics, because their views matter and because they're often more nuanced than the popular framing.

**Shinya Yamanaka**, who shared the 2012 Nobel Prize for discovering iPSCs, has been outspoken about both the promise and the ethical risks of pluripotent stem cell technology. His original motivation, in part, for iPSC reprogramming was to find an alternative to embryonic stem cells precisely because of the ethical concerns about embryo destruction. He's expressed mixed feelings about the application of iPSCs to synthetic embryo work. On the one hand, the research uses are important. On the other hand, the use of iPSCs to make embryo-like structures — possibly indistinguishable from embryos derived by fertilization — recapitulates the ethical concerns that iPSCs were originally meant to bypass.

Yamanaka has, in various public statements, supported careful research on synthetic embryos while expressing concern about the trajectory. His position, simplified, is roughly: this work should proceed, with strong oversight; the question of what counts as a human embryo for ethical purposes is an open question that society needs to engage with, not just scientists; and the field has an obligation to be transparent about what's being created and why.

**Jürgen Knoblich**, the Austrian developmental biologist who co-pioneered cerebral organoids and has thought carefully about brain organoid ethics, has commented on the relationship between organoids and synthetic embryos. His view — and I find this view persuasive — is that organoids and synthetic embryos sit on a continuum, and that the ethical issues are not entirely different in kind. A complex cerebral organoid, modeling early brain development, is also a structure made from stem cells that recapitulates aspects of human development. A synthetic embryo is, in some ways, a generalization of the organoid concept — instead of modeling one organ system, you're modeling the whole early embryo.

The difference, Knoblich has argued, is mostly about completeness and integration. A cerebral organoid is a piece — it has only some of the developmental machinery, it doesn't have the rest of the body, it isn't going anywhere. A synthetic embryo has, potentially, all the developmental machinery in coordinated form, and it could, in principle, continue developing if the right conditions were available. That difference is real and morally relevant, even though both kinds of constructs share the underlying stem cell biology.

\[Pause.\]

**Magdalena Zernicka-Goetz**, whose lab has produced some of the most advanced synthetic embryo work, has been a thoughtful public voice on the ethics. She has argued that the field should not be stopped — the scientific opportunities are too significant, and there are important questions about human development that can only be answered this way. But she has also argued that the work needs careful ethical scrutiny, that researchers in the field have an obligation to engage with the public and with ethicists, and that simple "research is good" framings are inadequate. She has been a participant in many of the ISSCR guideline discussions and has pushed for careful, transparent oversight.

**Jacob Hanna**, whose lab has produced perhaps the most striking demonstrations, has been somewhat more provocative in his framing. He has argued that synthetic embryos open new opportunities for treating infertility, for developing organs for transplant, and for understanding development at unprecedented depth. He has, in various interviews, talked about possibilities like growing organs in synthetic embryo environments — using a synthetic embryo as a kind of developmental scaffold for organ generation — and about the possibility of pushing synthetic embryos further developmentally as the technology improves. These are speculative proposals, and they've drawn both excitement and concern.

**George Church**, the Harvard geneticist, has discussed synthetic embryos in the context of broader biotechnology. His position — typical of his style — is that the technology is going to develop regardless, and the most important question is what kinds of regulation and ethical framework can be both rigorous and feasible. He has emphasized that international coordination matters — different jurisdictions taking very different approaches creates regulatory arbitrage where the most permissive jurisdiction effectively sets the global pace. We'll come back to the international landscape in Segment 17.

\[Pause.\]

What's common across these various positions is a recognition that the field genuinely is in new ethical territory. The arguments aren't between "this is fine" and "this should be banned" — they're between different ways of constructing oversight that's both rigorous and workable, different ways of defining what counts as a morally significant developmental milestone, different ways of weighing scientific value against ethical concern.

I want to note one thing that's often missing from this conversation, which is the perspective of people who actually use IVF, who have lost early pregnancies, or who have children with congenital developmental disorders. These are the people who would most directly benefit from improved understanding of early human development. They're often underrepresented in the ethics conversation, which tends to be dominated by ethicists, researchers, and policy-makers. To my mind, any responsible ethical framework for synthetic embryos has to take their perspective seriously — both their interest in the research being done and their concerns about how it's done.

Another thing worth noting: there are real religious and cultural perspectives on early human development that aren't easily summarized but that matter for policy. Different traditions have different views on when developmentally significant transitions occur, on what makes an embryo a moral subject, on whether and when destruction of embryos is acceptable. The ISSCR guidelines, and most national frameworks, try to be pluralistic — they don't take a position on the deeper metaphysical questions but they try to construct rules that can accommodate a range of reasonable views. This is hard to do and the resulting frameworks are always somewhat unsatisfying to everyone. But it's, I think, the best practical approach available.

\[Pause.\]

If I had to summarize the consensus among working scientists in the field — to the extent there is one — it would be something like this:

The science is important and should proceed. The structures being created are not full embryos, but they're not nothing either, and they deserve careful oversight comparable in seriousness, if not always identical in mechanism, to real embryo research. The 14-day rule, as a hard prohibition, has been overtaken by both technology and necessity, and a case-by-case approach is more workable — provided that the case-by-case review is real, not nominal. The international landscape is variable and that's a concern. And as the technology improves, the oversight framework will have to evolve too, with public input and not just scientific input.

That's not a satisfying answer if you want a clean ethical judgment. But it's an honest summary of where the field is, in the view of the people doing the work.

## SEGMENT 16 — Comparing with cerebral organoids: when does complexity become embryo-like?

I want to spend a few minutes on the comparison between synthetic embryos and cerebral organoids, because the relationship between the two is illuminating and because the question of where one ends and the other begins is genuinely fuzzy.

Cerebral organoids — the lab-grown mini-brains we talked about in the organoid lecture — are stem-cell-derived structures that model aspects of early brain development. They're made from pluripotent stem cells. They grow in defined media with specific signaling factors. They self-organize into structures with multiple cell types, ventricle-like cavities, layered cortical organization, even some functional electrical activity in advanced versions. They've been used for a wide range of research applications — modeling microcephaly, studying Zika virus infection, exploring autism-spectrum disorder genetics, and so on.

Cerebral organoids are clearly not embryos. They lack the other lineages — no trophoblast, no extraembryonic endoderm. They don't have the architecture of a whole embryo. They can never become a complete organism. They're tissue models, organ models, not embryo models.

Synthetic embryos, conversely, clearly mimic embryos. They have multiple lineages including the extraembryonic ones. They have the architecture of an embryo. They can, in principle and in the most advanced versions, develop into organisms (in mouse, at least up to day 8.5).

Between these two clearly distinct categories — clear non-embryo and clear embryo-mimic — there's a fuzzy middle, and that fuzzy middle is where the most interesting and difficult cases live.

\[Pause.\]

Here are some examples that sit in the fuzzy middle.

**Gastruloids.** These are stem-cell-derived structures that, in defined conditions, recapitulate aspects of gastrulation — the establishment of the three germ layers and the early body axis. They're made from pluripotent cells alone, without trophoblast or extraembryonic endoderm. They look like elongating, axially organized clumps with mesoderm, endoderm, and ectoderm-like domains. They model early body plan formation, particularly the events that happen during the third week of human development. They have less of an embryo's overall organization — no clear head fold, no trophoblast, no extraembryonic structures — but they do recapitulate one of the key events that the 14-day rule was protecting. Are gastruloids embryos? Probably not, by most reasonable definitions. But they engage some of the same ethical concerns.

**Trunk-like structures and somitoids.** These are stem-cell-derived structures that model the formation of the trunk and the segmentation of mesoderm into somites. They're more developmentally specialized than gastruloids — they focus on a particular subset of body plan formation. They're clearly not embryos. But they're modeling embryonic events.

**Heart organoids that beat.** These are stem-cell-derived cardiac structures that recapitulate aspects of early heart development, sometimes with spontaneous beating activity. They're clearly not embryos. But they're modeling a key embryonic organ.

**Cerebral organoids with extended development.** Advanced cerebral organoids, grown for many months, develop substantial complexity — multiple cortical layers, neural circuitry, oscillating electrical activity. They're still clearly not embryos — no other germ layers — but they're modeling a major embryonic organ to a degree of fidelity that increases over time.

\[Pause.\]

What this shows is that the synthetic-embryo concept isn't quite cleanly separable from a broader category of "stem cell models of embryonic events." Cerebral organoids are clearly on one end; integrated embryo models like the Hanna 2023 work are clearly on the other; gastruloids and other partial embryo models sit somewhere in between.

The ISSCR guidelines try to address this by distinguishing "integrated embryo models" from "non-integrated embryo models." An integrated model has all the major lineages of the early embryo (epiblast, trophoblast, extraembryonic endoderm) and is recapitulating the integrated architecture of an embryo. A non-integrated model has some but not all of the lineages, or models only specific embryonic events without the full architecture. Integrated models are recommended for stricter oversight; non-integrated models are recommended for somewhat more relaxed oversight.

This distinction is reasonable, but it doesn't fully dissolve the fuzziness. A particularly advanced non-integrated model — a gastruloid that goes through extensive axial organization, say — could plausibly engage more ethical concern than a relatively primitive integrated model. The category lines are useful starting points but they're not bright bright lines.

\[Pause.\]

There's a deeper point here. The distinction between organoids and synthetic embryos is, in some ways, a distinction of degree rather than kind. Both involve stem cells. Both involve self-organization. Both recapitulate aspects of normal development. They differ in scope — synthetic embryos try to model whole embryos, organoids try to model individual organs — but the underlying biology is continuous, not categorically distinct.

This matters because it means the ethical concerns about synthetic embryos are not entirely separable from the ethical concerns about advanced organoids. A cerebral organoid grown to such complexity that it has substantial neural circuitry and possibly even some kind of integrated activity is not a synthetic embryo, but it engages questions about consciousness and moral status that are at least adjacent to embryo ethics. As organoid technology and synthetic embryo technology both advance, the categories may need to be reorganized around the underlying biology rather than the historical accidents of how the work developed.

The two-axis framework I introduced in Segment 14 — developmental progress and morphological similarity — is one way to think about this unification. Cerebral organoids are high on one kind of fidelity (the fidelity of the modeled organ) but low on integrated developmental progress (they don't recapitulate the whole embryo's developmental trajectory). Synthetic embryos are higher on integrated developmental progress but their fidelity is more diffused across multiple lineages. Both have ethical implications. The implications differ in detail but overlap in kind.

The field is, I think, slowly converging on a more unified framework that treats organoids and synthetic embryos as related rather than distinct ethical categories, with concerns calibrated to the specific features of each construct rather than to which historical category it belongs in. That's the direction the ISSCR guidelines have been moving, and I expect it's the direction national regulations will increasingly take as well.

## SEGMENT 17 — The legal landscape: UK, US, China, and the rest

We've talked a lot about the ISSCR guidelines, which are influential but not legally binding. The actual legal status of synthetic embryo research varies a lot from country to country, and the differences matter because they shape where the most advanced work can be done and create real questions about international coordination.

**The United Kingdom** has perhaps the most developed regulatory framework for embryo research. The Human Fertilisation and Embryology Authority — the HFEA — was established by law in 1990 and oversees both clinical IVF and research on human embryos. Embryo research up to fourteen days post-fertilization, with appropriate licensing, is allowed. Embryo research beyond fourteen days is, currently, not allowed in law, even though the ISSCR has softened its position. Synthetic embryos sit in a more ambiguous legal status — the original Human Fertilisation and Embryology Act was written for embryos created by fertilization, and how it applies to stem-cell-derived structures is contested. The HFEA and the UK government have been actively considering whether and how to update the law. As of this lecture, the legal situation is still being clarified, with significant scientific and ethical input. The UK is, on balance, permissive of careful research but with structured oversight.

**The United States** has a patchwork. There's no federal law specifically regulating embryo research. Federal funding for embryo research has been restricted under various administrations — the Dickey-Wicker Amendment, in particular, prohibits federal funding for research that destroys human embryos, but doesn't ban the research itself if privately funded. As a result, most embryo research in the US happens in private and state-funded labs, with state-by-state variability. Some states are highly permissive (California, Massachusetts, New York), others are restrictive (some Southern states), and most have no specific synthetic-embryo regulations. Professional society guidelines, particularly the ISSCR's, fill some of the gap, but they're advisory rather than legally binding. The US landscape is permissive in some places, restrictive in others, and inconsistent overall.

**China** has been notably permissive for stem cell and embryo research. There are guidelines, including some that have been updated in light of international discussions, but enforcement has been variable. Several high-profile cases — including, in a different context, the 2018 CRISPR-edited babies — have raised concerns that the regulatory environment in China is not always providing real oversight. For synthetic embryo work specifically, China has substantial scientific capacity and is producing important work in the field, but the international community has, at times, expressed concern about the level of oversight.

**Japan** has updated its guidelines in recent years to allow careful research on synthetic embryos, broadly aligned with the ISSCR framework. Japan has been a major contributor to the field — Okae's trophoblast stem cells came from Tohoku — and has a generally permissive but regulated environment.

**Germany** has historically been one of the more restrictive countries, partly for historical reasons related to twentieth-century bioethics violations. German law has strict limits on embryo research, and the regulatory status of synthetic embryos is still being worked out.

**Other European countries** vary widely. France, the Netherlands, Belgium, Sweden have all been generally permissive of careful embryo research, with frameworks broadly aligned with the ISSCR. Italy has been more restrictive. The EU as a whole has not legislated specifically on synthetic embryos.

\[Pause.\]

The international landscape, then, is heterogeneous. Some countries are permissive with strong oversight. Some are permissive with weak oversight. Some are restrictive. Different jurisdictions are doing different work, with different rules, and the international scientific community is producing a global body of work that, no single national framework fully captures.

This creates several practical issues.

**Regulatory arbitrage.** If a particular kind of research is restricted in one country but permitted in another, researchers may relocate or collaborate across borders to do the work in the more permissive jurisdiction. This isn't necessarily bad — it's how science has always worked — but it means that the most restrictive frameworks don't actually prevent the research from happening, they just move it elsewhere. International coordination, where it can be achieved, is more effective than national restrictions.

**Inconsistent oversight.** Work done in jurisdictions with weak oversight may not meet the standards that more demanding jurisdictions require. This raises concerns about the quality of the science (poorly overseen work may be less rigorous) and about ethical standards (work that ignores international consensus on what's acceptable). The ISSCR guidelines are an attempt to provide a baseline of international standards, but they depend on individual researchers and institutions choosing to follow them.

**Publication and collaboration challenges.** Major journals — _Nature_, _Cell_, _Science_, and others — increasingly require synthetic embryo work to meet specified ethical standards before publication. This creates a de facto international standard, because researchers who want to publish in these journals have to meet the standards regardless of their national regulations. This is a useful informal mechanism for harmonizing standards, but it's incomplete — not all work is published in such journals, and not all journal standards are uniform.

**Public trust.** When the international landscape is inconsistent, public trust in the field can be undermined. If one country's work is held to different standards than another's, and both are publishing in the same journals and influencing the same regulations, the public may reasonably question whether the field as a whole is acting responsibly. Maintaining public trust — through transparency, through international engagement, through clear public communication of what's being done and why — is part of what serious researchers in the field take to be a responsibility.

\[Pause.\]

The trend, I think, is toward more international coordination over time. The ISSCR guidelines are influential globally. Major research funding agencies are starting to coordinate. National regulators are watching each other and adjusting. It's slow, and it's imperfect, but the direction is toward harmonization.

The remaining work, both scientifically and ethically, is going to require this harmonization to deepen. As the field pushes further — toward longer time windows, toward more complete recapitulation of human development, toward applications that could be clinical — the regulatory framework needs to be both consistent enough that the work can proceed and rigorous enough that it doesn't outrun ethical scrutiny. We're not there yet. We may never be fully there. But the trend is reasonable, and the field is engaging seriously with the challenge.

## SEGMENT 18 — The frontier: extending time, organogenesis-in-a-dish, and in-vitro gestation

We're approaching the end. Let me end with the frontier — where the field is going, and what some of the further-out possibilities look like.

**Frontier one: extending the time window.** The most immediate frontier is pushing synthetic embryos further along the developmental timeline. In mouse, the current ceiling is around day 8.5 with Hanna's system. The next step is pushing further — toward day 10, day 12, day 14, and beyond. Each additional day of in vitro development is a major technical challenge, requiring better culture systems, better gas exchange, better waste removal, better nutrient delivery, better vascular support. Multiple groups are working on this. Progress is incremental but real.

In humans, the equivalent push is from the current limit — early post-implantation, around day 14 — toward later stages. The technical challenges are even greater than for mouse, and the ethical scrutiny is more intense. But the same trajectory applies. Each year, the time ceiling moves slightly. Where we'll be in five years is uncertain but likely meaningfully further than where we are now.

**Frontier two: organogenesis-in-a-dish.** As synthetic embryos push past the gastrulation stage, they enter organogenesis — the period when individual organs first form. A synthetic embryo at the right developmental stage has the makings of a heart, a brain, a kidney, a liver — all developing in their proper embryonic positions, with their proper signaling environments. This is potentially transformative for studying organogenesis, for modeling congenital disorders, and for understanding how the body plan unfolds.

There's a more speculative version of this idea: using synthetic embryos as developmental scaffolds for organ generation. The thought is that if you can get a synthetic embryo to develop a particular organ in its proper environment, you might be able to isolate that organ — or guide its development toward producing transplant-quality tissue. This is, currently, speculative. The synthetic embryos don't develop far enough to make any organs transplant-ready. But the underlying logic is interesting and may, over a longer timescale, become a real application.

\[Pause.\]

**Frontier three: vascularization.** One of the biggest limitations of all stem cell-derived models — synthetic embryos, organoids, you name it — is that they don't have functional blood vessels supplying nutrients to the interior of the structure. As the structure grows beyond a millimeter or so, the interior starves and dies. Solving the vascularization problem would allow much larger, more functional models. Multiple approaches are being pursued — co-culture with vascular cells, engineered vascular networks, microfluidic perfusion systems, and so on. Progress is happening. Vascularized synthetic embryos, with internal circulation, would be a major advance.

**Frontier four: combining with maternal-tissue models.** Real embryos develop in interaction with maternal tissue — the uterus, the placenta, the maternal blood supply. Synthetic embryos in dishes don't have any of this. Several groups are working on combining synthetic embryos with endometrial organoids — stem-cell-derived models of the uterine lining — to recreate the embryo-maternal interaction in vitro. Early work shows that this can be done at the implantation stage. More advanced versions could allow modeling of placentation, of pregnancy-related disorders, of maternal-fetal cross-talk.

**Frontier five: artificial wombs.** This is the most speculative possibility, and it deserves a careful framing. Right now, in 2025, no one is gestating mammals from synthetic embryos in artificial wombs. The technology doesn't exist. But there are research efforts in two adjacent areas that, taken together, point in this direction. One is the kind of work Hanna's lab is doing — pushing synthetic embryos further developmentally in ex utero culture. The other is **artificial placenta work** for premature infants — the "biobag" experiments by the Children's Hospital of Philadelphia group and others, which have kept extremely premature lambs and other animals alive ex utero for weeks, supporting late-fetal development outside a real uterus.

Neither of these is the same as gestating an organism from a synthetic embryo. But you can see, in principle, how progress on both fronts could eventually meet in the middle. If synthetic embryos can be pushed further toward viability, and artificial placentas can be pushed further toward earlier-stage support, the two could, eventually, converge. This is decades away, at minimum. It may never happen at all — there are many technical reasons it might not work. But it's a possibility that's been discussed seriously enough that the ISSCR guidelines and other ethical frameworks have started to address it.

\[Pause.\]

I want to be careful here, because this kind of speculation is the part of the field that most readily becomes science fiction, and the gap between current capability and full in vitro gestation is enormous. We shouldn't oversell the proximity. But we also shouldn't dismiss the trajectory, because the underlying technologies are improving, and the question of what we'd do if it became feasible is worth thinking about now rather than after the fact.

If, at some future date, it became technically possible to gestate a mammal from a synthetic embryo to viability in a fully artificial environment, the ethical implications would be profound. It would, depending on your view, either be a remarkable medical advance — potentially saving lives, transforming reproduction, allowing pregnancies that couldn't otherwise happen — or a deeply concerning extension of human technological intervention into the most fundamental biological process. Probably both. The international community would, presumably, want to have agreed on a framework before any such thing was attempted in humans. Getting that framework right requires the conversation to start now, not later.

\[Pause.\]

Let me bring this all together.

Synthetic embryos started, in 2018, with mouse blastoids — proof that stem cells could be coaxed to self-organize into structures resembling early embryos. In 2021, human blastoids appeared. In 2022 and 2023, mouse synthetic embryos reached day 8.5 with hearts and brains. In 2023, human ETX models pushed through early post-implantation and into the start of gastrulation. The frontier continues to move.

These structures are scientifically important. They give us, for the first time, real experimental access to the period of human development that has been hidden from us for as long as we've been trying to understand ourselves. The first two to four weeks of human life — the period when most miscarriages happen, most birth defects originate, most failed IVF attempts fail — has been a black box. Synthetic embryos are opening it.

These structures are also ethically important. They blur the definition of embryo. They challenge the frameworks that bioethics has used for decades. They raise the question of what we owe to embryo-like structures that aren't, in the traditional sense, embryos. The 14-day rule has been modified. The ISSCR has updated its guidelines. National regulations are catching up. The conversation is ongoing.

I don't think there are easy answers here. The science is going to keep advancing — the trajectory is clear, the techniques are spreading, the funding is in place. The question isn't whether the field will continue but how it will continue, with what oversight, and toward what applications.

If you walk away from this lecture remembering one thing, let it be this. **You take stem cells, put them together with the right signals, and they self-organize into structures that look and behave like embryos. The cells contain the program for being an embryo. We've found, with great care and effort, how to evoke that program in a dish, without an egg, without a sperm, without a uterus.** That fact — once you understand it — is going to keep showing up in the news, in the science journals, in the bioethics literature, in the legal and political conversations about what humans should and shouldn't do with the technology we have. You'll encounter this in many forms over the coming years. I hope that, having walked through it with me today, you'll be able to recognize what's going on, distinguish careful science from sensational reporting, and think clearly about the questions the field is raising.

The story of mammalian development used to start with a fertilized egg. It still does, in nature. But in our labs, increasingly, it can start with a skin cell. And what that means for biology, for medicine, and for our concepts of embryo, pregnancy, and reproduction — that's the question of the coming decade.

That's all I have. Thank you for your attention, and good luck with the rest of your studies.

\[End of lecture.\]

**Core analogies used in this lecture:**

- Synthetic embryos = assemble pluripotent cells with the right signaling cocktail and they SELF-ORGANIZE into embryo-like structures — you don't sculpt them, you give them the cues and they self-assemble
- The trophoblast missing piece = for a long time we had embryonic stem cells but couldn't model the trophoblast (placenta precursor); Niakan and Okae finally derived stable human trophoblast stem cells
- Blastoid = iPSCs + trophoblast stem cells, gelled together with the right cocktail, gives you a structure that looks and behaves like a blastocyst
- Hanna's mouse synthetic embryo = the most striking demonstration — mouse model develops to day 8.5 in vitro with heart beating and brain forming, from stem cells alone
- The 14-day rule = the longstanding embryo-research ethical limit — primitive streak by day 14, gastrulation, individuation; synthetic embryos blur whether the 14-day clock applies
- ISSCR 2021 update = removed the 14-day rule from forbidden; case-by-case review now; the ethics is evolving in real time
- Defining "embryo" = the field's open question — if a structure has trophoblast + ICM + extraembryonic and can develop, is it an embryo? Different jurisdictions, different answers
- Comparison with organoids = cerebral organoids are clearly not embryos; synthetic embryos clearly mimic embryos; somewhere in between the line is fuzzy and ethics committees are catching up
- Two-axis framework = developmental progress (how far through development the structure goes) vs morphological similarity (how closely it resembles a real embryo at that stage); ethics calibrated to both axes

**Three core ingredients for an integrated synthetic embryo:**

1. Pluripotent stem cells (ESCs or iPSCs) — to make the inner cell mass / epiblast
2. Trophoblast stem cells — to make the outer layer / placenta precursor
3. Extraembryonic endoderm (XEN) cells — to make the hypoblast / yolk sac

**Three core limitations of current synthetic embryos:**

4. Low efficiency — only a small fraction of starting aggregates form proper structures
5. Anatomical drift — proportions and architecture can deviate from real embryos
6. Developmental ceiling — current best in mouse is ~day 8.5; in human, early post-implantation
