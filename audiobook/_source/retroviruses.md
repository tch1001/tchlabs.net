## SEGMENT 1 — The central dogma, and the virus that broke it

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. Today and for the next two hours, we're going to talk about retroviruses. And I want to begin with something you already know, because the whole reason retroviruses are interesting starts with a piece of knowledge most of you learned in your first molecular biology course, and then learned, eventually, was wrong.

The central dogma. You've all met it. DNA makes RNA, RNA makes protein. Francis Crick laid it out in 1957, refined it in 1970, and for about a decade it was taught as one of the cleanest, most satisfying organizing principles in all of biology. Information flows from DNA to RNA to protein. One direction. A river, not a tide.

Now, _retro_ — the word — means backward. And retroviruses, the family we're about to spend two hours with, are called retroviruses because they go _backward_. They take an RNA genome, and inside the cell they copy it into DNA. RNA to DNA. The wrong way down the river. The enzyme that does this is called **reverse transcriptase**. It is the namesake enzyme of the entire family, and the discovery of that enzyme in 1970 was a genuine paradigm shift — the kind of finding where the textbook had to be rewritten.

So that's the central conceptual surprise I want you to hold onto. Forget everything else for a moment. The defining feature of a retrovirus is that it carries, packaged inside the virion, an enzyme that breaks the central dogma. Every other detail of retrovirus biology — the structure of the particle, the genome organization, the way these viruses enter cells, how they integrate into our chromosomes, how they cause disease, how we treat HIV, how we use HIV-derived vectors to do gene therapy in children with leukemia — every one of those details ultimately hangs off that one fact. RNA goes in. DNA comes out. And then that DNA integrates into your genome, becoming, in a real and permanent sense, _part of you_.

If you walk out of this lecture in two hours and you can do three things — one, sketch the life cycle of a retrovirus from receptor binding to budding; two, walk a confused colleague through reverse transcription step by step and explain _why_ the strand transfers happen; three, look at any antiretroviral drug and tell me what step of that cycle it interrupts — then we've succeeded. Everything else is decoration on those three.

Let me also tell you why this matters beyond just being intellectually striking. The retroviruses are a tiny family, taxonomically. Maybe forty or so genera, depending on who's counting. But they include the virus that caused, and is still causing, one of the most consequential pandemics in human history — HIV. They include the only confirmed human oncoretroviruses, which gave us the first window into what an oncogene actually _is_. They have left fossils inside our own DNA — about eight percent of your genome is the rotted-out remains of ancient retroviral infections, and one of those genes is essential for forming the placenta you developed inside. And in the last two decades they've become the workhorse of gene therapy — when a child with severe combined immunodeficiency gets cured, when a CAR-T cell is engineered to hunt down a leukemia, an HIV-derived vector is doing the work.

So this is not a niche family. This is a family that has been weaponized against us, that has been a tool we've turned against itself, and that is _part_ of us in ways that earlier biology never anticipated.

\[Pause.\]

Let's start at the beginning.

## SEGMENT 2 — A short history: from chicken tumors to the Nobel Prize

The first retrovirus was found in a chicken, in 1911. Peyton Rous, a young pathologist at the Rockefeller Institute, was given a Plymouth Rock hen with a large lump growing on its breast. Sarcoma. A connective-tissue tumor. He ground up the tumor, passed the homogenate through a fine porcelain filter — fine enough to exclude bacteria, even the smallest known bacteria — and injected that cell-free filtrate into healthy chickens. They grew tumors. Same kind of tumor, same location.

This was extraordinary. Cancer, in the early twentieth century, was understood to be a disease of cells gone wrong. The idea that something filterable, something acellular — what we'd now call a virus, though the word barely meant anything yet — could _cause_ cancer was, at best, an eccentric outlier and, more commonly, dismissed entirely. Rous published. The world shrugged. For most of his career he worked on other things. He won the Nobel Prize for that 1911 experiment in 1966, fifty-five years later, at the age of eighty-seven. There's a moral in that, but I'll let you find it.

The virus he discovered we now call **Rous sarcoma virus**, RSV. It is a retrovirus. We didn't know that, of course, because the concept of a retrovirus didn't exist for another sixty years.

Forward to the 1960s. A young virologist named Howard Temin is working at the University of Wisconsin, studying Rous sarcoma virus. And he keeps running into a problem. When he treats infected cells with **actinomycin D**, a drug that blocks DNA-dependent transcription — that is, it stops cells from making RNA from a DNA template — RSV production stops. But RSV, as far as anyone knew, was an RNA virus. Its genome was RNA. Why would blocking DNA transcription stop an RNA virus?

Temin proposed, in 1964, what he called the **provirus hypothesis**. He suggested that RSV, despite being an RNA virus, must somehow generate a DNA copy of itself, integrate that DNA copy into the host chromosome, and then transcribe new viral RNA from that integrated DNA template. That would explain the actinomycin D sensitivity. It would also require the existence of an enzyme that copies RNA into DNA — an enzyme that, in 1964, no one had ever seen and most people would have told you couldn't exist.

The reception was, to put it kindly, frosty. Temin spent the next six years being told he was wrong. The central dogma was settled physics. Information did not flow from RNA to DNA. He kept working.

Then in 1970, two papers landed back-to-back in the journal _Nature_. One was from Temin and his student Satoshi Mizutani at Wisconsin. The other was from David Baltimore at MIT, working entirely independently on a different retrovirus, murine leukemia virus. Both groups reported the same thing: there was, in fact, an enzyme inside retroviral particles that took an RNA template and made DNA from it. RNA-dependent DNA polymerase. **Reverse transcriptase**.

The community absorbed this very quickly, considering. The textbooks were rewritten within a couple of years. Temin and Baltimore shared the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1975, along with Renato Dulbecco. The provirus hypothesis was vindicated, the central dogma was amended — not broken, exactly, but generalized to allow information to flow backwards under specific enzymatic conditions — and a whole family of viruses suddenly made sense.

That history matters for more than the trivia. It matters because every retrovirology lecture you ever give, every antiretroviral drug development program, every gene therapy vector — they all rest on a finding that the field rejected for six years. The actinomycin D experiment is the seed. The provirus hypothesis is the tree. Everything we discuss for the rest of these two hours grows out of that.

\[Pause.\]

## SEGMENT 3 — Anatomy of the particle

Alright, with that context, let me show you what a retrovirus actually looks like.

A retroviral particle — a **virion** — is roughly spherical, about 100 nanometers across. So you'd fit a thousand of them across the width of a human hair. They are **enveloped**, meaning the outer layer of the particle is a lipid bilayer membrane, which the virus steals from the host cell on its way out. Studded in that membrane are the viral envelope glycoproteins. We'll come back to those when we talk about entry.

Inside the membrane is a layer called the **matrix**, made of the matrix protein MA. The matrix sits between the membrane and the next structure inward, which is the **capsid** — CA protein — and the capsid is the iconic structure. In HIV-1, the mature capsid is a conical structure that looks, in cryo-electron tomograms, like a tapered ice cream cone. In murine leukemia virus and most other retroviruses it's roughly spherical. Either way, the capsid encloses the genome and the viral enzymes.

Now, inside the capsid you have the **genome** itself. Here's something a little unusual: the genome of a retrovirus is two copies of single-stranded, positive-sense RNA, each about 7 to 10 kilobases long, joined near their 5' ends by base-pairing. So a retrovirus is _diploid_ — it carries two genome copies per virion. This is going to matter when we talk about recombination during reverse transcription. The two RNAs are coated by the nucleocapsid protein, NC, which is a small basic protein with zinc-finger motifs that grips the RNA.

Packaged into the virion alongside the genome are three viral enzymes that the particle absolutely must bring with it, because the host cell does not provide them. These are:

**Reverse transcriptase**, RT — the enzyme of the family name. It is an RNA- and DNA-dependent DNA polymerase with an intrinsic ribonuclease H activity. It walks down an RNA template laying down DNA, and along the way it can chew up the RNA template it has just used. We will spend an entire segment on what this enzyme does because it is, frankly, beautiful and a little bit insane.

**Integrase**, IN — the enzyme that takes the double-stranded DNA product of reverse transcription, finds an accessible spot in a host chromosome, and covalently inserts the viral DNA into the host genome.

**Protease**, PR — a small aspartyl protease that cleaves the viral polyproteins as the new particle matures. Without protease, you get immature, non-infectious particles. We'll come back to this when we discuss protease inhibitors.

So that's the cargo. Lipid envelope studded with Env glycoproteins on the outside. Matrix under the membrane. Capsid in the middle. Diploid RNA genome and three enzymes inside. That is a complete retroviral particle.

Now, two structural notes that matter clinically and conceptually.

First — the diploid genome. _Why_ does the virus carry two copies? It looks wasteful. It turns out that during reverse transcription, the enzyme can jump from one RNA template to the other. If those two RNAs carry slightly different sequences — for instance, if a cell was co-infected by two different HIV variants and packaged one copy of each into the same virion — then RT can mix and match, producing a recombinant DNA product. This is **retroviral recombination**, and it is one of the reasons HIV evolves drug resistance so fast. Two genome copies per particle is the structural basis for one of the most clinically relevant features of the family.

Second — those Env glycoproteins on the surface. There are two of them, **SU** (surface) and **TM** (transmembrane), and they are cleaved from a single Env precursor by a host enzyme called furin in the late secretory pathway. SU sits on the outside and recognizes the host receptor. TM anchors the complex in the membrane and contains the **fusion peptide**, which buries itself in the host membrane during entry to drive fusion. In HIV-1, SU is gp120 and TM is gp41 — the numbers refer to the molecular weights in kilodaltons. Together they form a trimer of heterodimers on the surface, a tripod-like spike with three gp120s on top and three gp41s as the legs.

That's the particle. Now let's open the genome and look at what it encodes.

\[Pause.\]

## SEGMENT 4 — Genome organization: gag, pol, env, and the LTRs

Take a retroviral RNA genome and lay it out flat, 5' to 3'. Even though it's an RNA molecule, it looks structurally a lot like a host mRNA — it has a 5' cap, a 3' poly-A tail, and reads in the same direction as it'll be translated. That's not an accident. The retroviral RNA genome has to do double duty: it is the mRNA that ribosomes will translate, and it is also the genome that gets packaged into new virions.

The protein-coding content is organized into three main genes, in this order: **gag**, **pol**, **env**. That's the same order, in essentially every retrovirus you'll ever meet.

**gag** — short for group-specific antigen, a historical name — encodes the structural proteins of the particle. It's translated as a single polyprotein called Pr55-Gag in HIV-1, which after maturation gets cleaved by viral protease into the building blocks: matrix MA, capsid CA, nucleocapsid NC, and a small C-terminal piece called p6.

**pol** — encodes the enzymes. Protease PR, reverse transcriptase RT, integrase IN. Pol is _not_ translated as its own independent polyprotein; instead it's translated as a Gag-Pol fusion, via a clever mechanism called a ribosomal frameshift that we'll cover later. This is how the virus ensures the right stoichiometry — many Gag proteins for every Pol enzyme.

**env** — encodes the envelope glycoproteins. The Env precursor is translated from a separately spliced mRNA, trafficked through the secretory pathway, glycosylated, cleaved into SU and TM, and inserted into the plasma membrane where it gets incorporated into budding virions.

That's the protein content. Three genes, in order. Simple retroviruses like murine leukemia virus stop there. **Complex retroviruses** like HIV have additional accessory genes — tat, rev, nef, vif, vpr, vpu in HIV-1, and we'll meet several of those — but the gag-pol-env spine is universal.

Now, flanking those genes are the most important non-coding sequences in retrovirus biology. The **Long Terminal Repeats**, the LTRs. There's one at each end of the integrated DNA provirus, and they are identical in sequence — that's what "long terminal repeat" means, the same sequence repeated at both terminals.

Each LTR has three sub-regions: **U3**, **R**, and **U5**, in that order. U3 stands for unique-3-prime, U5 for unique-5-prime, and R for repeated. The reason for those names is something we'll see clearly when we walk through reverse transcription, but for now just remember: each LTR is U3-R-U5, and the LTRs are the regulatory powerhouse of the provirus. U3 contains the **promoter** and **enhancer** elements that drive transcription of the entire viral genome. R contains the **transcription start** and the **polyadenylation signal**. U5 contains sequences important for early reverse-transcription chemistry.

So when the integrated provirus is sitting in a host chromosome, transcription initiates at the boundary between U3 and R in the upstream LTR, runs through the whole provirus, and ends at the boundary between R and U5 in the downstream LTR. The RNA that gets produced therefore starts with R, ends with R, and contains R-U5-gag-pol-env-U3-R, with a cap and a poly-A. That same RNA can be translated by ribosomes or packaged into new virions.

Now, in addition to the LTRs, there are several short **cis-acting sequences** scattered through the genome that matter enormously for the life cycle. Let me name them, because they'll come up again.

The **PBS** — primer binding site — is a short sequence just downstream of the 5' LTR that is complementary to the 3' end of a specific cellular tRNA. The virus packages that tRNA, and uses it as the primer for reverse transcription. For HIV-1, the primer tRNA is tRNA-Lys-3.

The **PPT** — polypurine tract — is a short adenine- and guanine-rich sequence just upstream of the 3' LTR. It is the primer for plus-strand DNA synthesis. We'll see how that works in segment 6.

The **psi sequence**, also written ψ, is a packaging signal — a structured RNA element near the 5' end of the genome that is recognized by the nucleocapsid domain of Gag, telling the assembling virion "this is a genomic RNA, package me." Without psi, an RNA gets translated but not packaged. Gene therapy vectors are built around removing the viral genes but keeping psi, so the vector RNA still gets packaged.

The **att sites** at the ends of the LTRs are short sequences recognized by integrase during integration.

Memorize those names. PBS, PPT, psi, att. You'll see them again.

\[Pause.\]

## SEGMENT 5 — Entry: the receptor, the coreceptor, and how the virion gets in

Now we begin the life cycle proper. A free virion is drifting in extracellular fluid. It bumps into a cell. What happens next?

The first thing that happens is **receptor binding**. The viral SU glycoprotein on the surface of the virion has to recognize, with reasonable specificity, a protein on the surface of the target host cell. That host protein is called the receptor. Different retroviruses use different receptors. Murine leukemia virus uses cationic amino acid transporters. Avian leukosis virus uses a receptor in the TVA family. HIV-1 uses **CD4**, the surface protein that marks helper T cells.

The choice of receptor determines **tropism** — which cells the virus can infect. HIV-1 infects CD4-positive cells, so its target cells are helper T lymphocytes, macrophages, dendritic cells, and a few others. It does not infect cells that don't display CD4, period.

But for many retroviruses, and HIV-1 is the canonical example, the story is not that simple. Binding CD4 is necessary but not sufficient. After SU — that's gp120 in HIV — engages CD4, the gp120 undergoes a conformational change that exposes a previously buried surface. That newly exposed surface has to bind a second host receptor, called a **coreceptor**, before fusion can proceed.

For HIV-1, the two main coreceptors are **CCR5** and **CXCR4**. Both are chemokine receptors — seven-transmembrane G-protein-coupled receptors normally involved in immune cell trafficking. HIV variants are typed by which coreceptor they use. **R5 viruses** use CCR5. **X4 viruses** use CXCR4. Some viruses are dual-tropic and can use either. Most transmitted HIV-1 in the early stage of infection is R5. Many late-stage viruses, after years of immune escape, shift to X4.

Why does this matter clinically? Because there is a remarkable subset of the human population — about one percent of people of Northern European ancestry, homozygous — who carry a deletion in CCR5 called **CCR5-Δ32**, a 32-base-pair deletion in the coding sequence that knocks out functional CCR5. These individuals are largely **resistant to HIV-1 infection**, because the virus arrives, binds CD4, looks for CCR5, and doesn't find a working copy. They can still be infected by X4 viruses, but R5 viruses, which are most of what circulates, can't establish infection. This is why the Berlin patient — the first person ever cured of HIV — was cured by receiving a bone marrow transplant from a CCR5-Δ32 homozygous donor. We will return to that story.

Once both CD4 and the coreceptor are engaged, the TM glycoprotein — gp41 in HIV — undergoes its own dramatic conformational rearrangement. It springs open like a switchblade, ejecting a hydrophobic stretch called the **fusion peptide** which inserts itself into the host plasma membrane. Then gp41 collapses back, pulling the viral envelope and the host membrane together, forcing them to fuse. The contents of the virion — the capsid containing genome and enzymes — are now in the cytoplasm of the target cell.

\[Pause.\]

For some retroviruses, particularly the gammaretroviruses like MLV, fusion happens at the plasma membrane and the capsid pops straight into the cytosol. For others, including HIV-1, the picture has gotten more complicated in the last decade. There's now strong evidence that the capsid stays largely intact and is actually trafficked all the way to the nuclear pore, where it disassembles partially and the contents enter the nucleus. The capsid is not just a delivery shell; it's an active player in nuclear import. Postgraduate-level material, but worth flagging.

The bottom line for entry: receptor, coreceptor, conformational cascade, fusion. The virion's contents end up in the cytoplasm. The genome is still RNA. The enzymes are with it. The next thing that has to happen is the dogma-breaking step — reverse transcription.

## SEGMENT 6 — Reverse transcription, step by step

This is going to be the longest segment in this lecture, and I want you to bear with me, because if you understand reverse transcription you understand retroviruses, and if you don't understand reverse transcription you are going to spend the rest of your career taking certain things on faith. So let's slow down.

The substrate for reverse transcription is a single-stranded positive-sense RNA. The product is double-stranded DNA. RT is the enzyme. But the path from substrate to product is not a single uniform copying step — it involves _two strand transfers_, also called template switches, and the geometry of those transfers is what generates the long terminal repeats that you've already met as a key feature of the integrated provirus. The whole architecture of the LTR exists because of how reverse transcription unfolds. Let me walk you through it.

Start with the genomic RNA. Five-prime to three-prime, it reads: R-U5-PBS-gag-pol-env-PPT-U3-R. Notice the R sequence appears at both ends, the U5 at one end, the U3 at the other.

**Step 1 — Primer binding.** Inside the virion, a host tRNA — tRNA-Lys-3 in HIV-1 — is already base-paired to the PBS. So our starting configuration has a tRNA hanging off the genome, with its 3' end positioned right at the PBS, just downstream of U5. RT recognizes this primer-template junction and begins to synthesize DNA, extending the 3' end of the tRNA.

**Step 2 — Minus-strand strong-stop DNA.** RT walks backward — that is, in the 3'-to-5' direction _along the RNA template_, which means it synthesizes DNA in the 5'-to-3' direction — copying U5 and then R. When it gets to the very 5' end of the genome, it stops. There's no more template. The DNA it has just made is a short complementary strand running from the PBS through U5 through R. About 180 nucleotides for HIV-1. This piece is called **minus-strand strong-stop DNA**.

\[Pause. Let it land.\]

**Step 3 — RNase H degrades the template.** RT has a built-in second activity, **RNase H**, a domain that degrades RNA when it's hybridized to DNA. As RT walks along the RNA template laying down DNA, RNase H follows behind chewing up the RNA. So after the strong-stop DNA is made, the RNA U5 and R sequences at the 5' end of the genome have been digested. What remains is a free 3' end on our new DNA, with R sequence ready to base-pair to something.

**Step 4 — First strand transfer.** And here's where the magic starts. Remember, R appears at _both ends_ of the genome. The R sequence on the new DNA strand is complementary to the R sequence at the 3' end of the genome — the other end. So the freshly synthesized minus-strand strong-stop DNA, with its dangling 3' R sequence, hops over to the 3' end of the same RNA molecule — or, often, to the 3' end of the _other_ RNA molecule in the diploid virion — and base-pairs to R there. This is the **first strand transfer**.

If you can hold this picture in your head, it's the key to the whole process. The DNA primer made from the 5' end of the genome is now repositioned at the 3' end, base-paired to R, with its 3' OH pointing inward toward the rest of the template.

**Step 5 — Elongation of minus-strand DNA.** RT now extends from this transferred primer, copying U3, then env, pol, gag, PBS, all the way back toward the original 5' end of the template. RNase H follows behind, chewing up the RNA template. The product is now a full-length minus-strand DNA — that is, a DNA complementary to almost the entire genome.

There is one stretch of RNA that RNase H does _not_ digest efficiently, and that's the polypurine tract, the PPT. The PPT is resistant to RNase H cleavage and remains base-paired to the new DNA after the rest of the template is gone. That resistant RNA fragment becomes the primer for plus-strand DNA synthesis.

**Step 6 — Plus-strand strong-stop DNA.** RT now uses that PPT RNA fragment as a primer and copies the minus-strand DNA template. It synthesizes through U3, R, U5, and into the PBS region. Then it stops, because the tRNA at the original PBS, which has now been copied as DNA, contains a modified base — typically methyl-A — that RT cannot read through. This second strong-stop product is called **plus-strand strong-stop DNA**. It contains U3, R, U5, and the first 18 nucleotides of the PBS.

**Step 7 — Second strand transfer.** Once again, RNase H removes the tRNA primer that started the whole reaction. Now we have a plus-strand DNA segment ending in PBS sequence, and a minus-strand DNA ending in PBS sequence at the other end. They can base-pair to each other through PBS. The plus-strand DNA hops over and anneals to the 3' end of the minus-strand DNA. This is the **second strand transfer**.

**Step 8 — Bidirectional elongation.** From this configuration, RT extends in both directions, completing both strands. The plus strand fills in the rest of the genome on the minus-strand template, and the minus strand fills in to the end of the plus-strand template. The final product is **linear double-stranded DNA** with full-length LTRs at both ends.

Look at what just happened. The original RNA had U5 at the 5' end and U3 at the 3' end, with R at both ends. The DNA product has both U3 and U5 at _both_ ends. The first LTR — U3-R-U5 — was assembled by combining U3 from the 3' end of the template with R and U5 from the 5' end of the template. The two strand transfers, the two strong-stop products, exist precisely to construct those LTRs. The LTRs are not pre-existing structures in the RNA. They are _generated_ during reverse transcription by stitching the ends of the genome together.

\[Pause. Let it land.\]

That is reverse transcription. RNA in. Linear dsDNA out, with brand-new LTRs at both ends. It takes a few hours in a cell. It produces, on average, about one mutation per genome, because RT has no proofreading exonuclease. That error rate is going to become enormously important when we talk about drug resistance.

The next step is to get this DNA into a chromosome.

## SEGMENT 7 — Integration: how the provirus joins your genome forever

The product of reverse transcription is a linear double-stranded DNA with LTRs on both ends. Sitting in the cytoplasm. Bound by a complex of viral proteins — RT, integrase, and some others — collectively called the **pre-integration complex**, or PIC.

The PIC has two jobs. One, get to the nucleus. Two, splice the viral DNA into a host chromosome.

Getting to the nucleus is non-trivial. The nucleus, in an interphase cell, is bounded by the nuclear envelope, and the only way in is through nuclear pore complexes — which are gates with active import machinery. Most macromolecules need a nuclear localization signal recognized by importins to get through.

Here we see a major split in the retrovirus family. The **gammaretroviruses**, like murine leukemia virus, _cannot_ cross an intact nuclear envelope. They have to wait for the cell to enter mitosis, at which point the nuclear envelope breaks down and the PIC has free access to chromatin. This is why gammaretroviral vectors only efficiently transduce dividing cells — it's a major limitation in gene therapy.

The **lentiviruses**, which include HIV-1, _can_ cross intact nuclear envelopes. The capsid itself, with associated proteins, docks at the nuclear pore and uses cellular import machinery, including importin-7 and a host factor called CPSF6, to traverse the pore. This is enormously important biologically — HIV infects resting CD4 T cells and terminally differentiated macrophages, neither of which divide much — and it's the central reason lentiviral vectors have replaced gammaretroviral vectors for most gene therapy applications. You can transduce neurons, hematopoietic stem cells, post-mitotic cells, all because of this property.

Once the viral DNA is in the nucleus, integrase goes to work. The chemistry is two reactions.

**3'-end processing.** Integrase cleaves two nucleotides off the 3' ends of each strand of the viral DNA. This generates recessed 3'-OH groups at both ends, with characteristic CA overhangs. This happens in the cytoplasm before nuclear entry, actually.

**Strand transfer.** Inside the nucleus, integrase finds a stretch of host DNA, makes a staggered cut in the host duplex, and uses the 3'-OH ends of the viral DNA to attack the host backbone in a transesterification reaction. The viral DNA's 3' ends become covalently joined to the host DNA's 5' ends, on opposite strands of the host. The intermediate has the viral DNA flanked by short single-stranded gaps where the staggered cut was made. Host repair enzymes then fill in the gaps and seal the nicks, generating the characteristic **target site duplication** of about 4 to 6 base pairs flanking the integrated provirus.

The result is a viral DNA permanently inserted into a host chromosome. We call this the **provirus**. It is, for all practical purposes, now a Mendelian piece of the host genome. It will be replicated by cellular DNA polymerase every time the cell divides. It will be transcribed by cellular RNA polymerase II. It is, in a real sense, _part of the host_.

Now, here's a thing worth knowing for gene therapy and oncogenesis: integration is not entirely random. Different retroviruses have different **integration site preferences**. HIV-1 strongly favors integration into the bodies of actively transcribed genes. Murine leukemia virus favors transcription start sites and active enhancers. Avian sarcoma virus is much more random. These biases come from interactions between integrase and host chromatin factors — for HIV, integrase binds a host factor called LEDGF/p75 that tethers it near active genes.

This matters clinically because if a viral vector integrates near a proto-oncogene and turns it on, you can transform the cell. In the early 2000s, four out of about twenty children treated for X-SCID with a gammaretroviral vector developed leukemia because the vector kept integrating near the LMO2 proto-oncogene. This led to the development of **self-inactivating** lentiviral vectors and continued careful integration-site profiling in modern gene therapy trials. We'll come back to this in segment 16.

\[Pause.\]

## SEGMENT 8 — Transcription: the provirus as a cellular gene

Once integrated, the provirus is, from the perspective of host machinery, just another stretch of chromosomal DNA. It will be transcribed by host RNA polymerase II, packaged into chromatin, regulated by host transcription factors. There is no viral RNA polymerase in a retrovirus. Every viral RNA that ever gets made is made by your own RNA pol II reading your own chromosomes.

That has profound consequences. It means that **transcription of the provirus is regulated like a cellular gene**, with all the strengths and weaknesses that brings. It means that **latency** — a state where the provirus is integrated but transcriptionally silent — is a fundamental property of every retrovirus, because chromatin can be inactive. It means that the LTR is, structurally, a promoter, and we can analyze it like any other promoter.

The transcription start site sits at the boundary between U3 and R in the **5' LTR**. U3 contains the promoter and enhancer. For HIV-1, U3 contains:

A **TATA box** about 25 base pairs upstream of the start, which positions RNA pol II.

Three **Sp1 binding sites**, an upstream constitutive transcription factor binding region.

Two **NF-κB binding sites**, which are critical — NF-κB is the transcription factor that gets activated when a T cell is stimulated through its T-cell receptor or by cytokines like TNF-α. So when the cell carrying a latent HIV provirus gets activated as part of a normal immune response, NF-κB translocates to the nucleus, binds those LTR sites, and turns on transcription of the provirus. This is one of the central reasons HIV preferentially replicates in activated T cells: the virus has built its promoter to be turned on by the same signals that turn the cell on.

Various other sites: NFAT, AP-1, TCF/LEF, depending on cell type.

So transcription initiates. Pol II loads. It starts to elongate. And immediately runs into a problem.

There is a structured RNA element near the very beginning of every HIV-1 transcript called **TAR**, the trans-activation response element. TAR is a stable RNA hairpin that forms in the nascent transcript almost as soon as it's made. TAR acts as a pause site — Pol II, having transcribed about fifty nucleotides, falls off or pauses. In the absence of any countermeasure, transcription terminates here. Only short abortive transcripts are made. The cell has effectively shut down expression.

The countermeasure is a viral protein called **Tat**. Tat is encoded by HIV. It binds TAR. It recruits a host elongation factor called **P-TEFb** — positive transcription elongation factor b — which is a complex of cyclin T1 and CDK9. CDK9 phosphorylates the C-terminal domain of Pol II, kicks it out of pause, and turns it into an elongation-competent polymerase. The net effect is that Tat is essential for productive transcription of the HIV provirus. Without Tat you get pause and abort. With Tat you get full-length transcripts.

This is a marvelous example of a virus exploiting host machinery. Pol II pausing is a normal regulatory mechanism for cellular genes; HIV has installed an RNA element and a viral factor that releases that pause specifically for its own promoter.

Why is this elegant arrangement important to know? Two reasons. First, the Tat-TAR axis has been a drug-development target for decades, with compounds like didehydro-cortistatin A that can lock the virus in a deeply silent state — the "block-and-lock" approach we'll cover in segment 18. Second, Tat-deficient proviruses are profoundly silenced, which is one of the reasons HIV latency is so hard to break.

The full-length RNA produced by Tat-stimulated transcription is the **primary transcript** — it runs from R in the 5' LTR all the way to R in the 3' LTR, with a 5' cap, internal splice sites, and a 3' poly-A. That single transcript will be processed into multiple different mRNAs and will also serve as the genomic RNA for new virions. How it gets sorted into all those fates is the next segment.

## SEGMENT 9 — Splicing and Rev: getting the right RNA to the right place

So Pol II has finished its job. We have a 9-kilobase capped, polyadenylated, unspliced HIV-1 primary transcript sitting in the nucleus. From this single transcript, the virus needs to make at least three categories of mRNA — and also keep some of the transcript intact to use as the genome of new virions. This is non-trivial.

Let me lay out the categories.

**Unspliced RNA** — the full 9 kilobase transcript. Used as the mRNA for Gag and Gag-Pol. Also packaged as the genome.

**Singly spliced RNAs** — the genomic 5' end is joined to one downstream splice acceptor, skipping over gag and pol. These encode Env, and the accessory proteins Vif, Vpr, and Vpu.

**Multiply spliced RNAs** — both splicing events occur, producing very short mRNAs that encode Tat, Rev, and Nef.

Splicing in eukaryotes is normally tightly coupled to nuclear export — fully spliced mRNAs get exported, unspliced and partially spliced transcripts are retained in the nucleus and eventually degraded. This is a quality-control mechanism to prevent intron-containing transcripts from being translated. But HIV needs to get its unspliced and partially spliced transcripts _out_ of the nucleus, intact, to make Gag, Gag-Pol, and Env, and to package the genome.

The solution is a second viral regulatory protein called **Rev**.

Rev is a small protein. It's encoded by a multiply spliced mRNA, so the first viral proteins to be made early in infection are Tat, Rev, and Nef. Rev shuttles into the nucleus. It binds, with high specificity, a structured RNA element called the **Rev Response Element**, or **RRE**, which sits inside the env coding region. Because RRE is inside env, it is present in the unspliced and singly spliced transcripts — the ones the cell wants to retain — but it is _spliced out_ of the multiply spliced transcripts.

Once Rev binds RRE, it oligomerizes — typically about eight Rev molecules pile onto a single RRE — and recruits a host export factor called **CRM1** (also called exportin-1), which is the same factor cells use to export certain proteins and snoRNAs. CRM1 binds Rev, the whole complex moves through the nuclear pore, and the unspliced or singly spliced RNA goes with it.

So early in infection, before Rev levels build up, only the multiply spliced transcripts get out — making more Tat, more Rev, more Nef. Once Rev has accumulated, it starts pulling out the longer RNAs, and gag, pol, env, vif, vpr, and vpu protein production begins. This temporal switch from "early" to "late" gene expression is set by Rev concentration.

There's a famous drug, **selinexor**, used in multiple myeloma, that inhibits CRM1. It has been studied for HIV applications but is too toxic for that use. The principle is interesting nonetheless — block CRM1, you block Rev, you collapse the late phase of HIV gene expression.

A postgraduate footnote: simpler retroviruses don't have Rev. Murine leukemia virus, for instance, exports its unspliced RNA using a different cis-acting element called a **constitutive transport element**, CTE, that recruits a different host export pathway involving NXF1/TAP. So the regulatory architecture is varied, but the problem is universal — retroviruses must export their long, intron-containing RNAs against host quality control.

OK. We have all our mRNAs in the cytoplasm. We have full-length genomic RNA waiting to be packaged. Now translation.

## SEGMENT 10 — Translation, the frameshift, and trafficking to the membrane

Welcome back. We are now in the cytoplasm of an HIV-infected cell, with several flavors of viral mRNA available. Let's translate them.

The unspliced 9-kilobase transcript is loaded onto ribosomes. By default, ribosomes translate it from the first AUG and produce **Gag**, a 55 kilodalton polyprotein, Pr55. Gag contains, in order from N-terminus to C-terminus: MA, CA, NC, p6 — and a small spacer peptide called p2 between CA and NC, and another called p1 between NC and p6. So Gag is a single polyprotein that contains all the structural components of the future virion.

But Gag is not the only thing that gets made from this transcript. Gag is followed in the genome by Pol — protease, RT, integrase. And the virus needs those enzymes inside every new particle. The trick the virus uses to express Pol is one of the most elegant tricks in all of molecular biology: a **−1 ribosomal frameshift**.

Roughly 5% of ribosomes that translate Gag, on reaching a specific sequence near the C-terminus of Gag — a "slippery sequence" of the form X XXY YYZ — slip backwards by one nucleotide and continue translating in the −1 reading frame. Downstream of the slippery sequence is a stable RNA pseudoknot that physically blocks the ribosome and increases the probability of slippage. After the slip, the ribosome is now reading in a frame that fuses Gag to the entire Pol open reading frame, producing a **Gag-Pol fusion polyprotein**, Pr160.

The result is a defined stoichiometry: about 20 Gag polyproteins are made for every 1 Gag-Pol fusion. That ratio matters enormously. Gag is the structural building block; you need a lot of it. The enzymes need to be present but in only a handful of copies per particle. The frameshift sets the ratio precisely. Modify the slippery sequence or the pseudoknot, perturb the ratio, and particle assembly falls apart.

Env is translated separately, from a singly spliced mRNA. It enters the secretory pathway, gets heavily glycosylated in the endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi, and is cleaved by furin into SU and TM. SU and TM remain non-covalently associated as a trimer of heterodimers and traffic to the plasma membrane, where they wait to be incorporated into budding particles.

The Gag polyprotein, meanwhile, has a critical modification on its N-terminus: a **myristoyl group**, a 14-carbon saturated fatty acid added by host N-myristoyltransferase. The myristoyl group anchors Gag to the inner leaflet of the plasma membrane. Gag also has a basic patch that interacts with phosphatidylinositol-(4,5)-bisphosphate, **PIP2**, a lipid that is enriched in the plasma membrane. So Gag specifically targets the plasma membrane, and starts to oligomerize there.

There's important work in the postgrad literature on _where exactly_ Gag assembles. In T cells, Gag forms particles directly at the plasma membrane. In macrophages, particles often appear to assemble in intracellular compartments that are connected to the cell surface by narrow channels. The interpretation has gone back and forth. The current view is that even in macrophages, those "intracellular" compartments are continuous with the surface and represent specialized membrane microdomains.

Gag also has to find a copy of the genomic RNA. This is the role of the **NC** domain of Gag. NC has two zinc finger motifs that recognize the **psi** packaging signal in the unspliced genomic RNA. NC binds psi, and through this interaction Gag selects unspliced RNA for packaging — distinguishing it from cellular mRNAs and from spliced viral RNAs that have lost psi.

Two copies of genomic RNA are packaged per particle. The two RNAs are linked at their 5' ends through a structured region called the **dimerization initiation site**. So one Gag molecule, with one NC, grabs the RNA dimer; many more Gag molecules pile on through Gag-Gag interactions; the assembling structure curves outward and begins to bud.

That brings us to assembly and budding.

## SEGMENT 11 — Assembly, budding, and the maturation switch

Assembly happens at the plasma membrane. Several thousand Gag molecules — roughly 2,400 for HIV-1, with about 120 Gag-Pol molecules sprinkled in at the frameshift-determined ratio — oligomerize on the inner leaflet, latticing into a hemispherical shell that pushes the membrane outward into a budding particle. The C-terminus of each Gag points outward, the matrix domain interacts with the membrane, the capsid and NC domains face inward, NC clutches the genomic RNA, and the trimeric Env spikes that have accumulated at the plasma membrane get incorporated into the budding particle.

To pinch the bud off — to actually scission the membrane neck and release the particle — the virus needs help. It cannot do this alone. It hijacks a cellular pathway called the **ESCRT machinery**, the endosomal sorting complex required for transport. ESCRT is normally used by the cell to bud vesicles _into_ multivesicular bodies and to seal off the abscission point during cytokinesis. The geometry is exactly the same as a virus budding off the cell — membrane being pinched away from the cytoplasm — and HIV exploits ESCRT to perform that scission.

The hijack happens through small sequences in the p6 region of Gag called **late domains**, or L-domains. HIV-1 p6 has a PTAP motif that binds the ESCRT-I component TSG101, and a YPXL-type motif that binds the ESCRT-associated factor ALIX. These interactions recruit ESCRT-III and the ATPase VPS4 to the bud neck, which catalyze the membrane scission.

Mutations in the late domains produce a striking phenotype — particles that bud halfway but stay tethered to the cell by a thin membrane neck, sometimes called "frustrated budding." Beautiful electron microscopy images. Useful in the lab. Lethal for the virus.

OK. The particle has now budded off. It is floating in the extracellular space. Is it infectious?

Not yet.

The freshly budded particle is called the **immature** virion. The structure is a roughly spherical shell of intact Gag polyproteins, with intact Gag-Pol fusion proteins woven in. Inside, the genomic RNA is bound by NC, which is still attached to the rest of Gag. The capsid is _not_ the iconic conical structure yet — it's a roughly spherical shell of uncleaved Gag.

For the particle to become infectious, it must undergo **maturation**. The viral protease, which until now has been held inactive as part of the Gag-Pol polyprotein, gets activated — partly autocatalytically through dimerization — and cleaves Gag and Gag-Pol at a series of specific sites. Roughly ten cleavage events per Gag/Gag-Pol pair. The individual proteins — MA, CA, NC, p6, PR, RT, IN — are released. They rearrange. Capsid molecules reassemble into the famous conical structure, with NC and the genome packaged inside, RT and IN tucked in. Matrix stays attached to the membrane. Env trimers sit on the outside.

This rearrangement is dramatic and visible in cryo-EM. Immature particle: roughly spherical Gag shell. Mature particle: conical capsid with empty space around it. Same molecules, completely different architecture, all triggered by protease cleavage.

If protease is inhibited, maturation does not happen. The particle remains immature. The conical capsid never forms. The RT cannot perform reverse transcription efficiently in a target cell. The particle is non-infectious. This is the mechanism of action of every **protease inhibitor** in HIV therapy: they bind in the protease active site, block the cleavage of Gag and Gag-Pol, and produce immature, non-infectious particles. We'll cover the drug classes in segment 13.

So to recap the life cycle so far: entry, reverse transcription, integration, transcription, splicing, translation, frameshift, assembly, budding, maturation. We've covered the whole arc.

## SEGMENT 12 — The HIV life cycle as one story

Let me now do something I think is pedagogically valuable. I want to walk through the entire HIV-1 life cycle from start to finish, in one continuous narrative, using everything we've assembled. If you can tell this story end-to-end, you have the central content of this lecture.

Picture a free HIV-1 particle in the genital mucosa of a person who has just been exposed. Roughly 100 nanometers across, enveloped, with about 14 Env spikes sticking out. Inside, two copies of plus-sense RNA, coated by NC. Inside that, a conical capsid containing the RNA, reverse transcriptase, integrase, protease, and a handful of accessory proteins.

The particle encounters a CD4-positive cell — likely a CCR5-expressing memory T cell, or perhaps a dendritic cell that has come to sample antigen at the mucosal surface. The gp120 SU on the particle binds CD4. CD4 binding triggers a conformational change in gp120 that exposes the coreceptor-binding site. The coreceptor — CCR5 in early infection — binds. That triggers the gp41 TM to spring open, eject its fusion peptide into the host membrane, and collapse back, fusing the viral envelope with the host membrane. The conical capsid, with all its contents, is now in the cytoplasm.

The capsid traffics along microtubules toward the nucleus. Along the way — actually now we think it's mostly inside or at the nuclear pore — reverse transcription proceeds. The tRNA-Lys-3 primer that was already base-paired to the PBS gets extended. Minus-strand strong-stop DNA is made. First strand transfer to R at the other end. Full-length minus-strand DNA elongation. RNase H degrades the template. PPT-primed plus-strand strong-stop. Second strand transfer at PBS. Bidirectional elongation. The product is linear double-stranded DNA with LTRs at both ends. Roughly twenty hours from entry, in a typical infected cell.

The capsid, partially disassembled, traverses the nuclear pore with help from CPSF6 and the nuclear import machinery. The pre-integration complex finds host chromatin — preferentially in transcriptionally active gene bodies, biased by LEDGF/p75. Integrase performs 3'-end processing on the viral DNA in the cytoplasm and strand transfer in the nucleus. The viral DNA is now permanently inserted into the host chromosome. The cell is now a carrier of an integrated HIV provirus. It will be, for the rest of that cell's life.

Now two things can happen. Either the cell is in an activated state — NF-κB nuclear, P-TEFb available — and transcription begins, or the cell is in a resting state with closed chromatin around the integration site and the provirus goes **latent**.

In the active scenario, RNA pol II initiates at the U3/R boundary, makes a short transcript, pauses at TAR. Once Tat is available, P-TEFb is recruited, the pause is released, and full-length 9-kilobase transcripts are produced. Some get fully multiply spliced — making Tat, Rev, Nef. Rev accumulates. Rev binds RRE on incompletely spliced transcripts and exports them via CRM1. Now singly spliced transcripts make Env, Vif, Vpr, Vpu, and unspliced transcripts make Gag and Gag-Pol via the −1 frameshift.

Gag traffics to the plasma membrane via myristoyl-PIP2 interactions. NC grabs the genomic RNA dimer. Gag oligomerizes. Env spikes accumulate at the same membrane domains. The membrane bulges outward into a budding particle. ESCRT is recruited via p6 late domains. Membrane scission. An immature particle is released.

The immature particle activates its protease through dimerization. Protease cleaves Gag and Gag-Pol at the canonical sites. CA reassembles into the conical capsid. The particle matures. It is now infectious. It floats off to find its next CD4-positive cell.

That's the cycle. About twenty-four to forty-eight hours from entry to release of new virions. In an untreated HIV-infected person, roughly ten billion new virions are produced per day, and roughly ten billion CD4 T cells are infected and die per day. The immune system fights this for years, slowly losing ground, until CD4 counts collapse and AIDS develops.

That's the disease. That's also the target spectrum for therapy. Every step we just walked through is a drug-development opportunity. Let's go to the pharmacology.

## SEGMENT 13 — Antiretroviral drug classes

There are six main classes of antiretroviral drugs in clinical use. Each one targets a specific step we just walked through. Let me take them in the order of the life cycle.

**Entry inhibitors** block the first step — viral entry. There are two FDA-approved entry inhibitors and they work by different mechanisms. **Enfuvirtide**, brand name Fuzeon, is a peptide that mimics part of gp41 and binds the other half of gp41 during the fusion conformational change, blocking the structural collapse that drives membrane fusion. It must be injected, twice a day, which limits its use. **Maraviroc**, an oral small molecule, binds CCR5 in a way that distorts the binding pocket so that gp120 cannot engage it. It only works against R5 viruses — if a patient harbors X4 or dual-tropic virus, maraviroc fails. Tropism testing is required before prescribing it.

**Nucleoside / nucleotide reverse transcriptase inhibitors**, NRTIs, are the oldest class. **Zidovudine** — AZT — was the first antiretroviral, approved in 1987. These are nucleoside analogs that get phosphorylated by host kinases to their triphosphate form, are taken up by RT during DNA synthesis, and act as **chain terminators**. They look enough like the natural dNTP to be incorporated, but they lack a 3'-OH, so once they are in the growing DNA chain, no further extension is possible. RT stalls, the provirus is never completed. Current first-line NRTIs include tenofovir, emtricitabine, lamivudine, abacavir.

**Non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors**, NNRTIs, also target RT, but through a completely different mechanism. They bind an allosteric pocket in RT, near but not in the active site. They distort the geometry of the polymerase domain and inhibit DNA synthesis without competing with dNTPs. Examples: efavirenz, rilpivirine, doravirine. NNRTIs are typically very potent but suffer from a low genetic barrier to resistance — a single mutation, like K103N, abolishes activity for most of the first-generation drugs.

**Integrase strand transfer inhibitors**, INSTIs, block integration. They bind in the integrase active site at the strand transfer step, coordinating with the two divalent magnesium cations that integrase uses for chemistry. Once bound, they prevent the viral DNA from being inserted into the host. The viral DNA accumulates as unintegrated forms — linear, 1-LTR circle, 2-LTR circle — none of which can produce infectious progeny. Examples: raltegravir, dolutegravir, bictegravir, cabotegravir. INSTIs are now the backbone of first-line therapy because they are potent, well-tolerated, and have a relatively high genetic barrier to resistance. Bictegravir + tenofovir alafenamide + emtricitabine — three drugs in one pill, once daily — is a typical regimen.

**Protease inhibitors**, PIs, were the breakthrough drug class of the 1990s and the reason combination therapy became durable enough to actually save lives. They bind in the active site of HIV protease, block cleavage of Gag and Gag-Pol, and produce immature, non-infectious particles. Examples: darunavir, atazanavir. PIs are usually administered with a low-dose "booster" like ritonavir or cobicistat — these are pharmacokinetic enhancers that inhibit the host CYP3A4 enzyme that would otherwise rapidly clear the PI.

**Attachment / post-attachment inhibitors** are a smaller class. **Fostemsavir** binds gp120 and prevents CD4 attachment. **Ibalizumab** is a monoclonal antibody against CD4 that blocks post-attachment conformational changes. Both are reserved for highly drug-resistant infection.

Now — the central principle of HIV therapy is **combination therapy**. You never treat HIV with one drug. Always two or three from different classes. The reason is mathematical, and it comes from the error rate of reverse transcriptase. Let's go there.

## SEGMENT 14 — Resistance and the quasispecies

Reverse transcriptase has no proofreading exonuclease. It makes roughly one error per ten thousand to one hundred thousand bases incorporated. The HIV genome is about 10,000 bases long. So a single round of replication produces, on average, about one mutation per genome. Some of those mutations are silent, some are deleterious, some have no effect, but a small fraction are advantageous.

In an untreated HIV-infected person, the virus produces roughly **ten billion new virions per day**. Multiply that by one mutation per genome, and you generate, every day, roughly ten billion point mutations — which is far more than the size of the HIV genome itself. In other words, _every possible single-base mutation in the HIV genome is generated, somewhere, in that person, every single day_. And every possible double mutation arises every few weeks.

That has a profound implication. The viral population inside an infected person is not a single genome with a few minor variants. It is a **quasispecies** — a cloud of related but distinct genomes, all the variants that haven't yet been selected against by the immune system or by drugs, hovering around a consensus sequence.

Now think about drug pressure. Give a patient a single drug. Maybe ten million of the virions in their body, that day, already carry a mutation that confers some resistance to that drug. Those variants survive. They are selected for. Within weeks, the drug stops working. Monotherapy fails. This was learned bitterly with AZT in the late 1980s, when six months of zidovudine monotherapy was followed by viral rebound carrying multiple AZT resistance mutations.

Now give two drugs from different classes. The chance that a single virion already carries resistance mutations to _both_ drugs is the product of the individual probabilities — say, 10^-5 × 10^-5 = 10^-10. In ten billion virions per day you'd expect about one such variant. Marginal. With three drugs, 10^-15 — essentially never. Triple combination therapy — **HAART** as it was originally called, now **cART** for combination antiretroviral therapy — works because the probability of pre-existing resistance to three independent drugs simultaneously is too small to find in a typical viral population.

This is why HIV therapy, from the late 1990s onward, has always been at least three drugs. It is also why **adherence** matters so enormously. Skip doses, drug levels drop, partial selection pressure favors variants with some resistance. Skip enough doses and you essentially re-create monotherapy on a population that is now genetically diverse.

The major resistance mutations are worth knowing because they appear on real clinical resistance reports. **M184V** is the signature lamivudine and emtricitabine mutation — it abolishes activity of those drugs and reduces fitness of the virus modestly, which is why some clinicians actually accept M184V on board as a way to keep the virus partially crippled. **K103N** is the classic first-generation NNRTI mutation — it confers resistance to efavirenz and nevirapine with essentially no fitness cost. **K65R** affects tenofovir. **Q151M** and the thymidine analog mutations like T215Y affect zidovudine and other older NRTIs. **N155H** and **Q148K** affect integrase inhibitors.

You don't have to memorize that list. What you have to know is the underlying principle: high error rate, huge viral population, every mutation generated every day, combination therapy required to make the math work, adherence is everything.

That principle generalizes. The same logic applies, with different numbers, to influenza, hepatitis C, SARS-CoV-2, tuberculosis. Whenever you treat a rapidly evolving infectious agent, you treat it with multiple drugs that hit different targets. Single-target monotherapy of a high-mutation-rate pathogen is, mathematically, almost always going to fail.

## SEGMENT 15 — Endogenous retroviruses: the viruses that became us

Now I want to take you somewhere unexpected. So far we've talked about retroviruses as things that infect cells, integrate into chromosomes, and produce new infectious particles. But what if integration happens into a **germline** cell — a cell that will produce sperm or egg? Then the provirus is passed on to the next generation. It is no longer an infection. It is a heritable allele.

This has happened, in our ancestors, many, many times. The result is that the human genome contains an enormous amount of retroviral DNA. The number is striking: **about 8 percent of your genome is the rotted-out remains of ancient retroviral infections**. These are called **endogenous retroviruses**, or **ERVs** — in humans, **HERVs**, for human endogenous retroviruses.

For comparison, protein-coding genes make up about 1 to 2 percent of your genome. There is, by mass, several times more retroviral DNA in you than there are genes.

Most HERVs are dead. They have accumulated mutations, deletions, frameshifts; they cannot replicate; they have no LTR-driven transcription; many are reduced to **solo LTRs**, where the internal coding sequence has been removed by recombination between the two LTRs of a provirus, leaving a single LTR scar in the chromosome. There are tens of thousands of these scars in our genome.

But some HERVs retain function — and a few of them have been _co-opted_ for host purposes. The most striking example is **syncytin**.

Syncytin-1 is a protein essential for the formation of the placenta. The placenta has, on its maternal-facing surface, a layer called the **syncytiotrophoblast** — a multinucleated giant cell formed by the fusion of many trophoblast precursors. That fusion is mediated by syncytin-1, a fusogenic membrane protein that brings cells together and merges them.

Syncytin-1 is encoded by the env gene of a HERV called **HERV-W**, which integrated into a primate ancestor about 25 to 40 million years ago. The env gene of a retrovirus exists, of course, to fuse the viral membrane with a target cell membrane — that's how the virus enters a cell. The cellular host repurposed that same fusion machinery to fuse trophoblast cells into a syncytium for the placenta. Syncytin-1 in humans, syncytin-2 from another HERV insertion, and analogous syncytin proteins in other mammalian lineages — independent retroviral co-options that all produced the same placental cell-fusion architecture.

Without syncytin, no placenta. Without placenta, no live birth as we know it. So a retroviral protein, captured from an ancient infection, became absolutely essential for mammalian reproduction. You are here, biologically, in part because of an endogenous retrovirus.

There are other examples. Many HERV LTRs have been co-opted as enhancers or promoters for cellular genes. Some HERV elements regulate gene expression in embryonic stem cells. Some HERV proteins are expressed in the brain and may play roles in immune signaling. There is active research on whether HERV reactivation contributes to autoimmune disease, neurodegeneration, and certain cancers — the picture is messy and not yet settled.

What I want you to take from this segment is a shift in framing. Retroviruses are not just pathogens. They are an evolutionary force. They have been integrating into our genomes for hundreds of millions of years. Most of those integrations are inert junk. A small fraction have been domesticated and put to host use. The boundary between "host" and "virus," at the genomic level, is much more porous than you might think.

## SEGMENT 16 — Retroviruses as tools: gene therapy vectors

Now let's flip the frame again. If a retrovirus can stably insert a sequence of DNA into a host chromosome, and if we can engineer the contents of that virus, we have the world's most efficient way of installing a new gene into a cell. That insight is the foundation of modern **gene therapy**.

The basic recipe: take a retrovirus, delete the viral protein-coding genes — gag, pol, env — but keep the cis-acting elements that the virus needs to be packaged and reverse transcribed and integrated, namely the LTRs, the PBS, the PPT, and the psi packaging signal. In place of the viral genes, insert your therapeutic gene of interest. Then produce the vector by providing gag, pol, and env in trans from a separate construct or producer cell line. The vector RNA gets packaged into a particle containing all the viral machinery, but the particle can only do one round of infection — it transduces the target cell, integrates, expresses the therapeutic gene. No further replication, because the viral genes aren't in the vector.

That's the architecture. The history is a roller coaster.

The first vectors were **gammaretroviral**, derived from murine leukemia virus. These were used in early clinical trials for **adenosine deaminase deficient severe combined immunodeficiency**, ADA-SCID — a fatal pediatric disease where a missing enzyme prevents normal T cell development. The earliest trials in the 1990s were partial successes. The trials for **X-linked SCID**, X-SCID, looked even more promising — children with no functional T cells received their own bone marrow stem cells transduced with a working IL-2 receptor gamma chain gene, and their immune systems were reconstituted.

But around 2002, several of those X-SCID children developed **T-cell leukemia**. Four out of about twenty in the French trial. The cause was traced to **insertional mutagenesis**: the gammaretroviral vector had integrated near a proto-oncogene called LMO2, and the strong viral LTR enhancer activated LMO2 transcription, contributing to T-cell transformation. The field reeled.

The response, over the following decade, was a series of safety improvements. **Self-inactivating** vectors, SIN vectors, were developed in which the U3 region of the 3' LTR is deleted. During reverse transcription, U3 sequences are copied to both LTRs — so deleting U3 from the 3' LTR results in a provirus where both LTRs are missing their U3 enhancer-promoter regions. The vector still expresses the therapeutic gene from an internal promoter you provide, but the LTR can no longer activate nearby cellular genes. This dramatically reduces the insertional mutagenesis risk.

In parallel, **lentiviral vectors** — derived from HIV-1 — replaced gammaretroviral vectors as the workhorse for most applications. Lentiviral vectors have two big advantages. First, lentiviruses can transduce non-dividing cells, opening up neurons, hematopoietic stem cells, post-mitotic cell types. Second, lentiviral integration site preferences differ from gammaretroviral ones — lentivirus prefers gene bodies, not promoters, and is somewhat less prone to activate nearby genes.

Modern lentiviral vectors are SIN, third-generation — meaning the helper functions are split across multiple plasmids to prevent recombination back to a replication-competent virus — and use a heterologous envelope, typically the **VSV-G** glycoprotein from vesicular stomatitis virus, which gives the vector broad tropism. They are produced in HEK293T cells by co-transfecting four to five plasmids.

The clinical success rate has been substantial in the past decade. **Strimvelis** for ADA-SCID. **Zynteglo** for transfusion-dependent beta-thalassemia, where a functional beta-globin gene is delivered. **Skysona** for adrenoleukodystrophy. The CAR-T cell therapies — **Kymriah**, **Yescarta**, **Tecartus**, **Breyanzi**, **Abecma**, **Carvykti** — all use lentiviral vectors to install the chimeric antigen receptor into the patient's T cells. Those drugs have transformed the treatment of certain B-cell leukemias and lymphomas and multiple myeloma.

So a virus that, in its wild-type form, causes one of the worst infectious diseases of the modern era, has — through careful engineering — become a tool that cures previously fatal genetic diseases. That arc, from pathogen to therapeutic vector, is one of the more remarkable stories in modern medicine.

## SEGMENT 17 — Oncoretroviruses and the birth of the oncogene concept

I want to take one more historical detour, because it connects to something foundational in cancer biology that grew directly out of retrovirology.

Recall Peyton Rous and his sarcoma virus. RSV transforms infected cells into tumor cells. In the 1970s, the question was: how? What does this virus carry that makes a cell cancerous?

The viral genome was sequenced and analyzed. RSV, unlike most retroviruses, has gag, pol, env — and a fourth gene called **v-src**, for viral sarcoma. v-src was the transforming gene. Delete v-src and the virus still replicates but no longer transforms.

In 1976, Harold Varmus and Michael Bishop, at UCSF, asked: where did v-src come from? They used radioactive v-src DNA as a probe and went hunting through normal, uninfected chicken cells. To everyone's astonishment, they found a closely related gene already present in the chicken genome. They called it **c-src**, for cellular src. It was a normal cellular gene. v-src was the retrovirus's hijacked, slightly mutated version of a perfectly normal cellular gene.

This was a paradigm shift. It meant that the transforming gene of a retrovirus was not, in some fundamental sense, a viral invention. The virus had picked up a host gene at some point in its evolutionary history, mutated it, and was now wielding it against the host. More than that — it meant the host genome _already contained_ genes whose mutated forms could cause cancer. These genes were named **proto-oncogenes**. Their mutated, hyperactive versions were named **oncogenes**.

Varmus and Bishop won the Nobel Prize in 1989. The concept of cellular oncogenes — that cancer is driven, in significant part, by mutations in our own genes that knock them into the "on" position — became one of the central organizing principles of modern cancer biology. We now know dozens of proto-oncogenes — RAS, MYC, ERBB, EGFR, FOS, JUN, ABL, and many more — and many of them were originally identified through their viral counterparts in transforming retroviruses isolated from various animal cancers.

It's worth pausing on this. The provirus hypothesis from segment 2 rewrote the central dogma. The discovery of cellular oncogenes from acute transforming retroviruses rewrote our understanding of cancer. Retroviruses, this tiny family, have had an outsized influence on the foundations of molecular biology.

Now, in humans, the situation is rather different. There are very few human oncoretroviruses. The main one — and as of today, the only firmly established directly-oncogenic human retrovirus — is **HTLV-1**, human T-lymphotropic virus 1. HTLV-1 infects about 10 to 20 million people worldwide, with hotspots in southern Japan, the Caribbean, parts of sub-Saharan Africa, parts of South America, and Aboriginal Australia.

Most HTLV-1 infections are lifelong but asymptomatic. About 2 to 5 percent of infected individuals develop **adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma**, ATL, a usually fatal mature T-cell malignancy. Another small fraction develop a chronic neuroinflammatory disease called **tropical spastic paraparesis** or **HTLV-1-associated myelopathy**, HAM/TSP, in which the virus drives CD8 T cell infiltration of the spinal cord.

HTLV-1's oncogenic mechanism is different from RSV's. HTLV-1 doesn't carry a hijacked cellular oncogene. Instead, it encodes its own regulatory proteins — **Tax** and **HBZ** — that disrupt host cell-cycle control, activate NF-κB, and accumulate genetic damage in infected CD4 T cells over years and decades.

The other human retrovirus you'll hear about is HTLV-2, but it has no firmly established disease association. HIV is not directly oncogenic in the way HTLV-1 is, but immunosuppression from HIV does enable a cluster of cancers — Kaposi sarcoma, certain lymphomas, cervical cancer — driven by other viruses or by loss of immune surveillance.

So, in the human disease space: HTLV-1 is the rare directly-transforming retrovirus, HIV is the dominant disease-causing retrovirus, and a handful of others may have minor or yet-to-be-confirmed roles.

\[Pause.\]

## SEGMENT 18 — The cure problem, the latency frontier, and where we are now

Let me close with where the field is actually working hardest right now: the HIV cure problem.

Combination antiretroviral therapy, since the late 1990s, has been astonishingly successful. A person diagnosed with HIV today, in a country with reliable access to medication, can expect a life expectancy approaching normal. Daily oral therapy. Undetectable viral load. **U=U** — undetectable equals untransmittable — meaning that a person with undetectable HIV viral load on treatment cannot transmit the virus sexually. This was one of the great public health discoveries of the last decade.

But cART does not cure HIV. If you stop the drugs, the virus comes back, almost always within weeks. The reason is the **latent reservoir**.

When HIV integrates into a resting memory CD4 T cell, the chromatin around the integration site can be repressive, transcription factors like NF-κB are inactive, and the provirus produces essentially no RNA. The infected cell is invisible to the immune system because it's not displaying viral peptides, and it's invisible to cART because the drugs target steps in the replication cycle that aren't happening. The cell just sits there, with an integrated, silent, but fully replication-competent provirus.

When something activates that cell — antigen exposure, cytokine signaling, homeostatic proliferation — the provirus can wake up. Transcription resumes. New virions are produced. The infection rebounds.

Estimates of the latent reservoir size suggest something like one million latently infected cells in a typical person on long-term cART. That is small relative to the total CD4 T cell pool, but it is the reason a cure is hard. You cannot kill cells that are not flagging themselves as infected. Existing drugs cannot eliminate them.

Several cure strategies are under active investigation. Let me sketch them.

**Shock and kill.** The idea is to wake up the latent provirus with a **latency-reversing agent**, force it to express viral proteins, and then either let the immune system or a targeted therapy eliminate the now-visible infected cell. Various drug classes have been tried — HDAC inhibitors like vorinostat, PKC agonists like bryostatin, BET inhibitors. So far, no LRA has produced a meaningful reduction in reservoir size in clinical trials. It turns out that waking up the virus is necessary but not sufficient — the immune system, deeply scarred by chronic HIV infection, often fails to clear the reactivated cells.

**Block and lock.** The opposite approach: instead of waking the reservoir up, drive it into a deeper, more permanent latency. The idea is that if the provirus can be locked in a fully silenced state with extreme reliability, it might never reactivate. **Didehydro-cortistatin A**, a Tat inhibitor, can lock cells in a Tat-deficient state where the provirus simply cannot escape latency. This is a more recent strategy, and is in early-phase exploration.

**Gene therapy approaches.** Use a gene-editing tool — zinc finger nucleases, TALENs, CRISPR-Cas9 — to disrupt CCR5 in the patient's own hematopoietic stem cells. After re-engraftment, the new T cells lack the coreceptor and are resistant to R5 HIV. **Sangamo Therapeutics** ran clinical trials of CCR5-ZFN-edited CD4 T cells. CRISPR-based approaches are now in trials. The challenge is achieving high enough editing efficiency in the right cells without significant off-target damage.

**Stem cell transplant from a CCR5-Δ32 homozygous donor.** This is how the field's most striking cure cases happened. **Timothy Ray Brown**, the "Berlin patient," received an allogeneic bone marrow transplant for leukemia in 2007 from a CCR5-Δ32/Δ32 donor. He remained HIV-undetectable off all antiretrovirals for 14 years, until his death from leukemia recurrence in 2020. He is widely accepted as the first person cured of HIV. **Adam Castillejo**, the "London patient," was cured by the same approach in 2019. The Düsseldorf patient and others have since followed. But this is not a scalable cure — bone marrow transplant has substantial mortality and morbidity and is only justifiable in the context of treating another disease that requires it.

**Broadly neutralizing antibodies**, bNAbs. A small fraction of people living with HIV develop, after years of infection, antibodies that can neutralize a wide range of HIV variants. These antibodies — VRC01, 3BNC117, 10-1074, others — recognize highly conserved epitopes on Env. They can be cloned, manufactured, and administered as immunotherapy. Trials are exploring bNAbs as alternative or complementary therapy to cART, and as part of cure strategies. The field has not yet produced a fully sterilizing cure with bNAbs, but the results are encouraging for long-acting maintenance therapy.

**Long-acting cART.** Even without a cure, drug development has made enormous strides in convenience. **Cabotegravir + rilpivirine** is approved as a long-acting injectable given every one or two months instead of daily pills. **Lenacapavir** is a capsid inhibitor administered subcutaneously every six months. These don't cure infection, but they make sustained suppression far less burdensome.

The honest summary is that an HIV cure remains an unsolved problem. The reservoir is the wall. We have tools that can suppress the virus indefinitely. We do not yet have a reliable way to eliminate the integrated provirus. The field continues to work on it.

\[Pause.\]

So let me close where we began. The central dogma was one of the great organizing principles of twentieth-century biology, and the retroviruses broke it. From that one discovery, you can trace a remarkable arc — reverse transcriptase, the provirus, the integrated viral genome, the origins of cellular oncogenes, the HIV pandemic, the development of antiretroviral therapy, the engineering of lentiviral vectors that now cure genetic diseases, the discovery that nearly a tenth of our own genomes is retroviral, the realization that the placenta itself depends on a captured retroviral protein.

This is a small family of viruses that has had an outsized effect on biology, on medicine, and on us. They are pathogens, they are tools, they are fossils inside our chromosomes, and in at least one case they are essential for our own reproduction. They are simultaneously the worst infectious disease problem of the last half-century and the most useful gene delivery vehicle we have engineered.

When you encounter retroviruses again — and you will, in research, in clinic, in the news — I want you to bring back not just the molecular details, but that frame. RNA goes in. DNA comes out. It integrates. Forever. The simple chemistry of one enzyme generates all the rest.

Thank you. We'll take questions.

## APPENDIX — Quick-reference summary (not for reading aloud)

**Core idea**: retroviruses break the central dogma — RNA genome reverse-transcribed to DNA by virion-packaged reverse transcriptase (RT), then integrated into host chromosome as a permanent provirus.

**Particle**: ~100 nm, enveloped, with Env (SU + TM); matrix MA inside membrane; conical capsid CA (HIV) enclosing diploid (+)ssRNA genome bound by NC, plus RT, IN, PR enzymes.

**Genome**: 5' cap—R—U5—PBS—**gag**—**pol**—**env**—PPT—U3—R—poly-A. LTRs (U3-R-U5) are generated during RT, not pre-existing.

**Life cycle**: entry (receptor + coreceptor + fusion) → RT (two strand transfers, LTRs built) → integration (PIC, integrase strand transfer) → transcription (host RNA pol II from 5' LTR; Tat–TAR releases pause) → splicing/export (Rev–RRE–CRM1 for incompletely spliced RNAs) → translation (Gag, Gag-Pol via −1 frameshift, ~20:1 ratio; Env via secretory pathway) → assembly at PM (Gag oligomerizes, NC grabs psi-marked RNA) → budding (ESCRT via p6 late domains) → maturation (PR cleaves Gag/Gag-Pol → conical capsid → infectious).

**HIV-specific**: CD4 + CCR5 or CXCR4; gp120/gp41; CCR5-Δ32 homozygotes resistant to R5 virus; lentivirus crosses intact nuclear envelope (so transduces non-dividing cells).

**Drug classes by life-cycle step**:
- Entry: enfuvirtide (gp41 fusion), maraviroc (CCR5), fostemsavir (CD4 attachment), ibalizumab (anti-CD4 mAb).
- RT: NRTIs (chain terminators — AZT, tenofovir, lamivudine, emtricitabine, abacavir); NNRTIs (allosteric — efavirenz, rilpivirine, doravirine).
- Integration: INSTIs (raltegravir, dolutegravir, bictegravir, cabotegravir).
- Maturation: PIs (darunavir, atazanavir, usually boosted with ritonavir/cobicistat).
- Capsid: lenacapavir (long-acting).

**Resistance**: RT error ~10^-4–10^-5/base × ~10^10 virions/day × no proofreading → every mutation generated daily → quasispecies → monotherapy fails. Combination of 3 drugs from ≥2 classes is the foundation of cART. Signature mutations: M184V (3TC/FTC), K103N (1st-gen NNRTI), K65R (TDF), Q151M / T215Y (older NRTIs), N155H / Q148K (INSTI).

**ERVs**: ~8% of human genome. Syncytin-1 (HERV-W env) essential for placental syncytiotrophoblast formation — co-opted retroviral fusogen.

**Vectors**: SIN (self-inactivating, U3-deleted) lentiviral vectors with VSV-G envelope, third-generation split-packaging. Lentivirus transduces non-dividing cells. Insertional mutagenesis risk (gammaretroviral LMO2 → leukemia in X-SCID trials) drove SIN design. Clinical: Strimvelis (ADA-SCID), Zynteglo (β-thalassemia), Skysona (ALD), all CAR-T (Kymriah, Yescarta, Tecartus, Breyanzi, Abecma, Carvykti).

**Oncoretroviruses**: v-src in RSV → c-src in chicken genome → cellular proto-oncogene concept (Varmus/Bishop, Nobel 1989). Human: HTLV-1 → adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma and HAM/TSP, via Tax and HBZ regulatory proteins.

**Cure strategies**: shock-and-kill (LRAs: HDACi, BET inhibitors, PKC agonists), block-and-lock (Tat inhibitors like dCA), CCR5 gene editing (ZFN, CRISPR), CCR5-Δ32 allogeneic stem cell transplant (Berlin, London, Düsseldorf patients), broadly neutralizing antibodies (VRC01, 3BNC117, 10-1074). Reservoir is the central obstacle: long-lived latently infected resting memory CD4 T cells with integrated, silent, replication-competent provirus.

**Two organizing principles**: (1) every step of the retroviral life cycle is a drug target — anatomy of the cycle predicts the pharmacology; (2) the line between virus and host is porous — integration is permanent, ERVs are abundant, co-option happens, and gene therapy weaponizes that biology for medicine.
