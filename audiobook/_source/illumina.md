## SEGMENT 1 — Why Illumina dominates, and what changed when sequencing got cheap

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. Today and for the next two hours, we're going to talk about Illumina sequencing — specifically, the technology that single-handedly turned reading a human genome from a multi-billion-dollar international megaproject into a routine assay you can order online for a few hundred bucks and have results back in a couple of days. That sentence sounds like marketing copy. It's not. It's an understatement of one of the most dramatic technological shifts in the history of biology. And the engine of that shift is a single platform — sequencing by synthesis, abbreviated SBS — which Illumina perfected and now sells from instruments that range from a desktop box the size of a coffee maker to a refrigerator-sized monster that can read twenty thousand human genomes a year.

So let me start with a number that should genuinely shock you. The Human Genome Project, which ran from 1990 to 2003, cost roughly three billion dollars and took about thirteen years to produce the first complete human genome sequence. That's a single genome, by an international consortium of hundreds of labs, using the best technology available at the time — Sanger sequencing, which we'll come back to. By 2014, an Illumina instrument called the HiSeq X Ten could sequence a human genome for about a thousand dollars in a couple of days, on its own. By 2023, the NovaSeq X — Illumina's latest flagship — pushed that cost down to roughly two hundred dollars per genome. So in twenty years, the cost of sequencing one human dropped from three billion dollars to two hundred. That's a factor of fifteen million. Moore's law, the famous trajectory of computer chips, looks tame next to it. Sequencing got cheaper, faster than anything else in modern technology has ever gotten cheaper. And the reason is Illumina.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy I want you to hold in your head for the next two hours. Imagine you wanted to figure out, letter by letter, the contents of a very long book — say, three billion letters long, which is roughly the size of the human genome. The old way of doing it, Sanger sequencing, was like reading the book carefully with a single very precise person sitting at a desk, taking their time, reading one section at a time, then moving on to the next. The careful reader is accurate — they don't make many mistakes — but they're slow, and they're expensive, and if you want to read the whole book you have to wait. Illumina sequencing, by contrast, is like ripping the book into millions of tiny scraps — each scrap a couple of sentences long — pasting all the scraps onto a glass slide, and then having millions of tiny readers, each one assigned to one scrap, all reading their scrap at the same time while a camera takes a photograph every few seconds to record what each reader has just read. Millions of tiny sequencing reactions, happening side by side on a glass slide, all watched by a camera that takes a photo after each cycle. That's the picture. Hold it.

The key word in that sentence is **parallelism**. Sanger sequencing reads one fragment of DNA at a time. Illumina reads, depending on the instrument, somewhere between a few million and a few _billion_ fragments simultaneously, all on the same surface, in the same run. That parallelism is where all the speed and all the cost reduction come from. The chemistry of reading any single fragment, on an Illumina instrument, is not dramatically faster than reading a single fragment with Sanger. It's actually slower per base. But because you're reading ten billion fragments at the same time instead of one, the total throughput is something like ten billion times higher. That's the trick.

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, sketch the four-step cycle of Illumina sequencing on a whiteboard and explain what each step does and why; two, explain in plain English why bridge amplification and clusters are necessary, given how dim a single molecule's signal is; three, look at any modern genomics paper that says "Illumina sequencing was performed" and have a real intuition for what that means about cost, read length, error rates, and what the data looks like — then we've succeeded. Everything else hangs off those three.

\[Pause.\]

One more orienting fact before we go deeper. Illumina, the company, controls something like seventy to eighty percent of the global next-generation sequencing market — and at various times in the last decade that share has been higher than ninety percent. When you read a paper that says "we sequenced this with Illumina" or "we performed RNA-seq" or "we did whole-exome sequencing on these patients," what you're almost certainly reading about is data produced by an Illumina machine. The big competitors — and we'll meet a few of them in Segment 16 — are real but small. For now, when I say "next-generation sequencing," I mean, in the overwhelming majority of cases, Illumina. The platform is so dominant that "Illumina-style data" has become a kind of generic format that the rest of bioinformatics is built around — short reads, paired-end, with quality scores attached, in files called FASTQ. The whole downstream ecosystem of variant callers, aligners, expression quantifiers, and analysis tools was shaped by what Illumina produces.

So that's the contract. We're going to spend two hours on one platform — the platform — and we're going to do it the way you'd want to understand any dominant technology: by getting the chemistry right, then the engineering, then the economics, then the failure modes, then the competitive landscape. Let's start with where it came from.

## SEGMENT 2 — The pre-history: Solexa, 1998 Cambridge, Balasubramanian and Klenerman

Illumina sequencing did not begin at Illumina. It began at the University of Cambridge in the late 1990s, in the chemistry department, in the lab of two young researchers named Shankar Balasubramanian and David Klenerman. The story of how their academic curiosity became, two decades later, the dominant sequencing technology on Earth is worth telling, because it's a beautiful case study in how a piece of basic-science work — driven by an interest that had nothing to do with sequencing at first — becomes a globally important technology.

In 1997 and 1998, Balasubramanian and Klenerman were not trying to build a sequencer. They were trying to understand how DNA polymerase — the enzyme that copies DNA — works at the level of single molecules. The fundamental question was a physical one: can we watch a polymerase add a single nucleotide to a growing DNA strand and detect that single event optically? Both of them were chemists with strong interests in fluorescence, and their idea was to attach a fluorescent dye to a single nucleotide, drop a single DNA molecule with a polymerase onto a glass surface, and watch the polymerase pull in fluorescent nucleotides one at a time. This was a basic-science exercise. The goal was understanding the enzyme.

But here is the moment of realization. They sat down in a pub — there is, depending on who tells the story, a specific Cambridge pub involved — and they started thinking about what their setup actually amounted to. If you could detect single nucleotides being incorporated one at a time, by a polymerase copying a template, then in principle you were _sequencing_ that template. Watch which fluorescent color flashes each cycle, and you've just read out a letter. Repeat over and over, and you read out a sequence. Their basic-science fluorescence experiment was, structurally, a primitive sequencing reaction. The leap from "this is a single-molecule biophysics tool" to "this is a sequencing platform" took place over the course of one conversation.

\[Pause.\]

The technical idea that made the whole thing work was something called **reversible terminator chemistry**. Here's the problem you have to solve. If you just add four fluorescent nucleotides — fluorescent A, fluorescent T, fluorescent G, fluorescent C — to a polymerase and a template, the polymerase will add them as fast as it can, and you'll get a long synthesized strand with no way to read out one base at a time. The polymerase will add ten or twenty bases before you can take a photograph and figure out what happened. You need a way to make the polymerase add exactly one nucleotide, then stop, then wait while you take a picture, then resume. You need a brake.

The brake they invented — building on earlier chemistry from Bruno Canard and Simon Sarfati at the Pasteur Institute in Paris — was to chemically modify each nucleotide so that, after it gets incorporated into the growing strand, it physically blocks the polymerase from adding the next one. The blocker sits on the three-prime hydroxyl group of the nucleotide — the very chemical handle the polymerase needs to grab in order to attach the next base. Block that handle and the polymerase stalls, frozen at exactly one new base added. Take a photograph to see which dye lit up. Then chemically remove the blocker, washing it off with a mild reagent, freeing up the handle again. Now the polymerase can add the next nucleotide. Add the next round of fluorescent blocked nucleotides, repeat. One base per cycle. Take a picture. Cleave. Repeat. The elegance of the chemistry is that the same modification that carries the dye also carries the blocker, so when you chemically remove the blocker you also remove the dye, leaving a clean, unmodified nucleotide ready to extend further. One molecule, two functions, both reversed in one step.

Balasubramanian and Klenerman founded a company in 1998 called **Solexa** — the name a play on "solution exotic" or simply a catchy mash-up; the etymology is a bit murky — to commercialize this idea. For about eight years, Solexa was a small British startup, mostly invisible to the outside world, slowly working through every engineering problem you have to solve to turn a clever chemistry idea into a robust instrument. There were a lot of those problems. How do you immobilize DNA on a surface densely enough to be useful but sparsely enough that you can see individual signals? How do you amplify a single DNA molecule's signal so a camera can detect it? How do you make the polymerase tolerate the bulky modifications without slowing to a crawl? How do you do hundreds of wash steps in a row without the chemistry degrading? Every one of these is a separate research project, and Solexa solved them one by one over the better part of a decade.

\[Pause.\]

In 2007, Illumina, an American company that had built its reputation on a different platform — bead arrays for genotyping — bought Solexa for about six hundred and fifty million dollars. At the time, this was a striking acquisition; Illumina was betting that next-generation sequencing was about to take off, and Solexa's reversible terminator platform was the most promising candidate. The bet paid off spectacularly. Within five years, Illumina sequencing had effectively become the standard, displacing the competing technologies of the time — pyrosequencing from a company called 454, ligation-based sequencing from a platform called SOLiD, and a few others — into commercial obscurity. By 2014, the HiSeq X Ten launch announcement claimed the long-promised "thousand-dollar genome," and the dominance was complete.

What I want you to take from this segment is the lineage. The technology is sometimes called Illumina sequencing, sometimes Solexa sequencing, sometimes sequencing by synthesis, sometimes SBS, sometimes reversible terminator sequencing. These all refer to the same thing — the chemistry invented by Balasubramanian and Klenerman in Cambridge in the late 1990s, commercialized by Solexa, scaled up by Illumina. The fundamental chemistry has not changed in twenty-five years. What changed is the engineering around it — the density of clusters, the speed of imaging, the throughput of the optics, the chemistry's tolerance to longer reads. The cost reductions of the last two decades come almost entirely from engineering improvements layered on top of the same core invention. That's worth remembering: a single chemical insight, refined and scaled relentlessly, can collapse the cost of an entire field by seven orders of magnitude.

Now let's actually open the hood and look at the parts.

## SEGMENT 3 — The four ingredients: a flow cell, bridge amplification, reversible terminators, a camera

Every Illumina instrument, from the smallest iSeq to the largest NovaSeq X, runs on the same four core components. If you understand these four things, you understand the platform. Everything else is engineering scale — making each component bigger, denser, faster, cheaper — but the fundamental architecture is constant. So let me lay out the four ingredients, and then we'll spend the next several segments going deep on each.

**Ingredient one: a flow cell.** The flow cell is a small piece of glass, about the size of a microscope slide, with a series of channels etched into it. Through these channels you can flow reagents — nucleotides, enzymes, wash buffers — and on the inner surface of the channels, the actual sequencing happens. The flow cell is the stage where everything takes place. It is the most underappreciated component of the system, because all the chemistry needs to work on its inner surface, in repeated cycles, without contamination or degradation, sometimes for forty-eight hours straight. Think of it as a **microscope slide ruled with billions of anchor points where DNA fragments stick**. Each anchor point is the start of a single sequencing reaction. A NovaSeq X flow cell has on the order of fifty billion such anchor points. That's an absurd density. It's why throughput per run is what it is.

**Ingredient two: bridge amplification.** Here's the problem. A single DNA molecule, with a single fluorescent dye attached to one new base, gives off only a tiny amount of light when illuminated. Way too little for a camera to reliably detect at the speeds we need. So before you start reading, you take each DNA fragment and you make about a thousand identical copies of it, all clustered together in one tiny spot on the flow cell. When the polymerase adds a new base in that spot, all thousand molecules incorporate the same base at the same time, and all thousand dyes light up together. The signal from a cluster is a thousand times brighter than from a single molecule — bright enough for a camera to pick up cleanly. The technique that produces these clusters is called bridge amplification, and we'll spend Segment 5 on it. For now, the picture: **each starting DNA molecule grows into a cluster of about a thousand identical copies, like a colony of clones standing next to each other. You read the cluster's signal rather than the individual molecule's.** That's the second ingredient.

**Ingredient three: reversible terminator chemistry.** We met this in Segment 2. The four nucleotides, each carrying a distinct fluorescent dye and a chemical blocker that prevents the polymerase from adding more than one base per cycle. Add the nucleotides, let the polymerase incorporate one, take a photograph to record the color, chemically cleave off the dye and the blocker, repeat. This is the actual sequencing chemistry. **One base per cycle, color-coded, with a built-in brake.** That's ingredient three.

**Ingredient four: a camera and a light source.** After each cycle of incorporation, the instrument illuminates the flow cell with laser light at specific wavelengths and takes a series of photographs through a high-resolution microscope. Each photograph captures the fluorescence emitted by the clusters. The instrument moves the camera across the flow cell, tile by tile, photographing the entire surface. From these photographs, software extracts the color at each cluster's position in each cycle, and from that color, it infers which base was just added. **Take a photo, read out the color of every cluster, record what base each cluster just incorporated.** That's ingredient four.

\[Pause.\]

So here is the picture you should now have. A glass slide, ruled with billions of microscopic anchor points. At each anchor point, a tiny cluster of about a thousand identical DNA molecules, all derived from one starting fragment. Reagents flowing across the surface, delivering blocked fluorescent nucleotides. After each delivery, a wash, a flash of laser light, a photograph through the microscope, recording which color shone at each cluster's position. Then a chemical cleavage step to remove the dye and the blocker, getting the molecules ready for the next round. Repeat this cycle one hundred and fifty times, or three hundred times, depending on how long a read you want. At the end, the instrument has accumulated a stack of one hundred and fifty photographs of the same flow cell, and at each cluster's position, a sequence of one hundred and fifty colors — which is to say, a sequence of one hundred and fifty bases. Run this on a billion clusters in parallel and you have a hundred and fifty billion base pairs of sequence data, which is roughly fifty times the size of the human genome, in about a day.

The four-step cycle at the heart of all this — **incorporate, image, cleave, repeat** — is the master rhythm of Illumina sequencing. Memorize it. Sketch it. Hold it in your head. If you understand incorporate-image-cleave-repeat, multiplied by parallelism on a flow cell, you have the whole platform in compressed form. The rest of this lecture is just unpacking each of those four words and explaining the engineering required to make them work at scale.

Let's go through them in order. Library prep first, because before any of this can happen, you have to get your DNA into a form the flow cell can recognize.

## SEGMENT 4 — Library prep: fragmenting, adapter ligation, indexing

Before any DNA goes anywhere near a flow cell, it has to be turned into what Illumina calls a **library** — a collection of DNA fragments, each one of a defined size range, each one with specific synthetic sequences attached to both ends. The library is what gets loaded onto the instrument. Library prep is the step that converts whatever DNA you started with — genomic DNA from a blood sample, RNA from a tumor, environmental DNA from a soil sample — into a form that the Illumina chemistry can grab onto and read.

Library prep has three stages, in this order: fragmentation, end repair and adapter ligation, and indexing. Let me walk through each.

**Stage one: fragmentation.** Genomic DNA, as it comes out of a cell, is enormous — chromosomes hundreds of millions of base pairs long. Illumina can only read short fragments, typically a few hundred base pairs at a time. So the first thing you have to do is chop your starting DNA into pieces of roughly the right size. There are several ways to do this. The classical method is **sonication** — you put the DNA in a tube, blast it with high-frequency sound waves, and the mechanical shearing fractures the DNA into random fragments of a tunable size, depending on how long you sonicate and at what intensity. The newer and increasingly popular method is **tagmentation** — using a modified enzyme called a transposase that simultaneously cuts the DNA and inserts adapter sequences in one step. Tagmentation is faster, requires less starting material, and we'll come back to it in Segment 11 when we discuss the Nextera library prep family. Either way, the output is the same: a tube full of DNA fragments, somewhere between two hundred and a thousand base pairs long, with random ends.

**Stage two: end repair and adapter ligation.** Random fragmentation produces ragged ends — some single-stranded overhangs, some blunt, some with chemical groups in the wrong place. The first thing you do is enzymatically "polish" the ends, making them blunt and chemically compatible with the next step. Then you add an A-tailing step — appending a single A nucleotide to the 3-prime end of each fragment — to set up a sticky end for ligation. Now you bring in the adapters.

The adapters are synthetic, double-stranded pieces of DNA, designed in the lab, that get ligated onto both ends of every fragment in the library. Each adapter contains three functional regions. First, a sequence that's complementary to the oligonucleotides anchored on the flow cell — this is what lets the fragment bind to the flow cell surface in the next stage. Second, a primer binding site that the sequencing reaction will use to start reading. Third, an index sequence, which we'll come to in a moment. The adapters are the **plug-and-play interface** between your sample and the instrument. Without adapters, your fragment is just a random piece of DNA. With adapters, it has a barcode, a binding handle, and a starting line.

\[Pause.\]

**Stage three: indexing.** This is the trick that makes Illumina sequencing economically practical for studying many samples. Suppose you have a hundred patient samples that you want to sequence. You don't want to run a hundred separate flow cells — that would be expensive and slow. Instead, during library prep, you give each sample its own unique short sequence — typically eight to ten base pairs long — that gets ligated into the adapters. We call this the **index** or **barcode**. Sample one gets index AAGCTT, sample two gets index TTCAGG, sample three gets index CGATAC, and so on. After all the samples have been individually indexed, you pool them together into a single tube — _multiplexing_ — and load the pooled library onto a single flow cell. The instrument sequences everything together. Then, in the data analysis step, the software reads the index portion of each read and uses it to **demultiplex** — that is, to sort the reads back into per-sample bins based on which index they carry. A hundred samples on one flow cell. A thousand samples, if you're being aggressive. Indexing is the bookkeeping system that makes Illumina sequencing feasible at the scale of large clinical and population studies.

Here's the analogy. **Indexing is like adding return-address barcodes to a thousand different envelopes before you dump them all into the same mail sorting machine. The machine processes them all together — much faster than processing one envelope at a time — and the barcodes let you re-sort them into the right destinations at the end.** Without indexing, you'd be stuck running one sample per flow cell. With indexing, you can run thousands.

A subtle point that matters for any clinical work: modern Illumina libraries usually use **dual indexing** — two separate index sequences, one at each end of the fragment, both of which the instrument reads independently. Dual indexing is much more robust against a failure mode called index hopping, which we'll cover in Segment 14. For now, just know that the index sometimes lives at both ends rather than one.

\[Pause.\]

There are many variants on library prep, customized for different starting materials. **RNA-seq** libraries start by converting RNA to DNA with reverse transcriptase, then proceed with adapter ligation. **ChIP-seq** libraries start with DNA that's been pulled down by a specific antibody. **Exome** libraries undergo a hybridization-based capture step after adapter ligation, where biotinylated probes pull out only the protein-coding regions of the genome and discard everything else. **Methylation** libraries use a chemical treatment called bisulfite conversion before fragmentation, to read out which cytosines are methylated. Every one of these variants is a riff on the same theme: fragment your starting material, attach adapters, add an index, sometimes do an enrichment step, and then load it onto the flow cell.

The whole library prep workflow takes a few hours, can be automated on robots, and is now the most expensive and time-consuming bottleneck of a typical sequencing project. The instrument run itself, ironically, is often faster and cheaper than getting the samples ready for it. We'll come back to library prep variants in Segment 11. For now, you have a tube of fragments, each one with adapters on both ends and a barcode in the middle of the adapter. You're ready to load the flow cell.

## SEGMENT 5 — Bridge amplification and cluster generation: turning one molecule into a glowing dot

So you've made your library. You pipette a small volume — typically a few microliters — into the loading port of the flow cell, the instrument pumps it through the channels, and the fragments encounter the inner surface for the first time. What happens next is one of the most beautiful pieces of molecular choreography in the whole platform. We need to spend a segment on it, because the cluster generation step is where a single DNA molecule becomes a glowing dot bright enough for a camera to see.

The inner surface of the flow cell, as I mentioned, is decorated with billions of short DNA oligonucleotides — call them anchor oligos. Each anchor oligo is grafted to the glass by chemical bonds, sticking up from the surface like a tiny hair. There are actually two different anchor oligo sequences, intermingled across the surface — let's call them the **P5 oligo** and the **P7 oligo**, which are the actual names Illumina uses. These two sequences correspond to the two ends of the adapters you ligated onto your fragments during library prep. So your fragment, with a P5-complementary sequence on one end and a P7-complementary sequence on the other, has one end that can bind to the P5 anchor on the flow cell, and the other end that can bind to the P7 anchor.

When the library flows over the surface, each fragment finds a P5 oligo by chance — both ends are single-stranded after a denaturation step — and binds. One end is now anchored. The other end is floating free, dangling in the buffer. The instrument then performs an extension reaction: a polymerase comes in and uses the anchored DNA as a primer, copying the rest of the fragment. Now you have a single fragment, attached at one end to the flow cell, fully double-stranded. The next wash strips off the original template strand, leaving behind a single covalently attached copy of the fragment with its P7 sequence dangling free at the top.

Now here is the bridge amplification trick. The dangling P7 end of the molecule, instead of just floating around, _bends over_ — because the flow cell surface is densely covered with P7 anchor oligos too — and binds to a nearby P7 oligo. The fragment is now stuck to the flow cell at both ends, forming a little arch, or **bridge**. A polymerase comes in, extends from the second anchor, and synthesizes a complementary copy of the original fragment. After this round of bridge formation and extension, where there was one fragment attached at one point, there are now two fragments — the original and its complement — each attached at one point. The instrument then denatures the bridge, separating the two strands, and each strand is free to fold over and form a new bridge with another nearby anchor oligo. Another round of extension produces four molecules from two. Then eight. Then sixteen. After about thirty-five rounds of bridge amplification, you have something like a thousand molecules clustered together in one tiny patch on the flow cell, all derived from the single starting fragment.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy. **Imagine you drop a single seed onto a flat field that is covered, end to end, in fertile soil. The seed germinates. Every time it makes a new leaf, that leaf bends down, touches the soil, and roots itself, becoming a new plant. After many rounds, you've got a tight little colony of identical clones, all standing next to each other in one spot — clones of the original seed.** A cluster, in Illumina-speak, is exactly that: a clonal colony of about a thousand identical DNA molecules, derived from one starting fragment, all anchored densely on a tiny patch of the flow cell surface. You read the cluster's signal rather than the individual molecule's. Because the molecules in a cluster are all identical, when the polymerase adds a new base during sequencing, all thousand molecules in the cluster incorporate the same base at the same time, and the camera sees a bright, clean dot of light.

Why a thousand? Because that's about the minimum signal-to-noise ratio a sensitive camera can pick up reliably in a fraction of a second. Fewer copies would be too dim; you'd lose accuracy. More copies would take longer to amplify and would take up too much surface area, reducing the total density of clusters per flow cell. A thousand is the engineering sweet spot. It also means that if there's a rare error in any individual molecule of the cluster — say, the polymerase made a mistake on one strand during one round of amplification — the cluster's collective signal is still dominated by the majority, and the camera still reads the right color. The amplification is also an error-suppression mechanism, by majority vote.

\[Pause.\]

One subtlety. After bridge amplification, every cluster is a mix of both strands of the original fragment — the original sense strand and its antisense complement, alternating. For sequencing, you only want one strand at a time, or the two strands' signals would jumble together. So the instrument performs a step called **linearization**: it uses a chemical or enzymatic treatment that selectively cleaves one strand at every position, leaving only one strand attached to the surface. Now the cluster contains a thousand identical single-stranded molecules, all pointing the same way, all ready to be sequenced.

The last preparatory step is to add the sequencing primer — a short synthetic oligonucleotide that anneals to the adapter region of every molecule in every cluster, providing a starting point for the polymerase. Now everything is set. The flow cell is loaded with billions of clean, linearized, primed clusters, each one a clonal patch of about a thousand identical molecules. The first cycle of sequencing can begin.

A modern NovaSeq X flow cell has on the order of fifty billion clusters across its surface. Fifty billion. Each cluster a clonal patch maybe one micron across, packed so densely that the centers of adjacent clusters are only a few microns apart. The flow cell, by area, is mostly clusters. And every single cluster represents a separate sequencing reaction that will run in parallel, all controlled by the same flowing reagents, all imaged by the same camera, all of them generating their own independent read of their own piece of your starting DNA. That is parallelism, made physical.

## SEGMENT 6 — The sequencing-by-synthesis cycle: incorporate, image, cleave, repeat

Now we get to the actual reading. The four-step cycle that is the master rhythm of Illumina sequencing. I want to walk through each step in real detail, because once you have this cycle in your bones, everything else about the platform makes sense.

**Step one: incorporate.** The instrument flows a mixture of four nucleotides — modified A, modified T, modified G, modified C — across the surface of the flow cell. Each of these nucleotides has been chemically modified in two ways. First, a fluorescent dye has been attached, with each base getting a different color: in the classical four-channel chemistry, A gets red, C gets one shade of green or sometimes yellow, G gets dark blue, T gets a different green. Second, the three-prime hydroxyl of each nucleotide — the oxygen that the polymerase needs to grab to extend the chain — has been chemically blocked with a removable protecting group. The polymerase that's sitting on each cluster's primer-template junction grabs the correct nucleotide that matches the next base in its template, attaches it to the growing strand, and then — because of the three-prime block — stops. It physically cannot add another nucleotide. The brake is on. After about a minute of incorporation time, every cluster on the flow cell has had exactly one new base added.

Then a wash. All the unincorporated nucleotides — the ones that drifted in but never got attached — are flushed away. The flow cell is now in a clean state: each cluster has been extended by exactly one base, and that base carries a fluorescent dye corresponding to its identity.

**Step two: image.** The instrument illuminates the flow cell with laser light at the wavelengths that excite the fluorescent dyes, and a camera takes a series of high-resolution photographs through a microscope objective, moving across the flow cell tile by tile. Each cluster shines with a color that reveals which base it just added. In the four-channel chemistry, you take four separate exposures — one for each dye's emission wavelength — and you record, for every cluster, which color shone brightest. That color tells you, unambiguously, which base was just added. Software running on the instrument processes the images in real time, extracting the color at each cluster's known position and recording a base call. A is red, C is yellow-green, G is dark, T is bright green — that base call is appended to the sequence record for that cluster.

The imaging step is, in some ways, the most demanding piece of engineering in the whole instrument. To photograph a NovaSeq X flow cell with enough resolution to distinguish adjacent clusters, the optics have to be extraordinarily precise, the camera sensors extraordinarily sensitive, the focus has to be maintained over the entire surface despite tiny variations in flow cell thickness. The newest instruments use sensors based on the same kind of CMOS chips that go into smartphone cameras, scaled and tuned for this specific use. The throughput of the optics is one of the main things that has improved over instrument generations — newer cameras can image a whole tile in a fraction of a second, where older ones took several seconds per tile.

\[Pause.\]

**Step three: cleave.** Now you have to undo both modifications on the just-added nucleotide. The fluorescent dye has to come off, so it doesn't contaminate the next cycle's photograph. The three-prime block has to come off, so the polymerase can resume extending. Conveniently, both modifications are designed to be removed by the same chemical reagent, in a single wash step. The cleavage reagent is flowed across the surface, the dye drops off into solution, the three-prime block is converted back to a normal hydroxyl, and the cluster is now ready to receive the next nucleotide. The chemistry has, as my old advisor used to say, "rolled back to a clean slate" — except now the strand is one base longer than it was at the start of the cycle.

**Step four: repeat.** Flow in the next round of modified nucleotides. Wait for incorporation. Wash. Image. Cleave. Wash. Repeat. And so on, for as many cycles as you want reads. A typical Illumina run for short-read sequencing does either 150 cycles or 300 cycles, producing reads of 150 base pairs or 300 base pairs from each cluster.

Here's the analogy. **Reversible terminator chemistry is the elegant trick. Each cycle: add one of four fluorescent nucleotides with a built-in blocker, so only ONE base gets added per round. Take a photo. Chemically remove the blocker and the dye in one step. Go again.** That cycle, repeated 150 or 300 times, with the camera recording the color of every one of a billion clusters each cycle, is the whole reading process. Everything else — the cluster generation, the library prep, the paired-end reads, the indexing — is preparation or interpretation around that core rhythm.

\[Pause.\]

A few practical numbers. Each cycle takes a few minutes — incorporation, wash, image (which is the slowest part, because of the time to scan the whole flow cell), cleave, wash. A full 150-cycle run takes somewhere between four and twelve hours on a modern instrument, depending on the model and the chemistry kit. Throw in cluster generation and the additional steps for paired-end reads and indexing, and a full run on a NovaSeq is more like a day to a day and a half. That gets you, on a NovaSeq X with a full flow cell, on the order of fifty billion clusters times 300 cycles, or about fifteen terabases — fifteen trillion base pairs — of sequence. That's roughly five thousand human genomes' worth of data in one run. In one machine, in one day.

Compare that to Sanger sequencing — the technology that produced the original Human Genome Project. A single Sanger reaction produces maybe a thousand base pairs of sequence and takes a few hours. To get fifteen trillion base pairs from Sanger sequencing, you would need to run something like ten billion separate reactions, which would consume the global capacity of every Sanger sequencer ever built, running continuously for decades. Illumina does this in a day. That is what parallelism, applied relentlessly to the four-step cycle, will buy you.

Let's pause on the cycle. Incorporate, image, cleave, repeat. Four words. Burn them in. Every Illumina paper, every clinical sequencing test, every genome assembly, every variant call, every RNA-seq experiment ever published — somewhere underneath, there is a flow cell doing those four words, billions of times in parallel, until the read length is reached. Now let's add one more layer of cleverness — paired-end reads — and then we'll talk about how this all adds up in terms of what the data looks like.

## SEGMENT 7 — Paired-end reads and insert size: what they enable

So far I've described the sequencing cycle as if it just runs one direction along the fragment, from the adapter inward, producing a single read of some length — call it 150 base pairs. That's called a **single-end read**. It's perfectly fine for some applications. But almost every serious Illumina sequencing project today does something more clever: after finishing the first read in one direction, the instrument actually goes back and reads the _same fragment_ from the _other end_, producing a second read. We call this **paired-end sequencing**, and the two reads from the same fragment are called the "mate pair" or just "the pair."

Here's the mechanics. After completing the first 150-cycle read from the forward adapter, the instrument performs another short series of operations on the cluster. It does what's called a **read 2 turnaround**: it resynthesizes the cluster from the other primer, doing a kind of mini-bridge amplification step to regenerate the strand starting from the opposite anchor, then attaches a new sequencing primer that's complementary to the _other_ adapter. Now, when the next round of incorporate-image-cleave-repeat starts, the polymerase begins at the other end of the original fragment and reads back toward the middle. Another 150 cycles, another 150 base pairs of sequence, but this time from the opposite end of the same fragment that produced the first read.

So at the end, every cluster gives you not one read but two — a forward read of 150 bp and a reverse read of 150 bp — both derived from the same original fragment. We call them Read 1 and Read 2. They are linked. We know they came from the same fragment because they came from the same cluster on the flow cell, and the instrument keeps track of that linkage.

\[Pause.\]

Why do this? Why pay the time cost of running essentially two sequencing runs on the same flow cell? Three reasons, and they're worth understanding because they shape almost everything about how Illumina data is used downstream.

**Reason one: insert size as a constraint for mapping.** If your library was prepared so that the original fragments were about 400 base pairs long, and you read 150 bp from each end, then you know — for every read pair — that the two reads should map back to the genome roughly 400 base pairs apart, with the forward read on one strand and the reverse read on the other strand. That distance and orientation constraint is enormously useful for the bioinformatics step. When you're aligning short reads back to a reference genome and you hit ambiguous regions — places where the reference has repeated sequences, places where the read could plausibly map to several locations — the paired-end constraint disambiguates. Among the possible mapping locations for Read 1, the right one is almost always the one where Read 2 also maps to a location about 400 bp away with the correct orientation. The pair-mate-distance becomes a check that resolves ambiguity. Without paired-end reads, you'd lose a substantial fraction of your mapped data to repeat-region ambiguity.

**Reason two: detecting structural variants.** Sometimes when you map a paired-end library, you find a read pair where Read 1 and Read 2 map to the genome, but with the wrong distance between them — way too far apart, or way too close, or on completely different chromosomes. Those discordant pairs are biological signal. They tell you that the original DNA molecule had a structural rearrangement compared to the reference — a deletion (which makes the apparent insert size larger than expected), an insertion (which makes it smaller), an inversion, or a translocation between chromosomes. The whole field of structural variant detection in cancer genomics, in rare disease, in population studies, relies on this signal. A single discordant read pair is just noise. A cluster of dozens of discordant pairs all suggesting the same rearrangement is a structural variant call. Paired-end sequencing is what makes this kind of analysis possible.

**Reason three: better quality at the fragment ends.** Illumina sequencing tends to be slightly less accurate at the ends of reads — particularly the 3-prime end, the part read last. By reading from both ends, you get the high-quality early bases of Read 1 at one end of the fragment, and the high-quality early bases of Read 2 at the other end. The middle of the fragment is more reliably called. For variant calling in particular, this matters: a paired-end run with 150-bp reads from a 400-bp fragment gives you very high-confidence reads at both ends, with overlap or near-overlap in the middle.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy I want you to hold. **Paired-end reads are like reading both ends of a long sentence. You don't see the middle, but knowing the beginning and end gives you a strong constraint on what the sentence is and how long it is.** If a sentence on the original page started with "The quick brown fox" and ended with "over the lazy dog," and you know it was a sentence of roughly that span, then you can place it in the original book even if you can't read the middle. Single-end reads give you the beginning of every sentence. Paired-end reads give you the beginning _and_ the end, with the distance between them as a verification.

In modern practice, almost all Illumina sequencing is done paired-end. Notation: a run described as "2 by 150" means paired-end with 150 bp from each end — so 300 bp of sequence per fragment, with a gap of unknown sequence in the middle if your insert is longer than 300 bp. A run described as "2 by 75" is paired-end with 75 bp reads from each end. Single-end runs are still done for some applications — small RNA sequencing, certain types of expression profiling, very low-depth survey sequencing — but for genomics, exome sequencing, structural variant detection, anything where you care about precision and completeness, paired-end is the default.

One more practical detail. The choice of insert size matters and depends on what you want to do. For variant calling and de novo assembly, you usually want inserts in the 350-to-500 base pair range — long enough to bridge most short repeats but short enough that the fragments stay intact through library prep and amplification. For applications like ChIP-seq, where the input DNA is already fragmented by the experimental protocol, the insert size is determined by the biology and is typically shorter, maybe 200 base pairs. For special applications like mate-pair libraries — which are an older technique for spanning long repeats — you can construct libraries with much larger effective insert sizes, but we won't get into that today. The headline is: paired-end is standard, insert size is something you choose during library prep, and the pair-mate-distance is one of the most useful pieces of information in your data.

## SEGMENT 8 — Read length, throughput, error modes: Illumina's strengths and limits

Let me now try to give you an honest, calibrated picture of what Illumina sequencing actually produces — what's good about it, what's not, and where the limits are. Because there's a lot of marketing around sequencing platforms, and it's easy to come away with either an inflated or a deflated sense of what these machines do.

**Read length.** Illumina's defining strength has always been throughput. Its defining limitation has always been read length. The longest reads Illumina produces in routine use today are 300 base pairs per end — so 600 bp total per paired-end fragment, with a gap in the middle if the insert is longer. Compare that to Sanger sequencing, which produces single reads of about 800 to 1000 bp. Compare it to long-read platforms like Pacific Biosciences and Oxford Nanopore, which we'll meet in Segment 16 — those routinely produce reads of 10,000, 30,000, sometimes over a million base pairs. Illumina's reads are short. There's no getting around it. The chemistry doesn't scale gracefully past a few hundred bases per cycle because errors compound, and a thing called dephasing — which we'll cover in Segment 14 — gradually degrades the signal as the cycles progress.

Why is short-read length a problem? Mainly for two reasons. First, **repeat regions** — places in the genome where the same short sequence is repeated dozens or hundreds of times in a row, like the centromeres of chromosomes, or the tandem repeats associated with diseases like Huntington's. If your read is shorter than the repeat, you can't tell where in the repeat your read came from, and you can't tell how long the repeat is. Illumina reads simply cannot resolve a repeat region longer than the read length. Second, **de novo assembly** — when you don't have a reference genome and you're trying to piece together the sequence from scratch. Short reads make assembly hard, because every repeat in the genome becomes an ambiguous junction in the assembly graph. The original human genome was assembled from much longer Sanger reads partly for this reason. Modern de novo assembly of a new organism's genome usually combines Illumina short reads (for accuracy) with long-read data from PacBio or Nanopore (for spanning repeats). Long-read platforms exist precisely because of these failure modes of short reads.

\[Pause.\]

**Throughput.** Here's where Illumina is unbeatable. A NovaSeq X at full configuration produces about 16 terabases of data per run, in roughly a day to a day and a half. That's about five thousand human genomes' worth of data per machine per run. A single NovaSeq X facility can sequence twenty thousand human genomes a year. That throughput — the sheer number of base pairs per dollar, per day, per machine — is what makes population-scale genomics possible. Projects like the UK Biobank, which is sequencing half a million genomes; the All of Us Research Program in the United States, which is sequencing a million; large clinical implementations like national newborn screening programs — none of these would be financially possible without Illumina-scale throughput.

For smaller-scale work, Illumina also makes smaller instruments. A MiSeq, the workhorse for small labs, produces a few gigabases per run — enough for a few small bacterial genomes, or a focused panel, or a low-throughput RNA-seq experiment. A NextSeq sits in the middle — a few hundred gigabases per run, enough for a few dozen exomes or a hundred-sample transcriptome study. There's an instrument scaled to almost every project size. We'll cover the instrument family in Segment 9.

\[Pause.\]

**Error modes.** Illumina sequencing has an overall error rate, in the well-behaved middle of a read, of about one error per thousand bases — what we call a Q30 quality. (Quality scores get a whole segment, Segment 12.) This is excellent. Other technologies — Oxford Nanopore in particular — have much higher raw error rates, in the range of one error per fifty bases or worse, though they've improved enormously in recent years. Illumina's per-base accuracy is one of the major reasons it dominates clinical sequencing and variant calling.

But — and this matters — the errors Illumina makes are not random. They have specific patterns. The dominant error mode is **substitution errors**, where the wrong base is called in place of the correct one. Insertions and deletions are rare in Illumina data, which is excellent for variant calling. Errors are more common at the 3-prime end of reads — the bases read last — because of the dephasing problem. Errors are more common in homopolymer runs — long stretches of the same base — though much less so than in some competing platforms. Errors are biased toward particular base substitutions; one common pattern is that G-to-T substitutions are over-represented because of damage to the DNA during library prep, particularly oxidative damage. Errors are also concentrated in regions of extreme GC content — very high or very low GC — because these regions don't amplify well during bridge amplification and PCR steps.

Understanding the error profile is critical for variant calling. When you see a single read with a particular variant, it might be a real biological variant, or it might be a Illumina error — and the probability of each depends heavily on which type of variant it is, where in the read it appears, what the surrounding sequence context is. Variant callers like GATK and DeepVariant have been trained on millions of Illumina runs to know these patterns and adjust the calling accordingly. A modern Illumina variant call is, statistically, extremely reliable when the right tools are used.

\[Pause.\]

So the calibrated picture. Illumina: short reads, around 150 to 300 bp, paired-end. Phenomenal throughput, billions to tens of billions of reads per run. Excellent per-base accuracy, around one error per thousand, with specific error patterns concentrated at read ends and in extreme-GC regions. Bad at long repeats, bad at de novo assembly of repeat-rich genomes, bad at directly reading methylation or other modifications without special chemistry. Excellent at variant calling, at expression profiling, at any application where having millions of accurate short reads matters more than having a few really long ones. The platform that built modern genomics is, in every sense, the right tool for the most common jobs and the wrong tool for some specific others. The competition we'll discuss in Segment 16 has carved out niches in exactly those wrong-tool areas.

## SEGMENT 9 — The instrument family: iSeq, MiSeq, NextSeq, NovaSeq, NovaSeq X

Illumina sells a family of instruments at very different scales, and which one you pick depends entirely on what you want to do. They're all built on the same chemistry — the same four-step cycle, the same reversible terminators, the same flow cell paradigm — but they differ enormously in throughput, run time, cost per base, and physical footprint. Let me walk you through the family, smallest to largest.

**The iSeq.** This is the smallest Illumina instrument, introduced around 2018. Roughly the size of a small countertop appliance — think a heavy toaster oven. It uses a single-use cartridge, costs maybe twenty thousand dollars to purchase, and produces about 1.2 gigabases per run — enough for a small bacterial genome, a small targeted panel, or a teaching exercise. Run time is a day or two. The iSeq is designed for very small labs, individual researchers, or specific point-of-use applications where you just need a little bit of sequence on demand. It's the only Illumina instrument that uses a CMOS-chip-based imaging system rather than a separate camera, which is part of how it stays small and cheap.

**The MiSeq.** The MiSeq is the workhorse for small to medium labs. It's been around since 2011 and has gone through several generations. About the size of a small desktop computer. Costs around a hundred thousand dollars. Produces about 15 gigabases per run — enough for a few small genomes, a 16S microbiome study, a small RNA-seq experiment, or a clinical panel of dozens of patients. Run time is about a day. Reads up to 2 by 300 bp — actually the longest reads in the Illumina family, because MiSeq users tend to need reads long enough to span amplicons and small targets. The MiSeq is the instrument you'll see in clinical labs doing inherited disease panels, in microbiology labs doing amplicon sequencing, in academic labs doing focused projects.

**The NextSeq.** Sits in the middle. The current NextSeq 1000 and 2000 produce a few hundred gigabases per run — enough for several dozen exomes, a hundred-sample RNA-seq study, or a few low-coverage genomes. Run time around a day. Cost in the few hundred thousand dollar range to buy, and consumables a fraction of NovaSeq cost per sample. The NextSeq is the right instrument for a core facility serving a small institution, or for a clinical lab doing exomes or panels at modest volume.

**The NovaSeq 6000.** Until recently, this was Illumina's flagship for big genomics. About the size of a large copier. Costs close to a million dollars. Produces about 6 terabases per run — enough for around 50 human genomes at 30x coverage, or thousands of exomes, or hundreds of bulk RNA-seq libraries, or tens of thousands of single-cell RNA-seq libraries. Run time about a day and a half. The NovaSeq 6000 was the workhorse of population-scale sequencing for the late 2010s and early 2020s. If you've seen a study with thousands of samples, it was probably run on NovaSeqs.

**The NovaSeq X.** Launched in September 2022. The current flagship. Bigger, faster, denser. Produces about 16 terabases per run — almost three times what the NovaSeq 6000 does. Reduces cost per genome to roughly two hundred dollars. Uses new chemistry called XLEAP-SBS that's faster per cycle, and new flow cells with patterned wells at higher density. A single NovaSeq X can sequence around twenty thousand human genomes per year. Targeted at large genome centers, national sequencing programs, big pharmaceutical companies, large clinical genomics operations. Refrigerator-sized, costly to purchase — over a million dollars — but per-base cost has never been lower.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy that makes the family make sense. **The Illumina instrument family is like an espresso machine product line. The iSeq is the home pod machine — one cup at a time, slow, but cheap and small enough to sit on a kitchen counter. The MiSeq is the entry-level commercial machine for a small cafe — handles dozens of cups a day. The NextSeq is the mid-tier commercial machine for a busy restaurant. The NovaSeq is the industrial machine for a high-volume hotel banquet operation. The NovaSeq X is the machine that supplies an entire stadium concession. Same fundamental espresso chemistry. Same beans, same hot water, same pressure. But the throughput, the price, the physical footprint scale across roughly four orders of magnitude depending on which one you pick.**

The right instrument depends on your use case. A boutique clinical lab doing rare disease panels on a few hundred patients per year: MiSeq, maybe NextSeq. A core facility supporting an academic institution: NextSeq for routine work, with access to a NovaSeq elsewhere for big projects. A national sequencing center sequencing a population study: NovaSeq X. A field epidemiologist doing pathogen surveillance during an outbreak: iSeq or MiSeq, depending on volume. The platform decision is mostly throughput economics: you want the smallest instrument that meets your monthly volume, because consumable costs scale with run size and a NovaSeq X run for ten samples is enormously more expensive per sample than a MiSeq run for ten samples.

There's also an emerging consideration around turnaround time. For some clinical applications — say, rapidly sequencing a baby in the NICU with an undiagnosed disease — you want the fastest possible run. Smaller instruments can complete a run in a few hours; the big NovaSeq runs take a day or more. So even when your total volume might justify a big machine, the time-to-result consideration can push toward a smaller, faster instrument. Illumina has a specific instrument called the NextSeq 550Dx and a rapid mode on certain platforms targeted at this clinical use case.

The instrument family is also the lens through which Illumina makes most of its money. Most clinical labs and big sequencing facilities buy the instruments outright — that's a one-time big-ticket purchase. But the real revenue model is the **consumables**: the flow cells, the reagents, the cluster generation kits, the sequencing kits. Every run consumes a kit, and the kits are priced to keep margins high. Illumina's business model is, structurally, the razor-and-blades model — sell the razor at a reasonable price, make the money on the blades, forever. The patents on the consumable chemistry are what protect that revenue stream, and we'll come back to the patent question in Segment 18, because some of those patents have expired or are about to expire, and that's reshaping the competitive landscape.

## SEGMENT 10 — Two-channel vs four-channel chemistry: the recent compression trick

Alright, welcome back. I want to start the second half with a piece of clever engineering that you might not encounter unless you read the Illumina chemistry notes carefully: the shift from **four-channel chemistry** to **two-channel chemistry** that happened in the mid-2010s on some of their instruments. It's a small detail in some ways, but it shows how the platform has continued to be refined long after the original Solexa invention, and it illustrates how Illumina has driven cost down.

In the original Illumina chemistry — and still in some current instruments, including the MiSeq, the original HiSeq, and the latest NovaSeq X — each of the four bases is labeled with a distinct fluorescent dye. A gets one color, C another, G another, T another. To determine which base was just incorporated at each cluster, the instrument takes four separate exposures, one for each dye's emission wavelength, and looks at which color is brightest at each cluster position. This is **four-channel chemistry**. It's straightforward, accurate, and works fine. But it has costs. Four exposures means four times as much imaging time per cycle. It means four lasers or filter changes per cycle, which adds mechanical complexity. And it means the optical system has to be sensitive enough to distinguish four different wavelengths cleanly.

In 2014, with the launch of the NextSeq 500, Illumina introduced a clever alternative called **two-channel chemistry**, designed to halve the imaging time and simplify the optics. The idea: use only two dyes — say, green and red — and encode the four bases as combinations of which dyes are present at each cluster. Specifically: T is labeled with green dye only. C is labeled with red dye only. A is labeled with both green and red dyes — so it appears orange when imaged. And G is labeled with no dye at all — so it appears dark.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy. **Imagine you're trying to distinguish four soccer team flags from a distance. Four-channel chemistry is like having a flag of a different solid color for each team — red, blue, green, yellow. You need a camera that can distinguish four colors. Two-channel chemistry is like only having two flag colors — say, red and green — and using combinations to encode four teams: green flag only for one team, red flag only for another, both red and green flag for a third, no flag at all for a fourth.** You only need a camera that can see red and green. You take two exposures instead of four. And from the two-exposure data, you can still uniquely identify which team is at each cluster.

So in two-channel chemistry: green only means T, red only means C, both colors means A, no signal means G. The instrument takes two exposures per cycle — one for green emission, one for red emission — extracts the per-cluster brightness in each, and looks up the combination to determine the base. Faster, simpler, cheaper optics, with the same per-base accuracy as four-channel chemistry when implemented well.

There's a tradeoff: the "G is dark" base call relies on the absence of signal, which means that any unrelated source of dimness — a low-density cluster, a bubble in the flow cell, optical drift — can be mistakenly read as G. Two-channel chemistry has a slight tendency to over-call G, particularly in clusters with low signal. Illumina has tuned the chemistry and the base-calling algorithms over the years to manage this, and in practice the two-channel platforms perform very nearly as well as the four-channel ones.

\[Pause.\]

The NovaSeq 6000 used a four-channel chemistry but with a different optimization to reduce imaging time. The newest NovaSeq X uses yet another variant, called XLEAP-SBS, which streamlines the chemistry further with faster reactions and improved nucleotide design. The general trend: each generation has chipped away at the per-cycle time and the per-base cost by improving the chemistry, the optics, or the base-calling algorithms, often in combination. The platform from 2008 and the platform from 2024 are recognizably the same technology — same four-step cycle, same flow cell paradigm — but every component has been refined dozens of times over.

This is worth pointing out because it explains how Illumina maintained dominance for so long. They didn't just hold the patents on the original Solexa chemistry. They invested constantly in incremental improvement, year after year, layering optimization on optimization, and the cumulative result has been a roughly five hundred-fold reduction in cost per base since 2007. The two-channel trick is one of many such optimizations. The lesson, if you're thinking about how technology platforms maintain market leadership: relentless small improvement, compounded over a decade, can dwarf the impact of any single big invention. The original chemistry was the seed. The compounding optimization is what built the tree.

## SEGMENT 11 — Library prep variations: PCR-free, transposase-based, capture-based

We mentioned library prep variants briefly in Segment 4. Let me come back to them now in more detail, because the choice of library prep dramatically affects what you can and can't see in your data, and there are three or four library prep families that dominate modern Illumina sequencing. Each is optimized for a specific use case.

**PCR-free library prep.** The basic library prep workflow I described in Segment 4 typically includes a PCR amplification step after adapter ligation — to bulk up the amount of DNA before loading the flow cell. PCR works, but it introduces some biases: regions of extreme GC content amplify less efficiently than balanced regions, and the polymerase introduces a small number of errors. For projects where you have plenty of starting DNA and you want the cleanest possible data — particularly germline variant calling, where you want to be sure your variants are real and not PCR artifacts — Illumina sells **PCR-free** kits that skip the amplification step entirely. The fragments come straight off ligation and onto the flow cell. The result is more uniform coverage across the genome, slightly lower error rates in some failure modes, and reduced false positives in variant calling. PCR-free is the gold standard for clinical germline genome sequencing. The tradeoff is you need more starting DNA — a few hundred nanograms versus a few nanograms for PCR-amplified protocols.

**Transposase-based library prep — Nextera.** This is a faster, lower-input alternative to traditional library prep. Instead of fragmenting DNA with sonication and then doing separate end-repair and adapter-ligation steps, Nextera uses a modified bacterial transposase enzyme loaded with synthetic DNA fragments that include adapter sequences. When you add the enzyme to your DNA, it simultaneously **cuts the DNA and inserts the adapter sequences at the cut sites**. The process is called **tagmentation** — tagging plus fragmentation. It's faster, requires less starting material (sometimes as little as one nanogram of DNA), and is more automatable. Nextera kits dominate single-cell sequencing, low-input clinical sequencing, and any project where speed and minimal hands-on time matter. The tradeoff is somewhat less control over fragment size distribution and slightly higher GC bias in some applications.

Here's the analogy. **Tagmentation is like having a magic staple gun that cuts paper and attaches a colored tab at each cut, in one motion. Traditional library prep is like cutting the paper first with scissors, then stapling each piece by hand. Same end result — a stack of tabbed paper pieces — but the magic staple gun is much faster.**

\[Pause.\]

**Capture-based library prep — exome and targeted panels.** Sometimes you don't want to sequence the whole genome. You only want to sequence specific regions — like just the protein-coding parts (the exome, which is about 1.5 percent of the genome), or just a panel of cancer-relevant genes, or just a specific set of inherited disease loci. Sequencing only what you care about means you can run many more samples per flow cell, at much higher coverage per region, at lower total cost.

The way Illumina-compatible capture works: after fragmentation and adapter ligation, you do an additional step called **hybridization capture**. You design or buy a set of biotinylated DNA or RNA probes whose sequences match the regions you care about — for an exome, that's tens of thousands of probes targeting all the protein-coding exons. You mix your library with the probes; the probes hybridize to fragments containing your target regions. Then you pull down the probes using streptavidin-coated magnetic beads, which grab the biotin. Everything that didn't bind a probe — the vast majority of the library, the boring intergenic stuff you don't care about — gets washed away. What's left, eluted off the beads, is an **enriched** library: only the fragments overlapping your target regions. You then PCR-amplify that enriched library and sequence it.

The dominant exome capture kits are made by a few companies: Illumina's own TruSeq Exome, plus competitors like Agilent's SureSelect and Twist Biosciences' exome panels. They all work on the same principle — biotinylated probes plus magnetic bead pulldown — and they all produce enriched libraries that you sequence on the same Illumina instruments. The economics are powerful: an exome sequencing run at 100x coverage gives you very deep, high-confidence calls on protein-coding regions, and costs maybe a quarter of what a whole genome at 30x coverage would cost.

**Amplicon-based library prep.** For very focused targets — say, you want to sequence the spike protein of a SARS-CoV-2 virus, or you want to look at the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes in a breast cancer panel — you can skip hybridization entirely and use PCR to amplify your specific regions of interest directly from sample DNA. Each region gets its own pair of primers. The amplicons are then prepared for Illumina sequencing by adding adapters, sometimes through a second PCR step. Amplicon sequencing is the cheapest and fastest library prep, gives you very deep coverage on a small number of regions, and dominates infectious disease surveillance, microbiome 16S studies, and many clinical panels of just a few genes.

**RNA-seq library prep.** RNA can't be sequenced directly — Illumina sequences DNA. So RNA-seq libraries start with a reverse transcription step that converts RNA to complementary DNA, then proceeds with fragmentation, end repair, adapter ligation, and amplification just like any other library. There are many variants — poly-A selection to pull down only messenger RNAs with poly-A tails, ribosomal RNA depletion to remove the abundant ribosomal RNA, strand-specific protocols to preserve which strand of the gene was transcribed. Each protocol is optimized for a different question: gene expression quantification, splicing analysis, novel transcript discovery, small RNA profiling. The RNA-seq family of protocols is enormous, with new variants published regularly.

\[Pause.\]

The library prep is, in many ways, the most important decision in a sequencing project. The instrument and chemistry are determined by your scale. But the library prep determines what's in your data: which regions get sequenced, with what biases, at what depth, with what artifacts. A botched library prep can ruin a million-dollar sequencing project. A well-chosen library prep can squeeze useful data out of a sample that would otherwise have been hopeless. The library prep is where the sample scientist's expertise still matters most.

## SEGMENT 12 — Quality scores, Phred scaling, and what Q30 really means

When you get your data back from an Illumina run, every single base call comes with a number attached to it — a **quality score** — telling you how confident the base caller is that the call was correct. This score is one of the most important pieces of information in the entire data file, and almost every downstream analysis tool uses it heavily. We need to spend a segment on what these scores mean and how to read them, because they show up everywhere.

The quality score Illumina uses is called the **Phred score**, named after a much older base-calling program called Phred that was originally developed in the late 1990s for Sanger sequencing data. The Phred score, abbreviated Q, is logarithmically related to the probability of error in the base call. The formula is:

Q equals negative ten times the base-ten logarithm of P, where P is the probability that the base call is wrong.

Let me unpack that with concrete numbers, because the log scaling is where most people lose intuition. A Phred score of 10 means a one-in-ten chance of error, or 10 percent error rate. A Phred score of 20 means a one-in-a-hundred chance, or 1 percent error. A Phred score of 30 means one-in-a-thousand, 0.1 percent error. A Phred score of 40 means one-in-ten-thousand. Each ten units of Phred is one decimal order of magnitude in error probability. The higher the Q, the more confident the call. Q30 — one error in a thousand — is the standard threshold for "high-quality" base calls in most pipelines, which is why you'll hear people talking about the percentage of bases above Q30 as a quality metric for a sequencing run. A good Illumina run typically has more than 80 percent of bases above Q30, often 90 percent or more.

\[Pause.\]

Here's the analogy. **A Phred quality score is like a confidence rating on a weather forecast. Q10 means "I'm 90 percent sure it'll be sunny" — there's a noticeable chance you'll get rained on. Q20 means "99 percent sure" — basically reliable but not guaranteed. Q30 means "99.9 percent sure" — you'd plan a wedding around it. Q40 means "99.99 percent sure" — you can essentially treat it as certain for any practical purpose.** The log scaling matches how we intuitively reason about probabilities of rare events: each extra decimal of confidence costs about the same amount of effort to achieve.

How does the instrument actually assign quality scores? In the early days, Illumina used hand-tuned base-calling algorithms that estimated quality from the intensity, sharpness, and color purity of each cluster signal. The current generation uses machine-learning models — neural networks trained on huge datasets of real reads with known correct sequences — that look at the raw signals in the context of surrounding cycles and predict the probability of error for each base. These ML-based base callers have substantially improved both accuracy and the calibration of quality scores over the last several years. A modern Illumina Q30 call really is correct about 999 times out of 1000, as a well-calibrated quality score should be.

\[Pause.\]

Quality scores are stored in the data files using a notation called **ASCII offset encoding**. Each quality score is converted to a printable character — Q30 becomes the character `?`, Q40 becomes `I`, and so on — by adding 33 to the score and looking up the corresponding ASCII character. This is what produces those weird-looking strings of seemingly random punctuation in FASTQ files, the standard Illumina output format. Every base in the FASTQ has its corresponding quality character right below it. If you're a bioinformatician you stop noticing the encoding after a while; if you're not, those FASTQ files look like alphabet soup, but every character is a calibrated probability.

What do you do with quality scores? Several things. **Filtering**: variant callers and aligners typically apply a quality cutoff, ignoring bases below some threshold like Q20. **Recalibration**: for clinical variant calling, the GATK pipeline includes a step called base quality score recalibration, which adjusts the instrument's reported quality scores against known true-call sets to correct for systematic biases. **Variant call confidence**: when calling a variant, the underlying base qualities contribute to the variant's confidence score, so a variant supported by many high-quality reads scores higher than one supported by a few low-quality reads. **Trimming**: most analyses include a step that trims the low-quality tails off the ends of reads, because the last few bases of an Illumina read are often noticeably worse than the middle — that dephasing problem again. The standard tool, called Trimmomatic or fastp, uses a sliding-window quality calculation to clip off the bad ends while keeping the good middle.

The point of quality scores is that they let you reason quantitatively about confidence. Without them, a base call is just a letter; with them, a base call is a calibrated probability distribution, and downstream tools can propagate that uncertainty through the entire analysis. The single most useful piece of metadata in sequencing data is the quality score, and getting it right is one of the reasons Illumina has been so dominant: their quality scores are reliable, well-calibrated, and consistent across instruments and runs. Other platforms have struggled with quality calibration; Illumina, in the modern era, has it nailed.

## SEGMENT 13 — The $1000 genome, the $200 genome: what the cost trajectory tells us

Let me come back to the cost story, because it's worth dwelling on. The dollar-per-genome trajectory of Illumina-driven sequencing is, by any reasonable measure, the most dramatic price reduction in the history of biotechnology, and it's worth understanding what drove it and what it means.

In 2001, when the first draft of the human genome was published, the total cost of that project — when you include the international collaboration, the parallel private effort by Celera, the years of labor, the infrastructure — was estimated at around three billion dollars for that single genome. By 2007, when next-generation sequencing was just starting to take hold, the per-genome cost had dropped to maybe ten million dollars. By 2010, with the first generation of Illumina HiSeq instruments in widespread use, it had dropped to about a hundred thousand dollars. By 2014, with the HiSeq X Ten, Illumina officially announced the **$1000 genome** — for the first time, the cost of sequencing a human genome at the standard 30x coverage was a thousand dollars in reagents, on the assumption that you ran the instrument at full utilization on a high-throughput project. By 2022, with the launch of the NovaSeq X, that cost had dropped further to about two hundred dollars per genome.

\[Pause.\]

Two hundred dollars. Less than the cost of a typical clinical blood panel. Less than a fancy dinner for two. Cheaper than a lot of consumer electronics. And what you get for two hundred dollars is the complete, three-billion-base-pair sequence of one human being. That fact, by itself, is one of the most extraordinary things in modern biology.

Here's the analogy that makes the cost trajectory feel real. **In 2003, sequencing a human genome cost about as much as building a hundred-million-dollar pharmaceutical R&D program. In 2014, it cost about as much as a high-end laptop. In 2024, it costs about as much as a nice meal out. That's the trajectory.** Imagine if any other technology had dropped fifteen million-fold in two decades — if airline tickets had gone from three thousand dollars to fraction of a penny — and you can get a sense of how disruptive this has been.

What drove it? Several things, in roughly equal measure. First, Moore's-law-like improvements in the optics: higher-resolution cameras, denser pixel arrays, faster image processing. Second, advances in surface chemistry that allowed denser cluster packing on flow cells — going from a few million clusters per square millimeter in the early days to billions per square millimeter now. Third, improvements in the reversible terminator chemistry itself: faster reactions, more efficient incorporation, better dyes. Fourth, scaling effects on the instruments: bigger flow cells, parallelized imaging, more cycles per run. Fifth, the engineering of the reagents themselves: economies of scale, better synthesis methods, cheaper enzymes. No single factor accounts for the cost reduction; it's the product of many factors compounded over fifteen years of intense engineering.

\[Pause.\]

What does the cheap genome enable? The list is long. **Population-scale genomics**: projects like the UK Biobank, which has sequenced half a million genomes; the All of Us project in the US; gnomAD, the genome aggregation database that has collated over 100,000 exomes and tens of thousands of genomes to map human genetic variation. None of these projects could have been done at 2010 prices. **Clinical genomics at scale**: routine exome sequencing for rare disease diagnosis is now standard of care in many healthcare systems, with thousands of new diagnoses per year that would otherwise have been impossible. **Cancer genomics**: tumor sequencing for treatment selection is now routine, with panels of dozens to hundreds of genes screened for actionable mutations in most major cancer centers. **Pathogen surveillance**: during the COVID-19 pandemic, the global community sequenced millions of SARS-CoV-2 genomes and used that data to track variants in near real time. None of this would have been remotely feasible at pre-NGS prices.

And there's a deeper point. **At a thousand dollars per genome, sequencing was something you considered carefully — a major investment for any specific patient or project. At two hundred dollars per genome, sequencing is becoming something you do reflexively — like ordering a CBC or a metabolic panel, just one more test in the workup.** The mental model shifts from "should we sequence this?" to "sure, sequence it, the data will be useful for something." That shift, more than any specific clinical application, is what cheap sequencing means for the long-term role of genomics in medicine. We're moving from sequencing as a precious resource to sequencing as a commodity.

The current target Illumina has discussed publicly is the hundred-dollar genome, which the NovaSeq X is approaching for high-volume customers. After that, the next milestone is the fifty-dollar genome, and after that, maybe ten dollars. There's no obvious physical limit yet. The chemistry isn't anywhere near saturated. The optics aren't near their theoretical limit. The flow cells could in principle be packed more densely. Whether competition forces the price down faster, or Illumina chooses to harvest margin and let prices stabilize, is one of the more interesting business questions in the field right now.

## SEGMENT 14 — Failure modes: index hopping, low diversity, GC bias, dephasing

No technology is without failure modes, and Illumina's failure modes are well-characterized — which means they're predictable, which means you can design experiments to avoid them. Let me walk you through the main ones, because if you ever run an Illumina experiment that doesn't quite work, you'll find yourself diagnosing one of these.

**Index hopping.** When you pool multiple indexed samples on the same flow cell, you're trusting that the index sequence on each fragment correctly identifies its sample of origin. But in some library prep methods, particularly the ones that rely on PCR amplification of the pooled library, indices can swap between samples — fragments from sample A can end up carrying the index of sample B during the bridge amplification step. This is called index hopping or index switching, and at certain rates and in certain experimental designs it can be a serious problem. For most applications the hopping rate is well under one percent and doesn't matter. For sensitive applications — particularly single-cell sequencing, where you're trying to assign each read to a specific cell, or low-frequency variant detection where a hopping rate of 0.5 percent is enough to obscure real signal — it matters a lot. The standard mitigation is dual indexing, with both indices having to match correctly for a read to be assigned to a sample. Dual indexing reduces the hopping problem by orders of magnitude, because the chance of both indices hopping coordinately is much smaller than the chance of one hopping. Modern Illumina protocols use dual indexing almost universally for this reason.

**Low diversity.** Illumina's base-calling algorithms work best when the four bases are roughly equally distributed across the clusters being imaged at each cycle. In a typical genomic library, that's automatically true — random DNA is roughly 50 percent A and T, 50 percent G and C, mixed across many fragments. But in some applications — particularly amplicon sequencing of a single PCR product, or 16S microbiome sequencing of a specific gene — every cluster on the flow cell has the same sequence at the same position. When the base caller looks at cycle 30, every cluster might be calling G. That's a problem for the imaging optics: with all clusters dark (in two-channel chemistry) or all clusters one color (in four-channel chemistry), it's harder to focus the optics and harder to calibrate per-cluster brightness. The standard mitigation is to add a small amount of unrelated DNA — called a PhiX spike-in, named after the bacteriophage genome that's used as the source — to the library, providing the base-call diversity the algorithm needs. PhiX is a genome that just happens to have nicely balanced composition and has become the universal calibration substrate for Illumina runs. A typical run includes 1 to 30 percent PhiX, depending on how diverse the main library is.

\[Pause.\]

**GC bias.** Some genomic regions are very GC-rich — sometimes 75 percent or more GC — and some are very AT-rich. Both extremes amplify less efficiently than balanced regions during the PCR steps of library prep and the bridge amplification on the flow cell. The result is uneven coverage across the genome: GC-rich regions get fewer reads, AT-rich regions get fewer reads, the well-behaved middle gets more reads than its share. For variant calling, low-coverage regions are blind spots — you can't call a variant if you have no reads. For copy-number analysis, GC bias creates systematic artifacts that can look like deletions and duplications where there are none. The standard mitigations are PCR-free library prep (when you can afford it), specialized polymerases tuned for difficult templates, and bioinformatic correction algorithms that estimate the GC bias from the data and adjust coverage accordingly.

**Dephasing.** This is the most fundamental error mode of Illumina sequencing, and it's worth understanding in detail. Remember that each cluster contains about a thousand identical DNA molecules, and during each cycle the polymerase is supposed to add exactly one new base to each molecule. In practice, this isn't perfect. Some small fraction of the molecules don't incorporate a new base in a given cycle — maybe the polymerase missed, maybe the blocker wasn't fully cleaved from the previous cycle. Some small fraction incorporate two bases — maybe the blocker on the new nucleotide was defective. So after cycle one, the cluster is mostly synchronized — all molecules at position 1 — but a few are at position 0, and a few are at position 2. After cycle two, the distribution spreads further. After cycle 100, the cluster contains molecules at many different positions: most at position 100, some at 99, some at 98, some at 101, some at 102, and so on. This phase drift is called **dephasing**, and it causes the cluster's signal to become a mixture of multiple bases rather than a clean single-base signal. As dephasing grows over the cycles, the signal-to-noise ratio degrades, and quality scores drop. By cycle 300, the signal is noticeably mushier than at cycle 1. This is why Illumina reads can't be made arbitrarily long — past a few hundred cycles, dephasing overwhelms the signal.

Modern instruments and chemistries are designed to minimize dephasing — more efficient incorporation, better blocker chemistry, more complete cleavage — and base-calling algorithms include explicit dephasing models that try to deconvolve the mixed signal back to the dominant base. But the fundamental limit is real. If you ever look at a quality-score-versus-cycle plot from a long Illumina run, you'll see quality starting high at cycle 1 and gradually declining toward the end. That decline is dephasing at work.

**Cluster density problems.** Too few clusters on the flow cell — you waste capacity, your data is lower-volume than expected. Too many clusters — adjacent clusters overlap, the base caller can't distinguish them, you lose data to "overclustering." Getting the cluster density right requires careful library quantification before loading. Underloading and overclustering are the two most common reasons a sequencing run gives less data than expected. Modern instruments are more forgiving than older ones, but cluster density is still something experienced operators pay attention to.

\[Pause.\]

These are the main failure modes. There are others — adapter dimers, where two adapter pieces ligate to each other without an insert in between and end up dominating the read population; chimeras, where two fragments accidentally join during library prep; barcode crosstalk with similar index sequences; cross-contamination between adjacent flow cell lanes — but they're relatively rare and well-managed by current protocols. The Illumina platform is robust, but it's not magical. Knowing the failure modes is the difference between troubleshooting a problem in an hour and being baffled for a week.

## SEGMENT 15 — Clinical Illumina: diagnostics, prenatal screening, tumor panels

We've talked about the technology, the chemistry, the cost. Let me spend a segment on what Illumina sequencing actually does in clinical medicine today, because the answer is: a great deal. Illumina sequencing isn't just a research tool. It's a routine clinical assay, used millions of times per year for specific diagnostic purposes. Let me walk you through the main categories.

**Rare disease diagnosis through exome and genome sequencing.** A child is born with a constellation of unusual features — developmental delay, unusual facial features, organ malformations — that don't match any known disease. The clinical workup before NGS would have involved targeted gene testing, often dozens of single-gene tests done sequentially, costing tens of thousands of dollars and taking months to years, with a diagnosis rate of maybe 10 to 20 percent. Today, that child gets whole-exome or whole-genome sequencing as a first-line test. The lab sequences the proband — the patient — plus both parents (a so-called "trio"), looks for variants in the proband that are absent from the parents or that match an inheritance pattern, and filters those candidates against databases of known disease-associated variants. The diagnosis rate jumps to 30 to 50 percent in some categories, and the time to diagnosis drops from years to weeks. For some critically ill newborns in NICUs, rapid whole-genome sequencing turnaround in 24 to 48 hours has become standard at some major children's hospitals.

**Non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT).** This is a startling clinical application. Pregnant women have small amounts of fetal DNA circulating in their bloodstream — cell-free fetal DNA shed from the placenta. A blood draw from the mother can be sequenced, and by looking at the fraction of reads mapping to each chromosome, you can detect fetal aneuploidies — Down syndrome (trisomy 21), Patau syndrome (trisomy 13), Edwards syndrome (trisomy 18) — without any invasive procedure. The technique relies on the statistical power of sequencing millions of fragments and noticing that, in a Down syndrome pregnancy, the chromosome 21 reads are slightly elevated in proportion, reflecting the extra copy in the fetal portion. NIPT has largely replaced the older serum-marker screens, has detection rates above 99 percent for the common trisomies, and now accounts for a substantial fraction of routine prenatal care in many countries. It's one of the largest-volume clinical Illumina applications, with millions of tests run per year worldwide.

\[Pause.\]

**Cancer tumor profiling.** Modern oncology routinely uses Illumina sequencing to profile tumor genetics for treatment selection. The clinical question is: what mutations does this patient's tumor have that might be drug-targetable? A biopsy is taken, DNA and sometimes RNA are extracted, libraries are prepared, and a focused panel of cancer-relevant genes — typically a few hundred — is sequenced at high depth. The result identifies actionable mutations: a BRAF V600E in a melanoma might predict response to BRAF inhibitors; an EGFR mutation in a lung cancer predicts response to EGFR inhibitors; HER2 amplification suggests trastuzumab. The use of these panels has become standard of care for many cancer types in the major treatment centers.

There's also a more sensitive use case called **liquid biopsy** — sequencing the small amount of tumor DNA that circulates in the blood of cancer patients (analogous to cell-free fetal DNA in pregnancy). Liquid biopsies can monitor disease burden over time without repeat tissue biopsies, detect emerging resistance mutations during treatment, and increasingly serve as a screening tool to detect cancers early before they're symptomatic. The technical challenge is that circulating tumor DNA is rare — often less than 0.1 percent of total circulating DNA — so extremely deep sequencing and careful error-suppression methods are required. Companies like Guardant, Natera, and Grail (formed inside Illumina, briefly acquired, then divested) have built businesses around this application.

**Carrier screening and pharmacogenomics.** Many couples planning a pregnancy now undergo carrier screening for recessive genetic diseases — sequencing a panel of disease-associated genes in both parents to assess the risk that any future child would inherit two copies of a disease allele. Pharmacogenomic testing, which sequences genes affecting drug metabolism, is increasingly used to guide drug selection and dosing — particularly for psychiatric medications, anticoagulants, and certain chemotherapies. These are all panel-based Illumina applications running in clinical labs at scale.

**Infectious disease and microbial sequencing.** Hospitals use Illumina sequencing for hospital-acquired infection tracking — sequencing the genomes of isolates from different patients to determine whether an outbreak is one strain spreading or many independent infections. Public health labs use it for foodborne illness investigation — the PulseNet system in the US tracks pathogens like Salmonella and Listeria using whole-genome sequencing of clinical and food isolates. And of course COVID-19 surveillance famously used Illumina sequencing at unprecedented scale, with millions of viral genomes contributing to real-time variant tracking through GISAID and similar databases.

\[Pause.\]

The pattern across these clinical applications: Illumina sequencing has gone from a research tool used by a few academics to a clinical workhorse used in mainstream medicine. The decisions made on the basis of Illumina data — drug selection in cancer, prenatal management, diagnosis of pediatric rare disease — affect millions of patients per year. The chemistry that started in a Cambridge pub now drives clinical decisions in every major hospital. That progression — from invention to widespread routine clinical use — took about twenty-five years, which is reasonably fast for a transformative biomedical technology.

For all this clinical use, regulatory approval matters. Illumina has obtained FDA approval for specific instruments and reagents used as diagnostic devices — the NextSeq 550Dx, for example, is the IVD (in vitro diagnostic) version of the NextSeq, with regulatory clearance for specific clinical assays. The chemistry is the same; the regulatory documentation, the quality systems, the labeling are different. As clinical adoption deepens, the regulatory side of the platform has become an increasingly significant business consideration.

## SEGMENT 16 — The competitive landscape: MGI, Element, Ultima

For most of the 2010s, Illumina was effectively without serious competition in short-read sequencing. The early NGS competitors — 454 Life Sciences (pyrosequencing), Applied Biosystems SOLiD (sequencing by ligation), Helicos (single-molecule sequencing without amplification) — had all either been acquired and discontinued, or had simply failed in the market. Illumina was the platform. That has been changing in the last few years, as patents on the core Solexa chemistry have expired and new entrants have brought competing technologies to market. Let me walk you through the current competitive landscape.

**MGI Tech.** MGI is a subsidiary of BGI — the Beijing Genomics Institute, one of the world's largest sequencing centers. After years of using Illumina instruments, BGI acquired Complete Genomics, a US sequencing company with its own platform, in 2013, and developed it into a competing short-read sequencer that BGI now sells globally as MGI. The MGI platform — called DNBSEQ — uses **DNA nanoballs** instead of bridge amplification. Each starting fragment is amplified by rolling-circle replication into a long concatemer that condenses into a tight ball about 200 nanometers across, and these nanoballs are deposited onto patterned flow cells. The sequencing chemistry is similar to Illumina — fluorescent nucleotides, cyclic incorporation and imaging — though with some chemistry differences. MGI claims slightly lower per-base costs than Illumina and has been gaining share, particularly in Asia and Europe. The platform's data is broadly compatible with Illumina-style analysis pipelines, and many studies have found the data quality is comparable for most applications.

**Element Biosciences.** A US startup founded by former Illumina engineers, Element launched its AVITI platform in 2022. AVITI uses an interesting twist on the SBS chemistry called **avidity sequencing**: instead of fluorescent dyes attached directly to individual nucleotides, the system uses unlabeled nucleotides for the actual chain extension, followed by separate detection with multivalent fluorescent probes that bind specifically to the newly incorporated base. This decouples the incorporation chemistry from the imaging chemistry and apparently enables higher per-base accuracy and lower reagent costs. Element targets the mid-throughput market — somewhat similar to Illumina's NextSeq — and has been growing.

**Ultima Genomics.** Another startup that emerged from stealth in 2022 with a quite different approach. Ultima uses a circular wafer that spins like a hard disk during sequencing, with the chemistry happening at the spinning surface. The sequencing chemistry uses unblocked nucleotides — no reversible terminators — and instead controls incorporation by limiting the local availability of nucleotides. This gives them somewhat longer reads in some applications and very high throughput. Ultima has positioned itself as a high-volume, low-cost-per-base platform, with claims approaching the $100 genome.

\[Pause.\]

What do all these short-read competitors have in common? They share the fundamental design: fragments amplified into clonal clusters or balls on a surface, fluorescent detection after each base, parallel imaging of billions of features simultaneously. The Solexa architecture — clusters, cyclic chemistry, parallel imaging — has become the universal pattern for short-read sequencing. The competition is on details: chemistry efficiency, optical engineering, cost structure, throughput per run, error profile. But the basic shape of the platform is the same. In a sense, Illumina's dominance shaped the entire competitive landscape — the competitors all look like variations on the Illumina theme.

The competitors are real, but Illumina's lead is still substantial. Illumina has the largest installed base of instruments, the deepest set of regulatory approvals for clinical use, the most extensive partnerships with library prep companies and analysis tools, and the largest engineering organization. Competition has driven prices down — the NovaSeq X launch was partly in response to competitive pressure — but Illumina's market share, while no longer at the peak of 90+ percent, is still dominant at roughly 70 to 80 percent of the NGS market.

\[Pause.\]

**Long-read platforms — PacBio and Oxford Nanopore.** These are technically not direct competitors to Illumina, because they solve a different problem: reading much longer fragments at the cost of higher per-base error. Pacific Biosciences (PacBio) uses a real-time single-molecule sequencing-by-synthesis approach with a different chemistry, producing reads typically 10,000 to 30,000 base pairs long with current accuracy near Illumina's after circular consensus correction. Oxford Nanopore uses a completely different paradigm — reading DNA by threading it through a protein nanopore and measuring changes in electrical current — producing reads of up to several million base pairs, though with higher raw error rates than Illumina or PacBio.

Long-read platforms shine where Illumina struggles: spanning long repeats, de novo assembly of complex genomes, characterizing structural variants, detecting DNA modifications like methylation directly without bisulfite conversion. Many modern genome projects use a hybrid approach: Illumina for the bulk of the data (cost-effective, accurate at the base level) plus a smaller amount of long-read data (for the parts that Illumina can't resolve). The two technologies are complementary more than competitive, and the field is converging on multi-platform analyses as standard for high-quality genome work.

Where this whole landscape goes in the next decade is unclear. Long-read costs are dropping fast — Nanopore in particular keeps cutting prices on its small instruments — and at some point the cost-versus-accuracy tradeoff may shift. Whether short-read sequencing continues to dominate as a routine assay, or whether long-read becomes cheap enough and accurate enough to displace it for some applications, is one of the big open questions in the field.

## SEGMENT 17 — Where Illumina struggles: long contiguous reads, low-frequency variants, methylation

Let me close in on Illumina's weaknesses more sharply, because understanding what a platform can't do is as important as understanding what it can. There are several application areas where Illumina is the wrong tool, and either a different platform or extensive engineering work-arounds are required.

**Long contiguous reads.** I've mentioned this already, but it's worth restating clearly. Illumina cannot produce reads longer than a few hundred bases. Any application that requires longer reads — phasing variants over long stretches of a chromosome, resolving large structural variants in cancer, assembling complex repeat regions, characterizing very long mRNA isoforms — has to use long-read platforms instead, or use elaborate work-arounds like linked reads (a now-defunct 10x Genomics technology), Hi-C scaffolding, or optical mapping. There is no Illumina sequencer that gives you a ten-thousand-base read. The chemistry simply doesn't support it. This is the single biggest fundamental limitation of the platform.

**Low-frequency variants.** Illumina has a per-base error rate of about one in a thousand. That's excellent for finding germline variants — variants present in 50 or 100 percent of the cells in a tissue — because the signal is overwhelming. It's a problem for finding low-frequency variants, particularly in cancer (where a subclonal mutation might be present in 1 percent of tumor cells) or in liquid biopsy (where circulating tumor DNA might be 0.1 percent of total). When you're looking for variants at 0.1 percent allele frequency in a sequencing run with 1-in-1000 error rate, the signal and the noise are at the same level. You can't distinguish a real low-frequency variant from a sequencing error.

There are mitigations. **Unique molecular identifiers (UMIs)** — short random sequences added to each fragment during library prep — allow you to tag every original molecule individually, so that you can identify reads that came from the same starting molecule and use consensus calling within that group to suppress errors. With UMIs, the effective error rate can drop to one in a hundred thousand or better, enabling reliable detection of variants at very low frequencies. Most modern liquid biopsy and clonal evolution studies use UMI-based protocols for this reason. But the standard Illumina chemistry without UMIs hits a wall at about 1 percent allele frequency, and that's a real limitation for some clinical applications.

\[Pause.\]

**Methylation and other DNA modifications.** DNA in cells is decorated with various chemical modifications — most importantly, methylation of cytosines, which has major regulatory functions and is heavily studied in development, cancer, and aging research. Illumina sequencing, by default, doesn't read methylation directly. The polymerase that copies the DNA during library prep and the sequencing chemistry both treat methylated and unmethylated bases identically — both look like cytosine. To read methylation with Illumina, you have to chemically convert the DNA first, using **bisulfite treatment** (which deaminates unmethylated cytosines to uracils while leaving methylated cytosines intact, so that the methylation status shows up as a C-versus-T difference in the sequencing data). Bisulfite conversion is harsh chemistry — it fragments DNA, reduces complexity, and adds bias — and it doubles the sequencing depth required for the same coverage because half your "T"s are now actually unmethylated C's. There are newer enzymatic conversion methods that are gentler, but the fundamental issue is that Illumina chemistry doesn't natively read modifications. Long-read platforms like PacBio and Nanopore can read methylation directly during normal sequencing, by detecting the small differences a modification makes in the chemistry or electrical signal. That's a significant Illumina disadvantage for the methylation field.

**De novo assembly of complex genomes.** Building a genome from scratch — without a reference to align to — is much harder with short reads than with long reads. Repeat regions become ambiguous junctions in the assembly graph. Heterozygous regions in diploid organisms cause the assembler to get confused between the two parental alleles. The result is fragmented, incomplete assemblies. The original human genome was assembled from much longer Sanger reads. Modern de novo assemblies of complex genomes — plants, fungi, large animal genomes — almost always require long reads. Illumina-only assemblies are usable for simple bacterial genomes but increasingly inadequate for anything larger.

\[Pause.\]

**Single-cell applications and very low input.** Illumina sequencing in its standard form requires at least nanograms of input DNA. For some single-cell or low-input applications, you have only picograms — a thousandth of that — and you need to amplify the input dramatically before library prep. The amplification step introduces bias and noise, and even with the best protocols, single-cell sequencing has more uneven coverage and more dropouts than bulk sequencing. Specialized library prep methods like Smart-seq for single-cell RNA-seq, or whole-genome amplification kits for single-cell DNA-seq, have made this work but with significant data quality tradeoffs.

**Tandem repeat regions.** Disease-associated repeats like the CAG expansion in Huntington's disease or the CGG expansion in fragile X can be hundreds to thousands of repeat units long. Illumina cannot resolve these — the read length is shorter than the repeat. These remain in the territory of long-read sequencing or targeted amplicon methods.

These limits are real. The Illumina platform is dominant because it's the best general-purpose short-read sequencer, not because it's the best at everything. For applications where the limits matter, you need a different tool. And as long-read platforms get cheaper and more accurate, the fraction of work that gets done on long reads — or in hybrid Illumina-plus-long-read protocols — is going to grow. The future of sequencing is probably multi-platform, with each technology used for what it does best.

## SEGMENT 18 — The next decade: patent cliffs, AI base calling, NovaSeq X and beyond

Let me close with some speculation about where the platform and the field are going in the next ten years. I'll be careful to flag what's confident and what's speculative.

**Patent cliff and competition.** Many of Illumina's core patents on the original Solexa chemistry — reversible terminators, bridge amplification on flow cells, cluster-based imaging — have expired in the last few years or are expiring soon. That's why the competitors I mentioned in Segment 16 have been able to build broadly similar platforms. The patent landscape used to be a moat around Illumina's business; now it's an increasingly leaky moat. Over the next decade, we'll see more competitors, more pricing pressure, and probably continued downward movement in cost per base. Illumina's response has been to invest heavily in next-generation chemistry like XLEAP-SBS that they hope to patent and use as the basis of a new moat. Whether that strategy works or whether the field commoditizes is a major open question.

**AI base calling.** The base calling step — converting the camera images into base sequences with quality scores — has historically been done with hand-tuned algorithms. Over the last few years, both Illumina and outside researchers have shifted to **machine-learning-based base callers**, particularly neural networks trained on huge datasets of real reads with known truth. These ML base callers have meaningfully improved accuracy and quality calibration, particularly for difficult cases like homopolymers, low-density clusters, and long reads where dephasing degrades signal. The trend is toward all base calling becoming neural-network-based. The same trend is even more pronounced in long-read sequencing — Oxford Nanopore's accuracy gains over the last few years have come largely from progressively better neural-network base callers. Expect the AI layer of sequencing to keep improving for years.

\[Pause.\]

**The race toward the $100 genome and below.** NovaSeq X put Illumina at roughly $200 per genome at the very high end of volume. The next milestone is $100 per genome, which Illumina has explicitly discussed as a target. Below that is $50, then $10. There's no obvious chemistry limit yet. The question is mostly about whether competition forces price cuts faster than incremental engineering or whether the field accepts the current cost as adequate. My guess — and this is genuinely speculation — is that we'll see the $100 genome become routine within five years and the $50 genome within ten, driven by some combination of NovaSeq X's successor, MGI's pricing, and pressure from new entrants.

**Population-scale and routine clinical genomics.** As costs drop and clinical pipelines mature, genome sequencing will increasingly become a routine part of medical care. National-scale projects — Genomics England, the All of Us program, Singapore's PRECISE, Estonia's Biobank — have shown that sequencing hundreds of thousands or millions of citizens is feasible. Over the next decade, expect more countries to fold genome sequencing into routine national health screening, particularly for newborns, for prenatal care, and for high-risk populations. The challenge will shift from "can we sequence everyone?" to "what do we do with the data?" — questions of interpretation, privacy, equitable access, and the integration of genomic data into clinical decision-making.

**The integration with other omics.** Illumina sequencing isn't just for genomes. The same instruments produce data for RNA-seq, ChIP-seq, methylation profiling, chromatin accessibility, single-cell multiomics, spatial transcriptomics, and more. As the cost of basic sequencing drops, the bottleneck shifts to the upstream library preps and the downstream analysis. Expect a flowering of new specialized library prep methods that turn the Illumina platform into a generic read-out device for whatever molecular question you want to ask. The instruments are general-purpose; the assays built on top of them are increasingly creative.

\[Pause.\]

**Long-read convergence.** Whether the next decade sees long-read platforms displace Illumina for routine work, or remain specialized complements, is genuinely uncertain. If Oxford Nanopore can drive its accuracy up to consistent Q30 and its cost down toward Illumina's, the case for short reads becomes much weaker. If long reads remain expensive and somewhat noisy, the current division of labor — short reads for variant calling, long reads for assembly and structural variants — will persist. My guess is that hybrid pipelines will dominate for high-value applications, while short reads will remain the workhorse for high-volume routine genomics. But this is the kind of prediction that ages poorly.

**The deeper shift: sequencing as infrastructure.** Maybe the most important long-term consequence of the Illumina cost trajectory is that DNA sequencing has stopped being a "project" and started being **infrastructure**. You don't think about sequencing a sample any more than you think about printing a document or running a database query. It's a routine operation that happens in the background, with predictable cost and turnaround, and the interesting work is at the layers above — what to sequence, what to analyze, what conclusions to draw. That infrastructure status is what the platform has enabled. And the platform itself — the four-step cycle of incorporate, image, cleave, repeat, parallelized across billions of clusters on a glass flow cell — has become so reliable, so cheap, and so widely available that the entire field of modern genomics has grown up around its assumptions.

\[Pause.\]

Let me close with the headline. Twenty-five years ago, Shankar Balasubramanian and David Klenerman sat in a pub in Cambridge and sketched out a way to detect single nucleotides being added to a DNA strand. The idea was a piece of basic-science fluorescence chemistry that nobody outside their lab cared about. Twenty-five years later, the descendants of that idea sequence over fifty thousand human genomes a day worldwide, drive clinical decisions for millions of patients per year, underpin the entire fields of genomics, transcriptomics, and personalized medicine, and have collapsed the cost of reading life's source code by a factor of fifteen million. That trajectory — from pub conversation to global infrastructure — is one of the great stories of modern biology. The chemistry is the seed. The engineering is the tree. The science the tree enables — population genomics, precision oncology, rapid pandemic response, the routine diagnosis of children with genetic disease — is the fruit. And the fruit, for the rest of your careers in biology and medicine, is going to keep getting larger and more abundant and more transformative. Sequencing isn't done changing the world. It's just getting started.

## APPENDIX

**Glossary of key terms**

- **Adapter** — Synthetic DNA sequence ligated to both ends of every fragment during library prep, providing binding sites for the flow cell, primer binding sites for sequencing, and the index sequence.
- **Bridge amplification** — The flow cell process by which a single anchored DNA fragment is amplified into a clonal cluster of about 1000 identical copies through repeated cycles of strand folding, hybridization to a second anchor, and polymerase extension.
- **Cluster** — A patch of about 1000 identical DNA molecules on the flow cell surface, produced by bridge amplification from one starting fragment, that produces a single signal during sequencing.
- **Dephasing** — The gradual loss of synchronization within a cluster as cycles progress, caused by occasional missed or double incorporations, leading to declining quality scores at the ends of long reads.
- **FASTQ** — The standard data file format containing both base calls and per-base quality scores from Illumina runs.
- **Flow cell** — The glass slide, internally channeled and surface-functionalized with anchor oligonucleotides, on which all the sequencing chemistry takes place.
- **Index / barcode** — A short synthetic sequence in the adapter that identifies which sample a read came from, enabling multiplexing of many samples on one flow cell.
- **Indexing / multiplexing** — Pooling many indexed samples for sequencing in a single run, with sample assignment recovered by demultiplexing the index sequences from each read.
- **Library** — The collection of DNA fragments, each with adapters and an index, ready to be loaded onto a flow cell.
- **PhiX** — A bacteriophage genome with balanced base composition used as a spike-in control to maintain base-call diversity in low-complexity libraries.
- **Phred score / Q score** — Log-scaled measure of confidence in a base call. Q30 means one error in a thousand.
- **Paired-end sequencing** — Reading each fragment from both ends, producing two linked reads per cluster, used for mapping and structural variant detection.
- **Reversible terminator** — A modified nucleotide carrying both a fluorescent dye and a 3-prime blocker that prevents the polymerase from adding more than one base per cycle. Both modifications are removed by a single cleavage step.
- **Sequencing by synthesis (SBS)** — The general term for Illumina's approach: read a DNA template by watching a polymerase incorporate fluorescent nucleotides one at a time.
- **Tagmentation** — The Nextera library prep technique using a transposase to simultaneously fragment DNA and insert adapter sequences in one step.
- **UMI (Unique Molecular Identifier)** — Short random sequence added to each fragment during library prep, allowing identification of reads derived from the same starting molecule for error suppression.

**Quick numerical reference**

- Read length: typically 2 by 150 bp paired-end; up to 2 by 300 on MiSeq.
- Cluster signal: ~1000 molecules per cluster after bridge amplification.
- Flow cell cluster count: ~25 million on MiSeq, ~50 billion on NovaSeq X.
- Throughput: ~15 Gb per MiSeq run, ~16 Tb per NovaSeq X run.
- Run time: ~24 hours on MiSeq, ~24-44 hours on NovaSeq X.
- Per-base error rate: roughly 0.1% in well-behaved middle of read (Q30).
- Cost per human genome: ~$3B (2003), ~$1000 (2014), ~$200 (2022).
- Illumina market share of NGS: ~70-80% as of mid-2020s.

**Key dates**

- 1998 — Balasubramanian and Klenerman conceive the reversible terminator approach; Solexa founded in Cambridge.
- 2006 — Solexa launches first commercial sequencer.
- 2007 — Illumina acquires Solexa for ~$650M.
- 2010-2014 — HiSeq dominance era; cost drops 100-fold.
- 2014 — HiSeq X Ten launch; "$1000 genome" announced.
- 2017 — NovaSeq 6000 launch.
- 2018 — iSeq launch (smallest Illumina instrument).
- 2022 — NovaSeq X launch; cost drops toward $200 per genome; competition from MGI, Element, Ultima visible.

**A final note for the lecturer**

The cost trajectory and instrument family will continue to shift. When you reuse this script, check the current price per genome, the current top-of-line instrument, and the current state of the long-read competition before delivering. The core chemistry — the four-step cycle, bridge amplification, reversible terminators — is stable and is unlikely to be displaced soon. But the numbers around it shift every couple of years, and you want to keep your students' picture of the field current.
