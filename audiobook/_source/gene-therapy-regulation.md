# Gene Therapy Regulation: The FDA / EMA Pathway from Preclinical to Approval

**A 2-hour undergraduate lecture on how a gene therapy actually becomes an approved drug — full spoken script**

---

_How to use this script:_ This is written to be read aloud. It runs about 12,000 words. At a typical lecturing pace — roughly 120 words per minute once you include natural pauses, emphasis, and the marked \[Pause.\] beats — that fills close to one hundred minutes, plus a mid-point break, landing comfortably inside a two-hour slot. (Read faster, ~140 wpm, and you'll have generous room for questions or board work.) Timing cues and stage directions appear in \[brackets\] — these are for you, the speaker, and should not be read aloud. ## SEGMENT 1 — Why regulation matters as much as the biology — the gauntlet between bench and bedside

Alright. Let's get started. Today, for the next two hours, we are going to talk about something that almost nobody finds glamorous, that almost nobody writes popular books about, and that almost nobody outside of a very specific professional community ever bothers to learn. But here is the claim I want to make in the first sixty seconds of this lecture, before you have any reason to believe me, and that I will spend the next two hours trying to defend.

**The regulatory pathway for a gene therapy is, in its own way, as important to whether that therapy ever reaches a patient as the underlying science.** A perfect molecular insight that cannot navigate the regulatory gauntlet is, clinically, worth nothing. And a regulatory framework that is too permissive or too restrictive doesn't just slow down a particular drug — it shapes which kinds of diseases get attacked, which patients get treated, and which biotechnology companies survive long enough to bring anything to market at all. The regulators are not, contrary to the way they are sometimes portrayed in the press, a bureaucratic obstacle dropped on top of the real work. They are, in a very real sense, **co-architects of what modern medicine becomes**.

So this lecture is the regulatory tour. We're going to walk, end to end, through how a gene therapy actually becomes an approved drug in the United States and in the European Union. We will start at the conceptual stage — somebody has an idea — and we will end at a registry following patients for fifteen years after they were dosed. Along the way we will meet the agencies — FDA, CBER, OTAT in the US; EMA and CAT in Europe — we will meet the milestones — preclinical, pre-IND, IND, Phase 1, Phase 2, Phase 3, BLA, post-market — and we will meet the gene therapies themselves: Luxturna, Zolgensma, Hemgenix, Casgevy, Lyfgenia. Each of them has a story about how it cleared the gauntlet, and each of those stories teaches you something a little different about how the system actually works.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy that I want you to hold in your head for the entire two hours. **The regulatory pathway is a gauntlet.** Imagine a long hallway with a series of gates in it, each of them locked, each of them opened only when you pass a specific test. You start at one end of the hallway with an idea. You end at the other end of the hallway with an approved, marketable drug. To get from the start to the end, you have to clear every gate in order. Concept gates open into preclinical gates, which open into IND gates, which open into Phase 1 gates, which open into Phase 2 gates, which open into Phase 3 gates, which open into the BLA gate, which finally opens into approval. And after approval there is still a kind of long tail of post-market commitments that you carry for years afterwards.

Here is the number I want you to remember from this opening. **About ninety percent of drugs that enter Phase 1 clinical trials never make it to approval.** Ninety percent. That means for every ten gene therapies that get permission to be tested in a single human being, nine of them, on average, will fail somewhere along the way — fail for safety reasons, fail for efficacy reasons, fail for manufacturing reasons, fail because the company runs out of money, fail because a competitor gets there first, fail because the FDA asks for another trial that the sponsor cannot afford to run. The gauntlet is not a formality. It is a winnowing process, and the winnowing is severe.

\[Pause.\]

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, draw the development pipeline on a napkin and explain in plain English what happens at each stage, from preclinical mouse studies through the BLA filing to post-market surveillance; two, name and briefly characterize the four big accelerated pathways the FDA offers — Fast Track, Breakthrough Therapy, RMAT, and Priority Review — and explain why gene therapies in particular tend to qualify for several of them at once; three, tell me the story of at least one approved AAV gene therapy and at least one CRISPR gene therapy and explain what regulatory choices made each of them possible — then we have succeeded. Everything else, all the detail about what the agencies look at and how the meetings are structured and what the advisory committees do, hangs off those three.

One more thing before we open the box. This is a story that has been moving fast. Most of the major regulatory frameworks for gene therapy that we are going to discuss — the RMAT designation, the CBER reorganization that produced OTAT, the long-term follow-up guidance for AAV vectors, the platform technology designation — these are creations of the last fifteen years. The framework you are about to learn is not the framework that existed when the first gene therapy trials happened in the 1990s, and it almost certainly is not the framework that will exist twenty years from now. We are going to spend most of our time on the current state of things, but I want you to hold the historical contingency in mind. Regulation is a living organism. It evolves with the science it regulates.

## SEGMENT 2 — FDA, CBER, OTAT — the structure of the US gene therapy regulator

Let me orient you geographically, organizationally, within the agency that approves gene therapies in the United States. Because once you understand the structure, you will understand a great deal about why certain decisions get made the way they do.

The **Food and Drug Administration** — the FDA — is a federal agency under the Department of Health and Human Services, headquartered in Silver Spring, Maryland. Its mandate is to regulate, among other things, all drugs, all biologics, all medical devices, and most food sold in the United States. It is one of the oldest consumer protection agencies in the world; its lineage goes back to the late nineteenth century. Today it employs roughly eighteen thousand people across multiple centers, and it reviews — in any given year — thousands of drug and biologic applications.

For our purposes, the relevant subdivision is called the **Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research**, or **CBER** — pronounced "see-ber," not "sea-bear," and you should mostly call it by the acronym because nobody in the field uses the full name. CBER is the part of FDA that handles biologics — meaning drugs that are derived from living systems rather than synthesized by traditional chemistry. Vaccines, blood products, allergenic extracts, cell therapies, gene therapies, and tissue products all fall under CBER. The other major drug center at FDA, by contrast, is **CDER** — the Center for Drug Evaluation and Research — which handles small-molecule drugs and most monoclonal antibodies. **Gene therapy is regulated as a biologic. That means CBER, not CDER, is the agency you deal with.**

\[Pause.\]

Within CBER, gene therapies have historically been reviewed by an office originally called the **Office of Tissues and Advanced Therapies**, or **OTAT** — pronounced "oh-tat" — which until recently was the specific office handling gene and cell therapy applications. In 2023, CBER reorganized OTAT into a larger structure called the **Office of Therapeutic Products**, or **OTP**, reflecting the rapid growth in cell and gene therapy submissions and the need to scale up review capacity. The functions are essentially continuous — the reviewers and the framework moved more or less wholesale — but if you read older literature you will see "OTAT" and in newer literature you will see "OTP." For this lecture I will use both interchangeably, because the work being done is the same. The reorganization was a recognition that this field is no longer a niche; it is one of the busiest areas of biologics review in the agency.

OTAT or OTP, sitting inside CBER, sitting inside FDA — that is the chain of command you need to know. When you submit a gene therapy application, your file lands on the desk of a reviewer who sits in this office. That reviewer is, very often, a person with a PhD in molecular biology or biochemistry, perhaps a clinical degree on top of it, who has spent years developing expertise specifically in how viral vectors and editing systems behave in patients. These are not generalists. The depth of expertise inside CBER on, say, AAV serotype selection or the manufacturing of lentiviral vectors is, in many cases, comparable to or better than the expertise inside any individual biotech company. **The agency knows the field. They are not bureaucrats in a vacuum. They are a peer-review body with regulatory authority.**

\[Pause.\]

Now let me say a word about the European counterpart, because I will keep coming back to it. The **European Medicines Agency**, or **EMA**, is the body that approves drugs across the European Union. It is headquartered in Amsterdam, having moved there from London after Brexit. Within the EMA, the committee that specifically handles gene therapy and other "advanced therapy medicinal products" — the EU's umbrella term for cell therapy, gene therapy, and tissue-engineered products — is called the **Committee for Advanced Therapies**, or **CAT** — yes, the acronym is CAT, and yes, people in the field make jokes about it. CAT does the scientific review and writes the recommendation; the recommendation then goes to a higher-level committee called the **CHMP**, the Committee for Medicinal Products for Human Use, which formally adopts the opinion that becomes the basis for the European Commission's marketing authorization.

So in the US, the answer to "who approves a gene therapy" is FDA, via CBER, via OTAT/OTP. In the EU, the answer is the European Commission, on the advice of CHMP, on the recommendation of CAT, inside EMA. Different acronyms, different structures, similar functions. We'll see throughout this lecture that the two agencies usually arrive at similar decisions, but they get there on slightly different schedules and with slightly different emphases.

## SEGMENT 3 — The development pipeline — concept to approval, end to end

Let me draw the pipeline for you. I want you to be able to sketch this on a napkin by the end of this segment.

You start, at the leftmost end of the pipeline, with a **concept**. Somebody — usually an academic researcher, sometimes a company scientist — has the idea that a particular disease could be treated by delivering a particular gene, or by editing a particular target, in a particular tissue. The idea might come from a study showing that loss of a gene causes a disease, or that overexpression of a gene rescues a phenotype in a mouse, or that a particular pathway is dysregulated in a tumor. The concept stage is, in regulatory terms, completely unregulated. Anybody can have an idea. The agency does not get involved until you start to take real steps toward making the idea into a drug.

The next box on the pipeline is **preclinical development**. This is the stage at which you start doing the actual experiments — first in cell culture, then in animal models — that would justify dosing the therapy into a human being. We will spend an entire segment on what the preclinical package for a gene therapy looks like, because it has specific requirements that differ from those for small molecules. For now, hold in your head that preclinical is the bench-and-animal stage, it lasts typically two to five years for a gene therapy, and it is where the majority of gene therapy programs die without ever reaching a human.

If preclinical work is going well and you are starting to imagine a clinical trial, you enter the **pre-IND** stage. This is the period during which you start having formal conversations with FDA about your program — meetings called Type B or Type C meetings, which we will discuss in detail in Segment 5. The pre-IND phase is, in a sense, the "are we on the right path" check. You go to the agency with your preclinical data, your manufacturing plan, your proposed trial design, and you ask them: does this look like something we could file an IND on? The agency gives feedback. You go back, do more work, address the feedback, and eventually you decide you are ready to file.

\[Pause.\]

The next box is the **IND** — the **Investigational New Drug** application. This is the document — actually a set of documents, often running to thousands of pages — that you submit to FDA to ask for permission to begin human trials. The IND is, in a sense, the **first regulatory checkpoint that has real teeth**. You submit it, and you have to wait thirty days. If the agency does not put you on a clinical hold during that thirty-day window, you can begin dosing patients. If they do put you on hold, you cannot start until you have addressed their concerns and they have lifted the hold. We will spend a full segment on the IND because it is the most important single document in the early life of a drug.

If the IND clears, you enter the **clinical phase**, which traditionally has three sub-stages: **Phase 1**, **Phase 2**, and **Phase 3**. Phase 1 is the first time the drug is given to humans, usually a small number — twenty to a hundred — and the primary question is safety. Phase 2 expands the population, typically several hundred patients, and starts to ask whether the drug actually works. Phase 3 is the big pivotal trial — often hundreds to thousands of patients — designed to definitively demonstrate efficacy and safety in a population that approximates the eventual market. For gene therapy, as we will see, the boundaries between these phases are often blurred — Phase 1 and Phase 2 are frequently combined into a single trial, because the patient population is so small and the dose-finding work bleeds naturally into efficacy assessment.

After the trials are complete, you assemble what is called the **BLA** — the **Biologics License Application** — and you submit it to FDA. The BLA is a colossal document, typically tens of thousands of pages, that compiles everything you have ever learned about the drug: the manufacturing process, the preclinical data, every clinical trial result, every adverse event, every analytical method. The agency reviews the BLA, sometimes consults with an advisory committee, and either approves the drug, requests more information, or rejects it. **Approval is not the end.** There is a long tail of **post-market commitments**: continued safety surveillance, long-term follow-up registries, sometimes additional confirmatory trials, manufacturing monitoring. For gene therapy in particular, the post-market commitments can extend for fifteen years after a patient is dosed, which is a longer follow-up obligation than for any small-molecule drug.

\[Pause.\]

So that is the whole pipeline, in one paragraph. **Concept, preclinical, pre-IND, IND, Phase 1, Phase 2, Phase 3, BLA, approval, post-market.** Ten stages, each gating the next, each with its own expectations and timelines and ways of failing. The whole journey, from the moment somebody first sketches a vector on a napkin to the moment a pharmacist hands a vial to a clinician, takes ten to fifteen years for a typical successful program. It costs, depending on how you count, hundreds of millions to over a billion dollars. And as I said in Segment 1, about nine out of ten programs that enter Phase 1 never get all the way to the end.

In the next segment, we are going to zoom in on the preclinical box and see what specifically a gene therapy has to demonstrate before the agency will let you dose a human being. After that, in Segment 5, we go to the pre-IND meeting, and then in Segment 6 we file the IND itself. By the time we hit the mid-point break after Segment 9, you will have walked the first half of the pipeline in detail and we will be ready in the second hour to look at the specific approved gene therapies that have made it all the way through.

## SEGMENT 4 — The preclinical package — what you have to show before you dose a human

Let me zoom into the preclinical box. Because the preclinical package for a gene therapy is not the same as the preclinical package for a small-molecule drug, and the differences are instructive.

When you are developing a small-molecule drug — say, a new antibiotic or a new blood pressure medication — your preclinical package focuses on a fairly standard set of questions. Does the drug bind to the target you intended? Is it absorbed when you give it orally or by injection? How is it metabolized? How is it cleared? Is it toxic in animals at doses many times higher than the expected human dose? Does it cause cancer in long-term rodent studies? Does it cross the placenta and affect a developing fetus? These questions have been refined over fifty years of drug development, and the agency has well-established expectations for what kinds of data answer them.

For a gene therapy, several of those questions are still relevant — toxicity, for instance, you still have to study in animals — but the package has additional, gene-therapy-specific elements that don't exist for small molecules. **Let me walk you through five of the big ones.**

\[Pause.\]

First, **proof of mechanism**. You have to show that your gene therapy actually does what you claim it does, mechanistically, in a relevant biological system. If you are delivering a healthy copy of a gene to treat an enzyme deficiency, you have to show that your vector actually produces the enzyme inside cells. If you are using CRISPR to disrupt a target gene, you have to show that the editing actually happens at the target site, at a quantifiable efficiency. This is more demanding than for a small-molecule drug because the mechanism of a gene therapy is not abstract chemistry — it is a specific molecular event in a specific cell, and the agency wants you to demonstrate that event with real experimental data.

Second, **biodistribution**. When you inject a vector into a patient, where does it actually go? Does it stay in the target tissue you intended, or does it leak out into other organs? Does it cross the blood-brain barrier? Does it accumulate in gonads, where it could in principle affect future generations? Biodistribution studies are usually done in mice or non-human primates by injecting a vector, then sacrificing animals at multiple time points and quantifying vector DNA in every major organ using PCR. The agency wants to see a map: this much vector in liver, this much in spleen, this much in heart, this much in brain, this much in testes. The distribution map shapes the safety conversation. If most of your vector ends up in a tissue you weren't targeting, you need to explain why that is acceptable, or you need to re-engineer your delivery.

Third, **integration analysis**. Some gene therapies — particularly lentiviral vectors — integrate into the patient's chromosomes. Integration is necessary for some applications, but it carries the risk of disrupting an important host gene or activating an oncogene, which is the mechanism that caused leukemias in some of the early gamma-retroviral gene therapy trials in the early 2000s. For any integrating vector, the agency wants you to do extensive analysis of where in the genome the vector integrates. Is integration random? Is it biased toward certain sites? Are any of the integrations in or near known oncogenes? For non-integrating vectors like AAV, the picture is different — AAV mostly persists as a non-integrated episome — but you still have to characterize the low-frequency integration events that do occur and demonstrate that they are not biased toward dangerous sites.

\[Pause.\]

Fourth, **immunogenicity**. The patient's immune system is going to react to whatever you put into their body, and for a gene therapy, the immune reaction can be a particularly serious problem. There are antibodies against the viral capsid that may exist before treatment — pre-existing immunity, which excludes a significant fraction of the population from receiving certain AAV serotypes. There are T-cell responses against the transduced cells, which can in principle eliminate the very cells you are trying to treat. There are complement activations against high doses of capsid that have caused some of the most serious adverse events in the AAV field. You have to characterize all of this in your preclinical models — what kinds of immune responses does your vector provoke, at what doses, and what are the consequences?

Fifth, **GLP toxicology in two species**. This one is the big one. GLP stands for **Good Laboratory Practice** — a set of rigorous standards for how animal studies are designed, conducted, and documented. The FDA generally expects pivotal toxicology studies for a new drug to be done in two species, often a rodent and a non-rodent, and to be conducted under GLP conditions, meaning every detail is documented in a way that could be audited years later. The studies involve dosing animals at the expected human dose, at higher doses to define toxicity, and following them for an extended period — often months — while monitoring every conceivable clinical, biochemical, and pathological endpoint. At the end, every animal is necropsied, every organ is examined, every adverse finding is catalogued. This is the data set that lets the agency say, with reasonable confidence, that the drug is unlikely to cause unexpected toxicity in a human at the proposed starting dose. **GLP tox in two species is the safety dossier. It is the prerequisite to dosing humans.** Without it, you don't get the IND.

\[Pause.\]

Now, one important nuance for gene therapy. The traditional two-species expectation comes from the small-molecule world, where the same compound generally has comparable pharmacology across species. For gene therapy, that is often not true. An AAV vector designed to deliver a human transgene may not work properly in a mouse, because mouse tissues don't express the human receptors that the human capsid uses for cell entry, or because the human promoter doesn't fire well in mouse cells. So the agency has flexibility here. You can sometimes use a single most-relevant species — often non-human primate — if you can justify that a second species would not add meaningful safety information. The agency has been clear that the goal is the underlying safety question, not a rigid checklist. But you should expect to do extensive work in at least one species and often two, under GLP conditions, before you are allowed to file an IND.

So that is the preclinical package for a gene therapy in summary. **Proof of mechanism. Biodistribution. Integration analysis. Immunogenicity. GLP toxicology in two species.** That is the safety dossier you need to assemble before you go to the agency and ask for permission to give your drug to a human being. The work takes two to five years. It costs tens of millions of dollars. And it is the gate that more gene therapy programs fail at than any other single step of the pipeline.

## SEGMENT 5 — The pre-IND meeting — Type B, Type C, and the "are we on the right path" check

So you have done your preclinical work. You have a vector. You have biodistribution and tox data. You have, in your head and in a thousand-page binder, a plan for how you would dose the first human. Before you actually file the IND, you have a choice: you can file cold, dropping a giant binder on the agency and hoping they don't put you on clinical hold; or you can request a meeting and find out, in advance, what the agency thinks of your plan. Almost every serious sponsor takes the meeting. Filing cold is a way to get put on clinical hold for thirty days while the agency works through concerns that a conversation could have resolved in an hour.

These pre-IND conversations have a structured format. FDA divides sponsor meetings into a small number of types, named with letters. The two types you care about for the pre-IND phase are **Type B** and **Type C**.

A **Type B meeting** is what you request when you want to discuss specific, well-defined milestones in the development of your drug. The pre-IND meeting itself is a Type B meeting. So is an end-of-Phase-2 meeting, where you discuss whether your Phase 2 data is sufficient to design the Phase 3 trial. So is a pre-BLA meeting, where you discuss the structure of your BLA submission. Type B meetings have a regulatory expectation of a sixty-day turnaround from request to meeting, plus written responses from the agency. They are the workhorse format for big formal program-level discussions.

A **Type C meeting** is broader — used for any other discussion about a drug under development that doesn't fit the Type B categories. Sponsors use Type C meetings for things like discussing a novel manufacturing change, or a new biomarker, or a specific scientific question that doesn't tie to a particular milestone. Type C meetings have a longer expected turnaround, seventy-five days, but they are flexible enough to accommodate almost any question.

\[Pause.\]

The pre-IND meeting itself — almost always a Type B — is, I think, the single most important conversation in the early life of a gene therapy program. You go to the agency with a briefing document that summarizes your preclinical data, your proposed clinical protocol, your manufacturing plan, and the specific questions you want them to address. The questions are usually some version of: is our preclinical package adequate? Is our proposed starting dose reasonable? Is our trial design acceptable? Are there additional studies you would want to see before we file?

The agency, on its side, has reviewed your briefing document in advance. The meeting itself is short — typically an hour — and is structured around your specific questions. The agency gives you their preliminary answers, often hedged with caveats, and then sends you written responses formalizing what was said. Those written responses become the regulatory record of the meeting. You can refer back to them when you file the IND. If you did what the agency told you to do at the pre-IND meeting, and the IND reviewer disagrees with the pre-IND advice, you have a basis to push back.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy. **The pre-IND meeting is the "are we on the right path" check.** Imagine you are about to embark on a long hike through unfamiliar terrain. You have a map. You have a route planned. Before you set off, you stop at the ranger station and you ask the ranger: does this look like a reasonable route? Are there any hazards along this trail I should know about? Is my equipment adequate for the conditions? The ranger has seen many hikers attempt many routes. They know which trails have washed out, which rivers are running high, which bears are active this time of year. Their advice is not binding — they cannot stop you from hiking the route you planned — but if you ignore it, you do so at your own risk. And if you take their advice and something still goes wrong, you have at least the cover of having checked in advance.

The pre-IND meeting is exactly that, in regulatory form. The agency is the ranger. They have seen hundreds of programs come through. They know what kinds of preclinical packages tend to clear and what kinds tend to get held up. They know which trial designs have caused problems in past gene therapy trials. Their advice is not a guarantee — they reserve the right to change their mind once they see the full IND — but it is enormously valuable, and the sponsors that engage with it tend to have smoother regulatory paths than the sponsors that try to navigate alone.

One last note. The pre-IND meeting is also, increasingly for gene therapy, the venue where you discuss whether your program might qualify for one of the accelerated pathways — Fast Track, Breakthrough, RMAT — that we will talk about in Segment 7. The agency can give you a preliminary view of whether your program looks like a fit for one of those designations. The designations themselves are formal regulatory grants that come later, but the conversation about fit often starts at the pre-IND meeting. Many gene therapy sponsors leave their pre-IND meeting with a clear sense of which accelerated pathway they should aim for, and they design the rest of their program around qualifying for it.

## SEGMENT 6 — The IND — your permission slip, and the thirty-day clock

So now you have done your preclinical work, you have had your pre-IND meeting, you have addressed the agency's feedback, and you are ready to file. Welcome to the IND.

The **Investigational New Drug application** — IND — is the regulatory submission that gives you permission to begin clinical trials. It is not, despite the name, an approval of the drug. It is a permission to test the drug in humans. The distinction matters. The IND is a research permit, not a marketing authorization. The drug itself remains unapproved throughout the entire clinical trial process. You are conducting investigations under the IND. You are not selling a product.

What is in an IND? Let me give you the rough table of contents. An IND has three big sections. **First, the chemistry, manufacturing, and controls section — usually called CMC — which describes how the drug is made, what its physical and chemical properties are, what tests you do to confirm it is what you say it is, and what your quality system looks like.** We are going to spend a whole segment on CMC later because it is often the limiting step for gene therapy. **Second, the preclinical pharmacology and toxicology section — which is the safety dossier we discussed in Segment 4, presented in the format the agency expects.** **Third, the clinical protocol — the actual plan for the trial: who will be enrolled, how they will be dosed, what will be measured, what will count as a success or a failure, and what will trigger stopping the trial early.**

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy. **The IND is your permission slip.** When you were a kid in school and you wanted to go on a field trip, your teacher gave you a piece of paper to bring home, and your parents had to sign it and you had to bring it back the next day. The signed paper was the permission slip. Without it, you couldn't get on the bus. The IND is the same thing, except the field trip is "first-in-human dosing of a brand new gene therapy" and the parent whose signature you need is the FDA.

But here is the twist. The IND does not work by waiting for the agency to say yes. **It works on a clock.** You submit your IND. You then wait thirty days. **If the agency does not put you on hold within thirty days, your IND is automatically active, and you can begin dosing patients.** Silence is consent. The agency is not required to formally approve your IND. They are required to act on it within thirty days if they have concerns. If they have no concerns, or if they have concerns they are willing to address through ongoing dialogue rather than a formal hold, you simply start your trial on day thirty-one.

Now, in practice, almost every IND for a complex product like a gene therapy gets some kind of agency response within the thirty-day window. The response might be a clinical hold — meaning you cannot dose patients until the agency lifts it — or it might be a letter listing concerns the agency wants you to address but that does not formally block the trial. Sometimes you get partial holds, where you can start enrolling at a low dose but not escalate until specific issues are resolved. The thirty-day clock is real, but it is not a clean binary; it is the structure within which a back-and-forth dialogue happens.

\[Pause.\]

The most important thing the agency can do during that thirty-day window is put you on **clinical hold**. The clinical hold is a particularly important regulatory mechanism, and I want you to understand it as a standing power that the agency has throughout the entire clinical life of a drug, not just at the start. **The FDA can place a clinical hold on your trial at any time.** Before you start dosing. After you have dosed five patients. In the middle of a Phase 2 study. The day before you file your BLA. If a serious adverse event occurs, or if new data emerges that calls the safety profile into question, the agency can require you to stop. You cannot dose another patient until the hold is lifted.

The analogy here is straightforward. **The clinical hold is the emergency brake.** It is the cord on the train that anyone can pull. The agency holds the cord throughout the entire ride. Most of the time they don't pull it. But the fact that they can pull it changes how sponsors behave. You design your trial knowing that if a safety signal emerges, the agency can stop you. You report adverse events promptly knowing that withholding could trigger a hold and a much bigger problem. You build relationships with your CBER reviewers so that when an issue arises you can have a substantive conversation about it before they reach for the brake.

\[Pause.\]

For gene therapy, clinical holds have been a particularly important feature of the regulatory landscape. The field has had several high-profile holds — some after serious adverse events, including deaths, in specific trials — and each hold has reshaped how the field operates afterwards. Holds in the AAV space following hepatotoxicity events at high systemic doses, for instance, led to new guidance on dose selection and on the structure of safety monitoring committees. The hold is not just a punishment; it is a feedback mechanism that the field as a whole learns from, even when it is painful for the individual sponsor on the receiving end.

So that is the IND. Permission slip. Thirty-day clock. Clinical hold as the standing emergency brake. Get through the thirty days, address whatever response the agency sends back, and you are in clinical trials. Which, of course, is when the real work begins.

## SEGMENT 7 — Accelerated approval pathways — the fast lanes through the gauntlet

Before we walk into the clinical trial segments, I want to stop and tell you about a set of regulatory mechanisms that are quietly transformative for how gene therapies move through the system. These are the **accelerated pathways**, sometimes called the **expedited programs**, and they are designed to speed up the development and review of drugs that target serious or life-threatening conditions with significant unmet medical need. Gene therapies, by their nature, almost always meet those criteria, so gene therapy programs disproportionately qualify for one or more of these designations.

There are four major designations in the US system you need to know. **Fast Track. Breakthrough Therapy. Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy — RMAT. Priority Review.** Each one provides slightly different benefits. Each one has slightly different qualification criteria. And clever sponsors stack them — applying for Fast Track first, then Breakthrough or RMAT once they have early clinical data, then Priority Review at the time of the BLA — to compress the timeline as much as possible.

\[Pause.\]

**Fast Track** is the oldest of the four. It was created in the late 1990s. The criteria are broad: a drug for a serious condition with the potential to address unmet medical need can qualify. The benefits include more frequent communication with the agency, eligibility for rolling review — meaning you can submit your BLA in pieces as they are ready rather than all at once — and accelerated review timelines. Most gene therapies qualify for Fast Track, and most apply for it early, sometimes at or even before the IND stage.

**Breakthrough Therapy** is more demanding. Created in 2012, it requires that you have **preliminary clinical evidence** suggesting your drug may demonstrate substantial improvement over existing therapies on a clinically significant endpoint. That means you generally can't apply for Breakthrough until you have at least some Phase 1 data showing real efficacy. The benefits are bigger than Fast Track: intensive guidance from FDA on efficient drug development, organizational commitment of senior reviewers, and rolling review. Breakthrough designation is, in my experience talking to people in the field, the single most useful accelerated designation because of the dedicated FDA attention it brings. You essentially get a more engaged review team.

**RMAT — Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy** — is the newest and most gene-therapy-specific. It was created by the 21st Century Cures Act in 2016, and it was designed explicitly to accommodate the unusual features of cell and gene therapy development — small patient populations, durable single-dose effects, the difficulty of running traditional Phase 3 trials. To qualify for RMAT, your product has to be a regenerative medicine therapy — which includes cell therapies, gene therapies, and tissue-engineered products — for a serious condition, and you need preliminary clinical evidence indicating potential to address unmet need. The benefits are similar to Breakthrough — intensive FDA guidance, eligibility for rolling and Priority Review — but with the addition of explicit allowance for using surrogate or intermediate endpoints, post-approval studies in lieu of pre-approval ones, and other accommodations that recognize that gene therapy doesn't fit the small-molecule template. **For a gene therapy program, RMAT is usually the right ask.**

**Priority Review** is different from the other three. It is not a development-phase designation; it is a review-phase designation. When you file your BLA, you can request Priority Review, which shortens the FDA's target review timeline from ten months to six months. The agency grants Priority Review to applications that, if approved, would provide significant improvement in safety or effectiveness of treatment for a serious condition. Many gene therapies, by the time they reach BLA, easily qualify.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy. **The accelerated pathways are the fast lanes.** Imagine the regulatory gauntlet as a highway, and most drugs travel in the regular lanes — they sit in traffic, they wait their turn, they cover the same distance as everyone else. The accelerated pathways are dedicated lanes on the side of that highway, with fewer cars, fewer stops, and direct on-ramps to the agency's attention. If you qualify, you can shave **months or even years off the development and review timeline**. For a small patient population dying of a fatal disease, those months matter enormously.

I'll mention the EU side briefly. The European equivalent of the US accelerated pathways is called **PRIME — Priority Medicines** — administered by EMA. PRIME provides enhanced interaction with the agency and is broadly comparable to Breakthrough Therapy in concept. Separately, the EU has an **adaptive licensing** approach for advanced therapy medicinal products, run through CAT, that allows for staged approvals based on accumulating evidence. The mechanics differ in detail, but the underlying philosophy — speeding up access for serious diseases with unmet need — is the same on both sides of the Atlantic.

One important caveat. **Accelerated pathways do not lower the bar for approval.** They do not change the safety or efficacy standards your drug ultimately has to meet. They change the speed and the structure of how you get there. A drug that gets Breakthrough designation and then fails in Phase 3 has still failed. The fast lane gets you to the gate faster. It does not change what the gate requires.

## SEGMENT 8 — Phase 1 and Phase 2, especially their combination in gene therapy

Now let's walk into the clinical trials. Phase 1 and Phase 2.

In the traditional small-molecule drug development model, Phase 1 and Phase 2 are clearly separate. Phase 1 is the first-in-human trial. It enrolls a small number of subjects — often healthy volunteers, sometimes patients with the disease in question — and it focuses almost entirely on safety. You give a single low dose to a single subject. You wait. If nothing bad happens, you give a slightly higher dose to the next subject. You escalate cautiously through a pre-specified dose ladder, monitoring for adverse events, characterizing pharmacokinetics. By the end of Phase 1, you have a sense of what doses are tolerable, what the dose-limiting toxicities are, and what dose to take forward into efficacy testing.

Phase 2 then expands the population. You enroll a couple hundred patients with the disease. You dose them at the dose Phase 1 identified as safe. You measure efficacy — does the drug actually do anything to the disease? — and you continue to gather safety data. By the end of Phase 2, you should have a clear sense of whether the drug works, in whom it works, and what dose to use in the big confirmatory Phase 3 trial.

That is the traditional template. For gene therapy, the template often does not apply cleanly.

\[Pause.\]

The reason is that gene therapy patient populations are usually small — often very small. If you are developing a treatment for a rare genetic disease that affects a thousand patients in the United States, you cannot run a Phase 1 trial in healthy volunteers — gene therapies are too risky and too expensive for that. You have to enroll patients with the disease. And there are not enough patients to support separate Phase 1 and Phase 2 cohorts, each of substantial size, before you get to Phase 3.

So gene therapy trials are often run as **Phase 1/2 combinations**. A single trial enrolls a small cohort of patients, escalates them through a couple of dose levels, monitors safety, and simultaneously measures efficacy endpoints. The boundary between Phase 1 and Phase 2 dissolves. The single trial does both jobs, and the data from it can sometimes — given regulatory flexibility — support the pivotal evidence package for approval, without a separate large Phase 3.

This blurring is not informal. It is a structural feature of how cell and gene therapy trials are designed, and the FDA has been explicit in its guidance that for these products, the agency expects unconventional development paths. Sometimes a single Phase 1/2 trial in twenty patients is enough to support approval, if the disease is severe enough, the unmet need is high enough, and the effect size is dramatic enough. That is a deeply unusual situation by traditional drug development standards. It is the standard situation in modern gene therapy.

\[Pause.\]

Let me tell you about how a Phase 1/2 gene therapy trial actually works in practice. You start with a low dose — typically chosen based on the no-observed-adverse-effect-level from your animal studies, scaled down with a safety factor. You dose one patient. You wait — often weeks or months, depending on the readouts you are tracking. You assess safety. If there are no concerning adverse events, you may dose a second patient at the same dose, or you may escalate to a higher dose, depending on how the protocol is structured. You walk up the dose ladder cautiously, with often a single patient per dose level at the lowest doses, and small cohorts of three to six patients at higher doses. **This is dose escalation. It is the most cautious part of the entire clinical pipeline, because the consequences of getting it wrong are catastrophic.**

Once you have identified a dose that appears safe and that produces a biological effect — say, a measurable level of the therapeutic protein in the patient's blood, or a measurable reduction in some disease biomarker — you may expand the cohort at that dose to gather more data on efficacy. The expansion cohort is what would traditionally have been called Phase 2; in the combined-phase model, it is just the next part of the same trial.

For gene therapies that show strong early efficacy, this Phase 1/2 dataset can become the centerpiece of the eventual BLA. The agency might require a confirmatory Phase 3 — or might accept a single-arm Phase 2 with a natural-history comparator — depending on the disease, the effect size, and the strength of the regulatory designations the program has accumulated. **The pathway is not fixed. It is negotiated, drug by drug, with the agency.**

## SEGMENT 9 — Phase 3 endpoints — surrogate, clinical, and the Casgevy negotiation

If a gene therapy is going to be tested in a traditional Phase 3 trial — a larger, often randomized, often controlled study designed to definitively establish efficacy — one of the most important conversations between sponsor and agency is about **endpoints**. What are you going to measure that will determine whether the drug works?

Endpoints come in two broad categories. **Clinical endpoints** are direct measures of how the patient feels, functions, or survives. Death. Hospitalization. Pain. Mobility. Cognition. These are the things that matter directly to patients and their families. They are also, often, very hard to measure efficiently — because some of them happen rarely, some of them take years to manifest, and some of them require enormous sample sizes to detect a difference between treatment and placebo.

**Surrogate endpoints** are biomarkers or other measurements that are believed to predict clinical benefit but are not themselves clinical benefit. Blood pressure for cardiovascular disease, viral load for HIV, LDL cholesterol for cardiovascular risk, hemoglobin A1C for diabetes complications — these are all surrogates. They are easier to measure than the underlying clinical outcome, and if they have been validated as predictors of clinical benefit, the agency may accept changes in them as the basis for approval.

For gene therapy, surrogate endpoints are particularly attractive. Many gene therapies produce dramatic, measurable changes in a biomarker — say, levels of a missing enzyme, or numbers of edited cells — long before any clinical endpoint becomes measurable. If the surrogate has been validated, the trial can be smaller, faster, and feasible in a population where running it to a hard clinical endpoint would be impractical.

\[Pause.\]

The challenge is that for any given disease, the validity of a particular surrogate is debatable. Just because a biomarker correlates with disease does not mean that changing the biomarker will change the disease. The history of medicine is full of cautionary tales — drugs that lowered the surrogate but didn't help patients, and sometimes hurt them. Antiarrhythmic drugs that suppressed PVCs but increased mortality. Drugs that raised HDL cholesterol but didn't reduce cardiovascular events. The agency, having learned these lessons, is properly cautious about accepting new surrogate endpoints.

The negotiation between sponsor and agency over what endpoint a pivotal trial will use is, often, one of the most consequential conversations in a gene therapy program. And the example I want to tell you about, because it shaped how the field thinks about this kind of negotiation, is **Casgevy for sickle cell disease**.

Sickle cell disease is, you know, an inherited disorder of hemoglobin. Patients have a single amino acid change in beta-globin that causes red blood cells to sickle under low oxygen, leading to episodes of severe pain called vaso-occlusive crises, organ damage over time, and shortened life expectancy. Casgevy — the CRISPR therapy approved in December 2023 — uses gene editing to disrupt a regulator called BCL11A in patients' bone marrow stem cells, reactivating fetal hemoglobin production and bypassing the sickling problem.

The endpoint question for the Casgevy trial was: what should you measure to demonstrate that the therapy works? Mortality would be the cleanest clinical endpoint, but mortality from sickle cell takes decades to manifest, and you could not run a trial that long. Pain itself is hard to standardize. What the sponsors and the agency eventually converged on was **the elimination of vaso-occlusive crises over a defined follow-up period.** Patients in the trial were assessed for their history of crises before treatment, treated with Casgevy, and then followed for at least twelve consecutive months after engraftment. The pre-specified endpoint was the proportion of patients who experienced no vaso-occlusive crises during that twelve-month window.

\[Pause.\]

This was, in a sense, an intermediate endpoint — neither a pure surrogate like a blood biomarker, nor a hard mortality endpoint, but a clinically meaningful event count over a defined period. The agency accepted this design because vaso-occlusive crises are themselves the proximate cause of much of the disease's morbidity, are reliably countable, and represent something patients and clinicians both care about deeply. The trial data — published in the New England Journal of Medicine, presented at advisory committees — showed that the vast majority of treated patients were free of crises throughout the follow-up window. That was the basis for approval.

The Casgevy endpoint negotiation is a template now. Future CRISPR and gene therapy programs in other diseases are looking at this case and asking: what is the equivalent endpoint for our condition? What is the clinically meaningful event we can count over a defined window that the agency will accept as the basis for approval? This is, in a real sense, a regulatory creativity problem as much as a scientific one. The answer for sickle cell was vaso-occlusive crises. The answer for a different disease will be something different. But the framework — choose an intermediate clinical endpoint that is countable, meaningful, and measurable within a feasible trial timeline — is the model.

## SEGMENT 10 — BLA versus NDA — gene therapy is regulated as a biologic

Welcome back. I hope you got some coffee. We are now into the second half of the lecture, and in this segment I want to address what might sound like a small distinction but is actually structurally important: the difference between a **BLA** and an **NDA**.

The **NDA** is the **New Drug Application** — the regulatory submission that traditional small-molecule drugs file at the end of development to seek approval from FDA. NDAs are reviewed by CDER, the Center for Drug Evaluation and Research. The regulatory framework for NDAs is built on the foundations of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, originally passed in 1938.

The **BLA** is the **Biologics License Application** — the equivalent submission for biologic products. BLAs are reviewed by CBER, the center we discussed in Segment 2, which is the home of gene therapy regulation. The regulatory framework for BLAs is built on a different statutory foundation — the Public Health Service Act of 1944 — which gives FDA the authority to license biologic products. The terminology even reflects this lineage: a biologic is **licensed**, not just approved, because the original framework was about licensing manufacturers of biological products like vaccines and antitoxins.

**Gene therapy is regulated as a biologic. You file a BLA, not an NDA. You deal with CBER, not CDER.** That is the bottom line, and it has practical consequences.

\[Pause.\]

Why does the distinction matter? Three reasons.

First, **the manufacturing oversight is more intensive for biologics.** Because biologic products are made in living systems, and because small changes in the manufacturing process can change the properties of the final product, CBER pays close attention to every step of how a gene therapy is made. The agency may inspect manufacturing facilities before approval, and the BLA itself contains an enormous amount of detail about the manufacturing process that is not typical for small-molecule NDAs. We will see in Segment 16 that CMC — chemistry, manufacturing, and controls — is often the limiting step for gene therapy. The intensity of biologic-product manufacturing oversight is one reason why.

Second, **the post-market commitments tend to be longer and more demanding for gene therapies.** For a small-molecule drug, post-market surveillance is mostly about catching rare adverse events that did not show up in clinical trials. For a gene therapy, post-market surveillance is also about characterizing the long-term durability and safety of a one-time treatment — questions that, by definition, you cannot fully answer in a clinical trial of finite duration. We will see in Segment 15 that the FDA expects up to fifteen years of follow-up for some AAV gene therapies, which is the longest post-market commitment in modern medicine.

Third, **biologics have different exclusivity protections than small molecules.** For a small-molecule NDA, the standard data exclusivity period is five years. For a biologic licensed via the BLA pathway, the standard data exclusivity period is twelve years. That difference matters enormously to the commercial calculus of developing a gene therapy. The longer exclusivity period reflects, in part, the more intensive development process — and it provides the financial runway that makes investing in a small-population gene therapy commercially viable.

\[Pause.\]

So when you read in the news that a gene therapy has been "approved" — for instance, "Casgevy was approved by FDA in December 2023" — what has actually happened, in technical regulatory terms, is that CBER has issued a biologics license to the manufacturer, authorizing them to produce and market the product. The license, like all FDA licenses, can be amended over time as new information emerges. The manufacturer can be required to update labeling, conduct post-market studies, or in extreme cases withdraw the product. The license is not a one-time event; it is the start of a continuing regulatory relationship.

The EU equivalent is a **marketing authorization**, issued by the European Commission on the basis of a favorable CHMP opinion based on a CAT recommendation. The conceptual structure is similar — it is an authorization to market, subject to ongoing obligations — but the legal mechanics differ. For both jurisdictions, "approval" is the start of the post-market phase, not the end of regulatory engagement.

## SEGMENT 11 — Luxturna — the first US AAV approval and what it proved

So now, finally, having walked through the framework, let me tell you the stories of the gene therapies that have made it through. I am going to give you five, in chronological order: Luxturna, Zolgensma, Hemgenix, Casgevy, and Lyfgenia. Each one is a regulatory milestone as much as a scientific one. Each one taught the field something about what was possible.

We start with **Luxturna**. December 19, 2017. Voretigene neparvovec. The first AAV gene therapy approved in the United States. A turning point.

Luxturna treats a rare form of inherited blindness called **Leber congenital amaurosis** caused by mutations in the gene **RPE65**. RPE65 encodes an enzyme essential for the visual cycle — the biochemical process by which the retina converts light into electrical signals. Patients with two broken copies of RPE65 are born with severe vision impairment that progresses over time to complete blindness. The patient population is small — a few thousand worldwide — and before Luxturna there was no treatment.

The product itself is an AAV2-based vector carrying a healthy copy of RPE65, injected directly under the retina in each eye in a separate surgical procedure. The total amount of vector administered per eye is small. The injection delivers the healthy gene to the retinal pigment epithelial cells where RPE65 normally functions. The cells, having received a functional copy of the gene, start producing the enzyme. The visual cycle resumes. Patients gain functional vision.

\[Pause.\]

The clinical trial that supported Luxturna's approval was a Phase 3 randomized controlled trial in 31 patients, with the endpoint being a **multi-luminance mobility test** — essentially, the ability of patients to navigate a standardized obstacle course under low-light conditions. The treated patients showed dramatic improvements; the untreated control group did not. The effect size was big enough, the safety profile was clean enough, and the patient population was small enough that the trial was sufficient as a pivotal study.

But the regulatory significance of Luxturna was not just that it worked. It was that it proved several things to FDA, to the field, and to the broader medical community.

**First, it proved that an AAV gene therapy could navigate the full regulatory pathway and emerge as an approved product in the United States.** Until December 2017, that had not been done. There had been earlier approved gene therapies elsewhere — Glybera was approved in Europe in 2012 but commercially failed and was withdrawn — but the US had been a holdout. Luxturna broke the seal.

**Second, it proved that a Phase 3 trial in 31 patients could be sufficient for approval, provided the disease was rare enough, the effect size was big enough, and the endpoints were well-chosen.** This established a template that future small-population gene therapies would build on.

**Third, it proved that the price would be high.** Luxturna launched at approximately $425,000 per eye, or $850,000 per patient for the bilateral treatment. The pricing provoked controversy. But it also established that the gene therapy market could sustain very high per-patient prices for transformative one-time treatments — which, in turn, created the financial conditions for the rest of the field to invest in gene therapy development.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy. **Luxturna was the first US AAV approval — for RPE65 retinal degeneration. It gave the field the first proof that AAV gene therapy could be a real drug.** The molecular framework had been validated in academic papers for years. The clinical safety had been demonstrated in early-phase trials. But there is a difference between "demonstrated in trials" and "approved by FDA, with a label, with a commercial product, with insurance reimbursement, with patients getting it in a regular clinical setting." Luxturna closed that gap for the first time. After Luxturna, every other AAV gene therapy program had a regulatory precedent to point to. The conversation with the agency was no longer "is this kind of product approvable in principle?" It was "given that AAV gene therapies are approvable, what specifically do you want to see for our product?"

That shift in the default assumption — from theoretical possibility to demonstrated reality — was, I would argue, more important to the trajectory of the field than the specific clinical benefit Luxturna provided to its specific patient population, important as that was.

## SEGMENT 12 — Zolgensma — the SMA cure and the $2.1M price tag

The second story is **Zolgensma**. Onasemnogene abeparvovec. Approved by FDA in May 2019, about a year and a half after Luxturna. The drug that broke the conversation about gene therapy pricing wide open.

Zolgensma treats **spinal muscular atrophy** — SMA — a devastating genetic disease in which infants are born with mutations in a gene called **SMN1** that encodes a protein essential for the survival of motor neurons. Without SMN1, motor neurons in the spinal cord progressively die. In the most severe form, type 1 SMA, untreated infants typically die or require permanent ventilator support by age two. It is one of the most heartbreaking conditions in pediatric neurology.

Zolgensma is an AAV9-based vector carrying a healthy copy of SMN1, administered as a single intravenous infusion. AAV9 was chosen because it crosses the blood-brain barrier and reaches motor neurons in the spinal cord — a property that distinguishes it from most other AAV serotypes. The dose is very high — on the order of two times ten to the fourteenth vector genomes per kilogram — because you need to reach motor neurons distributed throughout the central nervous system, and the cumulative number of vector particles required is enormous.

\[Pause.\]

The clinical results in the pivotal trial were striking. Infants who would, untreated, have died or been ventilator-dependent by their second birthday were instead sitting unsupported, hitting motor milestones, breathing on their own. The disease was not entirely cured — some patients required additional supportive care, and long-term durability of effect was still being characterized — but the transformation in clinical trajectory was, to use the technical term, dramatic. The FDA approved Zolgensma in May 2019. EMA followed with European approval in 2020.

The label is specific. Zolgensma is approved for pediatric patients less than two years of age with SMA. The age restriction reflects the biology — you want to deliver SMN1 before too many motor neurons have already died — and reflects the safety profile, since the high doses required for whole-body distribution become risky in larger patients.

\[Pause.\]

Now, the price. Zolgensma launched at $2.125 million per dose. **$2.125 million.** For a single intravenous infusion. At the time, it was the most expensive drug in the world by a substantial margin. The price provoked an immediate and intense public conversation about gene therapy pricing — a conversation that, frankly, is still ongoing.

The arguments in favor of the price went something like this. SMA, untreated, costs an enormous amount over the patient's lifetime — chronic respiratory support, intensive nursing care, multiple surgeries, and ultimately a death that is itself extraordinarily expensive medically. A one-time treatment that prevents all of that, while not actually less expensive than the lifetime cost of disease, is comparable, and provides incalculable benefit to the patient and the family. The development cost of Zolgensma — across the company that developed it, AveXis, eventually acquired by Novartis — was substantial. The patient population is small, so the per-patient price has to be high to support recovery of development costs.

The arguments against the price went something like this. Two million dollars is a number with no precedent in pharmaceutical pricing. It is a number that strains every reimbursement system in the world. It is a number that, even with insurance, places enormous financial stress on the families and health systems involved. And it sets a precedent — every subsequent gene therapy is now negotiated against the Zolgensma benchmark.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy. **Zolgensma was the SMA cure — single-dose AAV9 for spinal muscular atrophy. The $2.1 million price tag broke the discussion open about gene therapy pricing.** Before Zolgensma, the public conversation about gene therapy was mostly about the science — would it work, how soon, in what diseases? After Zolgensma, the conversation shifted. The science was working. The clinical benefit was real. The remaining question, increasingly, was: who is going to pay for this, and how, and at what point does the financial machinery of healthcare break under the strain?

That conversation has spawned a whole subfield of health economics around gene therapy pricing. Value-based contracts, in which payers only pay if the treatment works long-term. Risk-sharing arrangements. Outcomes-based reimbursement. Subscription models for population-wide treatment. None of these have stabilized into a standard yet. The Zolgensma launch was, in many ways, the moment the field started having to take these questions as seriously as the regulatory ones.

There is also a post-market component to the Zolgensma story that we will come back to in Segment 15. Because the durability of the effect is still being established, Novartis was required by FDA to maintain a long-term patient registry called **RESTORE** to follow treated patients for many years. That registry is, today, one of the most important data sources in the entire AAV field for understanding what happens to gene therapy patients five, ten, fifteen years out from their single dose. The registry is itself a regulatory creation, and it shapes how the agency thinks about long-term follow-up requirements for all subsequent AAV approvals.

## SEGMENT 13 — Hemgenix — etranacogene dezaparvovec, the most expensive single dose

The third story is **Hemgenix**. Etranacogene dezaparvovec. Approved in November 2022 for the treatment of **hemophilia B** — the inherited bleeding disorder caused by deficiency of clotting factor IX. At the time of approval, Hemgenix took the title from Zolgensma as the most expensive single-dose drug in the world, launching at $3.5 million per dose.

Let me give you the disease context first. Hemophilia B is an X-linked recessive disorder, so it predominantly affects males. The gene that is broken is **F9**, which encodes factor IX, one of the proteins in the coagulation cascade. Without factor IX, the blood does not clot properly. Patients experience spontaneous bleeding into joints — which over time destroys those joints — and serious bleeding after even minor trauma. The standard treatment for hemophilia B for decades has been intravenous infusions of factor IX concentrate, several times a week, for life. The infusions work, but they are expensive — annual costs of $400,000 to $800,000 per patient are typical — and they require ongoing patient adherence.

The clinical promise of a gene therapy for hemophilia B is exactly the promise of converting a chronic, lifelong treatment regimen into a one-time intervention. Deliver a healthy copy of F9 to hepatocytes — liver cells, which are the natural site of factor IX production — and have those cells produce factor IX for the rest of the patient's life. No more weekly infusions. No more chronic management. One dose. Permanent fix.

\[Pause.\]

Hemgenix uses **AAV5** as its vector, with a particularly potent liver-directed promoter driving expression of a high-activity variant of factor IX called the Padua variant. The high-activity variant is an interesting wrinkle — it produces a factor IX protein with roughly eight-fold higher activity per molecule than the wild-type, which means you can achieve clinically meaningful clotting activity with less total protein production, which means a lower vector dose is needed. The Padua variant was discovered in a single family in Italy whose members had unusually elevated clotting activity; that natural human variant became the engineered payload for a global drug.

The clinical trial supporting Hemgenix was a single-arm Phase 3 study in 54 patients with severe or moderately severe hemophilia B. The endpoint was the annualized bleeding rate following treatment, compared to the patients' historical bleeding rates while on factor IX prophylaxis. The trial showed substantial reductions in bleeding rates, with many patients able to discontinue prophylactic factor IX infusions entirely. The agency approved the product.

The price — $3.5 million — provoked a second wave of the conversation that Zolgensma had started. The justification was again largely actuarial: the lifetime cost of factor IX infusions for a hemophilia B patient is in the range of $20 to $30 million, so a one-time $3.5 million treatment is, in pure dollar terms, a substantial saving over the patient's lifetime. The challenge is that the saving accrues over decades, while the cost is incurred upfront, which creates real cash flow problems for the payers who have to write the check today.

\[Pause.\]

Hemgenix also illustrates another important feature of the gene therapy regulatory landscape: **the role of competition and timing**. Hemgenix was not the first approved gene therapy for hemophilia. Earlier products had been approved in Europe but had complex commercial trajectories. The CSL Behring product that became Hemgenix was developed in part by uniQure, a Dutch biotech that had spent years optimizing the AAV5 platform. When Hemgenix received FDA approval, it had a window of market exclusivity, but it knew that competing AAV-based hemophilia B therapies were also in development. The pricing was set partly with an awareness that this window of exclusivity might be limited, and partly with the actuarial argument we discussed.

What Hemgenix demonstrated, regulatory-wise, was that the AAV liver-targeting approach — which had been talked about for two decades as the "next big thing" — had finally arrived as a commercially viable, FDA-approved modality. The same AAV-to-liver framework that supports Hemgenix is now the basis for development programs targeting other liver-expressed proteins — Fabry disease, MPS II, hereditary angioedema, ornithine transcarbamylase deficiency, many others. Each of those programs has, in its regulatory file, a pointer to the precedent that Hemgenix established.

## SEGMENT 14 — Casgevy and Lyfgenia — the first CRISPR approvals

December 8, 2023. Two products approved on the same day for the same disease — sickle cell disease — using two different mechanisms. **Casgevy**, the CRISPR-based therapy from Vertex Pharmaceuticals and CRISPR Therapeutics. **Lyfgenia**, the lentiviral-based therapy from Bluebird Bio. Both for sickle cell disease in patients twelve years and older with recurrent vaso-occlusive crises.

The simultaneous approval was, in retrospect, a perfectly chosen moment. Sickle cell disease had been the moral and scientific cause célèbre of gene therapy for years — a disease that disproportionately affects Black patients in the United States and around the world, a disease for which the gene therapy approach is mechanistically compelling, a disease whose treatment had been promised by gene therapy advocates for decades. The simultaneous approval of two different gene therapies for the same disease on the same day was a statement about the maturity of the field.

\[Pause.\]

Let me focus on Casgevy first, because it carries the additional historical weight of being **the first CRISPR-based therapy approved anywhere in the world**.

Casgevy works ex vivo. Stem cells are collected from the patient's bone marrow. The cells are edited in a laboratory using CRISPR-Cas9 to disrupt a regulatory element in the BCL11A gene, which is a transcriptional repressor of fetal hemoglobin. With BCL11A disrupted, the patient's red blood cell precursors start producing fetal hemoglobin instead of the defective adult hemoglobin that causes sickling. The edited cells are then re-infused into the patient after a course of conditioning chemotherapy that wipes out their original bone marrow. The edited cells engraft and become the new source of red blood cells, producing fetal hemoglobin that does not sickle.

The regulatory significance of Casgevy is enormous. It is the first time CRISPR — a technology that was first described in its therapeutic form barely over a decade earlier, in the 2012 Doudna-Charpentier paper — has been approved as a drug. Every future CRISPR program now has a regulatory precedent to work from. The agency has demonstrated that it is willing to approve a gene-edited cell therapy. The conversation about CRISPR therapeutics has shifted, definitively, from "could this be a drug?" to "what should the next CRISPR drug be?"

\[Pause.\]

The clinical data for Casgevy was striking. In the pivotal trial, the vast majority of treated patients — somewhere around 90 percent — experienced no vaso-occlusive crises during the twelve-month follow-up window. Treated patients showed dramatic increases in fetal hemoglobin levels, dramatic reductions in indicators of hemolysis, and qualitative improvements in their quality of life. The safety profile was dominated by the conditioning chemotherapy — which is itself an aggressive and potentially toxic treatment — rather than by the CRISPR editing per se.

Lyfgenia, approved the same day, takes a different approach. Instead of CRISPR editing, Lyfgenia uses a lentiviral vector to add a healthy copy of a modified beta-globin gene to the patient's stem cells. The cells are then re-infused. The added beta-globin produces a hemoglobin variant that disrupts sickling. The end result is similar to Casgevy — patients have reduced or eliminated vaso-occlusive crises — but the mechanism is gene addition rather than gene editing.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy I want you to hold. **Casgevy was the first CRISPR drug — sickle cell plus beta-thalassemia. The regulatory template for future CRISPR therapeutics.** Every CRISPR program — for cardiovascular disease, for cancer, for neurological disorders, for liver diseases, for everything that is in the development pipeline today — looks back at the Casgevy regulatory file as the template. The agency's expectations for CRISPR-specific data — characterization of editing efficiency, characterization of off-target editing, long-term genomic stability of edited cells, durability of clinical effect — were laid down in the Casgevy review. Future programs build on that foundation.

The fact that two different gene therapies were approved for the same disease on the same day also illustrates something important about the regulatory landscape: **the FDA does not pick winners.** It evaluates each application on its own merits. If two products meet the standard, both get approved. The market then decides — through pricing, through clinical outcomes, through patient and physician preference — which one becomes the dominant treatment for a given disease. This is, in some sense, the system working as designed. The regulator's job is gating, not selection.

## SEGMENT 15 — Advisory committee meetings and post-market commitments

I want to spend this segment on two related features of the regulatory landscape that often get less attention than they should: **advisory committee meetings** and **post-market commitments**.

Advisory committees are panels of outside experts — clinicians, statisticians, patient representatives, ethicists — that FDA convenes to provide independent input on specific drug applications. For gene therapies, the relevant advisory committees are the **Oncologic Drugs Advisory Committee** — ODAC — for cancer-related products, and the **Cellular, Tissue, and Gene Therapies Advisory Committee** — CTGTAC — for cell and gene therapies specifically.

The advisory committee meeting is, structurally, a public event. The committee members review the application materials in advance. The sponsor presents the data. The FDA presents its analysis. Patients and patient advocates often present testimony. The committee then deliberates in public — often for hours, with substantive disagreement among members — and at the end takes a formal vote on specific questions posed by FDA. The questions might be: does the data support approval? Is the benefit-risk profile favorable? Are the post-market commitments adequate?

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy. **The advisory committee is the public hearing.** Imagine a court case in which the prosecutor — the FDA — and the defense — the sponsor — both present their evidence in front of a jury, but the jury is made up of outside experts in the technical field, and the deliberations happen in front of an audience that includes the press, patients, advocates, and academic observers. The jury's verdict is advisory only — **FDA is not bound by the committee's vote** — but in practice, FDA usually follows the committee's recommendation, and the public deliberation itself shapes the eventual regulatory decision in subtle ways. The questions the committee members ask. The concerns they raise. The voices of patients in the room. All of it feeds back into the agency's thinking.

There have been famous and consequential advisory committee meetings for gene therapies. The Luxturna advisory committee — which was actually CTGTAC's predecessor — voted unanimously in favor of approval in 2017, on the basis of the dramatic clinical data. The Casgevy advisory committee in 2023 was more contentious, with substantial discussion of long-term safety risks of CRISPR editing — what about off-target edits we haven't found yet? what about the unknown effects fifteen years from now? — and the committee ultimately recommended approval but with attached recommendations for extensive post-market follow-up. Those recommendations directly shaped the post-market commitments that Vertex agreed to as a condition of approval.

\[Pause.\]

Now let me talk about post-market commitments specifically, because for gene therapy they are uniquely intense.

When an AAV gene therapy is approved, the FDA generally requires the sponsor to commit to **long-term follow-up of treated patients for up to fifteen years**. Fifteen years. That is the longest post-market follow-up commitment in modern medicine for any class of product. The reasoning is that a one-time vector administration could have effects — particularly late-emerging adverse events related to vector integration, or sustained immune responses, or the durability of expression — that simply cannot be characterized in a clinical trial of finite duration. The fifteen-year window is meant to capture those late effects if they occur.

Practically, the commitment takes the form of a **patient registry** — a structured database that tracks every patient who received the therapy, captures their clinical outcomes at regular follow-up intervals, and reports back to FDA. The Zolgensma RESTORE registry I mentioned in Segment 12 is the model. Hemgenix has a similar registry. Casgevy and Lyfgenia have registries. Every approved gene therapy carries a registry obligation, and those registries are now an enormous body of post-market data that the field as a whole learns from.

The registries are not just for safety. They also generate the durability data that the field desperately needs. Does Zolgensma's effect last five years? Ten? Permanent? Does Hemgenix's factor IX expression remain at clinically meaningful levels a decade after dosing? These are questions you can only answer by following real patients for real time. The registries, awkwardly and slowly, are answering them.

\[Pause.\]

For a sponsor, the post-market commitments are a real ongoing obligation. You don't get to declare victory at approval and walk away. You have to staff the registry. You have to keep contact with patients and treating clinicians. You have to publish the data periodically. You have to report adverse events as they emerge. You have to be ready, fifteen years out from approval, to address whatever the data shows. **Approval is not the finish line. It is the start of a fifteen-year continuing engagement.**

This is, in some ways, the right design. Gene therapy is a fundamentally new modality, and we genuinely do not yet know the long-term consequences of single-dose vector administration. The registries are how we will learn. They are also a real cost of doing business in this field, and they shape how sponsors plan their commercial strategy.

## SEGMENT 16 — CMC — chemistry, manufacturing, and controls, the limiting step

I have been promising for several segments now that we would come back to CMC, and here we are. Chemistry, manufacturing, and controls. The unglamorous part of the regulatory file. The part nobody writes news articles about. And, in my experience, the part that more gene therapy programs trip over than any other single component.

CMC, as a regulatory acronym, refers to the section of any drug application that describes how the drug is made, what its physical and chemical properties are, what quality testing it undergoes, and what manufacturing controls ensure that every batch is the same. For a small-molecule drug, the CMC section is substantial but reasonably tractable — chemistry has been a precise science for two centuries, and the tools for characterizing organic compounds are mature.

For a gene therapy, CMC is harder. Much harder. Because you are not making a defined small molecule. You are making a viral particle, in a living cell culture, with all the variability that living systems introduce. Every batch of AAV is, at some level, a population of slightly different particles. Some have empty capsids — no DNA inside. Some have full capsids with the right DNA. Some have full capsids with the wrong DNA — fragments, host DNA contamination, weird recombinants. Some have aggregates. Some have post-translational modifications that vary from batch to batch. The job of CMC is to characterize all of this, quantify it, control it, and demonstrate that each batch meets specifications consistent enough to be a reliable drug.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you the analogy I want you to hold. **CMC is the unglamorous gating step — making your gene therapy at clinical scale, with documented purity and consistency, often takes longer than the biology.** I have known gene therapy programs where the science was completely done — the vector was designed, the preclinical work was complete, the clinical protocol was ready — and the entire program was held up for months or years because the manufacturing was not yet at the standard the agency required. The science said go. The manufacturing said wait.

The specific problems in gene therapy CMC are several. Let me walk through them.

First, **scale**. The doses required for gene therapy — particularly for systemic AAV — are enormous. A single Zolgensma dose contains on the order of ten to the fourteenth vector genomes. Manufacturing enough vector to dose a clinical trial cohort, much less a commercial population, requires bioreactors and downstream processing at substantial scale. The field has been chronically capacity-limited; for years, there were simply not enough GMP-compliant AAV manufacturing slots to keep up with demand.

Second, **characterization**. Demonstrating that your AAV product is what you say it is requires a battery of analytical methods — quantification of full versus empty capsids by analytical ultracentrifugation or charge-detection mass spectrometry, characterization of the packaged DNA by sequencing, measurement of potency in a cell-based assay, profiling of host cell protein and DNA contaminants, and so on. Each of these methods has to be validated to GMP standards. The validation packages themselves are enormous.

Third, **consistency**. The agency wants to see that batch-to-batch variability is small and well-controlled. You demonstrate this by making multiple batches over time and showing that the analytical results fall within specified ranges. Establishing those ranges, and producing the consistent batches, takes time.

Fourth, **GMP — Good Manufacturing Practice — compliance**. Every step of the manufacturing process has to be documented in detail, conducted in a controlled environment, and subject to formal change control. Facilities have to be designed to GMP standards. Personnel have to be trained. Quality systems have to be in place. Building or contracting for a GMP AAV manufacturing capability is, for many small biotech sponsors, the single largest capital and operational commitment in their entire development program.

\[Pause.\]

For some gene therapy programs, the manufacturing complexity has been more limiting than the biology. There have been clinical trials delayed for years waiting for manufacturing capacity. There have been programs that succeeded scientifically but failed commercially because they could not produce the drug at a cost that allowed for sustainable pricing. There have been BLAs that received complete response letters — the FDA's formal "we need more from you" — primarily because of CMC concerns rather than safety or efficacy concerns.

The field has been working on this. The growth of dedicated AAV contract manufacturing organizations — Catalent, Lonza, ThermoFisher, and others — has expanded the available capacity. New cell line technologies and production platforms are increasing yields. Analytical methods are maturing. But CMC remains a chronic bottleneck, and any gene therapy program that does not start serious investment in manufacturing at the same time as the biology will, in my observation, hit a wall later. **The manufacturing has to develop in parallel with the science. If it doesn't, your drug will be ready and you will have nothing to put in a syringe.**

## SEGMENT 17 — Expanded access and the N-of-1 path — the milasen lessons

In Segment 1 I promised this lecture would intersect with the milasen story — the N-of-1 antisense oligonucleotide drug developed for a single patient with a unique mutation — and now we have arrived at the segment where it does. Because the milasen case sits inside a regulatory framework called **expanded access**, sometimes called **compassionate use**, and this framework is one of the most important and underappreciated features of the modern FDA system.

The basic problem expanded access addresses is this. Suppose you have a patient with a life-threatening illness. There is a drug in development that might help them. The drug is not yet approved. The clinical trial that would eventually establish whether the drug works is enrolling, but the patient does not qualify, or the trial is full, or the patient cannot wait the years it will take for approval. What do you do?

The expanded access framework provides a path for that patient to receive the unapproved drug outside the clinical trial, under specific conditions, with FDA oversight. Three criteria generally apply. **First, the patient has a serious or immediately life-threatening illness.** **Second, no comparable approved alternative is available.** **Third, the potential benefit justifies the risk.** If those criteria are met, and if the manufacturer is willing to provide the drug, FDA can authorize expanded-access use.

\[Pause.\]

There are three types of expanded access in current FDA practice. **Individual patient access — for a single named patient.** **Intermediate-size patient population access — for a small cohort of patients.** **Treatment IND — for a larger population access in advance of approval.** The mechanics differ slightly across the three types, but the underlying principle is the same: provide a path for patients with serious diseases to access investigational drugs when no other option exists.

For milasen, the situation was even more unusual than standard expanded access. Mila Makovec had a completely unique mutation — a single SVA retrotransposon insertion in MFSD8 that had never been seen in any other patient. There was no clinical trial of milasen because milasen had never existed; it was designed from scratch specifically for her. The drug was conceived, manufactured, tested in animals, and submitted to FDA under an N-of-1 individual-patient IND in less than a year. The FDA cleared the IND in record time. Mila received her first dose. The drug was administered intrathecally, into her cerebrospinal fluid. The treatment stabilized parts of her clinical picture, including a substantial reduction in her seizures.

\[Pause.\]

The regulatory significance of milasen extends beyond the single patient it was made for. It established that the agency was willing to support, organizationally and regulatorily, the development of a drug for an individual. It established that the timeline from molecular diagnosis to clinical dosing could be radically compressed for the right kind of intervention — in this case, a splice-switching antisense oligonucleotide. And it raised, sharply and publicly, the question of what the right regulatory framework is for ultra-rare and N-of-1 medicines.

This question is now alive in the field. The agency has been developing thinking about **platform technology designations** that would allow a master IND covering an entire class of related drugs — for example, all ASOs of a certain chemistry, or all CRISPR therapeutics for a defined class of targets — rather than requiring a separate IND for each individual sequence. The idea is that the safety and pharmacology of the platform can be established once, and individual N-of-1 applications can ride on the platform with abbreviated regulatory submissions. The platform approach is gaining traction. It will, if it works, be transformative for the economics and timelines of N-of-1 medicine.

The milasen case is, in this sense, the proof of concept that the regulatory system can rise to meet the science. It is also a reminder of the limits of that system. As I mentioned in passing earlier — Mila Makovec died in 2021, at the age of ten. The drug slowed her disease but did not save her life. The agency's flexibility, the speed of the development, the brilliance of the molecular design — all of it produced an extraordinary medical achievement that nevertheless was not enough for this particular patient. The expanded access pathway gave her a chance she would not otherwise have had. That is what the pathway is for. It is not a guarantee of benefit. It is a regulatory mechanism for trying.

## SEGMENT 18 — Frontier — platforms, in vivo editing, EU and Chinese pathways, AI-driven trial design

Let me close with the frontier. Where is gene therapy regulation going? What are the developments that will shape the next decade of how these drugs get developed, reviewed, and approved?

I want to touch on five things. They are partially independent and partially interrelated, and they are all reshaping the field as we speak.

**First, platform technologies.** I just mentioned the platform technology designation in the context of milasen. The idea is that instead of treating each ASO sequence, each CRISPR guide, each CAR construct as a separate drug requiring a separate IND, you treat the platform — the chemistry, the delivery mechanism, the manufacturing process — as the regulated entity, and individual sequences become smaller variations within an established framework. The FDA has been developing thinking on this through a 2024 designation called the **Platform Technology Designation**, codified in the FDORA legislation. The expectation is that this designation will reduce the regulatory burden for N-of-1 and ultra-rare disease therapeutics by an order of magnitude, perhaps more. Whether it lives up to that promise depends on how the agency implements it in practice over the next several years, but the direction is clear.

\[Pause.\]

**Second, in vivo CRISPR and in vivo base editing.** The first wave of CRISPR therapeutics — Casgevy, Lyfgenia, others in development — are ex vivo. You take cells out of the patient, edit them in the lab, put them back in. The next wave is in vivo. You inject the editing machinery directly into the patient and let the editing happen inside their body. This is technically much harder, but the payoff is bigger — no need for stem cell collection, no need for conditioning chemotherapy, much broader applicability to diseases where you cannot collect and engraft the target cell type. Verve Therapeutics and others have shown promising early results with in vivo base editing of PCSK9 to lower cholesterol. Intellia has shown promising in vivo CRISPR editing of TTR for transthyretin amyloidosis. These programs are in the clinic now. The regulatory framework for in vivo editing — what kind of biodistribution data does the agency want, what kind of off-target characterization, what kind of long-term follow-up — is being defined as they progress. The next decade of gene therapy regulation will be substantially shaped by these in vivo programs.

**Third, the EU CAT and PRIME pathway.** I have mentioned the European framework in passing throughout this lecture. The EMA's Committee for Advanced Therapies — CAT — and the broader EU regulatory framework for advanced therapy medicinal products have been quietly evolving. The PRIME designation, the adaptive licensing approaches, the hospital exemption that allows individual hospitals to manufacture customized ATMPs for individual patients without full marketing authorization — all of these are EU-specific mechanisms that, in some cases, are more flexible than the corresponding US pathways. For sponsors developing globally, the strategy increasingly involves engaging with both FDA and EMA in parallel, leveraging the different flexibilities of each system, and timing approvals to maximize market entry.

\[Pause.\]

**Fourth, the Chinese NMPA pathway.** The Chinese National Medical Products Administration — NMPA — has been rapidly building its capacity for cell and gene therapy review. China has substantial domestic biotech investment in this space, a regulatory framework that has been modernizing fast, and a patient population that is in some categories larger than any other single jurisdiction. NMPA approvals of cell and gene therapies are accumulating, and the regulatory standards are converging — slowly, with continuing differences — toward those of FDA and EMA. For a global gene therapy program, the question is no longer "FDA or EMA first." It is "FDA, EMA, and NMPA in what order, with what local trial requirements, with what local manufacturing footprint." The three-jurisdiction strategy is the modern reality.

**Fifth, AI-driven trial design.** The use of artificial intelligence and machine learning in clinical trial design, patient stratification, and regulatory analytics is exploding. Agencies are exploring how to incorporate AI-derived evidence into their decision-making. Sponsors are using AI to design more efficient trials, to identify which patients are most likely to respond, to predict adverse events, to mine real-world evidence at scale. The regulatory framework for AI in drug development is, frankly, still being written. The FDA has issued draft guidance, but the field is moving faster than the guidance can. Over the next decade, expect to see AI play a substantially larger role in how gene therapy trials are designed and reviewed, and expect the agency to develop new frameworks for evaluating AI-derived evidence.

\[Pause.\]

So that is the frontier. Platform designations that compress the regulatory burden for ultra-rare therapeutics. In vivo editing that expands the addressable disease space. EU and Chinese pathways that complement the US framework. AI that reshapes trial design and analytical evidence. Each of these is reshaping the field. Each of them is creating new regulatory questions that the agencies — FDA, EMA, CAT, NMPA — are scrambling to address.

If I close with a single observation, it is this. The regulatory system for gene therapy is, today, more flexible, more sophisticated, and more open to scientific innovation than it has ever been in the history of medicine. The agencies have built mechanisms for accelerated development, for accommodating small patient populations, for handling unique mutations, for supporting platform technologies. The mechanisms are imperfect. The system is still slow and expensive. But it is not the rigid, formulaic obstacle that the popular caricature sometimes makes it out to be. It is, in the best moments, a partner in figuring out how to bring new therapies to patients responsibly. That partnership has produced Luxturna, Zolgensma, Hemgenix, Casgevy, Lyfgenia, and milasen — and the partnership will produce, over the next decade, hundreds more.

The science gets the credit when a new gene therapy is approved. The regulatory system mostly stays invisible. But the invisibility is, in some sense, the system working as intended. You only notice the regulator when something is wrong. When the system works — when a sponsor and an agency together find a path from a molecular insight to a patient receiving a transformative one-time treatment — the work of the regulator disappears into the background, and the science takes the stage.

That is, I think, the right framing. Not the regulator as obstacle. The regulator as co-architect. The gauntlet as a feature, not a bug. The system that produces the discipline that produces the medicines that change lives.

\[Pause.\]

Thank you for staying with me through two hours of regulatory detail. The path from concept to approved gene therapy is long, expensive, uncertain, and gated at every step. The path also works. Patients are alive today because of gene therapies that, twenty years ago, did not exist. The framework we have walked through today is what made that possible.

I will take questions for the next fifteen minutes. Then I will be in my office for the rest of the afternoon if you want to come by and discuss any specific program or any specific regulatory mechanism in more detail. Good luck on your problem sets.

**Pipeline summary, one line**

Concept → Preclinical → Pre-IND meeting (Type B) → IND filing → 30-day clock → Phase 1 (often Phase 1/2 combined for gene therapy) → Phase 2 → Phase 3 → BLA filing → FDA review (with possible advisory committee) → Approval (licensure) → Post-market commitments (registry, long-term follow-up up to 15 years for AAV).

**Accelerated pathway summary, one line each**

- **Fast Track** — serious condition with unmet need; rolling review and more frequent communication.
- **Breakthrough Therapy** — preliminary clinical evidence of substantial improvement; intensive FDA engagement.
- **RMAT** — regenerative medicine for serious condition; flexibility on endpoints and post-approval studies; designed for cell and gene therapy.
- **Priority Review** — review-phase shortening from 10 months to 6 months; granted at the BLA filing stage for products that would significantly improve safety or effectiveness.

**Approved AAV and CRISPR gene therapies referenced in this lecture**

- **Luxturna (voretigene neparvovec)** — AAV2 — RPE65 LCA blindness — FDA approval December 2017 — first US AAV approval.
- **Zolgensma (onasemnogene abeparvovec)** — AAV9 — SMA — FDA approval May 2019 — $2.1M list price.
- **Hemgenix (etranacogene dezaparvovec)** — AAV5 — hemophilia B — FDA approval November 2022 — $3.5M list price.
- **Casgevy (exagamglogene autotemcel)** — CRISPR ex vivo BCL11A — sickle cell and beta-thalassemia — FDA approval December 2023 — first CRISPR drug.
- **Lyfgenia (lovotibeglogene autotemcel)** — lentivirus ex vivo — sickle cell — FDA approval December 2023.

**Three takeaways, in plain English**

1. Gene therapy goes through a regulated pipeline — concept, preclinical, IND, trials, BLA, approval, post-market — and each gate matters. About 90 percent of drugs that enter Phase 1 don't reach approval.
2. The FDA, through CBER and OTP, has built specific accommodations for gene therapy: RMAT designation, flexible trial design, intensive guidance, and post-market registries that capture long-term outcomes.
3. The major approved gene therapies — Luxturna, Zolgensma, Hemgenix, Casgevy, Lyfgenia — each established a regulatory precedent that subsequent programs build on. The system is learning, and the next decade will see platform technologies, in vivo editing, and AI-assisted trial design reshape the landscape further.
