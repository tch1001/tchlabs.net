## SEGMENT 1 — What an organoid is, and why you should care

Alright, settle in. Today, for the next couple of hours, we're going to talk about one of the most quietly astonishing technologies to come out of biology in the last twenty years. I say "quietly" because organoids didn't get the kind of public fanfare that CRISPR or mRNA vaccines did — there's no Nobel announcement that everyone tweeted about, no Time magazine cover. And yet, if you walk into any modern biology department, any cancer center, any developmental biology lab, any pharma company doing serious early-stage drug discovery, you will find people with little plastic dishes full of these things. Tiny clumps of tissue, sitting in a clear gel, that have grown themselves into miniature versions of real organs. Mini-guts. Mini-livers. Mini-brains. Mini-kidneys. Patient-specific tumor avatars, growing in dishes, being treated with experimental drugs before those drugs are ever given to the patient. This is real, this is happening now, and by the end of these two hours I want you to understand both what organoids are and why their existence is changing how biology gets done.

If you walk out of here today and remember nothing else, I want you to remember this single sentence. **A stem cell, placed in the right gel with the right cocktail of signals, will self-organize into a miniature simulacrum of a real organ — with the right cell types, in roughly the right positions, often with a hollow lumen, often with proper apical-basal polarity, and often capable of doing some of the things the real organ does.** That's an organoid. You don't sculpt it. You don't direct each cell with tweezers. You don't print it. You give the cells the right environment and the right molecular cues, and they build the thing themselves. The architecture is encoded in the cells. We just turn the cells loose and let them do what evolution taught them to do.

\[Pause.\]

Let me unpack that sentence, because every word in it does real work.

**Three-dimensional.** This is the first crucial distinction. For a hundred years, when biologists wanted to study cells in dishes, they grew them as flat monolayers — cells stuck to the bottom of a plastic dish, splayed out, sitting in a thin film of liquid medium. Most of what we know about cell biology comes from this flat-cell paradigm. But cells in your body are not flat. They are surrounded on all sides by neighbors, embedded in a three-dimensional protein scaffolding, talking to other cells in every direction. Organoids grow in three dimensions. They sit suspended in a clear gel rather than stuck to plastic, and they form spheres, cups, branches, ribbons — actual shapes — rather than flat sheets. That dimensionality alone changes the biology dramatically. Genes turn on that don't turn on in monolayer culture. Cells acquire polarity they never had on plastic. Architecture emerges that flat dishes simply cannot support.

**Self-organizing.** This is the magical word. The cells are not being told where to go. There's no scaffold pre-shaped like a gut crypt that the cells just fill in. There's no 3D printer building the structure. The cells, given the right starting conditions, work it out themselves. One cell becomes a stem cell at the bottom of a depression. Its daughters migrate upward. Some of those daughters become absorptive cells, some become secretory cells, some become hormone-producing cells. They arrange themselves into a crypt-villus axis, or a cortical layer structure, or a branching tree of tubules, depending on the organ. The blueprint, in some sense, is already in the cells. We just provide the substrate and the signals; the cells provide the architecture.

**Multiple cell types of the target organ.** This is not a clump of one cell type. A real gut has absorptive enterocytes, goblet cells that make mucus, Paneth cells that make antimicrobials, enteroendocrine cells that make hormones, and Lgr5-positive stem cells at the base of each crypt. A real intestinal organoid contains all of those cell types, in the right proportions, in roughly the right positions. A cerebral organoid contains forebrain progenitors, radial glia, cortical neurons of different layer identities, intermediate progenitors, even early astrocytes. The organoid recapitulates the diversity of the real organ, not just a single cell lineage.

**Organ-like architecture.** This is where it stops feeling like a culture trick and starts feeling like developmental biology. An intestinal organoid has crypt-like depressions and villus-like protrusions. A kidney organoid has nephron-like tubules and glomerulus-like blobs. A retinal organoid forms a cup with a layered structure that mirrors the layers of a real retina, including photoreceptors at the outer edge. The organoid doesn't just have the cells; it has the spatial relationships among them that make those cells functional.

\[Pause.\]

Now, the obvious question. **Why?** Why does any of this matter? Why have we spent the last fifteen years figuring out how to grow these things?

Three answers. First: organoids are the closest we've ever come to studying human organs at the laboratory bench. Until organoids, if you wanted to understand human biology, you had three options. You could study cells in flat dishes — easy but unrealistic. You could study animals — useful but the biology often doesn't translate, because mouse livers and human livers and mouse brains and human brains differ in important ways. Or you could study patient tissue directly — gold standard but ethically and practically constrained, you can't experiment on living human organs. Organoids are a fourth option. They are human tissue, grown from human cells, that you can experiment on freely. They occupy a sweet spot between "too simple to mean anything" and "too real to touch."

Second: organoids enable a kind of personalized medicine that wasn't possible before. Take a piece of a cancer patient's tumor. Grow it as a tumor organoid in a dish. Test a hundred candidate drugs in parallel on that organoid. The drugs that shrink the dish are, with high probability, the drugs that will shrink the patient. This is being done right now, in working clinical trials, in real hospitals. Patients are getting better treatment decisions because someone grew a miniature version of their cancer in a dish first.

Third: organoids give us a way to ask questions about human development that we cannot ask in any other system. How does a cortical layer form? When in development does a particular neuron type get specified? What happens to a developing brain if it's infected by Zika virus, or by SARS-CoV-2, or exposed to a candidate drug? You cannot answer these questions in human embryos for obvious ethical reasons. You can answer them in cerebral organoids, which recapitulate enough of human brain development to be informative, while remaining a piece of tissue in a dish rather than a person.

\[Pause.\]

So here's our trajectory for today. We'll start with the seminal experiment — Hans Clevers and Toshiro Sato in 2009, the intestinal-organoid paper that essentially launched the field. We'll then walk through the basic recipe — what ingredients you need to grow any organoid. Then we'll tour the major organ systems, one by one: intestine, liver, kidney, retina, brain. We'll talk about what organoids can do and what they can't. We'll get into personalized medicine with tumor organoids. We'll discuss the ethical questions that brain organoids raise — questions that are uncomfortable but unavoidable. And we'll end on the frontier, including the still-unsolved problem of getting blood vessels into these things so they can grow larger than a millimeter or two.

By the end, you should be able to explain to a friend at dinner what an organoid is, why it's useful, and why the existence of mini-brains in dishes is both an extraordinary scientific achievement and a thing we should think carefully about. Let's begin.

## SEGMENT 2 — The Clevers and Sato breakthrough: a stem cell in Matrigel becomes a gut

The year is 2009. The lab is Hans Clevers' at the Hubrecht Institute in Utrecht, Netherlands. The postdoc is a young Japanese researcher named Toshiro Sato. And the experiment they're about to publish in _Nature_ is one of those quiet bombshells that, in retrospect, you can point to and say: that's where the field began.

To understand why what they did was such a leap, you have to understand what people had been trying to do with intestinal stem cells for the previous several decades. The intestine is one of the most rapidly self-renewing tissues in the body. The lining of your gut — the epithelial layer that absorbs nutrients and produces mucus — completely replaces itself every four or five days. Every four to five days, a whole new lining. That's an enormous amount of cell turnover, and it's driven by stem cells living at the base of little pit-like structures called crypts of Lieberkühn. Each crypt has a handful of stem cells at its base, and those stem cells produce daughters that migrate up the crypt walls, differentiate as they go, and eventually populate the finger-like protrusions — the villi — that stick up into the lumen of the gut.

For decades, researchers had wanted to isolate those intestinal stem cells and grow them in a dish. They had tried, repeatedly, and they had failed. You could get the cells out. You could put them on plastic. They would die. Or they would survive but stop being stem cells. Or they would survive as stem cells but refuse to differentiate into anything else. Nobody could find the right conditions to keep them happy and productive at the same time.

The first piece of the puzzle came in 2007. Clevers' lab identified a specific marker — a gene called **Lgr5** — that was expressed on the intestinal stem cells and nowhere else in the crypt. Using mouse genetics, they showed that Lgr5-positive cells, and only Lgr5-positive cells, could generate all the other cell types in the gut lining. They were the true stem cells. The Lgr5 marker became a flashlight you could shine on a sample of gut tissue and see, lighting up at the bases of crypts, exactly the cells you wanted to grow.

\[Pause.\]

Two years later, Sato cracked the culture problem. The recipe that worked, after a lot of trial and error, had four critical ingredients beyond the cells themselves.

**Ingredient one: Matrigel.** Matrigel is a commercially produced extract from a particular mouse tumor cell line that secretes huge amounts of basement membrane proteins — laminin, collagen IV, entactin, and a stew of growth factors. At low temperatures it's a liquid. At body temperature it gels into a clear, soft, three-dimensional protein scaffold. Think of Matrigel as a synthetic version of the protein matrix that, in the body, sits just underneath every epithelium and provides the substrate that the cells crawl on and attach to. If you put a stem cell directly on plastic, it has no idea where it is — plastic looks nothing like the inside of an animal. If you put it in Matrigel, suddenly the cell is sitting in something that, biochemically, resembles the basement membrane of a real organ. The cell can polarize. It can orient itself. It can start to know which way is up.

**Ingredient two: Wnt signal.** Wnt is one of the most ancient and important developmental signaling pathways. In the gut, Wnt is what keeps stem cells stem-like — without Wnt signaling, intestinal stem cells differentiate and disappear. So Sato's recipe added a Wnt agonist to the medium. Without it, no stem cell maintenance. With it, stem cells stay stem cells.

**Ingredient three: R-spondin.** R-spondin is a protein that potentiates Wnt signaling — it binds to Lgr5 (the very receptor that defines the stem cells) and amplifies the Wnt signal even further. Without R-spondin, the Wnt signal isn't strong enough to keep stem cells robust. With it, the stem cells thrive.

**Ingredient four: Noggin.** Noggin is an inhibitor of a different pathway called BMP — bone morphogenetic protein — which would otherwise push the cells toward differentiation. Blocking BMP with Noggin keeps the cells in a more progenitor-like state.

And one more: **EGF**, epidermal growth factor, which promotes proliferation. That's the basic "ENR plus EGF" cocktail — EGF, Noggin, R-spondin, plus Wnt — that became the canonical recipe for intestinal organoids and a template for every organoid recipe to come.

\[Pause.\]

So here's what Sato did. He isolated single Lgr5-positive cells from mouse intestinal crypts. He embedded them in a drop of Matrigel. He bathed the gel in medium containing EGF, Noggin, R-spondin, and a Wnt source. And he waited.

What happened, over the course of a few days, was extraordinary. The single stem cell didn't just divide. It organized. It produced daughters of multiple lineages — absorptive cells, goblet cells, Paneth cells, enteroendocrine cells, and more stem cells like itself. Those cells arranged themselves into a structure with a central hollow lumen surrounded by epithelium, and from the epithelium budded out little crypt-like extensions, each with stem cells at its base and differentiated cells migrating outward. The whole thing was a few hundred microns across, and it looked, under a microscope, like a tiny twisted-up version of a real intestinal lining. Crypts and villus-like domains. Differentiated cell types in the right places. A lumen filled with mucus and dead cells being shed exactly as they would be in a real gut.

The 2009 _Nature_ paper that reported this — title: "Single Lgr5 stem cells build crypt-villus structures in vitro without a mesenchymal niche" — is one of those papers that, when you read it now, you can feel the moment biology changed. They had grown a piece of organ from a single cell. Not a stem cell sphere, not a clump of cells, but an organized, multi-cell-type, architecturally correct piece of intestinal tissue. And it could be passaged indefinitely. You could break up the organoid, take out a few cells, replate them in fresh Matrigel, and they would grow new organoids. You had a system that, in principle, could give you unlimited human gut tissue from a single biopsy.

\[Pause.\]

The conceptual leap embedded in that experiment, the thing that mattered beyond just the practical recipe, was this: **the architectural program is in the cells, not in the surrounding tissue.** The orthodoxy before 2009 had been that you needed all the supporting tissue — the mesenchyme, the muscle layers, the immune cells, the blood vessels — to build a gut. Sato showed that you don't. The epithelium can build itself, in the right gel with the right signals, without any of the rest of the body present. The Lgr5 stem cell is, on its own, sufficient to generate the entire epithelial architecture. The instructions for the organ are in the stem cell.

That's the founding insight of the organoid field. Self-organization is real. Cells, given a permissive environment and the right cues, will build organ structures on their own. We don't have to figure out how to assemble organs from parts. We just have to figure out the right conditions, and let the cells do the assembly.

In the years following, every other organoid system has come from variations on this insight. Different tissue source, different gel, different cocktail of growth factors — but the same core idea. Get the conditions right, the cells will do the rest.

## SEGMENT 3 — The basic recipe: stem cells, gel, cocktail, time

Let me pause for a moment and walk through, in slightly more concrete terms, what's actually in an organoid dish. Because this is the kind of thing where it's easy to talk in abstractions, and I want you to have a physical mental picture of what's happening on the bench.

You start with **stem cells**. The source matters, because different organoids use different starting points. For intestinal organoids and most other adult-tissue organoids, you use **adult tissue stem cells** — cells dissociated from a piece of biopsy or surgical specimen, with the stem cells of interest enriched either by sorting for a surface marker (like Lgr5) or just by trusting that the conditions will favor stem cell outgrowth. For cerebral organoids and a lot of the developmental work, you use **pluripotent stem cells** — embryonic stem cells (ES cells) or, more commonly today, induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) generated by reprogramming someone's skin or blood cells back to a pluripotent state. The choice of cell source determines a lot about what the organoid can be. Adult stem cells give you the adult tissue. Pluripotent cells let you start from scratch and push the system down a particular developmental lineage.

You take those cells — usually as a single-cell suspension, or as small clumps — and you mix them with **Matrigel** that's still in its liquid state, on ice. You pipette droplets of this cold mixture onto a culture dish. As the gel warms to body temperature, it solidifies, encapsulating the cells in three-dimensional protein scaffolding. You now have islands of cells suspended in jelly. Then you flood the dish with **culture medium** containing your cocktail of growth factors and small molecules, and you put the whole thing in a 37-degree incubator at 5% carbon dioxide. That's it. That's the setup.

\[Pause.\]

The Matrigel piece deserves a closer look, because it's the unsung hero of most organoid protocols. Matrigel comes from a tumor called the Engelbreth-Holm-Swarm sarcoma, which secretes an unusual amount of basement membrane material. When you process the tumor, you get a soup of laminin, collagen IV, entactin, perlecan, and a bunch of bound growth factors — exactly the kind of protein cocktail that, in a real animal, sits underneath every epithelium and provides the substrate that those epithelial cells adhere to. Matrigel is, in effect, the **scaffolding the cells climb on** — a soup of basement-membrane proteins that gives them a three-dimensional structure to build around.

The reason Matrigel works so well is that real cells in real tissues use these very proteins as their attachment substrate. The integrin receptors on the cell surface have evolved to grab laminin and collagen IV. When you put a cell in Matrigel, those receptors find their natural ligands, and the cell experiences the chemical environment it would experience in vivo. It can polarize properly — meaning, develop a "top" (the apical surface, facing a lumen) and a "bottom" (the basal surface, attached to the basement membrane). Polarity is the prerequisite for all epithelial organization. Without polarity, you have a clump. With polarity, you have the makings of an epithelium.

Now, Matrigel has problems. It's mouse-derived, so it's lot-to-lot variable and not appropriate for clinical applications. The growth factor content varies. It's a defined-but-not-fully-defined system. A lot of the recent engineering effort in the field has gone into developing synthetic alternatives — chemically defined hydrogels made from synthetic peptides or modified polysaccharides that mimic the relevant features of Matrigel without its variability. Those alternatives are improving but aren't yet as universally good as Matrigel itself. So for now, Matrigel remains the workhorse.

\[Pause.\]

The growth factor cocktail is the other half of the recipe, and it's where most of the variation between organoid types lies. Think of the cocktail as **the conductor's score** — each growth factor tells the cells what to become, when, and in what order. Wnt tells cells to stay stem-like. R-spondin amplifies that signal in cells expressing Lgr5. Noggin blocks BMP and prevents premature differentiation. EGF promotes proliferation. FGF, in various flavors, is used in lung and liver and kidney organoids to drive different aspects of organ development. SHH — sonic hedgehog — is used in cerebral organoids to ventralize the tissue. RA — retinoic acid — is used to specify anterior-posterior axis identity. The list is long, and every organ has its own cocktail.

What's important to understand is that this cocktail is not random. It's reverse-engineered from developmental biology. We know, from decades of work in mice and frogs and zebrafish, which signals the embryo uses to specify each organ. The organoid cocktail is, essentially, a synthetic recapitulation of those embryonic signals. The cells in the dish are being told, through the soluble factors in the medium, "you're in the intestine now" or "you're in the developing forebrain now" or "you're in the early liver bud now." And the cells, recognizing these cues from their evolutionary playbook, do what they would have done in that location in a real embryo.

This is why I sometimes describe organoids as **developmental biology in a dish**. We're not just culturing cells; we're rerunning a portion of embryonic development, in vitro, with the embryo's own signals reconstituted from purified proteins. The cells don't know they're in a dish. They think they're in an embryo. And they do what cells in an embryo do.

\[Pause.\]

A few more practical notes. The medium is typically **serum-free** — most organoid systems have moved away from fetal calf serum, which is variable and contains uncharacterized factors, toward fully defined media supplemented with the specific growth factors needed. This matters because reproducibility is hard enough without random batch-to-batch variation in serum.

Organoids are typically **passaged** every one to two weeks — meaning, you take the established organoids, break them up mechanically or with enzymes into smaller fragments or single cells, and replate those in fresh Matrigel. Many organoid lines can be passaged for months or years, accumulating few mutations and maintaining their tissue identity. Some — particularly tumor organoids — are essentially immortal lines that can be passaged indefinitely.

And they can be **frozen**. You can take a successful organoid line, dissociate it, freeze it down in liquid nitrogen, and thaw it years later. This is what makes biobanks possible — we'll talk about the Hubrecht's enormous tumor organoid biobank in a later segment — and it's what makes organoids practical as a research resource rather than a precious one-off.

That's the basic recipe. Now let's see what people have grown with it.

## SEGMENT 4 — Intestinal organoids and the rise of personalized cancer medicine

The intestinal organoid was the original, and it remains, in many ways, the most useful and most extensively developed of all organoid systems. Let me give you a sense of how the field has built on Sato and Clevers' 2009 paper over the subsequent decade and a half.

The first big extension was to **human tissue**. The original 2009 paper used mouse intestinal cells. By 2011, the same group had adapted the protocol to grow organoids from human gut biopsies. The recipe needed tweaking — more Wnt, slightly different growth factors — but the basic principle held. You could take a tiny pinch of tissue from a colonoscopy, dissociate it, drop it in Matrigel with the right cocktail, and within a few weeks you'd have human intestinal organoids growing in your dish, derived from that specific patient.

This opened up a whole new mode of clinical research. You could now study, in dishes, the gut biology of specific people. You could compare organoids from healthy donors to organoids from patients with inflammatory bowel disease. You could derive organoids from patients with cystic fibrosis — whose gut epithelia carry the disease-causing mutation in the CFTR chloride channel — and use those organoids to test which CFTR-modulator drugs would actually work on that patient's particular CFTR variant. There's a beautiful little assay called the **forskolin swelling assay**: you treat the organoid with forskolin, which activates CFTR, which pumps chloride into the lumen, which draws in water by osmosis, which causes the organoid to swell up like a balloon if the channel is working. If you treat a cystic fibrosis patient's organoid with a candidate drug and then with forskolin, the amount of swelling tells you whether the drug rescued that patient's CFTR function. In the Netherlands, organoid-based forskolin swelling assays are now used to direct cystic fibrosis treatment decisions for patients with rare CFTR mutations that the standard clinical trials never included.

\[Pause.\]

But the real takeoff, the application that brought organoids into the clinical mainstream, was **tumor organoids**.

Here's the idea. Cancer is, at its heart, a disease of bad cell behavior. Tumor cells grow when they shouldn't, ignore signals to die, invade tissues they shouldn't be in. For decades, we've tried to develop drugs that fix this — and we've had some success — but cancer is heterogeneous. Two patients with what looks like the same cancer can respond very differently to the same drug. The reason is that, genetically and biochemically, their tumors are different. One patient's lung cancer has mutations in EGFR; another's has mutations in KRAS; another's has none of those but some other driver entirely. The right drug for one patient may be useless for another.

What if you could test each patient's tumor, individually, in a dish, before deciding what drug to give them?

This is what tumor organoids enable. The recipe is essentially the Sato and Clevers protocol with some modifications: you take a piece of a patient's tumor — from surgery, or from a needle biopsy — dissociate the tumor cells, embed them in Matrigel, and feed them a cocktail of growth factors. Tumor cells, it turns out, are perfectly happy to form organoids. In fact, because they're already dysregulated for proliferation, they often grow more easily than their normal counterparts. Within a few weeks, you have organoids that carry the genetic alterations of the original tumor, that grow at roughly the same rate as the original tumor, and that respond to drugs in a way that, in most cases, predicts how the original tumor would respond.

The Marc van de Wetering and Hans Clevers group at the Hubrecht has been at the forefront of this work. They've built a biobank of patient-derived colorectal cancer organoids — hundreds of different patient tumors, all preserved, all characterized, all available for drug screening. They've shown that you can take a patient's tumor organoids, screen them against a panel of 80 or so different cancer drugs, and identify which drugs kill that patient's organoids best. And in clinical trials, those organoid-screening results are starting to predict patient outcomes.

\[Pause.\]

Picture what this means practically. **Take a piece of your tumor. Grow it as an organoid. Test a hundred drugs in parallel. Treat you with whichever one shrinks the dish.** That's the dream of personalized cancer medicine in one sentence, and tumor organoids make it possible. Not yet for every cancer — some tumors don't grow well as organoids, some patients can't wait the few weeks it takes to establish a culture, some drug responses don't translate cleanly — but the proof of concept is there, and the technology is getting faster and more predictive every year.

There are now organoid biobanks for colorectal cancer, breast cancer, pancreatic cancer, prostate cancer, lung cancer, liver cancer, bladder cancer, and more. Each biobank is a repository of patient-derived tumors that are, essentially, replayable. Researchers who want to study why a particular drug works in some patients but not others can take the existing biobank, find tumors that responded and tumors that didn't, and ask what's different about them. The biobank is, in effect, **a library of patient avatars** — each one a tiny biological replica of a real person's cancer, available for experimentation in ways the real patient never could be.

The Hubrecht's biobank alone now contains organoids from thousands of patients across many cancer types. It's a public resource — academic labs can request access for research — and it's already enabled studies that would have been impossible by any other means. You can ask: of all the breast cancers in the biobank, which ones respond to drug X? And then look at the genetics of those tumors and try to find the signature that predicts response. That signature, validated, becomes a clinical biomarker.

\[Pause.\]

There's a lovely conceptual point hidden in the tumor organoid story. Cancer organoids work because cancer is, in some ways, a hijacking of normal stem cell biology. Many tumors contain a population of cells with stem-like properties — they self-renew, they generate the bulk tumor cells, they resist treatment. Those cancer stem cells respond to the same signaling pathways — Wnt, R-spondin, EGF, the rest — that intestinal stem cells respond to. So the very cocktail Sato developed for normal intestinal organoids works, often with minor modifications, for intestinal tumor organoids. Cancer didn't invent new biology; it corrupted existing biology. And the existing biology, once we understand it, gives us the tools to study cancer in dishes.

The bigger lesson: once you have the right culture system for a tissue, you often get the cancer of that tissue as a bonus. Liver organoids led naturally to liver cancer organoids. Pancreatic organoids led to pancreatic cancer organoids. The technology generalizes, and so the personalized-medicine approach generalizes too.

We'll come back to this in a later segment when we talk about the future of clinical organoid use. For now, let's move on to the next major organ system that surrendered to the organoid approach.

## SEGMENT 5 — Liver organoids: Takebe's organ buds and Clevers' hepatocytes

The liver was an obvious next target after the intestine. Liver disease is enormously common — hepatitis, fatty liver, cirrhosis, liver cancer all kill millions of people every year — and the liver, like the gut, is a relatively self-renewing organ with identifiable stem and progenitor cells. If you could grow human liver tissue in a dish, you could study liver disease, screen drugs for liver toxicity (which is one of the leading causes of drug failure in development), and maybe even produce hepatocytes for cell-replacement therapy.

Two parallel approaches emerged in the early 2010s.

The first approach, from the Clevers group, was the direct extension of the intestinal protocol. They isolated cells from adult human liver biopsies — specifically, ductal cells that express Lgr5 and similar markers — and showed that with an appropriate cocktail (Wnt, R-spondin, EGF, FGF, plus a couple of additional factors), these cells could form organoids that, depending on the conditions, could differentiate into hepatocyte-like cells, the major functional cell type of the liver. These liver organoids could perform classical hepatocyte functions: they produced albumin, they metabolized drugs via cytochrome P450 enzymes, they stored glycogen. They weren't quite as functional as primary human hepatocytes, but they were close, and crucially, they could be grown from a small biopsy and expanded over weeks to provide a lot of material.

The second approach, which made the bigger immediate splash, came from a young Japanese researcher named **Takanori Takebe**, working at Yokohama City University. Takebe's 2013 paper in _Nature_ — titled "Vascularized and functional human liver from an iPSC-derived organ bud transplant" — took a different approach. Rather than starting from adult liver stem cells, Takebe used pluripotent stem cells differentiated into early hepatic endoderm — basically, the cell type from which liver buds first emerge during embryonic development. Then he did something clever: he mixed those hepatic endoderm cells with two other cell types — endothelial cells (which form blood vessels) and mesenchymal stem cells (which provide supporting tissue). And he didn't put them in Matrigel. He just plated them on a soft surface and let them aggregate.

What happened was extraordinary. The three cell types self-organized into a structure that looked, under the microscope, exactly like an embryonic liver bud — the structure that, in a normal embryo, forms when liver tissue first emerges from the foregut endoderm during the third week of development. The hepatic endoderm formed the body of the bud, the endothelial cells started laying down vascular channels through it, and the mesenchymal cells provided the surrounding support. It was a recapitulation, in a dish, of an early developmental event.

\[Pause.\]

Then Takebe did something even better. He transplanted these liver buds into mice — into the abdomen, where they had access to the host's blood supply. The transplanted buds matured. The endothelial cells in the buds connected to the host's blood vessels, forming functional perfusion. And the hepatocyte-like cells started producing human albumin, detectable in the mouse's blood, indicating that human liver tissue was functioning inside a mouse. Even better, when the mice were given a hepatotoxic injury, the transplanted human liver tissue protected them from dying. The mini-livers were doing real work.

The Takebe paper was important for two reasons. First, it showed that organoids could be transplanted and integrated into a host organ system, with proper vascularization. The vascularization problem — which we'll come back to — is the biggest single limitation of organoids, and Takebe's approach of co-culturing with endothelial cells and then transplanting into a host was a way to sidestep it. Second, it demonstrated that the **organ bud** stage of development was a powerful starting point. Rather than trying to grow a fully mature organoid in a dish, you could grow the early developmental precursor and then let it mature in vivo, after transplant, using the host's circulatory system as the maturation engine.

The Takebe approach has since been extended to other organs — pancreas, kidney, intestine — using similar three-cell-type co-cultures. The "organ bud" paradigm sits alongside the "epithelial organoid" paradigm as one of the two main approaches in the field today.

\[Pause.\]

Liver organoids have been used for some genuinely important work. They've been used to model alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, a genetic liver disease, by deriving organoids from patient iPSCs and showing the characteristic protein-trafficking defect. They've been used to model fatty liver disease by exposing organoids to high concentrations of fatty acids and watching them develop lipid droplets and inflammation. They've been used in drug screening — liver organoids are now part of many pharmaceutical companies' early hepatotoxicity panels, replacing some of the work that used to require primary human hepatocytes (which are scarce, expensive, and variable). And there are early efforts to use liver organoids for cell replacement therapy in patients with end-stage liver disease, though those efforts are still in the preclinical phase.

The frontier here is to get organoids that combine hepatocytes with the full complement of supporting cell types — endothelial cells, Kupffer cells (the liver's resident macrophages), hepatic stellate cells (which mediate fibrosis), and bile duct epithelium. Most current liver organoids are dominated by one or two cell types. Getting the full menagerie of liver cells, arranged in proper architecture, with vasculature, is still a work in progress. But it's progressing.

## SEGMENT 6 — Kidney organoids: Melissa Little, Morizane, and the problem of nephrons

The kidney is, mechanically, one of the more impressive feats of organoid engineering. The reason is that kidney architecture is intricate. A real human kidney contains about a million nephrons — the functional units of the kidney — and each nephron is a tube with multiple distinct segments: glomerulus, proximal tubule, loop of Henle, distal tubule, collecting duct. Each segment does different chemistry. Each segment has different cell types. And the whole thing is woven together with a vast blood supply, because the kidney's job is to filter blood. Building this in a dish, even in miniature, is a serious challenge.

Two groups, working in parallel, cracked the kidney organoid problem in the mid-2010s.

The first was **Melissa Little**'s group, originally in Brisbane, Australia, now in Melbourne. Little had been working on kidney development for years. In 2015, her group published a paper in _Nature_ showing that you could take human pluripotent stem cells through a carefully timed sequence of differentiation cues — first inducing mesoderm with Wnt activation, then specifying intermediate mesoderm (the embryonic precursor of the kidney) with FGF, then transitioning the cells into kidney developmental programs — and end up with organoids containing recognizable nephrons with proper segmentation. Glomerulus-like structures with podocytes. Proximal tubules with brush borders. Distal tubules. Collecting duct-like structures. The organoids weren't whole kidneys — they were collections of dozens to hundreds of nephrons in a clump, without a real ureter or pelvis to drain to — but they were nephron-like enough to be a genuine model of kidney tissue.

The second group, working roughly in parallel, was **Ryuji Morizane**'s, then in Joseph Bonventre's lab at Harvard. Morizane developed a slightly different protocol with somewhat different timing but achieved a similar result: pluripotent stem cell-derived organoids with structured nephrons. The two protocols differ in their details but converge on similar end products, and labs around the world now use one or both depending on their experimental needs.

\[Pause.\]

What can you do with a kidney organoid? Quite a bit, actually.

You can model genetic kidney diseases. **Polycystic kidney disease** — PKD — is a common inherited disorder where the kidneys develop fluid-filled cysts that gradually destroy kidney function. If you derive iPSCs from a PKD patient and make kidney organoids, the organoids develop cysts. You can watch the disease form, in a dish, in real time. You can test drugs that might block cyst formation. You can do gene editing on the iPSCs to correct the mutation and see if the corrected organoids stop forming cysts (they do). This is the kind of mechanistic experiment you can do in an organoid that you simply cannot do in a real kidney.

You can do **nephrotoxicity screening**. The kidney is one of the most common targets of drug toxicity, because it filters everything in your blood and concentrates it. Many drug candidates fail late in development because they turn out to be kidney-toxic. Kidney organoids let you screen for nephrotoxicity earlier, in human tissue, before committing to expensive animal studies and clinical trials.

You can study **acute kidney injury** — the kidney damage caused by ischemia, sepsis, or toxic insults. Organoids can be subjected to controlled injuries and you can watch the molecular and cellular responses, identify which pathways drive damage versus recovery, and look for therapeutic targets.

And there's longer-term interest in using kidney organoids for **transplantation** — ultimately growing replacement kidney tissue from a patient's own cells, to address the chronic shortage of donor kidneys. That dream is still far away, but pieces are being assembled. Vascularized kidney organoids transplanted into mice can integrate with host vasculature and develop more mature nephrons. Some groups are working on assembling larger kidney structures from multiple organoids fused together. The path is long but visible.

\[Pause.\]

I want to make a small philosophical point about the kidney work, because it generalizes. Kidney organoids are imperfect models. They lack the proper ureteric and collecting duct architecture in many cases. They lack mature vasculature. The cells in them are often immature compared to adult kidney cells, more resembling fetal kidney. You cannot, today, take a kidney organoid and use it as a stand-in for an adult human kidney in most contexts.

But you can use it as a model of **developing** human kidney. You can use it to ask questions that no other system lets you ask, because real human fetal kidneys are not available for experimentation. You can use it as a starting point that captures real human cell biology where mouse models don't. You can use it as a substrate for asking specific questions: how does this genetic mutation affect nephron formation? Which drug damages this particular tubule segment? What kinds of cells are present and what are they doing?

This is the right way to think about organoids in general. They are not replacements for real organs. They are partial recapitulations — models that capture some aspects of real biology, miss others, and are useful precisely insofar as you understand what they capture and what they don't. A good organoid researcher knows the limitations of their system as well as its strengths.

We'll come back to this when we discuss what organoids can't do. For now, on to the next organ.

## SEGMENT 7 — Retinal organoids and the Sasai story

The retina is one of the most beautiful organoid systems to look at under the microscope, and the history of how it came to exist is one of the more poignant in this field. Let me tell you about Yoshiki Sasai.

Sasai was a developmental biologist at the RIKEN institute in Kobe, Japan. He was, by all accounts, an extraordinary scientist — meticulous, creative, deeply intuitive about how embryonic tissues organize themselves. He had spent most of his career working on the development of the central nervous system in vertebrates, with a particular interest in how the brain and eye form from sheets of neuroectodermal cells in the early embryo. He understood, better than perhaps anyone of his generation, the signaling logic of how an eye gets built.

In 2011, Sasai's group published a paper in _Nature_ that has to be on any short list of the most striking organoid papers ever produced. The title was "Self-organizing optic-cup morphogenesis in three-dimensional culture." What they had done was take mouse embryonic stem cells, aggregate them into small floating clumps in a specific cocktail of factors, and watch what happened over the following weeks.

What happened was that the cell aggregates spontaneously evaginated — bulged outward — to form vesicles that resembled the optic vesicles that, in a real embryo, evaginate from the neural tube during week three or four of development. Then those vesicles **invaginated** — folded back inward — to form cup-shaped structures that resembled the optic cups of a real eye. Within the cups, the cells differentiated into a layered structure: outer pigmented epithelium on the outside, neural retina on the inside, with photoreceptor precursors at the outermost layer of the neural retina (which corresponds, anatomically, to the deepest layer of the in-vivo retina). The whole structure was built without any external scaffold, without any guiding mesh, without any sculpting. The cells did it themselves.

\[Pause.\]

To grasp how astonishing this is, you need to understand what's happening in a normal embryo when an eye forms. Optic cup morphogenesis is one of the more complex morphogenetic events in development. It requires precise temporal and spatial coordination of cell migration, proliferation, shape change, and tissue folding. It involves mechanical forces, signaling gradients, geometric constraints — the whole repertoire of developmental physics. To recapitulate it in a dish, with just stem cells and a defined medium, was almost magical. Sasai's group had shown that the entire morphogenetic program for building an eye was, in some sense, **encoded in the cells themselves**, and given the right initial conditions, the cells would execute that program without any external guidance.

A year later, in 2012, Sasai extended this work to human pluripotent stem cells. Same general approach, slightly modified protocol. The result: human retinal organoids — three-dimensional cups of human retinal tissue, with proper lamination, with photoreceptor cells (both rods and cones) appearing in the right layer at roughly the right developmental timing. You could look at these things under the microscope and see structures that you could only otherwise see in a human fetal eye, which is to say, structures most biologists never get to see at all.

\[Pause.\]

These retinal organoids have become a real workhorse in vision research. They've been used to model inherited retinal degenerations — diseases like retinitis pigmentosa and Leber congenital amaurosis — by deriving organoids from patient iPSCs and watching the photoreceptors die in the dish, mimicking what happens in the patient's actual retina over years. They've been used to test gene therapy approaches. They've been used to study the development of photoreceptor subtypes and to ask basic questions about how cone diversity is specified. They've even been used in transplant experiments, where small pieces of organoid retinal tissue have been grafted into the eyes of degeneration-model mice and shown to integrate and form connections.

The retinal organoid story is also notable because it was, in some ways, the first really clean demonstration that **structural self-organization** — not just cell-type specification, but actual tissue morphogenesis with shape changes and folding — could happen in vitro. Before Sasai's optic cup paper, most people thought you'd need external mechanical inputs, or scaffolds, or some kind of guided assembly, to get organ-like shapes. Sasai showed you didn't. Cells, given the right signals and the right boundary conditions, build the shapes themselves. That insight was foundational.

I want to acknowledge the tragic personal coda. In 2014, Yoshiki Sasai was caught up in a scandal involving a paper from a colleague's group on a phenomenon called STAP cells, which turned out to be fraudulent. Sasai was not directly involved in the falsified data, but he had been the senior co-author on the paper and the institutional fallout was severe. He took his own life in August of that year, at age 52. The field lost one of its great minds, and the story of organoid research carries that loss with it. The retinal organoid work, his most enduring contribution, continues to influence the field every day.

## SEGMENT 8 — Cerebral organoids: Lancaster, Knoblich, and the mini-brain

We come now to the organoid system that, more than any other, has captured the public imagination — and raised the deepest ethical questions. The cerebral organoid. The mini-brain.

The seminal paper is from 2013, from the lab of **Jürgen Knoblich** at the Institute of Molecular Biotechnology in Vienna. The first author was a postdoc named **Madeline Lancaster**, who has since become a major figure in the field in her own right. Their paper, in _Nature_, was titled "Cerebral organoids model human brain development and microcephaly." It introduced a protocol that, with refinements, is essentially the standard cerebral organoid method today.

The recipe is roughly this. You start with human pluripotent stem cells — embryonic stem cells or iPSCs. You aggregate them into small spheroids called embryoid bodies, in low-attachment plates so they don't stick to the bottom and stay rounded. You expose them to a defined sequence of media: first, conditions that push them toward neuroectoderm (the embryonic precursor of the central nervous system); then, conditions that favor expansion of neural progenitors. After about a week, you embed the aggregates in Matrigel droplets — this is the moment they have the substrate to start building three-dimensional architecture — and then transfer them to a **spinning bioreactor**, which keeps the medium swirling around the organoids and improves nutrient and oxygen delivery to the interior.

Over the next several weeks, something remarkable happens. The organoids grow, ballooning outward in irregular shapes. As they grow, they develop distinct regions — clusters of cells expressing markers of specific brain regions. Forebrain regions. Hindbrain regions. Some optic-cup-like structures. And critically, within the forebrain regions, the cells organize into structures that resemble the developing cortex: a layer of neural progenitors lining a fluid-filled cavity (resembling a ventricle), with newly born neurons migrating outward from that progenitor zone toward the outside of the organoid. The cells produce neurons of multiple cortical layer identities — early-born deep-layer neurons first, later-born superficial-layer neurons later, in the right order. They produce intermediate progenitor cells and early glia. After two months in culture, you can record electrical activity from individual neurons; after six months, you can see early forms of neural network activity.

\[Pause.\]

The Lancaster paper used cerebral organoids to model **microcephaly**, a developmental disorder in which the brain fails to grow to normal size. By deriving iPSCs from a patient with a genetic form of microcephaly (caused by mutations in a gene called CDK5RAP2), making cerebral organoids from those iPSCs, and comparing them to organoids from healthy donors, they showed that the patient-derived organoids were significantly smaller, with reduced progenitor expansion and premature neuronal differentiation. They were modeling, in a dish, the very developmental defect that produces small brains in living patients. That's a powerful demonstration: human brain development, in its earliest stages, being recapitulated and disrupted on a benchtop, with the disruption revealing the cellular mechanism of a disease.

In the years since, cerebral organoids have proliferated into many variants. Some are **unguided** — like Lancaster's original — and produce organoids with a mixture of brain regions in unpredictable proportions. Some are **guided** or **region-specific** — produced with cocktails that bias development toward, say, just cortical tissue, or just thalamic tissue, or just midbrain tissue. The Sergiu Pasca lab at Stanford has been a leader in developing region-specific organoids, including organoids of cortex, striatum, thalamus, cerebellum, midbrain, and spinal cord. By choosing which region to grow, researchers can ask precise questions about that region's biology in human cells.

\[Pause.\]

What cerebral organoids recapitulate is remarkable. **Cortical layers.** **Ventricle-like spaces.** **Progressive neurogenesis with the right developmental timing.** **Migration of newborn neurons toward the cortical plate.** **The emergence of specific neuronal subtypes.** **Functional electrical activity, including action potentials and, eventually, synaptic communication.** Late-stage organoids — kept in culture for many months — show early forms of network synchronization and oscillatory activity that resemble the kind of activity seen in fetal brain.

What they don't recapitulate is also notable. They don't have proper vasculature, so they max out at a few millimeters and the interior becomes hypoxic and necrotic. They don't have microglia, the brain's resident immune cells, unless you co-culture them in. They don't have the full repertoire of subcortical structures connected to cortex. They don't have the precise lamination of a mature cortex. And — this is important — they don't reach the developmental maturity of an adult brain. Most current cerebral organoids correspond to mid-fetal human brain at best, around 15 to 25 weeks of gestation. They are early. They are immature. They are, biologically, fetal tissue.

We'll talk about the ethical implications of cerebral organoids in Segment 13. For now, hold this thought: a piece of human brain tissue, with electrical activity, with cortical layers, with developmental programs running — in a dish, on a benchtop, accessible to experimentation in ways no living human brain ever could be. This is an unprecedented capability, and it forces unprecedented questions.

## SEGMENT 9 — What organoids can't do: vasculature, immune cells, maturity, size

Before we break for the hour mark, let me ground us in reality by enumerating what organoids cannot, today, do. Because this is a field with a lot of justified excitement, and that excitement can sometimes spill over into overstatement. Organoids are extraordinary research tools, but they are not whole organs in any meaningful sense, and there are a few specific limitations that constrain what they can be used for.

**Limitation one: no vasculature.** This is the big one, and it's worth dwelling on. In your body, every cell in every tissue is within about 100 to 200 microns of a blood vessel. Beyond that distance, cells can't get enough oxygen and nutrients by diffusion, and they die. Your body solves this by weaving capillaries throughout every tissue. Organoids, growing in a dish, have no circulatory system. They rely entirely on diffusion from the surrounding medium for oxygen, nutrients, and waste removal. As the organoid grows, the cells in the center get further and further from the surface, and at some point — typically around 2 millimeters in diameter — the interior starts dying from lack of oxygen. You get a necrotic core. That sets a hard size ceiling on how big organoids can grow in a static dish.

**Limitation two: no immune cells.** Organoids don't have the immune cell complement of real organs — no T cells, no B cells, usually no macrophages or microglia. This matters for many applications. You can't study the immune component of inflammatory disease in an organoid that lacks immune cells. You can't model the interactions between cancer and the immune system if your tumor organoid is purely tumor cells. You can't fully recapitulate infection biology if there are no immune cells responding to the pathogen. Some labs are now adding immune cells back in — co-culturing organoids with T cells or microglia — but this is a recent development and it's far from perfect.

**Limitation three: maturity.** Most organoids are developmentally immature. They tend to resemble fetal or early-postnatal tissue more than adult tissue. Cerebral organoids look like mid-trimester fetal brain. Liver organoids often have fetal-like hepatocyte gene expression. This is partly because organoid growth depends on stem and progenitor cells, which by their nature are less mature; and partly because the cells in vitro lack many of the maturation cues — hormonal, mechanical, neural — that drive postnatal tissue maturation in vivo. The result is that organoids are better models of developing organs than of adult organs. For studying adult-onset disease, this is a real limitation.

**Limitation four: variability.** Different organoids from the same culture can look quite different from each other. The unguided cerebral organoids from Lancaster's protocol, for example, have substantial organoid-to-organoid variability in what brain regions they contain and in what proportions. Some labs have shown that the variability between organoids derived from a single iPSC line can rival the variability between iPSC lines from different donors. This makes some experiments hard to interpret. You need to use lots of organoids and apply careful statistical thinking. The field is working on this — guided protocols, defined media, better quality control — but variability remains a challenge.

**Limitation five: incomplete cellular composition.** Even within their core lineage, organoids often lack some of the cell types of the real organ. Intestinal organoids often have weak Paneth cells. Cerebral organoids have limited representation of certain neuronal subtypes. Liver organoids often lack stellate cells and Kupffer cells. Getting the full menagerie of cell types in proper proportions is an ongoing challenge.

\[Pause.\]

The way to think about all of this is: **organoids are partial models, not full replicas.** They capture a meaningful subset of real organ biology, and the subset they capture is enormously useful, but they don't capture everything. The mistake to avoid is treating an organoid as if it were the same thing as a real organ. A drug that works on a kidney organoid might fail in a patient because the organoid lacked the immune component that drives drug clearance, or because the organoid was developmentally immature and didn't have the adult kidney's full metabolic profile. A neural circuit you observe in a cerebral organoid might not correspond to a circuit in a real brain because the organoid lacks the long-range connectivity of an intact nervous system.

The field is acutely aware of these limitations and is actively working on each of them. **Organoid-on-chip** approaches — which we'll talk about in detail in Segment 11 — try to address the vasculature problem with microfluidic perfusion. **Co-culture** approaches try to address the missing-cell-type problem by adding back immune cells or endothelial cells or stromal cells. **Maturation** approaches try to extend culture times and add maturation cues to push organoids closer to adult identity. **Assembloid** approaches — Segment 14 — fuse organoids of multiple types to recapitulate the connections between organ systems.

But for any application, you should always ask: what does this organoid model and what does it not model? And does the thing I care about depend on what's modeled, or on what's missing? If you're asking about epithelial cell biology, missing immune cells may not matter. If you're asking about inflammatory disease, it matters enormously. Match the model to the question.

\[Pause.\]

That's the appropriate place to take a breath and stop for our break.

## SEGMENT 10 — Organoid-on-chip: microfluidics and the vasculature problem

Alright, welcome back. We're going to spend the second half exploring some of the most exciting and most rapidly developing areas in the field. Let's start with the technology that's most directly addressing the vasculature problem.

I mentioned in Segment 9 that organoids can't grow larger than a couple of millimeters in static culture because the interior runs out of oxygen. The biological reason is that diffusion is too slow over distances larger than a couple hundred microns; living tissue, beyond that distance, needs active perfusion — fluid actually flowing through it — to deliver oxygen and nutrients.

The natural solution is to build the perfusion ourselves. This is the idea behind **organoid-on-chip** systems — also called organ-on-a-chip when used with simpler cell cultures, or microphysiological systems in the more general framing.

The basic concept is a small device, typically made of clear silicone polymer, perhaps the size of a USB stick, with tiny channels carved into it. Cells or organoids are seeded into chambers within the device, and culture medium is pumped through the channels at a controlled flow rate. The flow brings nutrients and oxygen to the cells and carries away waste, just as blood does in your body. The flow can also apply mechanical forces — shear stress on the cells lining the channels — which turns out to be biologically important for many cell types. Vascular endothelial cells, for example, behave differently when they experience shear stress than when they're sitting in static medium. So the chip isn't just providing perfusion; it's providing a more biologically realistic mechanical environment.

\[Pause.\]

The first generation of organ-on-chip devices, pioneered by Don Ingber's group at the Wyss Institute at Harvard, were simpler than organoids — they typically had monolayers of cells (lung epithelial cells, gut epithelial cells, liver cells) arranged in two-layer chips with a porous membrane between two channels. One channel might have epithelial cells, simulating the lining of an organ; the other channel might have endothelial cells, simulating a blood vessel. Perfusion in both channels mimicked the physiological setup. These early chips were valuable for studying transport between blood and tissue, drug absorption, infection biology, and other interface phenomena.

The newer generation combines chip devices with actual organoids. You grow organoids using standard methods, then transfer them into a perfusion chamber on a chip, where they continue to grow under flow. The flow extends the size at which organoids can survive, slows down necrotic core formation, and brings them closer to physiologic conditions. Some groups are even building chips with engineered vascular channels lined with endothelial cells that connect to the organoid's surface, so that the perfusion happens through proper vessel-like structures rather than just through bulk medium flow.

The frontier here is **vascularized organoids on chips**. You co-culture stem-cell-derived endothelial cells with the organoid's parenchymal cells, and as the organoid grows, the endothelial cells form proto-vessel networks within it. When this organoid is placed on a chip with appropriate inflow and outflow channels, the chip's flow can be coaxed to enter the organoid's endogenous vessel network, perfusing the interior just as blood would perfuse a real tissue. Some labs are getting this to work, in primitive form, with kidney and liver and brain organoids. The results aren't yet at the point where you can grow a centimeter-scale organ, but they're substantially beyond what static culture can achieve. We're moving toward the point where the size ceiling on organoid biology becomes a function of how clever your perfusion is, not a fundamental physical limit.

\[Pause.\]

There's a related, even more ambitious frontier: the **body-on-a-chip** or multi-organ chip. The idea is to connect several different organoids — say, a liver organoid, a kidney organoid, a heart organoid, and a gut organoid — on the same chip, with shared perfusion that mimics blood circulation. You administer a drug to the system. The gut organoid absorbs it. The liver organoid metabolizes it. The kidney organoid clears the metabolites. The heart organoid experiences whatever cardiovascular effects the drug has. All of this happens in a coupled system that models, in primitive form, how the drug would behave in a whole organism. These systems are still experimental and the data they produce is still imperfect, but they offer a path toward replacing some of the animal studies that drug development currently requires. The FDA has been increasingly receptive to organoid-on-chip data as part of drug submissions, and there's a 2022 law — the FDA Modernization Act 2.0 — that explicitly allows alternative methods like organoid testing to replace some required animal testing. The regulatory framework is shifting in favor of these technologies.

The vasculature problem isn't solved. But it's no longer a brick wall. It's an engineering challenge being chipped away at, one perfusion design at a time.

## SEGMENT 11 — Personalized medicine with tumor organoids: the dream becoming routine

Let's come back to tumor organoids, because in the years since we first touched on them in Segment 4, the technology has moved from cool research demonstration to active clinical application, and I want to spend a segment on how that has happened and what it means.

The core idea is the one I sketched earlier: **take a piece of your tumor, grow it as an organoid, test a panel of drugs in parallel, treat you with whichever one shrinks the dish.** That's the dream of personalized cancer medicine, and tumor organoids have made it concretely operationalizable.

Here's how it works in a working clinical context. A patient is diagnosed with cancer — say, colorectal cancer. During surgery or biopsy, a small piece of the tumor is collected. The tissue is sent to a specialized lab that grows tumor organoids. Within 2 to 6 weeks (depending on the cancer type and the protocol), there's a healthy organoid culture established. The organoids are characterized to confirm they match the original tumor — by genetics (the same mutations), by histology (the same appearance under the microscope), and by markers. Once confirmed, the organoids are split into many wells and exposed to a panel of cancer drugs — sometimes a dozen, sometimes a hundred. The drugs that kill the organoids best are flagged. Those flagged drugs are reported back to the oncologist, who uses the information along with other clinical considerations to choose treatment.

\[Pause.\]

This is happening now, in real clinics, for real patients. The Dutch HUB Organoids consortium has run trials of organoid-guided treatment for several cancer types. The American group around the Wake Forest Institute for Regenerative Medicine has done similar work for colorectal and pancreatic cancers. In the UK, several NHS trusts are piloting tumor organoid testing for specific cancer indications. The results, in the published trials so far, have been encouraging — there's typically a strong correlation between organoid drug response and patient drug response, with positive predictive values in some studies above 80%.

The strongest validation has come from cancers where the standard of care includes a relatively well-defined panel of options. In **colorectal cancer**, for instance, there are well-established cytotoxic chemotherapy regimens plus several targeted therapies (cetuximab, bevacizumab, regorafenib, and so on). Testing patient organoids against these options in advance can help oncologists choose the most likely-to-work regimen for each patient. Multiple studies have shown organoid-based prediction is more accurate than standard genomic biomarkers alone.

In **pancreatic cancer**, where the prognosis is grim and the available drugs are limited, organoid testing has been used to identify the few patients whose tumors respond to specific regimens — and to spare the rest from cycles of ineffective and toxic treatment. This is sometimes called "the precision oncology dream" — the idea that we can stop blindly applying generic treatment protocols and start tailoring treatment to each tumor's specific vulnerabilities.

In **rare cancers** and **cancers of unknown primary origin** — where there are few standard treatment guidelines because the population of patients is too small to support large clinical trials — tumor organoids may be especially valuable. If you can't run a clinical trial because there aren't enough patients, you can still test drugs on each patient's organoid and make individualized decisions.

\[Pause.\]

There are important limitations to this approach that I should mention. First: not all tumors grow well as organoids. Some cancer types — certain leukemias, some glioblastomas — have established culture systems; others remain difficult. The success rate for establishing colorectal organoids is around 90%; for some other cancers, it's much lower.

Second: organoids lack the tumor microenvironment. They're cancer cells, possibly with some stromal cells, but they don't have the immune cells, the surrounding normal tissue, the blood vessels, the systemic factors that influence drug response in a patient. So a drug that works on the organoid might fail in the patient because the patient's immune system clears the drug before it can act, or because the tumor in vivo is protected by surrounding stroma. There's active work on **patient-derived organoids plus autologous immune cells**, where you grow the tumor organoid alongside immune cells isolated from the same patient, to model immune interactions. This is especially important for immunotherapy drugs, which work by activating the immune response.

Third: time. The few weeks it takes to establish and test organoids can be a barrier when treatment decisions need to be made urgently. Faster protocols are being developed, but there's a fundamental tradeoff between speed and reliability.

Fourth: cost and access. Organoid testing is currently expensive and not widely available. It's not yet routine for the average cancer patient. As the technology matures and protocols become standardized, costs should come down, but we're not there yet.

\[Pause.\]

The overall trajectory, though, is unmistakable. Patient-derived tumor organoids are becoming a tool that real oncologists are starting to use to make real decisions for real patients. They are bridging the gap between molecular characterization of a tumor (which we've been able to do for years through genomics) and functional characterization (actually testing what the tumor does and responds to). They are the closest thing we've had to a "preclinical trial in a dish" — a way to anticipate, with reasonable accuracy, what's going to happen in the patient before subjecting the patient to the treatment.

Five years ago, this was research. Ten years from now, my bet is that some form of patient-derived organoid testing will be standard of care for several major cancers. It's a remarkable trajectory: from a 2009 mouse intestinal organoid paper to clinical decision-making for thousands of cancer patients, in less than two decades.

## SEGMENT 12 — The Hubrecht biobank and the organoid commons

Let me pause here and tell you about a thing that I think is genuinely undersung in the public conversation about organoids. It's the existence of a large and growing **biobank infrastructure** — a network of repositories of organoid lines that researchers can access for study.

The most prominent of these is at the Hubrecht Institute in Utrecht, where Hans Clevers' group has built, over roughly a decade, an enormous collection of patient-derived organoids. The biobank now contains thousands of organoid lines spanning many cancer types: colorectal, breast, pancreatic, prostate, liver, bladder, lung, head-and-neck, and more. Each line is derived from a specific patient's tumor (or, for normal-tissue lines, a specific donor's healthy tissue). Each line has been genomically characterized — whole-genome sequencing, RNA sequencing, sometimes proteomics. Each line is frozen down and replenishable. And each line is, in principle, available to academic researchers who can demonstrate a valid scientific use.

\[Pause.\]

Think about what this means. If you're a researcher who wants to ask, "What makes some pancreatic cancers respond to drug X and others not?" — you don't have to recruit a hundred pancreatic cancer patients (which would take years and involve enormous ethical and logistical infrastructure). You can request, say, 50 different pancreatic cancer organoids from the biobank, treat them with drug X, identify which ones respond, and then look at the genomics to find the pattern. You can do, in months, the kind of correlative analysis that would take a clinical trial years.

If you're a pharma company developing a new targeted therapy, you can test it against the biobank's panel of, say, colorectal cancer organoids carrying different driver mutations, and predict in advance which patient subgroups are most likely to benefit. This informs trial design — you can enrich your clinical trial for the patients most likely to respond, increasing your chance of seeing a positive signal.

If you're a basic biologist studying tumor heterogeneity, you can study how organoids derived from different regions of the same tumor differ from each other, capturing intratumor heterogeneity in a way that wasn't possible before.

\[Pause.\]

The biobank is, in essence, **a library of patient-derived organoids spanning many cancers**, and it functions as a research commons that anyone in the academic world can draw on. It's a deliberate scientific public good. Hans Clevers and his colleagues built it, and continue to expand it, because they believe that having this resource shared widely accelerates research far more than hoarding it.

This pattern — open biobanks, shared organoid lines, deposited reagents that anyone can request — is becoming the norm in the field. There are now organoid biobanks for cystic fibrosis (in the Netherlands), for inflammatory bowel disease, for various rare diseases. There are organoid lines deposited in repositories like the European Collection of Authenticated Cell Cultures (ECACC) and the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC) and similar Japanese and Chinese registries. The shared resource model that started with plasmids in the Addgene era and continued with iPSC repositories is now playing out for organoids.

It's worth pausing to appreciate this. A patient consents to donate a piece of their tumor at the time of surgery. The tumor gets grown into an organoid line. That line gets deposited in a biobank. Years later, researchers around the world are using that line to test new drugs, identify biomarkers, understand disease mechanism. The patient may have long since died from their cancer, but their tumor — preserved, replicated, studied — continues to contribute to the science that helps the next generation of patients. There's something quietly humane about this. The patient becomes, posthumously, a contributor to the research effort that will help others.

\[Pause.\]

There are, of course, ethical and consent questions involved in this kind of biobanking. Patients need to be informed and consent to the use of their tissue for research. The use needs to be limited to research purposes, not commercial sale of identifiable material. The genetic information embedded in the organoid line — which carries the patient's full genome — needs to be protected. These issues have largely been worked out by the institutional biobanks, with carefully designed consent forms and governance frameworks. But they remain alive considerations as the biobanks grow and as the kinds of questions researchers want to ask of them expand.

The big takeaway: biological research is, increasingly, **a commons-driven enterprise**. The most valuable resources — organoid lines, iPSC lines, plasmid libraries, antibody panels — are shared across institutions, and the resulting scientific velocity is much greater than it would be if each lab had to generate everything from scratch. Organoid biobanks are one of the more recent and powerful examples of this commons-building, and they're a quiet revolution in how clinical and basic research interact.

## SEGMENT 13 — The ethics of mini-brains: when does a model become a moral patient?

Now we have to talk about the question that, in my experience, is the one students always want to ask but sometimes hesitate to bring up. Cerebral organoids contain neurons. The neurons fire action potentials. They form synapses. They show oscillatory network activity in some longer-grown preparations. They are, by any reasonable standard, brain tissue — small pieces of human brain tissue, alive, electrically active, growing in dishes.

So the question, unavoidable: **at what point does a brain organoid become something we owe moral consideration to?** When, if ever, does a mini-brain start to be a thing that can suffer, or perceive, or experience? And how would we even know?

\[Pause.\]

Let me try to frame this carefully, because it's a genuinely hard question and I don't think the field has a settled answer. The honest answer is that we don't fully know what we have. Cerebral organoids are pieces of organized neural tissue. They have neurons that talk to other neurons. They have something like a developing cortex. They have electrical activity. Whether any of this rises to the level of consciousness — or whatever the right metric is for moral concern — depends on theories of consciousness that are themselves contested.

Here's what we can say. Current cerebral organoids are small — typically a few millimeters in diameter, containing on the order of millions to tens of millions of neurons. A human brain has roughly 86 billion. So an organoid has, in raw cell count, about a hundred-thousandth of the neurons of an adult brain. They lack many of the structures whose absence in actual humans causes profound deficits — there's no thalamus connecting them to peripheral inputs, no brainstem regulating arousal, no developed networks for the kinds of integration that adult brains do. They are, as I said in Segment 8, fetal-tissue-equivalent in maturity, somewhere around mid-trimester fetal development.

By those measures, current organoids are very far from anything we'd ordinarily call conscious. They lack the structures, the connectivity, the inputs, and the maturity that would seem necessary. The activity we observe in them — synchronized oscillations, action potentials, primitive network rhythms — is comparable to what you'd see in fetal brain at the developmental stage they resemble, not what you'd see in an adult brain.

\[Pause.\]

But — and this is the uncomfortable part — the technology is improving. Organoids are being kept in culture longer (up to a year or more in some studies). They are being made more complex, with assembloids that combine multiple brain regions. They are being given sensory inputs through co-culture with optic-cup-like structures or muscle-derived afferents. They are being put on chips with perfusion so they can grow larger.

Each of these advances pushes the system a small distance closer to the territory where consciousness questions become real. If you take a forebrain organoid, fuse it with a brainstem organoid, add a thalamic organoid for sensory integration, perfuse the whole thing on a chip so it can grow to centimeter scale, and keep it for two years — you have moved meaningfully closer to something that might warrant moral consideration. Not there yet. Not close, by most reasonable estimates. But closer.

The right framing, I think, is this: **if a brain organoid can fire neurons in synchrony, is it conscious? Probably not at this size and complexity. But the question is on the radar, and we need to think about it carefully before the technology forces us to think about it under duress.**

\[Pause.\]

The bioethics community has begun to engage with these questions. There have been workshops, white papers, and consortium statements. The general consensus so far is: current organoids almost certainly do not warrant moral consideration in the sense that conscious beings do, but the field should establish principles before it's needed. Some of those principles include:

**Stop rules.** Establishing, in advance, what kinds of experiments should not be done. For instance, many in the field would draw a line at integrating cerebral organoids into living animals in ways that would create a chimeric brain with substantial human cognitive content — partly out of moral concern, partly out of unpredictable scientific consequence.

**Functional monitoring.** Developing methods to assess what an organoid is and isn't doing, with electrophysiology and imaging, so that judgments about its moral status are grounded in actual data rather than speculation.

**Limits on duration and complexity.** Some have proposed that experiments with cerebral organoids should be capped in duration or complexity unless there's a compelling scientific reason to go further. This is controversial — research thrives on the absence of arbitrary limits — but it's part of the conversation.

**Transparency and public engagement.** Cerebral organoid research benefits from being conducted openly, with public discussion of what's being done and why. The history of contentious bioscience — animal research, embryonic stem cell research, gene editing — suggests that transparency and engagement build legitimacy and trust.

My own view, for what it's worth: current cerebral organoids are research tools, not subjects. They are pieces of tissue, comparable in moral weight to other human tissue used in research. But the field is moving in a direction where the question matters more, and we should be doing the philosophical and ethical homework now, while we still have the luxury of theorizing in advance. The worst outcome would be to find ourselves with an organoid system that arguably crosses a moral threshold without ever having thought through what that means or how to respond.

This is one of those areas where biology is racing ahead of our conceptual frameworks for thinking about what we're doing. The field needs philosophers as much as it needs more developmental biologists.

## SEGMENT 14 — Pasca's assembloids: fusing organoids into circuits

Let's pivot now to one of the most exciting recent developments in the field, which has come from the lab of **Sergiu Pasca** at Stanford. Pasca is a physician-scientist who works on neurodevelopmental disorders, and his lab has developed an extension of cerebral organoid technology called **assembloids**.

Here's the idea. The brain isn't one tissue; it's many tissues connected together in a specific architecture. The cortex is connected to the thalamus, which is connected to brainstem, which is connected to spinal cord, which is connected to muscle. The cortex sends axons to the striatum to drive movement. The retina sends axons to the lateral geniculate nucleus, which sends axons to visual cortex. Most brain function depends on these long-range connections between specialized regions.

A standard cerebral organoid contains a mixture of regions, but the architecture is messy and the connections between regions are not particularly well-defined. To study a specific circuit, you'd want a more controlled system.

Pasca's insight was: **grow two organoids separately, then fuse them.** Take a cortical organoid. Take a striatal organoid. Bring them into physical contact in a dish. Let them fuse. The neurons in the cortical organoid will extend axons that grow across the boundary into the striatal organoid. The neurons in the striatal organoid will form connections back. After a few weeks of co-culture, you have a single fused organoid — an assembloid — that contains both cortical and striatal regions with axonal projections between them. You've recapitulated, in primitive form, a real anatomical connection from the brain.

\[Pause.\]

The first major assembloid paper from the Pasca group was published in 2017, in _Nature_, and showed exactly this for the cortex and a ventral forebrain region. They demonstrated that GABAergic interneurons — inhibitory neurons that are born in the ventral forebrain and migrate during development to populate the cortex — would, in the assembloid, migrate from the ventral region into the cortical region, just as they do in a real embryo. They watched cells migrate, in real time, between organoids — a developmental process that takes weeks of human gestation, recapitulated in a dish in a few days.

This was a stunning demonstration. **Two organoids that fuse — Pasca puts cortical plus striatal together and watches neurons migrate from one into the other.** It showed that organoid technology could be used to study not just single-region biology but inter-regional biology — the migration, connectivity, and circuit-formation events that depend on multiple brain regions interacting.

Since then, the Pasca lab and others have built increasingly elaborate assembloids. Cortex-thalamus assembloids, which model thalamocortical projections (the major pathway for sensory information into the cortex). Cortex-spinal cord-muscle assembloids, which model the corticospinal motor system, complete with muscle contractions in response to cortical neuron activity. Multi-region forebrain assembloids that include cortex, striatum, and thalamus together. Each assembloid type lets researchers ask circuit-level questions in ways that single organoids cannot.

\[Pause.\]

What are these systems useful for? Quite a lot.

Disease modeling. Many neurodevelopmental disorders — autism, schizophrenia, Timothy syndrome (a calcium channelopathy Pasca has worked on extensively) — are believed to involve disrupted circuit formation between specific brain regions. By making assembloids from patient iPSCs, you can ask whether the connection between region A and region B is abnormal in this patient, and what that means for circuit function. Pasca's group has done compelling work on Timothy syndrome assembloids, showing specific defects in cortical interneuron migration that recapitulate aspects of the disease.

Drug screening. If you can establish a circuit-level assay in an assembloid — say, the proportion of cortical interneurons that migrate appropriately — you can screen drugs that rescue that phenotype. This is a more functionally relevant assay than just looking at single-cell phenotypes.

Developmental biology. Assembloids let researchers replay specific aspects of human brain development that were previously inaccessible. When does this projection form? What signals guide it? What happens if you disrupt this signal? These questions can be answered in assembloids in ways they could never be answered in living humans.

\[Pause.\]

There's a beautiful conceptual point here. Real brain function depends not on cells, but on circuits — patterns of connectivity between cells in different regions. Single-region organoids capture cell biology. Assembloids start to capture circuit biology. That's a qualitatively different kind of model. And it opens up the possibility, in the future, of modeling much more sophisticated aspects of human neural function in dishes than was conceivable a decade ago.

It's also one of the developments that intensifies the ethical questions we just discussed. A multi-region assembloid with cortical, thalamic, and brainstem components is more sophisticated than a single-region organoid. It's not anywhere near a whole brain, but it's closer than what came before. The moral-status question gets sharper as the complexity increases. The field will have to keep grappling with this as assembloid technology matures.

## SEGMENT 15 — Infectious disease modeling: Zika, COVID, and the rest

One of the most important and timely applications of organoid technology has been in **infectious disease modeling** — using organoids to study how pathogens infect human tissue, what cellular damage they cause, and what drugs might treat them.

The classic example, and one of the most consequential, came in 2016 during the Zika virus outbreak. Zika was causing a wave of birth defects in newborns in Brazil — particularly microcephaly, the same small-brain phenotype that Lancaster and Knoblich had modeled with their original cerebral organoid paper. The hypothesis was that Zika virus was infecting the developing fetal brain in utero and disrupting cortical development. But proving that hypothesis required studying how Zika interacts with developing human brain tissue, which was — for obvious reasons — not directly accessible.

Cerebral organoids made it accessible. Several groups, working rapidly in early 2016, took cerebral organoids derived from healthy iPSCs, exposed them to Zika virus, and watched what happened. The results, published within months, were unambiguous. Zika virus selectively infected and killed neural progenitor cells in the organoids — the very cells responsible for generating cortical neurons. The infected organoids showed reduced size, depleted progenitor zones, and decreased neuron production. The cellular phenotype in the dish matched the developmental phenotype in newborns. The mechanism was clear: Zika kills cortical progenitors, which depletes the pool of cells that should be making neurons, which produces a small brain.

\[Pause.\]

This was an extraordinary demonstration of organoid utility. In a matter of months — fast, by the standards of medical research — the field had used cerebral organoids to establish the mechanism by which a newly emerging virus was causing a public health crisis. The same organoid systems were then used to screen for antiviral drugs, identifying compounds that could block Zika infection or rescue the affected cells. Several drug candidates emerged from these screens that have since been developed further.

The Zika story established a template that the field has used repeatedly since. When SARS-CoV-2 emerged in late 2019 and early 2020, organoid researchers were ready. Within months, multiple groups had used **lung organoids**, **intestinal organoids**, **liver organoids**, **kidney organoids**, **vascular organoids**, and **brain organoids** to study how SARS-CoV-2 infects various human tissues, what cell types it targets, what damage it causes, and what drugs might block it.

The findings were illuminating. Lung organoids showed how SARS-CoV-2 infects type II alveolar cells via the ACE2 receptor, with viral replication producing cell damage and inflammatory signaling. Intestinal organoids revealed that the virus can infect gut epithelium too, helping explain GI symptoms and viral shedding in stool. Vascular organoids — which we haven't discussed much, but which are derived from pluripotent cells and contain endothelial cells and vascular networks — showed direct infection of endothelium, partially explaining the vascular complications of COVID. Brain organoids demonstrated that SARS-CoV-2 could infect neurons and glia under certain conditions, providing a possible substrate for the neurological symptoms some patients experienced.

These organoid-based findings, generated quickly during a pandemic, fed into the broader effort to understand and treat COVID. Drug screens in organoid systems helped identify candidate antivirals, including some that were later evaluated in patients.

\[Pause.\]

The general lesson here: **organoids are a rapid, flexible tool for studying any pathogen that infects human tissue.** Within weeks of an emerging infection, you can have organoids of the relevant target tissue exposed to the pathogen, characterized for infection, and screened for drug response. This is a faster turnaround than animal models (which require breeding and infrastructure) and a more human-relevant turnaround than cell line models (which often don't capture the cell types and architectures that pathogens actually target).

Some specific applications that organoids have proven useful for: **Norovirus** — long considered impossible to grow in culture — was finally cultured in intestinal organoids in 2016. **Rotavirus**, **Cryptosporidium**, **adenovirus**, **CMV**, **hepatitis B and C viruses**, **herpes simplex virus**, **respiratory syncytial virus** — all have been studied productively in organoid systems. Some pathogens that resist traditional culture become tractable in organoids precisely because the organoids reproduce the specific cellular environment those pathogens require.

The future here is bright. We're heading toward a world where, when a new infectious disease emerges, organoid systems will be among the first lines of investigation, providing rapid answers about mechanism, target cells, and candidate drugs. That capability didn't exist twenty years ago. It does now.

## SEGMENT 16 — Drug discovery: toxicity, target ID, and the path to market

Let's talk about drug discovery, because this is where organoids are making, arguably, their biggest practical impact today — not in personalized medicine, not yet, and not in transplantation, also not yet, but in the unglamorous but enormously consequential work of getting better drugs through development.

The drug discovery pipeline is famously brutal. Of every 10,000 candidate compounds that enter preclinical evaluation, perhaps one reaches the market. The attrition rates at each stage are enormous, and most of that attrition happens because compounds fail to work in humans, or because they have unacceptable side effects in humans, that weren't predicted by the models used in preclinical development. The two most common reasons for clinical failure are lack of efficacy and unexpected toxicity. Both of these are, in principle, addressable by better preclinical models. Better models predict better; fewer drugs fail late; pipeline economics improve.

Organoids are starting to be deployed at multiple stages of this pipeline.

**Toxicity screening.** This is the area where organoid use is most advanced. Liver toxicity (hepatotoxicity) is one of the leading causes of drug failure — drugs that look fine in mice and rats turn out to damage human livers. Liver organoids, particularly when derived from human cells, predict hepatotoxicity better than rodent models in many cases. Major pharmaceutical companies now include human liver organoids in their hepatotoxicity testing panels. Similarly, kidney organoids for nephrotoxicity, cardiac organoids for cardiotoxicity, gut organoids for GI toxicity, and brain organoids for neurotoxicity are all being deployed for early-stage screening to filter out compounds with safety problems before they progress to expensive later-stage testing.

**Target identification and validation.** When researchers identify a new candidate drug target — a protein that, when inhibited, might treat a disease — they need to confirm that hitting that target in human cells actually produces the desired effect. Organoids are increasingly used for this. If you're working on a colorectal cancer drug, you can knock out your candidate target in colorectal organoids and see what happens. If you're working on an Alzheimer's drug, you can knock out the target in cortical organoids and look for the effect on neurons. This kind of human-cell target validation is much stronger evidence than equivalent work in mice.

**Phenotypic screening.** This is the approach where you don't have a specific target in mind but instead screen compounds for their ability to produce a desired phenotype — say, restoring normal function to a diseased organoid. Cystic fibrosis organoid screening, where compounds are tested for their ability to restore CFTR function (measured by the forskolin swelling assay we mentioned), is one example. The Vertex drug ivacaftor was originally identified in a CFTR-functional screen, and organoid-based versions of similar screens are now common.

**Disease modeling.** Many diseases lack good animal models, especially diseases with strong human-specific genetic components. Organoids derived from patient iPSCs can recapitulate disease phenotypes in human cells, providing models that mouse studies can't. Spinal muscular atrophy, Rett syndrome, Timothy syndrome, microcephaly, polycystic kidney disease, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency — all have published organoid models that have generated insight unavailable in other systems.

\[Pause.\]

The practical consequence of all this is that pharmaceutical R&D is undergoing a slow but real shift toward including organoid-based assays at multiple stages. Twenty years ago, the standard preclinical model for many compounds was a rodent. Today, it's increasingly a rodent plus a panel of human organoid assays. Ten years from now, my expectation is that organoid assays will be the primary preclinical evaluation for many indications, with rodent studies playing a more limited role.

The 2022 FDA Modernization Act 2.0 mentioned earlier removed a longstanding regulatory requirement that all new drug applications include animal testing data. The new statute explicitly allows non-animal methods — including organoids, organ-on-chip systems, and computational models — to substitute for animal data where appropriate. This regulatory shift is a major enabling event. It means companies can invest in organoid-based development programs without worrying that the FDA will demand redundant animal work. It will accelerate the migration of drug development toward human-cell-based models.

There are still real challenges. Organoid assays need to be validated and standardized before they can fully replace established assays. Different organoid protocols give different results. Variability between organoid batches and between iPSC lines is a real issue. The regulatory infrastructure for accepting organoid data is still maturing. But the trajectory is clear. Drug discovery is, gradually but unmistakably, becoming an organoid-and-chip enterprise, with the goal of more human-relevant predictions and lower attrition rates.

The economic stakes here are enormous. A pharmaceutical company spends, on average, $2 to $3 billion to bring one drug through development. If organoids can reduce attrition rates by even a small percentage, the savings are massive, and so is the public health benefit — more useful drugs reaching patients faster, with fewer late-stage failures eating up resources.

## SEGMENT 17 — Cardiac, lung, pancreas, prostate, breast: the organoid menagerie

We've covered the major organoid systems in detail, but I want to briefly survey the rest of the menagerie, because the speed at which organoids of new tissues have been developed since 2009 is itself a striking feature of this field. Within a few years of the original intestinal paper, recipes existed for organoids of essentially every major organ system. Let me walk through the highlights.

**Lung organoids.** Multiple groups, including Jason Spence's at Michigan, developed protocols for lung organoids in the mid-2010s. These come in different flavors — proximal airway organoids (with cell types resembling trachea and bronchi), distal alveolar organoids (resembling the gas-exchange surface of the lung), and mixed organoids. They've been used for respiratory infection studies (especially SARS-CoV-2 and influenza), cystic fibrosis modeling, lung cancer modeling, and drug screening for respiratory disease.

**Pancreas organoids.** Both endocrine (insulin-producing) and exocrine (digestive-enzyme-producing) pancreatic organoids have been developed. Pancreatic ductal organoids from the Clevers group are used heavily for pancreatic cancer modeling. There's substantial interest in producing beta-cell-containing organoids that could one day be transplanted into diabetic patients to restore insulin production — Doug Melton's group at Harvard and others have made significant progress here, though the work is now more often done with directed differentiation of pluripotent cells into beta cells rather than as organoids per se.

**Prostate organoids.** Important for prostate cancer modeling. Patient-derived prostate cancer organoids have been used to test androgen-deprivation therapy responses and to study castration-resistant prostate cancer.

**Breast organoids.** Mammary organoids, including patient-derived breast cancer organoids, are now used widely. The Clevers group and others have built breast cancer biobanks. There's also interest in normal mammary organoids for studying breast development and lactation biology.

**Cardiac organoids.** These are trickier than epithelial organoids because the heart's architecture is not simply epithelial — it includes cardiomyocytes, fibroblasts, endothelial cells, and the geometric arrangements that allow coordinated contraction. Several groups have developed cardiac organoids that beat spontaneously and show electrical properties resembling embryonic heart. The work of Sasha Mendjan at IMBA in Vienna, among others, has produced cardiac organoids that recapitulate aspects of chamber formation. These are being used for drug cardiotoxicity testing — a major cause of drug failure — and for modeling cardiac disease.

**Vascular organoids.** Developed by Josef Penninger's group, these are made from pluripotent cells differentiated toward endothelial and mural cell lineages, producing networks of blood-vessel-like structures complete with capillaries and supporting cells. These have been used to model diabetic vascular changes and SARS-CoV-2 vascular infection.

**Stomach organoids.** Gastric organoids, including those modeling _Helicobacter pylori_ infection and gastric cancer.

**Salivary gland organoids.** Used to study salivary gland regeneration after radiation damage in head-and-neck cancer patients.

**Thyroid organoids.** For studying thyroid development and disease.

**Endometrial organoids.** Used to study fertility, menstrual biology, and endometrial cancer.

**Choroid plexus organoids.** A relatively recent and interesting addition — the choroid plexus is the brain structure that produces cerebrospinal fluid. These organoids can actually produce fluid that resembles CSF, and they've been used to study how things move from blood to brain.

\[Pause.\]

The pattern across all these is consistent. A few groups, often building on Clevers' original recipe with appropriate modifications, work out the right cocktail of growth factors for their tissue of interest. The first paper shows that the organoids form. Subsequent papers establish disease models, drug screens, biobanks. Within a few years of the original publication, the technology is widely adopted and protocols are stable enough that any reasonably competent lab can produce them.

This is a remarkable acceleration of biological technology development. From "we can't grow any organ in a dish" in 2008 to "we have published protocols for essentially every major organ" by 2020. Twelve years. That's how fast a single conceptual breakthrough — the Clevers/Sato insight that stem cells in the right environment will self-organize — can transform a discipline once the basic protocol exists and the community starts iterating.

\[Pause.\]

Worth noting: not all organs are equally easy. Some — intestine, liver, prostate — produce robust organoids with relatively simple protocols. Others — heart, skin, complex brain regions — remain technically challenging and produce organoids that recapitulate only some features of the real organ. The frontier of organoid development is, in part, about pushing the technique into the hardest tissues and getting them to behave.

But the menagerie is now broad enough that for most tissues a researcher might care about, an organoid protocol exists or can be adapted from a closely related tissue. The field is at the point of maturation where new groundbreaking organoid systems are getting rarer, while refinement and application of existing ones is accelerating.

## SEGMENT 18 — Frontier: vascularized organoids, immune integration, transplant-ready tissue

To close, let me give you a sense of where the field is going, because organoid technology is far from a finished story. Three threads of active development define the frontier.

**Thread one: vascularization.** I've mentioned this several times because it's the single biggest practical limitation on organoid biology. Without internal blood vessels, organoids can't grow beyond a few millimeters and can't reach the functional density of real organs. The current generation of attempts to solve this includes:

Co-culture with endothelial cells, so the organoid contains vascular-lineage cells from the start. This produces primitive vessel networks within the organoid, though they're often disorganized.

Implantation into living animals (typically mice), where the host's vasculature invades and perfuses the transplanted organoid. This works reasonably well — Takebe's liver buds, transplanted into mice, get host-derived vascularization — but it requires an animal host, which limits scalability.

Engineered perfusion via microfluidic chips, where the chip provides external flow that supplements diffusion. This works for keeping organoids alive longer but doesn't truly provide internal vascularization.

3D bioprinting of vascular networks into organoid structures, then seeding with cells. This is technically demanding but is progressing rapidly. Several groups have demonstrated centimeter-scale tissues with engineered vascular trees that can be perfused.

The frontier here is integrating these approaches — pre-vascularized organoids, on chips with engineered perfusion, possibly with assistance from 3D-printed vascular scaffolds. The goal is centimeter-scale or larger tissue with proper internal perfusion. We're not there yet, but progress is real.

**Thread two: immune integration.** Most organoids lack immune cells, which limits their utility for inflammatory disease, infection biology, and immunotherapy modeling. Co-culture approaches are starting to address this. You can derive macrophages from iPSCs and add them to liver organoids to get Kupffer-like cells. You can add patient-derived T cells to tumor organoids to model immune checkpoint blockade. You can add microglia to cerebral organoids. The integration is still imperfect — adding immune cells doesn't always produce the right behaviors — but the field is converging on protocols that bring real immune function into organoid systems.

**Thread three: transplant-ready tissue.** The ultimate dream of regenerative medicine is to grow replacement tissues from a patient's own cells and transplant them to repair damaged organs. Organoids are one piece of this puzzle, though probably not the whole answer. For some tissues — pancreatic beta cells, retinal photoreceptors, liver hepatocytes — there's already early-stage clinical work using cells derived from iPSCs or stem cells. Organoid-based approaches, where the cells are pre-assembled into tissue-like structures before transplantation, may offer better engraftment and function than dissociated cell suspensions. We're at the very early end of this curve.

The path to growing a whole replaceable organ in a dish, vascularized and immune-integrated and functionally mature, is long. It probably requires solving several of the problems we've discussed in parallel: vascularization, immune integration, maturation, scale. There's no obvious shortcut. But each year, the pieces inch closer to each other. The notion of patient-specific, lab-grown replacement organs — once pure science fiction — is now a research program with intermediate milestones and credible progress.

\[Pause.\]

I want to close with a thought that ties this all back to where we started. Organoids began as a single observation: that an intestinal stem cell, placed in the right gel with the right signals, could build a tiny replica of the gut. Hans Clevers and Toshiro Sato in 2009. A simple experiment with profound implications.

In the fifteen-plus years since, that single observation has bloomed into a vast technology platform that touches almost every major organ, that enables personalized cancer treatment, that lets us model infections rapidly when new pathogens emerge, that gives us our best windows into human brain development, that may one day grow replacement tissues for transplant. The core insight — **that cells, given the right environment, will self-organize into organ-like structures** — has proven enormously generative.

If you walk away from this lecture remembering only one thing, let it be that. The architectural program for our organs is in our cells. We don't need to build the organs from outside; we need to give the cells the right cues, and they'll build themselves. Organoids are the laboratory expression of that principle. They are tiny demonstrations, in dishes on benches in labs around the world, that the instructions for who we are are written into our cells, and that with the right environment, those instructions can be replayed.

You'll encounter organoids in some form in essentially every modern biomedical context — in research papers, in clinical reports, in news stories about new drugs and new treatments. I hope that after these two hours, when you read about a mini-brain in the news, or hear about a patient whose treatment was guided by their own tumor organoid, you'll see not just a curiosity but a tool — a powerful, still-developing tool — that's quietly reshaping what biology and medicine can do.

That's all I have. Thank you for your attention, and good luck with the rest of your studies.

\[End of lecture.\]

## APPENDIX — Quick-reference summary (not for reading aloud)

**Core analogies used in this lecture:**

- Organoid = a stem cell put in the right environment grows itself into a miniature organ — you don't sculpt it, you give it the cues and it self-assembles
- Matrigel = the scaffolding the cells climb on — a soup of basement-membrane proteins that gives them a 3D structure to build around
- Growth factor cocktail = the conductor's score — each factor tells the cells what to become, when, in what order
- Intestinal organoid = the original — Clevers grew a tiny crypt-villus structure in 2009 and showed Lgr5+ stem cells could regenerate the whole gut lining in a dish
- Cerebral organoid = Lancaster grew mini-brains from iPSCs that recapitulate cortical layers, ventricles, even neurogenesis — at small scale, but real biology
- Tumor organoid for personalized medicine = take a piece of YOUR tumor, grow it as an organoid, test 100 drugs in parallel, treat you with whichever one shrinks the dish
- Tumor biobank = a library of patient avatars — each one a tiny biological replica of a real person's cancer, available for experimentation
- Assembloids = two organoids that fuse — Pasca puts cortical + striatal together and watches neurons migrate from one into the other
- What they CAN'T do = no blood vessels (so size limit ~mm), no immune cells (so no inflammation studies), no aging (so most are like a mid-trimester fetus)
- Brain organoid ethics = if a brain organoid can fire neurons in synchrony, is it conscious? probably not at this size — but the question is on the radar
- Organoid-on-chip = the engineering solution to the vasculature problem — pump fluid through, simulate blood flow, keep the interior alive
- Developmental biology in a dish = the cocktail reverse-engineers embryonic signals, so the cells think they're in an embryo and do what cells in an embryo do

**Three core architectural elements of any working organoid system:**

1. Stem cell source (adult tissue stem cells or pluripotent stem cells)
2. ECM gel (Matrigel or a defined alternative) providing 3D scaffolding
3. Growth factor cocktail recapitulating the developmental signals for the target tissue

**Three core limitations of current organoids:**

4. No vasculature — diffusion-limited size ceiling of a few millimeters
5. No immune cells — limits modeling of inflammation and immunotherapy
6. Immature — most resemble fetal more than adult tissue

**Key historical milestones:**

- 2007 — Clevers identifies Lgr5 as the intestinal stem cell marker
- 2009 — Sato and Clevers grow the first intestinal organoids from single Lgr5+ cells (_Nature_)
- 2011 — Sasai demonstrates self-organizing optic cup morphogenesis in mouse ES cells
- 2012 — Sasai extends optic cup work to human pluripotent cells

- 2015 — Melissa Little publishes the first kidney organoid protocol
- 2016 — Multiple groups use cerebral organoids to model Zika virus infection

- 2022 — FDA Modernization Act 2.0 explicitly permits non-animal models (including organoids) for drug development

**Key organ systems with established organoid protocols:**

Intestine, stomach, liver, pancreas (endocrine and exocrine), kidney, lung (proximal and distal), retina, brain (multiple regions), prostate, breast, bladder, salivary gland, thyroid, endometrium, choroid plexus, blood vessels, cardiac tissue.

**Where the field is heading:**

- Vascularization through co-culture, chip-based perfusion, and 3D-printed vascular networks
- Immune cell integration to model inflammatory and infectious disease
- Multi-region assembloids modeling circuit-level biology
- Body-on-a-chip systems linking multiple organoids for systemic drug modeling
- Clinical-grade organoid biobanks for routine personalized medicine
- Transplant-ready tissue grown from patient cells for regenerative medicine
- Ethical frameworks for advanced cerebral organoid research as complexity increases
