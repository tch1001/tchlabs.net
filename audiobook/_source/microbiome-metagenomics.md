## SEGMENT 1 — The hidden world, and why we couldn't see it

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. Today and for the next two hours we're going to talk about the microbiome — the staggeringly large and largely invisible world of microbes that live on you, in you, in the soil under your feet, in the ocean, in the deep subsurface, basically everywhere there is liquid water and a little bit of energy. And we're going to talk about the technology that finally let us see it: metagenomics. Culture-free DNA sequencing of entire microbial communities. The single most important methodological revolution in microbiology since Pasteur and Koch built the foundations of the field in the late 1800s.

I want to start with a number, because the numbers in this field are genuinely staggering and you should feel a little disoriented by them. Your body, by cell count, is roughly half microbial. The old number you might have heard was ten to one — ten bacterial cells for every human cell — and a careful 2016 estimate by Ron Sender, Shai Fuchs, and Ron Milo at the Weizmann Institute revised that downward to closer to one to one. So somewhere around thirty trillion of your own cells, somewhere around thirty-eight trillion bacterial cells. By count, you are about half microbe. By gene content, you are overwhelmingly microbe — your own genome encodes about twenty thousand protein-coding genes, and the collective genome of your gut bacteria encodes somewhere between two and twenty million. That's a hundred to a thousand times more genetic potential sitting in your colon than in all of your own chromosomes combined. And that potential is doing chemistry — digesting fibers you can't digest, synthesizing vitamins you can't make, training your immune system, producing neurotransmitters that talk to your brain, defending the gut against invading pathogens — chemistry you, the host, cannot do on your own.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy I want you to hold for the next two hours. **You are a trillion-microbe ecosystem walking around in a coat of skin.** You're not a single organism. You're a holobiont — a host plus its associated microbial communities — and the microbial part outnumbers the host part by cell, by gene, and by metabolic capacity. The lichen analogy is sometimes used: a lichen is a fungus and an alga so deeply intertwined that the combination behaves as a third thing. You are something similar, just much larger and with much more complicated bacteria. The fact that we used to think of ourselves as essentially sterile, with maybe a few germs floating around that occasionally caused infections, is not a small correction. It is a complete reframing of what an animal is.

So how could we have missed this for so long? How is it that bacteria were discovered by Leeuwenhoek in the 1670s — he literally looked at scrapings from his own teeth under his homemade microscope and was astonished to see tiny "animalcules" swimming around — and yet for the next three hundred years, microbiology remained largely blind to the diversity of microbial life? The answer is a single technical limitation, and it is the central frame of this whole lecture, so I want to plant it deep right now. **Microbiology, from Koch's postulates onward, was built on the ability to grow microbes in pure culture.** You take a sample, you streak it on an agar plate, you isolate a single colony, you grow it up, you study it. That's how Koch identified the anthrax bacillus, the tuberculosis bacillus, the cholera bacillus. The pure-culture method is the workhorse of classical microbiology, and it produced essentially everything we knew about bacteria for a century.

The problem is that **the overwhelming majority of microbes on Earth do not grow in pure culture in the lab.** They won't form a colony on agar. They need partners, or strange nutrients, or oxygen tensions, or temperatures, or chemical conditions that nobody has figured out how to replicate. Estimates vary by environment, but a useful rough number is that fewer than one percent of the bacterial species in a typical soil or marine sample have ever been cultured. Ninety-nine percent of them, when you put them on a Petri dish, just sit there and do nothing, or die, or refuse to form a visible colony. This was sometimes called the **great plate count anomaly** — the observation, made repeatedly through the twentieth century, that if you count bacterial cells in a soil sample under a microscope you get one number, and if you count colonies that grow from the same sample on a plate you get a number a hundred or a thousand times smaller. There were vastly more bacteria in the sample than the agar plate could grow.

\[Pause.\]

So picture the situation a microbiologist was in around 1980. You knew there were lots of bacteria out there. You could see them down a microscope. You could weigh them in a soil sample. But you couldn't grow most of them, and if you couldn't grow them, you couldn't get enough DNA to sequence, you couldn't identify them, you couldn't characterize what they did. The unculturable majority was a vast dark continent, visible at low resolution but impenetrable to the tools of the field. **You'd been looking at biology with one eye closed.** The eye that was open — the culturable minority, mostly fast-growing organisms that thrive on simple sugars in standard laboratory conditions — gave you a deeply skewed picture of what microbial life actually was. It was as if a zoologist had tried to catalog all animal life by looking only at pigeons and rats, because those were the only species that would live in his lab.

The thing that opened the second eye was DNA sequencing. Not the sequencing of whole genomes — that came later, and we'll get there — but the much narrower and more profound trick of pulling DNA directly out of an environmental sample, without culturing anything at all, and reading just enough of the right gene to identify what was there. That trick was the brainchild of a Berkeley microbiologist named Norman Pace in 1985, and we'll meet him properly in Segment 3. And once the trick existed, and once sequencing got cheap enough to do it at scale — which happened around 2005 to 2010, with the rise of next-generation sequencing platforms we covered in another lecture — the field exploded. The Human Microbiome Project launched in 2007. The American Gut Project started in 2012. By the late 2010s, every body site, every soil sample, every ocean transect was being characterized by direct DNA sequencing. The hidden world became visible.

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, sketch the difference between 16S rRNA surveys and shotgun metagenomics and explain when you'd choose each; two, explain what a MAG, a metagenome-assembled genome, is and why it matters; three, read any modern microbiome paper that says "we performed 16S sequencing" or "we did shotgun metagenomics" and have a real intuition for what the data looks like, what the analysis pipeline did, and what the conclusions can and cannot support — then we've succeeded. Everything else hangs off those three.

\[Pause.\]

One last orienting note before we dive into the history. The microbiome field is, in 2026, somewhere between the wild-frontier phase and the mature-clinical phase. We have one therapy — fecal microbiota transplant for recurrent _Clostridioides difficile_ infection — that genuinely works and is now FDA-approved in defined formulations. We have an enormous body of correlative data linking gut microbiome composition to inflammatory bowel disease, obesity, depression, autism, allergies, autoimmunity, and cancer immunotherapy response. And we have a small but growing number of engineered microbiome therapeutics in clinical trials. We also have an enormous amount of overhyped journalism, weak associations passed off as causation, probiotic products that probably do nothing, and a real risk that the field will overpromise and underdeliver. Part of my job today is to give you the tools to tell the strong science from the weak. We'll come back to that explicitly in Segment 10.

Now let's go meet the people who built the field.

## SEGMENT 2 — Carl Woese and the universal tree: the 16S revolution

Before Norman Pace pulled DNA out of an environmental sample, somebody had to figure out which DNA to pull. The whole subsequent field of culture-free microbial ecology depends on a single insight that came out of a remarkable, isolated, somewhat eccentric career in the American Midwest: Carl Woese, at the University of Illinois, working through the 1970s, on a question that almost nobody else was asking.

The question was deceptively simple. **How are all living things related to one another?** Darwin had drawn the famous tree of life sketch in his notebook in 1837 — that little branching diagram with the words "I think" above it — and ever since, biologists had tried to fill in the tree. For animals and plants, you could do reasonably well by comparing anatomy and embryology. But for microbes — bacteria — you couldn't. Bacteria are tiny featureless rods or spheres or spirals; they don't have skeletons or wing patterns. Classical bacterial taxonomy was based on what they ate, how they stained, what shape they were, and what diseases they caused. None of those properties tell you reliably about evolutionary relationships. Two bacteria that look identical under a microscope might be as distantly related as you are to a mushroom.

Woese decided to take a completely different approach: comparing sequences of a single molecule that every living thing has. If you could find a gene that was universal — present in every cell on Earth — and that evolved slowly enough that you could still align sequences from very different organisms, then differences in that gene's sequence would directly measure evolutionary distance. Lots of changes between two organisms in this molecule, distantly related. Few changes, closely related. A universal molecular clock.

The molecule Woese chose was **ribosomal RNA**. Specifically, in bacteria and archaea, the small subunit of the ribosome contains a chunk of RNA about 1,500 nucleotides long, called the 16S rRNA. In eukaryotes — that's us, plus fungi, plants, animals, all the things with nuclei — the equivalent molecule in the small subunit is called the 18S rRNA. They're homologous; they do the same job; they evolved from the same ancestral molecule billions of years ago. Ribosomal RNA is universal because every cell on Earth needs ribosomes to make proteins, and the ribosome is so central to life that its core RNA components have been conserved with remarkably little change since the last universal common ancestor.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy. **Imagine you're trying to figure out the family tree of every human being on Earth, and the only piece of evidence you have is a single sentence that everyone has written down somewhere. That sentence has been passed from parent to child for thousands of generations, copied by hand each time, accumulating small errors at each copy. People in the same family will have nearly identical versions of the sentence. People from distant lineages will have versions that have drifted apart, with many differences. Compare the sentences, count the differences, and you can reconstruct the family tree.** That's exactly what Woese was doing with 16S rRNA. The sentence had been copied for four billion years. The errors were the substitutions, insertions, and deletions that accumulated in the RNA sequence over evolutionary time. By sequencing 16S from many different bacteria and aligning the sequences, Woese could calculate how related any two organisms were, in a way that made no assumption about morphology or physiology.

The 16S molecule had two more crucial properties that made it perfect for this. First, it has a mix of highly conserved regions and highly variable regions. The conserved regions, which form the structural core of the ribosome, are essentially identical across all of life — these you can use to align sequences from organisms as different as _E. coli_ and a human. The variable regions, between the conserved blocks, accumulate mutations faster and are different even between closely related species — these are where you read the actual phylogenetic signal. The molecule has, built into it, both an alignment ruler and a discrimination ruler. Second, ribosomal RNA is present in every cell at very high copy number — there are tens of thousands of ribosomes in every bacterial cell — which made it experimentally tractable to isolate enough material to sequence in the era before PCR existed.

In 1977, Woese and his collaborator George Fox published what would turn out to be one of the most important papers in modern biology, almost completely ignored at the time. They had been sequencing 16S rRNA from a strange group of microbes called the **methanogens** — single-celled organisms that produce methane, found in swamps and the guts of cows and the deep ocean. The methanogens had been classified for decades as a kind of unusual bacterium. But when Woese compared their 16S sequences to those of normal bacteria and to those of eukaryotes, he discovered something shocking. **The methanogens were not bacteria at all.** Their 16S sequences were as different from regular bacteria as bacteria were from eukaryotes. They formed a completely separate third branch of life, equal in evolutionary depth to the other two.

\[Pause.\]

Woese named this third domain the **Archaebacteria**, later shortened to **Archaea**. The classical two-domain view of life — prokaryotes versus eukaryotes — was wrong. The real picture was three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya. The methanogens, plus a bunch of other organisms we now classify as Archaea — the extreme thermophiles that live near hydrothermal vents, the halophiles that live in concentrated brine, and many others — represent a vast and ancient branch of life that microscopy and culture-based microbiology had been quietly lumping together with bacteria for a century. They look like bacteria. They're single cells without nuclei. But their cellular biochemistry — membrane lipids, gene expression machinery, replication enzymes — is in many ways closer to ours than to the bacteria's.

This was a foundational discovery, and it was greeted with extraordinary skepticism at the time. Senior microbiologists were furious. The textbooks had to be rewritten. Woese, who was already considered slightly difficult and obsessive, was further marginalized. It took nearly a decade for the three-domain tree to become widely accepted. He was eventually given the Crafoord Prize and the National Medal of Science but never the Nobel, partly because, I think, the establishment never fully forgave him for being right about something so fundamental that the textbooks had to change.

For our purposes today, what matters is the methodological legacy. Woese's work established that **16S rRNA is the universal phylogenetic marker.** Every bacterium and archaeon has it. The sequence carries an evolutionary signal that lets you place any organism on the tree of life by reading a few hundred nucleotides. And — here is the key insight that Norman Pace would build on a few years later — **you don't need to grow the organism to sequence its 16S.** All you need is its DNA. If you can pull DNA out of an environmental sample and amplify the 16S gene from it, you can identify the organism without ever culturing it. The 16S is a barcode, written into every cell, that tells you who the cell is. Read the barcode, you've identified the bug.

That's the universal-barcode framing I want you to hold. The 16S is a slowly evolving, universally present, internally variable RNA gene whose sequence is a fingerprint of phylogenetic identity. Carl Woese figured this out by hand-sequencing RNA in the 1970s — a brutally laborious process. Norman Pace, in the next segment, will take Woese's insight and combine it with PCR to do what was previously impossible.

## SEGMENT 3 — Norman Pace 1985 and the birth of culture-independent microbiology

The year is 1985. Kary Mullis has just invented the polymerase chain reaction — PCR, the technique that lets you amplify any specific stretch of DNA exponentially, starting from vanishingly small amounts. Norman Pace, a microbiologist who had been working on RNA biochemistry at the University of Colorado at Denver, is sitting in his office thinking about Carl Woese's work on 16S rRNA, and about all the bacteria in the world that he can't culture, and about what PCR might let him do.

The idea that hit him is, in retrospect, beautiful in its simplicity. **You don't have to culture a microbe to read its 16S gene. You can extract DNA directly from an environmental sample — a scoop of soil, a milliliter of seawater, a fecal swab — design PCR primers that match the conserved regions of the 16S gene, and amplify the variable regions in between. The conserved regions are conserved in everything, so the primers will grab the 16S from every bacterium and archaeon in the sample. The variable regions are variable, so the amplified products will carry the phylogenetic signal that tells you who's there.** No culturing required. The unculturable majority becomes visible for the first time.

Pace and his collaborators published the foundational papers between 1985 and 1990. They went to a hot spring in Yellowstone National Park, took a sample of microbial mat, extracted total DNA, PCR-amplified the 16S genes, cloned them into bacterial vectors, and sequenced the clones one by one. What they found was astonishing. Most of the 16S sequences they recovered did not match anything in the existing databases of cultured organisms. They were entirely new lineages of bacteria and archaea — sometimes whole new phyla — that nobody had ever seen, because nobody had ever managed to grow them. The hot spring contained orders of magnitude more microbial diversity than had been suspected. And this pattern repeated everywhere Pace's group looked: the open ocean, the deep subsurface, hydrothermal vents, freshwater lakes, every environment they sampled was teeming with previously invisible microbial lineages.

\[Pause.\]

The implication was profound. **Microbiology, as it had been practiced for a century, had been studying a tiny, biased slice of microbial life — the slice that happened to grow on standard lab media.** Everything else, the vast majority, had been invisible. Pace's culture-independent approach exposed a hidden diversity that overturned essentially every estimate of how many kinds of microbes existed and what they were doing. The number of recognized bacterial phyla, which had been a few dozen for most of the twentieth century, exploded into the hundreds. Many of these new phyla had no cultured representatives at all — we knew they existed only because their 16S sequences kept turning up in environmental samples. We called these the **candidate phyla**, organisms that we had identified phylogenetically but had never seen alive in the lab. As of the mid-2020s, there are probably more candidate phyla than cultured ones. The known microbial world is a small island in a vast unknown sea, and the sea was first mapped by Pace and his successors using exactly this 16S sequencing trick.

Let me make the workflow concrete, because the basic recipe Pace established is, with some refinements, still how 16S microbiome surveys are done today. You start with a sample — let's say a stool sample, since that's where most of you will encounter this. Step one: **DNA extraction.** You lyse all the cells in the sample, releasing their DNA, and you purify the DNA away from proteins, lipids, and PCR inhibitors. This is harder than it sounds, because different bacteria have different cell wall structures — gram-positive bacteria with their thick peptidoglycan walls are much harder to break open than gram-negative bacteria — and any extraction method will be biased. If you don't lyse the tough cells efficiently, you'll under-represent them in your final dataset. Modern kits use a combination of mechanical bead-beating, enzymatic digestion, and chemical lysis to try to break everything open uniformly. The choice of extraction kit is one of the major sources of variability between microbiome studies, and people argue about it endlessly.

Step two: **PCR amplification of a 16S variable region.** You design primers that anneal to two conserved regions flanking one or two of the variable regions — most often the V3-V4 region, sometimes just V4, sometimes V1-V2, depending on the platform and the question. The primers have the conserved-region sequences at their 3' ends, so they bind to essentially every bacterial and archaeal 16S gene in the sample. At the 5' ends, the primers carry extra sequences that will let you load the products onto an Illumina sequencer — adapter sequences, sample-specific barcodes, and so on. You run PCR for, typically, 25 to 30 cycles, and you end up with a tube full of millions of copies of the V3-V4 region from every microbe in your original sample, each copy tagged with a sample barcode.

Step three: **sequencing.** You pool the PCR products from many samples together — multiplexing — and load them onto an Illumina MiSeq or NextSeq instrument, which reads the variable region from both ends in paired-end mode. Modern protocols target the V4 region with 250-base paired-end reads, which fully overlaps the 250-base V4 amplicon and gives you very accurate sequences. The MiSeq, in particular, became the workhorse of 16S microbiome surveys because its read length and error profile matched the V3-V4 amplicon almost perfectly. A single MiSeq run can sequence hundreds of samples at tens of thousands of reads each.

\[Pause.\]

Step four: **clustering and taxonomy.** You take all the millions of reads, you bin them by sample using the barcodes, and then you have to decide which reads come from which organism. This is where there's historically been a big methodological debate, and it leads to the OTU-versus-ASV distinction that I want to spend a moment on, because it's important.

The older approach is to cluster reads into **OTUs**, operational taxonomic units. You take all the reads, you compute pairwise similarities, and you lump together any reads that are more than 97% identical into a single OTU. The 97% threshold is a rough approximation of the species boundary — two 16S sequences that are 97% identical are usually from organisms classified as the same species. Each OTU is then treated as a single bacterial "species" for downstream analysis. The advantage of OTUs is that they're robust to sequencing errors — if a read has a few errors in it, it still falls into the same OTU as the correct version. The disadvantage is that you lose resolution. Two strains of the same species might have 16S sequences that differ by only one or two nucleotides, well under the 97% threshold, and they'd be lumped together as a single OTU even though they might be biologically very different.

The newer approach, which has largely won, is to call **ASVs**, amplicon sequence variants. Instead of clustering, you use a denoising algorithm — the most famous is **DADA2**, developed by Ben Callahan and Susan Holmes around 2016 — that statistically models the sequencing error process and tries to recover the exact original sequences that gave rise to the observed reads. The output is a set of unique sequences, often differing by just a single nucleotide, each treated as a separate biological entity. ASVs give you much higher resolution than OTUs and they're reproducible across studies, which OTUs weren't — if I cluster your reads at 97% in my lab and you cluster them in yours, we'll get different OTU definitions. ASVs are defined by exact sequence, so they're the same in every dataset that contains them.

The analogy. **OTU is like grouping people by family name — anyone named Smith goes in the Smith bucket, regardless of which specific Smith they are. ASV is like grouping people by their full name and birthdate — each individual is its own entry. OTU loses resolution but is forgiving of typos in the data. ASV preserves resolution and is unforgiving but more accurate when the underlying methods can handle it.** Modern 16S analysis essentially always uses ASVs now, and the standard pipeline is DADA2 followed by taxonomy assignment.

Step five: **taxonomy assignment.** You compare each ASV's sequence to a reference database of 16S sequences with known taxonomic labels. The major reference databases are **SILVA**, **GreenGenes**, and the **RDP** — Ribosomal Database Project. The algorithm finds the closest matches in the database and assigns a taxonomic label to your ASV: kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and sometimes species. You end up with a table where rows are ASVs, columns are samples, and each cell contains a count of how many reads of that ASV were found in that sample, plus a separate taxonomy table mapping each ASV to a taxonomic lineage. That table — the **feature table** — is the core data object of a 16S study. Every downstream analysis, every diversity calculation, every comparison between groups, starts from that table.

The whole pipeline — DNA extraction, PCR, sequencing, denoising, taxonomy assignment — has been packaged into a few standard software toolkits. **QIIME2** is the dominant one; **Mothur** is the older alternative; **DADA2** as an R package is what most analysts actually use for the denoising step inside QIIME2. If you read a microbiome methods section, you'll almost always see one of those three named. They are the workhorses.

That's the 16S workflow. It's now twenty years old in its essential form, refined enormously in the details, but the bones are still Norman Pace's 1985 idea: pull DNA from the sample, amplify the 16S, read the sequences, identify who's there. Let's move on to what 16S _can't_ tell you, and the technology that fills the gap.

## SEGMENT 4 — Shotgun metagenomics: from who is there to what they're doing

The 16S survey has a fundamental limitation, and it's worth being precise about, because it shapes what the data can support. **16S tells you who is there, but not what they are doing.** The 16S gene is a phylogenetic marker. It's a barcode. It identifies the taxonomic group present in your sample, but it carries essentially no information about what metabolic functions those organisms can perform, what genes they encode, what compounds they make or degrade. If two organisms have nearly identical 16S sequences but one carries an antibiotic resistance gene and the other doesn't, a 16S survey can't distinguish them. If the same species has acquired a new gene by horizontal transfer that radically changes what it can do, 16S can't see it. The 16S is a name tag. It tells you who's in the room. It doesn't tell you what they're carrying in their pockets.

To get at what the organisms are doing, you need to sequence not just the 16S gene but all the DNA in the sample. This is called **shotgun metagenomics**, and it is the dominant approach in the modern microbiome field. The name "shotgun" comes from genome sequencing, where it refers to randomly fragmenting DNA into many small pieces, sequencing them all, and then computationally reassembling the original sequence. In shotgun metagenomics, you do the same thing, but your starting material is not the DNA of a single organism — it's the total community DNA from an environmental sample, containing fragments from hundreds or thousands of different species simultaneously.

The workflow looks like this. You extract total DNA from your sample, the same way you would for 16S. But instead of PCR-amplifying one specific gene, you skip the PCR entirely. You take the bulk DNA, you fragment it into pieces a few hundred base pairs long, you attach Illumina adapters, you load it onto a sequencer, and you sequence everything. You get back, depending on the instrument and the run, somewhere between a few gigabases and a few hundred gigabases of sequence — billions of short reads from all the organisms in your sample, in proportion roughly to their abundance.

Now the analysis is harder. With 16S, you know exactly which gene you're looking at, and the reference databases are mature. With shotgun, you have raw sequence from everything — ribosomal genes, metabolic genes, mobile genetic elements, viral DNA, plasmid DNA, human host DNA if you didn't get rid of it — and you have to figure out what's there and what it's doing using only the sequences themselves.

\[Pause.\]

There are two main analytical strategies, and they answer different questions. The first is **read-based** profiling. You take all your raw reads and compare them against a reference database of known microbial genomes, asking, for each read, which organism's genome it best matches and which gene it's part of. Tools like **Kraken2** and **MetaPhlAn** do the taxonomic side of this — they assign reads to species based on which species' genomes contain those exact sequences. Tools like **HUMAnN** do the functional side — they map reads to gene families and metabolic pathways, telling you which biological functions are encoded by the community. The output of read-based profiling is two tables: a species-abundance table similar to what you'd get from 16S, but at much higher taxonomic resolution; and a gene-abundance table telling you which functional gene families and pathways are present and in what abundance.

The second strategy is **assembly-based**. You take all your raw reads and try to computationally assemble them back into longer contiguous sequences — contigs — by finding overlaps. This is much harder than assembling a single genome, because reads from many different organisms are jumbled together, and reads from closely related organisms can mis-assemble into chimeras. But when it works, assembly gives you long stretches of genomic sequence that you can search for genes, operons, and other features. The standard assemblers for metagenomics are **MEGAHIT** and **metaSPAdes**, both of which use modified versions of de Bruijn graph assembly tuned for the chaos of mixed communities.

After assembly, you have a soup of contigs from many different organisms. The next step is **binning**, which is where the magic happens. Binning is the process of grouping contigs that came from the same original organism, even though you didn't know in advance which contigs go together. The principle is that contigs from the same genome share two features: they have similar nucleotide composition — the same GC content, the same tetranucleotide frequency patterns — and they have similar abundance in the sample, because they were all derived from the same population of cells. Modern binning algorithms — **MetaBAT2**, **CONCOCT**, **MaxBin2** — use both of these signals to cluster contigs into bins, each bin representing an attempt to reconstruct one organism's genome from the community DNA.

The output of binning is what we now call a **MAG**, a **metagenome-assembled genome**. A MAG is a draft genome reconstructed entirely from community sequencing — no culture required, often from an organism nobody has ever isolated. You can assess the quality of a MAG by checking how many universal single-copy genes it contains (completeness) and how many are duplicated (contamination), using tools like **CheckM**. A high-quality MAG might be more than 90% complete with less than 5% contamination, which is good enough to do most of the things you'd do with a cultured genome — annotate genes, infer metabolic pathways, reconstruct phylogeny.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy that, in my experience, lands best. **16S is like walking into a crowded party, asking everyone their name, and writing a guest list. Shotgun metagenomics is like recording everyone's entire conversation, transcribing it, and figuring out from the content what each person does for a living, where they live, what languages they speak, and what they're planning to do tomorrow. The 16S survey tells you who showed up. The shotgun tells you everything about them you could possibly infer from what they said.** And MAGs, the metagenome-assembled genomes — those are like stitching together someone's entire life story from the fragments of their conversation, without ever asking them directly. You walk out with a near-complete biography of someone you've never met.

The trade-offs between 16S and shotgun are worth knowing because they determine which approach a study should use. 16S is cheap — a sample costs maybe $20 to $50 to sequence, and you can run hundreds in a single MiSeq run. Shotgun is expensive — you need typically a few gigabases per sample, which on a NovaSeq is more like $50 to $200 per sample, and that goes up if you want enough depth to assemble MAGs. 16S has well-developed reference databases and standard analysis pipelines that anybody can run. Shotgun has more rapidly evolving tools, more computational overhead, and requires significant expertise to do well. 16S, because it relies on PCR with universal primers, captures essentially all bacteria and archaea but is biased by primer preferences. Shotgun captures everything — bacteria, archaea, viruses, eukaryotes, free DNA — but is more sensitive to host contamination and to differences in DNA extraction efficiency between organisms.

The rule of thumb is: if your question is "who is here and how does community composition change between groups," 16S is often enough, and it's cheap. If your question is "what are these organisms doing, what genes do they carry, and can I reconstruct novel genomes," you need shotgun. The most rigorous microbiome studies these days do both: 16S as a cheap survey on hundreds or thousands of samples to establish broad patterns, and shotgun on a strategically chosen subset to drill into mechanism.

That's the methodological landscape. Now let me tell you what we did with it.

## SEGMENT 5 — The Human Microbiome Project and the catalog era

In 2007, with the cost of sequencing dropping rapidly and the 16S workflow becoming standardized, the U.S. National Institutes of Health launched an enormous coordinated effort called the **Human Microbiome Project**, or HMP. It was modeled, in a way, on the Human Genome Project — a large, well-funded, multi-center effort to produce a foundational reference dataset that the rest of the field could build on. The Human Genome Project gave us the sequence of one (composite) human. The Human Microbiome Project was supposed to give us a catalog of the microbes that lived in and on humans, sampled across body sites and people, sequenced deeply, and made publicly available.

The HMP ran in two phases. Phase one, from 2007 to 2014, sampled around three hundred healthy adults at five major body sites — gut (via stool), skin (multiple locations), oral cavity (multiple locations including tongue, gum, and tooth surfaces), respiratory tract (nasal passages), and urogenital tract (vagina, in women). At each site, they did 16S sequencing for hundreds of samples and shotgun metagenomics for a strategically selected subset. The output was a reference catalog of the human microbiome in apparent health — what bacteria and archaea live where, in what abundances, and with what variability between people. Phase two, from 2014 to 2016, called the **Integrative Human Microbiome Project** or iHMP, focused on three specific disease cohorts — pregnancy and preterm birth, inflammatory bowel disease, and prediabetes — and tracked microbiome dynamics longitudinally over time alongside host molecular measurements.

The findings from HMP phase one, published in a flurry of papers in 2012, established several foundational facts about the human microbiome. First, **every body site has its own distinct microbial community.** The bacteria on your tongue are different from the bacteria between your teeth, which are different from the bacteria in your gut, which are different from the bacteria on your skin, which differ between your forehead and your forearm. The composition is so distinctive that you can pretty reliably guess which body site a sample came from just by looking at its microbiome profile. Second, **there is enormous person-to-person variation, but specific body sites in a given person tend to be stable over time.** Your gut microbiome at age twenty is meaningfully different from your roommate's, but yours at age twenty is mostly similar to yours at age twenty-five, barring antibiotics or major dietary changes. Third, **functional capacity is more conserved across people than species composition.** Two healthy people might have very different gut bacterial species but similar abilities to ferment dietary fiber, synthesize vitamins, and metabolize bile acids. The redundancy in the system means many different species can do the same metabolic jobs.

\[Pause.\]

Around the same time, a parallel European effort called **MetaHIT** — Metagenomics of the Human Intestinal Tract — was doing similar work focused specifically on the gut, with an emphasis on shotgun metagenomics from the start. MetaHIT contributed enormously to the field, including an early and somewhat controversial idea called the **enterotype** hypothesis, proposed by Peer Bork's group in 2011. The idea was that human gut microbiomes might fall into a small number of discrete clusters — three were proposed, dominated respectively by _Bacteroides_, _Prevotella_, and _Ruminococcus_ — that you'd be sorted into based on long-term diet. The enterotype concept caught fire in the popular press and was for a while sold as something like a microbiome blood type, with predictive value for diet and disease. It hasn't held up well to scrutiny — most subsequent analyses find that gut microbiome variation is more continuous than discretely clustered — but the underlying observation, that long-term diet shapes gut microbiome composition in major ways, is solid.

A third large effort, this one citizen-science, deserves mention because of its scale and accessibility. The **American Gut Project**, founded by Rob Knight at UC San Diego in 2012, took a crowdfunding approach: you paid about a hundred dollars, you got a stool sampling kit, you sent in your sample plus a detailed questionnaire about your diet and lifestyle, and they did 16S sequencing on it and sent you back a report on your gut microbiome. By the mid-2010s, the American Gut Project had sampled over ten thousand people, making it the largest single dataset of human gut microbiomes ever assembled and the basis for many epidemiological analyses linking diet, lifestyle, and microbiome composition. The democratized, opt-in design meant the cohort was biased toward health-conscious people willing to mail their poop to a lab in California, but the sheer scale produced robust findings about, for instance, the effects of dietary plant diversity on gut microbiome diversity.

The combined output of HMP, MetaHIT, American Gut, and the dozens of smaller studies of the early 2010s was a **reference catalog** — a baseline picture of what healthy human microbiomes look like, in different body sites, across different populations. That catalog is what every subsequent study compares against. When you read a paper that says "the gut microbiomes of patients with disease X were significantly different from healthy controls," there is, somewhere in the background, an HMP-era dataset defining what "healthy" looks like. The catalog era was crucial. It gave the field its ground.

It also revealed something humbling: even after thousands of healthy people had been sequenced, the genus-level taxa identified were limited and the **functional core** — the set of microbial functions present in essentially everyone — was clearer than any specific species core. There is no single bacterial species that everyone has. There are functions — fiber fermentation, short-chain fatty acid production, bile acid transformation — that almost everyone's gut community can perform, but the specific species doing those jobs varies from person to person. Your microbiome is more like a guild of workers organized by trade than a fixed set of named individuals. That metaphor — guild rather than census — has stuck, and it's a useful corrective when you read sensational claims about specific bacteria curing or causing diseases.

## SEGMENT 6 — The gut microbiome up close: Bacteroidetes, Firmicutes, and the metabolic engine

Let me zoom in on the gut microbiome, because it's the body site that gets the most attention, both because of its size — your colon contains the densest microbial community on Earth, with something like 10^11 cells per gram of contents — and because of its plausible connections to systemic health. If you walk away from this lecture with a clear mental picture of what your gut microbiome looks like and what it's doing, you'll be ahead of most journalism on the subject.

Picture your colon. It's a thick-walled muscular tube about a meter and a half long, holding maybe a kilogram of partially digested food residue and water on its way out. The inner surface is lined with a mucus layer about a hundred microns thick, separating the bacteria from the actual gut epithelial cells. In and around that mucus, suspended in the luminal contents, live the bacteria — somewhere on the order of 30 to 100 trillion cells, depending on whose estimate you believe, encoding several million genes, doing chemistry around the clock.

The vast majority of those bacteria belong to two phyla: **Bacteroidetes** and **Firmicutes**. Together these two groups make up roughly 90% of the gut bacterial community in most healthy adults, with the remaining 10% split among Actinobacteria (which includes _Bifidobacterium_, the bacterium most probiotics try to deliver), Proteobacteria (which includes _E. coli_ and many pathogens), Verrucomicrobia (which includes _Akkermansia muciniphila_, a darling of the metabolic-health literature), and a long tail of minor phyla. The ratio of Firmicutes to Bacteroidetes — the "F:B ratio" — became a popular biomarker in the early 2010s, with claims that a high F:B ratio was associated with obesity, but as so often happens, the relationship turned out to be much messier than the headlines suggested. You'll see F:B ratios in papers; treat them with appropriate skepticism.

\[Pause.\]

What are these bacteria actually doing? The single most important function is **fermenting the dietary fiber that you can't digest yourself.** Your own digestive enzymes are great at breaking down starches, sugars, fats, and proteins, but they're useless against the complex carbohydrates in plant cell walls — cellulose, hemicellulose, pectin, resistant starches, beta-glucans, and the broader class of polysaccharides we lump together as "dietary fiber." These compounds pass through your small intestine essentially undigested. When they reach your colon, they encounter bacteria — particularly _Bacteroides_ species and many Firmicutes — that carry vast arsenals of enzymes called carbohydrate-active enzymes, CAZymes, capable of breaking down hundreds of different plant polysaccharides. The _Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron_ genome alone encodes more carbohydrate-degrading enzymes than your entire human genome does.

The bacteria ferment these fibers and produce, as their primary metabolic outputs, **short-chain fatty acids** — mostly acetate, propionate, and butyrate. These short-chain fatty acids are not waste products. They are absorbed across your colonic epithelium and used by your own cells as energy and signaling molecules. Butyrate, in particular, is the preferred fuel of your colonic epithelial cells — your gut lining literally runs on a metabolite produced by bacteria fermenting fiber. Propionate goes to your liver, where it modulates gluconeogenesis. Acetate enters the bloodstream and influences peripheral metabolism, including in the brain. The estimate is that gut bacterial fermentation contributes something like 5 to 10% of your total daily caloric intake — energy you couldn't have extracted without them.

So when nutritionists tell you to eat more fiber, they're not really telling you to eat fiber for the fiber's sake. They're telling you to feed your microbiome, so it produces more butyrate, propionate, and acetate, which keeps your colonic lining healthy and modulates your metabolism. The fiber is a substrate for the bacteria, and the bacteria are the engine that turns it into something useful.

The gut microbiome does many other things besides fiber fermentation. It synthesizes vitamins — vitamin K and several B vitamins, including folate, riboflavin, and biotin. It transforms bile acids, modifying them in ways that affect cholesterol metabolism and lipid absorption. It metabolizes drugs — there's a whole subfield called pharmacomicrobiomics that studies how gut bacteria modify oral medications. It produces neurotransmitters and neuroactive compounds — gamma-aminobutyric acid, serotonin precursors, tryptophan metabolites — that can signal to the host nervous system through the **gut-brain axis**, a topic we'll return to. It competes with potential pathogens for nutrients and binding sites, providing **colonization resistance** that keeps invaders like _Salmonella_ and _Clostridioides difficile_ at bay. And it trains and modulates your immune system from infancy onward, calibrating it to tolerate harmless inputs and respond aggressively to threats.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the picture I want you to hold. **Your colon is a giant fermentation vessel housing a microbial workforce that does industrial-scale chemistry on your behalf — chemistry your own cells lack the enzymes to perform.** The workforce is paid in fiber and other compounds that escape upstream digestion, and it pays you back in short-chain fatty acids, vitamins, signaling molecules, immune calibration, and pathogen defense. If you starve the workforce — by eating a low-fiber, highly processed diet — the workforce starts eating the mucus layer of your gut lining instead, which compromises barrier integrity and may contribute to a low-grade inflammatory state. If you blow up the workforce with broad-spectrum antibiotics, the colonization resistance breaks down, opportunistic pathogens move in, and you can end up with a _C. difficile_ infection. The whole system is a coupled host-microbe metabolic and immunological partnership, and disrupting it has consequences.

The composition of your gut microbiome is shaped by many factors — birth mode (vaginal versus C-section), infant feeding (breast milk versus formula), antibiotic exposure especially in early life, diet over weeks to years, geography, host genetics to some extent, and host immune state. But the single biggest knob, by most measures, is **long-term diet**. People who eat very different diets have very different gut microbiomes. The classical comparison is between people eating a high-fiber, plant-rich diet (such as rural African populations studied by Erica Sonnenburg, Justin Sonnenburg, and others) and people eating a typical Western low-fiber, high-sugar, high-processed-food diet. The high-fiber populations have more diverse gut microbiomes, with higher abundance of fiber-fermenting taxa and short-chain fatty acid producers. The Western populations have lower diversity, often with a relative expansion of mucus-degrading and bile-tolerant species. Diet shifts can move your microbiome within days for some taxa, but the deep, stable architecture of your community is largely set by what you eat over months and years.

That's the gut. Let's talk about the most successful clinical application of microbiome science so far, which is also the cleanest demonstration that gut microbiome composition causally matters for at least one human disease.

## SEGMENT 7 — Fecal microbiota transplant for C. difficile: the proven success

If somebody in twenty years asks you to name the one thing the microbiome era unambiguously delivered for medicine, the answer, today, is **fecal microbiota transplant for recurrent _Clostridioides difficile_ infection.** It is the cleanest, most reproducible, most clinically validated microbiome therapy in existence. It is also delightfully simple in concept, almost gross in execution, and a perfect demonstration that gut microbiome composition can be the difference between life and death for at least some patients.

Let me set up the problem. _Clostridioides difficile_ — formerly called _Clostridium difficile_, often shortened to **C. diff** — is an anaerobic spore-forming bacterium that lives at low levels in the gut of a small fraction of healthy people and at high levels in hospital environments, where its tough spores can survive on surfaces for months. In a healthy gut with an intact microbial community, C. diff is held in check by colonization resistance — it can't get a foothold against the established competitors. But take a course of broad-spectrum antibiotics — clindamycin is a notorious offender, but most broad-spectrum agents will do it — and you flatten the resident microbiota. C. diff, whose spores are antibiotic-resistant, is one of the few things still standing. It germinates, expands into the empty niche, and starts producing toxins that damage the colonic epithelium, causing severe diarrhea, abdominal pain, and in the worst cases pseudomembranous colitis and toxic megacolon. C. diff infection kills tens of thousands of people in the United States every year. It is the leading cause of hospital-acquired infectious diarrhea.

The first-line treatment is, paradoxically, more antibiotics — vancomycin or fidaxomicin, targeted at C. diff itself. This works for the acute episode in most cases. But there's a recurrence problem. In something like 20 to 30% of patients, the infection comes back within weeks of stopping antibiotic therapy, because the antibiotics that killed off C. diff also killed off everything else, and the gut community still hasn't recovered enough to provide colonization resistance, so the next batch of germinating spores has nothing to compete against. After two recurrences, the chance of a third is even higher. You can end up with a patient who has been on rotating courses of antibiotics for months, never quite clearing the infection, getting more depleted and miserable with each cycle.

\[Pause.\]

The intuition behind fecal microbiota transplant is almost insultingly simple. **If the problem is a missing microbial community, then transplant a healthy microbial community from somebody who has one.** Take stool from a healthy donor — screened for transmissible pathogens — and deliver it to the patient's colon, either by colonoscopy, nasoenteric tube, enema, or oral capsules. The transplanted community engrafts in the patient's gut, restores colonization resistance, and outcompetes C. diff. The infection clears. Cure rates in well-conducted trials run around 85 to 90% after a single FMT, compared to roughly 30% with another round of vancomycin. The effect size is so large that the original randomized trial — the famous van Nood trial published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2013 — was stopped early on ethical grounds, because the FMT arm was so dramatically better than the antibiotic arm that it would have been unfair to keep randomizing patients to the inferior treatment.

The history of FMT is older than you might think. Versions of the procedure have been described in traditional Chinese medicine going back to the fourth century, where it was called "yellow soup" and used for severe diarrhea. In modern Western medicine, a 1958 paper by Ben Eiseman and colleagues described using fecal enemas to treat what was then called pseudomembranous enterocolitis, with good results. But the technique remained a fringe practice for decades — partly because the science wasn't there to explain why it worked, partly because, frankly, nobody wanted to be the first major academic medical center to formally adopt poop transplants as a procedure. The combination of the 2007-onwards microbiome revolution, providing the scientific rationale, and the urgent clinical need driven by hospital-acquired C. diff epidemics finally pushed FMT into the mainstream.

Today, FMT for recurrent C. diff is widely practiced. The FDA has approved two defined microbial community products derived from screened donor stool — **Rebyota** (Ferring, approved 2022) and **Vowst** (Seres Therapeutics, approved 2023). Vowst is particularly interesting because it's an oral capsule formulation of purified spore-forming bacteria, lyophilized and standardized — a major step away from raw stool and toward a defined, industrially produced microbial drug. Both products have shown around 75 to 85% prevention of recurrence in their pivotal trials, comparable to traditional whole-stool FMT.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy that makes this concrete. **FMT is like fixing a wrecked ecosystem by introducing a healthy one. Your colon, after months of antibiotics, is like a forest that has been clear-cut and then taken over by an invasive species. You can keep spraying herbicide — more antibiotics — but as long as the soil and the early colonizers and the seed bank are all wrong, the invasive species will keep coming back. FMT is reseeding the entire forest from a healthy patch — bringing in the whole community of plants, fungi, soil microbes, the works — so the native ecosystem can re-establish and outcompete the invader. The herbicide killed the symptom; the transplant rebuilt the cause.**

There are caveats. FMT for C. diff is a triumph; FMT for almost everything else is, so far, much less successful. Trials of FMT for ulcerative colitis show modest benefit in some studies and none in others. FMT for irritable bowel syndrome, obesity, metabolic syndrome, autism spectrum disorder, and various other conditions have produced a mix of intriguing early-phase results and disappointing failures in larger trials. The reason, almost certainly, is that C. diff is a uniquely simple problem — one bad actor in an emptied niche — and most other microbiome-associated conditions are far more complex. There's no single bug to outcompete. There's a complicated host-microbe-immune-environmental tangle whose causal architecture we don't fully understand. FMT works for C. diff because the model is clear. For everything else, the model isn't.

There are also safety concerns. In 2019, two patients receiving FMT from the same donor in a clinical trial died of bacteremia caused by an antibiotic-resistant _E. coli_ that the donor was carrying — the donor had been screened, but not specifically for that resistance pattern. This led to FDA tightening of donor screening requirements. There have been cases of unexpected transfer of host phenotypes — most notoriously, a 2015 case report of a woman who received FMT from her overweight daughter and subsequently gained significant weight herself, which has not been definitively shown to be caused by the FMT but raised pointed questions. Donor screening is now extensive, with checks for transmissible pathogens, antibiotic resistance genes, and increasingly, broader microbial and metabolic profiles to ensure compatibility.

But the point I want to land here is: **FMT works for C. diff. It is the proof of principle that microbiome composition causally matters and that transplanting a community can cure a disease. Everything else in microbiome therapeutics — the engineered consortia, the defined live biotherapeutic products, the gut-brain interventions — is trying to build on this proof of principle by progressively making the therapy more defined, more reproducible, and more applicable to other conditions.** Whether they succeed is the open frontier we'll come back to in the last segment.

## SEGMENT 8 — Beyond the gut: skin, oral, vaginal, and lung microbiomes

The gut gets most of the attention, but every body surface that touches the outside world has its own distinctive microbial community, and each of those communities matters for host biology in ways the field is only beginning to map. Let me give you the highlights for the four other major body-site microbiomes.

The **skin microbiome** lives on the outer surface of you, which sounds like it should be a single environment but is actually wildly heterogeneous. Different skin sites have radically different ecologies. Oily sites — face, scalp, upper back — are dominated by lipophilic _Cutibacterium_ species (formerly called _Propionibacterium_), particularly _Cutibacterium acnes_, which feeds on sebum and is the bacterium associated with acne when it overgrows in occluded follicles. Moist sites — armpits, groin, between toes — are dominated by _Staphylococcus_ and _Corynebacterium_ species, which produce the volatile compounds responsible for body odor. Dry sites — forearms, lower legs — have lower microbial density and a more even mix of taxa. Your skin microbiome is shaped by sweat composition, sebum production, pH, immune surveillance, and the relentless mechanical and chemical assault of soap, deodorant, cosmetics, and clothing.

The clinical implications are real but understudied. Atopic dermatitis — eczema — is associated with reduced skin microbial diversity and overgrowth of _Staphylococcus aureus_. Acne is associated with specific strains of _C. acnes_, not just total bacterial load. There's growing evidence that early-life skin microbial exposure shapes immune tolerance, with some implications for allergy development. The frontier is engineered topical microbial products — applying live bacteria to the skin as a treatment for eczema or acne — and a few startups are pursuing this, though the clinical evidence is still early.

\[Pause.\]

The **oral microbiome** is arguably the most complex microbial community in the human body, with hundreds of bacterial species occupying distinct niches across the tongue, gums, cheek, palate, and tooth surfaces. The defining feature of the oral environment is **biofilms** — structured multi-species communities attached to tooth surfaces, called dental plaque. Plaque is a beautiful and somewhat terrifying example of microbial cooperation: early-colonizing species attach to the tooth surface, modify the local environment, and provide attachment sites for secondary colonizers, building up complex layered communities over hours and days. _Streptococcus mutans_ and related species ferment dietary sugars to lactic acid, which dissolves tooth enamel — that's the proximate cause of dental caries. Other species, particularly in subgingival plaque below the gumline, are responsible for periodontal disease — chronic inflammation of the gums that ultimately destroys the tissue holding teeth in place. _Porphyromonas gingivalis_ is the canonical keystone pathogen of periodontitis, with growing evidence that it also contributes to systemic inflammation and may play a role in cardiovascular disease and even Alzheimer's pathology — though the latter is still contested.

The oral microbiome also matters because the mouth is the entry point for the gut. Many of the bacteria found at lower abundance in the gut originated in the oral cavity, swallowed in saliva. And the oral microbiome has become surprisingly important in ancient DNA studies, which we'll come back to in Segment 17 — calcified dental plaque, called dental calculus, traps bacterial DNA from the time of death and preserves it for thousands of years.

The **vaginal microbiome** is, in healthy women of reproductive age, often dominated by a single genus: _Lactobacillus_. This is unusual — most body site microbiomes are diverse — but it's functional. _Lactobacillus_ species produce lactic acid, which keeps the vaginal pH low (around 4), and this acidic environment suppresses the growth of opportunistic pathogens. When the _Lactobacillus_-dominated community is disrupted — by antibiotic use, hormonal changes, douching, or other factors — the result is often **bacterial vaginosis**, a condition characterized by overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria and increased risk of sexually transmitted infections and preterm birth in pregnancy. The vaginal microbiome is also a key part of the iHMP phase-two study on pregnancy and preterm birth, which found that specific vaginal community compositions — particularly those low in _Lactobacillus crispatus_ and high in diverse anaerobes — correlate with preterm birth risk, though the causal mechanisms are still being worked out.

\[Pause.\]

The **lung microbiome** is the most recent body-site microbiome to be widely characterized, and its existence was controversial for a while. The lungs were classically thought to be sterile, with any bacteria found being interpreted as contamination from the upper airway during sampling. Sensitive sequencing has now established that healthy lungs do harbor low-density microbial communities, largely seeded by microaspiration of upper airway flora and shaped by host immune surveillance and mucociliary clearance. The lung microbiome is altered in many diseases — chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, asthma, cystic fibrosis, lung cancer — though again, separating cause from consequence is hard, because diseased lungs have altered immunology and mucus that itself changes microbial colonization.

There are smaller, less-studied microbiomes at other sites — the bladder (yes, urine is not sterile), the breast (with possible implications for breast cancer), even the eye and the inner ear in some recent reports. Every wet or partially wet surface that interfaces with the outside world has its own community. The microbiome is not just the gut. It's a body-wide patchwork of distinctive ecologies, each shaped by local physiology, each potentially relevant to local and systemic health.

## SEGMENT 9 — The microbiome and disease: the correlation cloud and the causality gap

We need to spend a segment on what is, frankly, the most overhyped and most genuinely interesting part of the microbiome field: the connections between microbiome composition and human disease. Almost every chronic disease you can think of has been associated, in some study somewhere, with altered gut microbiome composition. Inflammatory bowel disease, obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, depression, anxiety, autism spectrum disorder, Parkinson's disease, multiple sclerosis, rheumatoid arthritis, allergies, asthma, atopic dermatitis, colorectal cancer, response to cancer immunotherapy — pick a chronic disease, search PubMed for it plus "microbiome," and you'll find papers reporting associations. This is what the field privately calls the **microbiome correlation cloud**, and learning to read it critically is one of the most important skills you can develop as a consumer of this literature.

Here is the core methodological problem. Almost every microbiome-disease association is observational. You sample the gut microbiomes of people with disease X and people without it, you compare the compositions, you find some differences, and you publish. The differences are usually statistically significant, sometimes dramatic. But the design tells you nothing about causality. **Did the altered microbiome cause the disease? Did the disease cause the altered microbiome? Did some third factor — diet, medication, host genetics, environmental exposure — cause both? Or are the differences artifacts of how the samples were collected, when in disease course, in which subpopulation?** All four are usually consistent with the data, and untangling them is hard.

\[Pause.\]

Let me walk through three high-profile cases to make the issues concrete.

**Obesity.** A 2006 paper by Peter Turnbaugh and colleagues in Jeff Gordon's lab made a splash by reporting that obese mice had a higher Firmicutes-to-Bacteroidetes ratio than lean mice, and that transferring gut microbiota from obese mice to germ-free mice caused those mice to gain more weight than mice receiving lean microbiota. This was followed by similar reports in humans. The microbiome-causes-obesity story became wildly popular in the press. Subsequent large meta-analyses, however, have found the F:B ratio difference between obese and lean humans to be small, inconsistent, and not very predictive at the individual level. The human FMT trials for obesity have produced modest, transient effects at best. The current consensus is that gut microbiome composition probably contributes modestly to weight regulation in humans, but it is nothing like the dominant driver the early studies suggested. The mouse studies were real; their human translation has been disappointing.

**Depression and the gut-brain axis.** The gut-brain axis is a real phenomenon — the gut and brain communicate via the vagus nerve, via circulating microbial metabolites (especially short-chain fatty acids and tryptophan derivatives), via the immune system, and via the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis. Animal studies have shown that gut microbiome manipulations can affect mouse behavior in tests of anxiety, depression-like states, and social interaction. Human observational studies have reported microbiome differences between depressed and non-depressed people. A few small FMT trials and dietary intervention trials have shown some signals. But the human data remain noisy, the effect sizes when present are modest, and the field is rife with overreach. There's a real biology here; there's also a lot of speculation that has run far ahead of the evidence.

**Inflammatory bowel disease.** This is probably the strongest case for a causal microbiome contribution to a major chronic disease in humans. Patients with Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis have consistently altered gut microbiomes — reduced diversity, depletion of butyrate-producing Firmicutes, expansion of certain Proteobacteria. Genome-wide association studies have identified IBD risk genes involved in bacterial sensing and handling (NOD2, autophagy genes). Mouse models with specific gene knockouts develop IBD-like disease only in the presence of gut bacteria, not when raised germ-free. The microbiome looks genuinely involved in IBD pathogenesis. But here, too, the directionality is hard — inflammation alters the gut environment in ways that select for some bacteria over others, so the dysbiosis might be partly a consequence of inflammation rather than purely a cause. The truth, almost certainly, is that microbiome and host immunity interact in a feedback loop that, once disturbed, can stably shift to a disease state — and the therapeutic question is whether you can break the loop by intervening on either side.

\[Pause.\]

The single best tool the field has for testing causality in human-relevant ways is the **humanized-microbiome mouse model**, sometimes called the gnotobiotic mouse model. You take germ-free mice — raised in sterile isolators since birth, with no microbiome at all — and you colonize their guts with stool from a human donor. The mouse's gut now contains, more or less, the donor's microbial community. You can then test whether mice colonized with diseased-donor microbiomes develop disease-like phenotypes that mice colonized with healthy-donor microbiomes do not. If they do, you've established that the microbiome is sufficient to transfer the phenotype, which is the strongest evidence for causality short of a human trial. Humanized-microbiome mice have been used to study obesity, IBD, autism, depression, and many other conditions, with results that range from striking to inconclusive.

The catch is that humanized mice are expensive — germ-free facilities are specialized and limited — and that mice are not humans. A mouse colonized with human microbiota is still a mouse, with mouse physiology, mouse immune system, mouse diet. The microbiome that engrafts is often quite different from what was in the donor's gut. The results inform but don't settle the question.

So here is the analogy I want you to hold for the correlation cloud. **The microbiome literature is like an enormous scatter plot of correlations between bug abundances and disease phenotypes — many real associations mixed with many false positives, where the underlying causal arrows are mostly unknown. Almost any disease can be associated with some microbial signature; almost no association can be confidently called causal without intervention experiments. The path from correlation to causation runs through humanized mice, intervention trials in humans, and increasingly, mechanistic studies of how specific microbial metabolites act on specific host pathways. We are early in that journey, even though twenty years have passed since the field began.**

When you read a microbiome study, the questions to ask are: Is this association observational or interventional? How big is the effect size? Has it been replicated in independent cohorts? Are there obvious confounders — medications, diet, demographics? Has anybody done the humanized-mouse or FMT experiment? What's the proposed mechanism, and is it supported by mechanistic data, or just speculation? These questions will save you from a lot of overinterpretation, and they're the same questions you should ask of any observational health science.

That's the gut and disease landscape. Now we take our break.

## SEGMENT 10 — Environmental metagenomics: Tara Oceans and the planetary microbiome

Welcome back. Let's leave the human body for a while, because the microbiome story is much bigger than humans. The same culture-independent DNA sequencing approach that revealed the human microbiome has been applied to every environment we can sample — the ocean, soil, freshwater, deep subsurface, hot springs, ice cores, the atmosphere, the built environment — and what it has revealed is that microbes are everywhere, more diverse than we ever imagined, and doing planetary-scale chemistry that shapes everything from ocean productivity to atmospheric composition to the carbon cycle to the nitrogen cycle. The microbial world is the substrate on which all macroscopic life operates, and metagenomics is finally letting us see it.

The single most ambitious environmental metagenomics project of the past two decades is probably **Tara Oceans**, an expedition led by Eric Karsenti, Chris Bowler, and many collaborators, with key bioinformatics led by Shinichi Sunagawa at ETH Zurich and Peer Bork at EMBL Heidelberg. From 2009 to 2013, a research schooner called Tara sailed around the world, stopping at over 200 stations across all the major ocean basins, and at each station, sampling seawater at multiple depths — surface, deep chlorophyll maximum, mesopelagic — and filtering out everything from viruses to large plankton through a series of size fractions. The samples were preserved and shipped back to dozens of labs around the world for analysis. The result was the first systematic catalog of microbial and viral diversity in the global ocean.

The Tara Oceans dataset, published in a famous bundle of papers in _Science_ in 2015, was staggering. They identified roughly **40 million novel microbial genes**, the vast majority of them previously unknown. They constructed thousands of metagenome-assembled genomes of marine bacteria, archaea, and protists. They mapped the global distribution of microbial functions — nitrogen fixation, photosynthesis, sulfur cycling — and showed how those functions vary with latitude, depth, and ocean chemistry. They identified temperature as the single biggest predictor of marine microbial community composition, with major implications for how the ocean's biogeochemistry will respond to climate change. Subsequent extensions — Tara Polar, Tara Pacific — have expanded the coverage to polar and reef environments.

\[Pause.\]

The dominant microbes in the open ocean are not the ones you'd guess from textbook microbiology. The most abundant photosynthetic organism on Earth is a tiny cyanobacterium called **_Prochlorococcus_**, discovered by Sallie Chisholm and her collaborators in the late 1980s. _Prochlorococcus_ cells are smaller than most bacteria — under a micron across — and they thrive in the nutrient-poor open ocean by being extraordinarily efficient at low light and low nutrients. There are estimated to be around 10^27 _Prochlorococcus_ cells in the ocean, collectively responsible for something like 5% of global photosynthesis. They were essentially unknown until forty years ago, and their genomic diversity — multiple ecotypes adapted to different light and depth regimes — has been a textbook case for studying microbial ecology and evolution. Closely related is **_Synechococcus_**, another marine cyanobacterium with similarly global distribution.

The most abundant heterotrophic group in the ocean is the **SAR11 clade**, named after a clone library from Sargasso Sea sample 11, where they were first identified by 16S sequencing in 1990. SAR11 bacteria — the best-known of which is _Pelagibacter ubique_ — make up around a third of all bacterial cells in surface ocean waters and are perhaps the most numerous organism on the planet, with a global population on the order of 10^28 cells. They eat dissolved organic carbon at incredibly low concentrations and recycle it back into the marine food web. SAR11 was identified by 16S long before anyone managed to culture it; the first cultured representative wasn't isolated until 2002, and even then, it required highly specialized low-nutrient conditions. They are a perfect example of the unculturable-then-culturable trajectory: discovered by metagenomics, characterized phylogenetically, eventually grown in a lab once we understood enough about their biology to figure out what they need.

Beyond the ocean, **soil metagenomics** has revealed perhaps the most complex microbial communities on Earth. A single gram of soil can contain tens of thousands of microbial species and several billion cells. Soil microbiomes drive most of the terrestrial carbon and nitrogen cycles, mediate plant nutrient uptake through symbioses with mycorrhizal fungi and nitrogen-fixing bacteria, and process essentially everything that lives, dies, and decays on land. Large efforts like the **Earth Microbiome Project**, founded by Rob Knight, Jack Gilbert, and Janet Jansson in 2010, have sampled tens of thousands of environmental sites — soils from every continent, sediments, plant rhizospheres, animal guts — and built a global picture of how environmental conditions shape microbial communities. The Earth Microbiome Project standardized the 16S V4 sequencing protocol that became the field's default, and its database is now a foundational reference for environmental microbiome studies.

\[Pause.\]

The deep subsurface — the rocks kilometers beneath the surface, both on land and under the ocean floor — has its own microbiome, dominated by slow-growing chemolithoautotrophs that get energy from inorganic chemistry (oxidizing hydrogen, reducing iron, fixing carbon from CO2). Some of these organisms have generation times of years to centuries, lurking in the rock matrix at low cell densities, doing chemistry on geological timescales. The total biomass of the deep subsurface biosphere is estimated to rival or exceed that of all surface life. Most of it has never been seen alive in a lab. We know it's there only because of DNA pulled out of drill cores and amplified.

The take-home from environmental metagenomics: **most microbial diversity on Earth lives nowhere near us. The vast majority of microbial cells, genes, and metabolic functions are in oceans, soils, sediments, and the deep subsurface, doing chemistry that runs the biogeochemical cycles of the planet. The human microbiome, fascinating as it is, is a tiny corner of the global microbial world.** Metagenomics is the only tool that gives us access to the rest. And that access has profound implications not just for understanding the planet but for finding new useful molecules, which is where we go next.

## SEGMENT 11 — Functional metagenomics: the discovery engine

So far we've been talking about metagenomics mostly as a tool for cataloging — figuring out what's where, in the gut, in the ocean, in the soil. But metagenomics has another use that may, in the long run, be more important: it's a **discovery engine for new biology.** Every new microbial genome contains genes that nobody has seen before, encoding enzymes that do chemistry nobody has characterized, sometimes producing molecules with useful biological activity that could be turned into drugs, industrial catalysts, or new molecular tools. Functional metagenomics is the practice of mining environmental DNA for these novel functions.

Two of the biggest hits in modern molecular biology came out of, or were dramatically expanded by, functional metagenomics. The first is **CRISPR-Cas systems**, the now-famous bacterial defense systems that have been turned into genome-editing tools. The original CRISPR-Cas9 system, discovered in _Streptococcus pyogenes_ and developed for editing by Jennifer Doudna, Emmanuelle Charpentier, Feng Zhang, and others, was just the beginning. Subsequent surveys of environmental microbial genomes — both cultured and metagenome-derived — have identified an entire zoo of CRISPR systems with different properties. CRISPR-Cas12a (also called Cpf1) was identified by Eugene Koonin's computational analysis of microbial genomic data and developed into a different kind of editing tool with distinct PAM requirements and cut patterns. **CRISPR-Cas13**, which targets RNA rather than DNA, was identified by Koonin's group from metagenomic data and turned into a programmable RNA-targeting system useful for RNA editing and diagnostics — the SHERLOCK diagnostic platform is built on Cas13. The CasX system was identified from groundwater metagenomes by Jill Banfield's group at Berkeley. Functional metagenomics has, essentially, kept the CRISPR toolbox expanding for a decade.

\[Pause.\]

The second major area is **natural products and antibiotics.** The classical way to discover new antibiotics was to grow soil bacteria — mostly _Streptomyces_ species, as we discussed in the antibiotics lecture — and screen their fermentation broths for compounds that killed test bacteria. This worked beautifully for a few decades but ran into the rediscovery problem: the same compounds kept showing up over and over, because the same culturable soil bacteria kept being isolated. Functional metagenomics offered an alternative: clone DNA fragments from environmental samples directly into a heterologous host like _E. coli_ or _Streptomyces lividans_, induce expression, and screen for activity. If a fragment encodes a complete biosynthetic gene cluster for a small molecule, the host might produce that molecule and you can detect it. Various academic and industrial groups have used this approach to recover novel antibiotic candidates from soil DNA that came from organisms nobody had ever cultured.

A related approach is **bioinformatic mining**: take all the sequenced microbial genomes and metagenomes, scan them computationally for biosynthetic gene clusters (using tools like **antiSMASH**), and prioritize clusters that look novel for laboratory follow-up. The Sean Brady lab at Rockefeller, the Bradley Moore lab at Scripps, and many others have used this approach to identify and characterize new natural product classes. The discovery of **teixobactin**, a new antibiotic class identified by Kim Lewis at Northeastern in 2015 using a clever culturing device called the iChip that grows previously uncultivable bacteria in situ in soil, is a related triumph — not quite metagenomics, but in the same spirit of accessing the previously inaccessible majority.

The same logic extends to other classes of useful molecules. Novel enzymes for industrial catalysis — proteases, lipases, cellulases for biofuel production — are routinely identified from environmental metagenomes, particularly from extreme environments like hot springs (where the enzymes are thermostable, useful for industrial processes) and deep-sea sediments. Novel cold-adapted enzymes from polar metagenomes are useful in food processing. Some pharmaceutical companies and biotechs have substantial pipelines built around metagenomic enzyme discovery, with players like Ginkgo Bioworks and Zymergen (which had a notable corporate collapse, but the underlying technology persists) building entire businesses around it.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy. **Functional metagenomics is like turning the world's environmental DNA into a global library of unread books, then training computers to skim the books for plots and characters you've never seen before, and finally pulling the most interesting ones off the shelf to actually read them in a lab. The library is enormous — every gram of soil, every milliliter of seawater is a new collection — and most of it has never been opened. The tools to read it — both wet-lab heterologous expression and dry-lab bioinformatic mining — are getting better every year. The CRISPR systems and novel antibiotics we've already gotten are early returns from a library we've barely begun to explore. There is, almost certainly, much more.**

The limitations are real. Many environmental gene clusters don't express in standard heterologous hosts because they need specific regulatory cues or precursors. Bioinformatic predictions of biosynthetic clusters often turn out to be silent or to produce known compounds rather than novel ones. The hit rate per attempt is low. But the absolute number of attempts you can make scales with sequencing capacity, which keeps falling in cost. So even with low hit rates, the throughput grows exponentially, and the discovery pipeline keeps producing.

This is, in the long view, maybe the most underappreciated payoff of the metagenomics era. The microbiome era didn't just give us new tools to study humans. It gave us access to a vast, ancient, uncultured chemical and genetic reservoir, and we're slowly learning how to mine it.

## SEGMENT 12 — Metatranscriptomics, metaproteomics, metabolomics: the multi-omics view

DNA tells you what's possible. To know what's actually happening in a microbial community at a given moment, you need to measure the next layers of biological information: which genes are being expressed, which proteins are being made, and which metabolites are being produced and consumed. These are the domains of **metatranscriptomics**, **metaproteomics**, and **metabolomics** — the multi-omics view of the microbiome.

Let me build the picture layer by layer. Metagenomics, which we've been discussing, gives you the DNA — the **gene content** of the community. It tells you what the community could potentially do, but not what it's actually doing. A bacterium might carry a gene for a pathway that it only expresses under specific conditions; from DNA alone, you can't tell if that pathway is on or off in this particular sample.

**Metatranscriptomics** is the same idea applied to RNA. You extract total RNA from the community, you sequence it (usually after depleting the highly abundant ribosomal RNA), and the result is a snapshot of which genes are being actively transcribed at the moment of sampling. Highly expressed genes are represented by many reads; silent genes are absent. The output is a community-level gene expression profile, telling you not just what the community could do but what it's doing right now. The technical challenges are real — RNA is much less stable than DNA, so sample collection and preservation have to be fast and careful; the dominance of rRNA means most of your reads are uninformative unless you deplete it; and the dynamic range of expression is huge, so you need deep sequencing to detect lowly expressed transcripts. But when it works, metatranscriptomics is the difference between knowing what tools are in the workshop and knowing which ones are actually being used.

\[Pause.\]

**Metaproteomics** goes one step further by measuring the proteins themselves — the actual workhorses of the cell — rather than the transcripts encoding them. Proteins are measured by mass spectrometry: you digest community proteins into peptides, you run them through a mass spectrometer, and you identify peptides by matching their masses and fragmentation patterns against predicted proteins from a metagenome-derived database. The output is a list of proteins detected and their relative abundances. Metaproteomics is technically harder than metatranscriptomics because mass spectrometry is less sensitive than sequencing, and because protein identification depends on having a good reference database — usually metagenome-derived from the same sample — to match peptide masses against. The dynamic range of protein detection is also more limited than RNA. But proteins are closer to function than transcripts are; not every transcript is translated, not every translated protein is stable, so the protein-level measurement is the most direct readout of what the community is actually doing.

**Metabolomics** measures the small molecules — the metabolites — being produced and consumed in the community. These are the actual chemical outputs of microbial metabolism: short-chain fatty acids, bile acid derivatives, amino acid metabolites, vitamins, neuroactive compounds, the entire chemical conversation between microbes and between microbes and the host. Metabolomics is also done by mass spectrometry, usually combined with chromatographic separation (HPLC-MS, GC-MS), and the output is a list of detected metabolites with their abundances. Metabolomics is the layer most directly relevant to host physiology — the metabolites produced by your gut microbiome are what actually circulate in your blood and signal to your tissues. If you're trying to understand mechanistically how a microbiome difference affects host health, metabolomics is often the most informative layer.

The combination of all these — DNA, RNA, protein, metabolites — is called **multi-omics**, and it is the gold standard for microbiome studies that aim to do mechanism rather than just description. The HMP phase-two integrative project specifically applied multi-omics across their disease cohorts; modern high-end microbiome studies often integrate at least metagenomics, metabolomics, and host transcriptomics or proteomics.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy. **If a microbial community is a factory, metagenomics is the inventory of machines on the floor, metatranscriptomics is the daily production schedule, metaproteomics is the actual count of workers operating each machine, and metabolomics is the products coming off the assembly line. To understand what the factory really makes, you want all four layers. To understand why it sometimes makes the wrong things — disease-associated states — you also need to integrate with measurements of the customer, the host.** The full multi-omics view is expensive, technically demanding, and analytically complex, but it's the closest we can currently get to a mechanistic picture of microbiome function.

There's also the integration problem. Each of these omics layers produces its own data type with its own peculiarities, and combining them statistically is nontrivial. There's a whole subfield of **multi-omics integration** developing methods — joint dimensionality reduction, network-based analysis, machine learning approaches — for finding signal across layers. The field is young and the tools are still rough, but it's where microbiome science is heading. Single-layer studies are increasingly seen as preliminary; the rigorous mechanistic work integrates many layers.

The cost story matters here too. Metagenomics has become cheap. Metatranscriptomics is moderately expensive. Metaproteomics is expensive and slow. Metabolomics is moderately expensive but requires expertise and instrumentation that not every lab has. So even though everyone agrees multi-omics is the right approach, in practice most studies still use just one or two layers, and most public datasets are metagenomics-only. The full multi-omics treatment is what you do for your flagship study, not your screening study.

## SEGMENT 13 — Single-cell microbial genomics and the rare biosphere

Metagenomics, even at its best, has a fundamental limitation: it gives you the **aggregate** of many cells. The MAG you assemble is a composite of all the genomic variation in the population. If two strains of the same species coexist in a sample at similar abundance, their genomes can chimerize during assembly, and you can't tell which gene combinations came from which actual cell. For a homogeneous population this doesn't matter much; for a heterogeneous one it can be misleading. And for the **rare biosphere** — organisms present at very low abundance that constitute most of the species diversity in a sample but only a tiny fraction of the cells — bulk metagenomics may not give you enough reads from any one rare species to assemble its genome at all.

The solution is **single-cell microbial genomics**: physically separating individual microbial cells from a sample and sequencing the genome of each one independently. This is hard, because a single bacterial cell contains only a few femtograms of DNA — millions of times less than you'd typically need for a sequencing library. So single-cell microbial genomics requires whole-genome amplification, typically using an enzyme system called **multiple displacement amplification** (MDA), based on the phi29 polymerase, which can produce micrograms of DNA from a single starting cell over several hours of isothermal amplification.

The workflow looks like this. You take an environmental sample, you dilute or sort it to deposit single cells into separate wells of a microtiter plate — using fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) with a DNA-binding stain, or using microfluidic droplet platforms. You lyse the cells in each well, you run MDA in each well to amplify the genomic DNA, and then you make a sequencing library from each well's amplified DNA. Each library is barcoded so you can pool many and run them on a single Illumina lane. After sequencing, you assemble each cell's genome independently. The output is a collection of **single-amplified genomes**, or **SAGs**, each derived from one physical cell.

\[Pause.\]

SAGs have their own quirks. MDA is biased — some regions of a genome get amplified preferentially over others — so single-cell genomes are often incomplete, with patchy coverage that can leave large fractions of the genome unrecovered. A SAG might be only 30 to 60% complete in many cases, though high-quality protocols can push this higher. SAGs are also vulnerable to contamination, because MDA will amplify any contaminating DNA at the same exponential rate as the target genome. So sample prep has to be obsessively clean. Despite these limitations, SAGs have transformed our understanding of several important parts of the microbial world.

One of the major contributions of single-cell genomics has been to the **candidate phyla** — those phylogenetic groups identified only by 16S sequences in environmental samples, with no cultured representatives. By picking individual cells of these uncultivated lineages and sequencing their genomes, researchers have gotten our first looks at the gene content, metabolic potential, and likely lifestyle of organisms we've never grown in a lab. The Tanja Woyke group at the DOE Joint Genome Institute and many academic collaborators have produced thousands of SAGs covering vast swaths of microbial diversity that bulk metagenomics underrepresents. Findings include extremely streamlined genomes for some marine bacteria (down to less than a megabase, encoding essentially the minimum needed for life as a heterotroph in seawater), bizarre metabolic combinations in deep-subsurface organisms, and entirely new phyla — like the **Candidate Phyla Radiation**, a vast group of small-genome bacteria mostly identified through metagenomics and single-cell genomics, that may collectively represent more than 15% of bacterial diversity but had been mostly invisible in cultured surveys.

\[Pause.\]

Single-cell microbial genomics also reveals **population heterogeneity** within species. Even what looks like a single bacterial species, by 16S sequence, can contain many distinct strains with significantly different gene content — different antibiotic resistance genes, different metabolic capabilities, different mobile genetic elements. The pangenome concept — the union of all genes ever found in any member of a species — captures this. A typical bacterial species might have a **core genome** of a few thousand genes present in essentially every strain, plus an **accessory genome** that varies from strain to strain and can be many times larger than the core. Single-cell genomics is one of the best tools for sampling this pangenomic diversity, because it gives you one strain at a time rather than an averaged community.

The combination of metagenomics and single-cell microbial genomics is now standard for serious microbial ecology. Use the metagenome to get the dominant organisms, the community structure, and the abundant gene content. Use single-cell genomics to fill in the rare biosphere, to resolve strain heterogeneity, and to get clean genomes for organisms that don't bin well from bulk metagenomes. The two approaches are complementary; the modern field uses both.

There's an even more recent approach worth flagging: **microfluidic single-cell metagenomics**, where droplet-based platforms can process tens of thousands of single cells per run, producing single-cell genomic data at scale rather than one at a time. This is conceptually similar to single-cell RNA sequencing in eukaryotic biology, which has revolutionized cell-type discovery in animal tissues. Single-cell microbial genomics is the prokaryotic analog, and it's just hitting the throughput where it becomes a survey tool rather than a per-cell craft project. Watch this space.

## SEGMENT 14 — Viromics: the bacteriophage component and the dark matter

Everything we've talked about so far has been about cellular microbes — bacteria, archaea, eukaryotes. But the most numerous biological entities in your gut, in the ocean, in soil, are not cells at all. They are **viruses**, almost all of which are **bacteriophages** — viruses that infect bacteria — and they constitute what is sometimes called the **dark matter of the microbiome**, because we know they're there in huge numbers but we know remarkably little about what they're doing.

The numbers are arresting. In a typical environment with abundant bacterial life, virus particles outnumber bacterial cells by roughly ten to one. There are estimated to be around 10^31 virus particles on Earth, which is more than every other kind of biological entity combined and probably greater than the number of stars in the visible universe. In your gut, there are an estimated 10^15 virus particles, mostly bacteriophages, doing the work of constantly culling and reshaping the bacterial community. Phages are the dominant predators of bacteria; they are agents of horizontal gene transfer, shuttling genes between hosts; they sculpt bacterial population dynamics on hourly to daily timescales.

The subfield that studies viral communities by sequencing is called **viromics**, and methodologically it has been the hardest of the microbiome subfields to develop. The reason is that viruses don't have a universal marker gene like the 16S rRNA. Bacteria all have 16S; archaea all have 16S; you can use one set of PCR primers to amplify and identify every bacterium and archaeon on Earth. There is no equivalent for viruses. Different viral families have different genes, different genome types (DNA versus RNA, single-stranded versus double-stranded), different gene contents, and no single gene is present in all of them. So you can't do a 16S-equivalent survey for viruses. You have to use shotgun metagenomics, ideally with a viral enrichment step that physically separates virus-sized particles from cellular ones before extracting DNA.

\[Pause.\]

The viral enrichment workflow typically involves filtering the sample to remove cells (using filters with pore sizes of 0.22 or 0.45 microns, which lets virus particles through but excludes most bacteria), then concentrating the virus-sized particles by ultracentrifugation or tangential flow filtration, then treating with DNase to destroy free DNA, then lysing the virus particles and extracting their nucleic acids. Even with this enrichment, viral metagenomes are noisy and contain substantial bacterial contamination. And once you have the sequences, you face the second hard problem: **most viral sequences don't match anything in any database.** Bacterial and archaeal reference genomes are now numerous and well-annotated; viral reference genomes are sparse and biased toward viruses of human or agricultural importance. The vast majority of phages in any environmental sample produce sequences that are unrecognizable, which is exactly why this is the "dark matter."

Despite these difficulties, the field has produced some major insights. The **crAssphage** discovery in 2014 by Robert Edwards's group at San Diego State is a great example. By doing cross-assembly of human gut metagenomes — assembling sequences that appeared across many independent samples — they identified an extremely abundant phage that had been hiding in plain sight in every gut metagenome ever produced. CrAssphage turned out to be the most abundant phage in human guts, present in something like half of all people sampled, and entirely missed by previous viral databases because nobody had ever cultured its host. The discovery prompted the recognition of a whole new family of phages, the Crassvirales, and the realization that the gut virome is dominated by uncharacterized phage groups.

The **Gut Virome Database** and other recent efforts have now produced catalogs of hundreds of thousands of distinct phage genomes assembled from gut metagenomes worldwide. The dominant phages are temperate phages of common gut bacteria — _Bacteroidetes_, _Firmicutes_, _Bifidobacterium_ — that integrate into bacterial genomes as prophages and reactivate periodically to produce new virions. Lytic phages, the kind that just infect and lyse cells without integrating, are present too but less abundant in the steady-state gut.

The functional impact of the gut virome is starting to be characterized. Phages can carry **auxiliary metabolic genes** — bacterial-origin genes that the phage has acquired and now expresses during infection, often modifying host metabolism. Phages are also major drivers of bacterial pangenome diversity, because they shuttle genes between bacterial hosts during lysogenic conversion. Some studies have associated specific viral community shifts with IBD, with C. diff infection (where altered virome composition contributes to dysbiosis and recovery), and with response to immunotherapy. There's also intense interest in **phage therapy** — using carefully selected phages to treat bacterial infections, particularly antibiotic-resistant ones — which has had some dramatic clinical successes in compassionate-use cases and is being developed commercially by several groups.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy. **The viral component of the microbiome is like the predator layer of an ecosystem you only just realized existed. You've spent decades cataloging the herbivores — the bacterial cells — and you've ignored the predators that have been culling, shaping, and rearranging the herbivore populations the whole time. Now you've started looking at the predators, and you've discovered there are ten times more of them than you thought, that most of them are unknown species, and that they may matter enormously for the dynamics of the community you'd been studying. The herbivore-only picture was incomplete in a fundamental way.**

The field is moving fast, but it's still less mature than bacterial microbiomics. Reference databases are growing, computational tools for identifying viral sequences in metagenomes (like **VirSorter**, **DeepVirFinder**, **VIBRANT**) are improving, and the integration of viromics into routine microbiome studies is becoming more common. But we are essentially in the 1990s of viromics compared to where we are in bacterial microbiomics. There's a lot still to find.

## SEGMENT 15 — Ancient DNA microbiomes: reading the past from teeth and mummies

One of the most enchanting and methodologically beautiful subfields of microbiomics is **ancient microbiome research** — the use of high-throughput sequencing to recover microbial DNA from archaeological remains, sometimes thousands of years old. The same culture-free DNA sequencing approach that revealed the modern microbiome can be applied to skeletal material, mummified tissue, coprolites (fossilized feces), and other archaeological specimens to ask what the microbiome looked like in the deep past.

The challenge with ancient DNA is, of course, **degradation**. DNA in dead tissue breaks down rapidly under most conditions. After a few centuries, what remains is short, chemically damaged fragments — typically less than 100 base pairs long, with characteristic patterns of cytosine deamination at the ends. After a few thousand years, only specific preservation conditions allow recovery — cold environments, dry environments, anaerobic environments, or specific kinds of mineralized tissue. The ancient DNA field — pioneered by Svante Pääbo, who won the 2022 Nobel Prize for sequencing Neanderthal genomes — has spent decades developing methods to extract and sequence these tiny, damaged fragments while distinguishing genuine ancient sequences from modern contamination.

For microbiome research specifically, the most productive substrates have turned out to be **dental calculus** — the mineralized plaque that accumulates on teeth — and **coprolites**, the preserved feces. Dental calculus is remarkable because the calcified mineral matrix protects bacterial DNA from degradation, sometimes preserving it for thousands or even tens of thousands of years. When archaeologists excavate a skeleton, the teeth often still carry calcified plaque from the last weeks or months of the individual's life. Drill into that calculus, extract DNA, sequence it, and you get a snapshot of that person's oral microbiome at the time of death.

\[Pause.\]

The first systematic studies of ancient oral microbiomes by Christina Warinner, Cecil Lewis, and colleagues, around 2014, established that the technique worked and produced fascinating results. They found that the oral microbiomes of medieval Europeans were broadly recognizable — many of the same species you'd find in modern mouths — but with significant differences in composition that correlated with the agricultural transition, dietary shifts, and the introduction of refined sugar and grains. The advent of farming, around 10,000 years ago, was associated with a shift in oral microbiomes toward more cariogenic (cavity-causing) species. The industrial revolution and the introduction of refined sugar were associated with another major shift. The oral microbiome carries a record of human dietary and cultural change going back to the Neolithic.

Similar work on Neanderthal dental calculus by Laura Weyrich and others has produced glimpses of Neanderthal diets and oral microbial communities — including evidence that some Neanderthals were consuming plant-rich diets with possibly medicinal use of poplar bark (a source of salicylic acid, the precursor to aspirin), and the recovery of microbial sequences from extinct lineages of oral bacteria.

Coprolites are rarer than dental calculus because preservation conditions for feces are unusual, but where they exist — particularly in arid caves and ice mummies — they yield extraordinary information about ancient gut microbiomes. Studies of coprolites from pre-Columbian sites in the Americas have found gut microbiomes more similar to those of present-day rural, non-industrialized populations than to those of modern Western populations, supporting the broader narrative that **industrialization has progressively depleted human gut microbiomes** of diversity that was present throughout most of human history. The "missing microbes" hypothesis, articulated most prominently by Martin Blaser, builds on this — the idea that modern lifestyle factors (antibiotics, processed diet, C-sections, decreased microbial exposure) have collectively eroded the human microbiome in ways that may contribute to the rise of chronic diseases of industrialization.

\[Pause.\]

A particularly dramatic application of ancient microbiomics has been the recovery of **ancient pathogen genomes**. Sequencing of skeletal material from medieval plague victims has produced complete genomes of the _Yersinia pestis_ strains that caused the Black Death, allowing direct comparison with modern plague strains and detailed reconstruction of how the pathogen has evolved over centuries. Similar work has been done on tuberculosis, leprosy, syphilis, and Spanish flu. The ability to read the genomes of ancient pathogens turns the historical record of epidemics into something we can now study molecularly — which strains caused which outbreaks, how they spread, how they evolved in response to host immunity and changing conditions.

The methods have also been turned, more recently, on environmental samples — sediment cores from lake bottoms, permafrost cores, ice cores — to reconstruct ancient environmental microbial communities and trace how microbial ecosystems have changed in response to climate shifts, human impact, and geological events. The Joint Genome Institute, the Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology, and several other major centers have substantial ancient microbiome programs.

The take-home: **the same DNA sequencing technology that lets us read living microbiomes lets us read dead ones too, going back tens of thousands of years.** The microbiome is no longer just a snapshot of contemporary biology. It is also a historical archive, recorded in bones, teeth, frozen tissues, and preserved sediments, that we can now read with the same instruments we use for living samples. The past microbiome is partly open to us, and what we're finding is that the modern human microbiome — at least in industrialized populations — is in important ways a depleted, simplified version of what humans carried with them for most of our evolutionary history.

## SEGMENT 16 — Machine learning and microbiome biomarkers

Microbiome data is high-dimensional, sparse, and compositional — the kind of dataset where modern machine learning has a lot to potentially offer, both for finding patterns that simple statistical comparisons would miss and for building predictive models that might be clinically useful. The intersection of microbiome and machine learning has become a substantial subfield, with its own conferences, journals, and increasingly its own translational ambitions.

Let me describe the basic shape. After you do your 16S or shotgun analysis, you have a feature table: rows are samples, columns are taxonomic or functional features (ASVs, species, gene families, pathways), entries are abundances. You also have metadata for each sample — disease status, age, diet, medications, geography, whatever you collected. The machine learning question is: can you train a model that takes the microbiome features as input and predicts the metadata of interest? Can you predict who has IBD, or who will respond to a particular drug, or who will progress from prediabetes to diabetes, based on their microbiome composition?

The standard approaches range from straightforward — random forests, gradient-boosted trees, regularized linear models — to more sophisticated deep learning approaches that try to learn meaningful embeddings of microbial community data. Random forests have been the workhorse for years, because they handle high-dimensional sparse data well, are robust to outliers, and provide interpretable feature-importance scores. More recent work has explored neural network architectures designed specifically for compositional or tree-structured microbiome data, sometimes incorporating phylogenetic information directly into the model architecture.

\[Pause.\]

The results are mixed and instructive. For some questions, microbiome-based prediction works surprisingly well. **Colorectal cancer detection** from stool microbiome has been one of the more robust applications — multiple large studies have shown that specific microbial signatures, particularly involving _Fusobacterium nucleatum_ and other oral-associated species translocating to the colon, can identify colorectal cancer patients with reasonably high accuracy. There are commercial diagnostic products in development based on this. **Response to checkpoint-inhibitor immunotherapy** has also shown robust microbiome associations across multiple cancer cohorts — patients whose gut microbiomes contain certain bacterial taxa, particularly _Akkermansia muciniphila_ and certain Firmicutes, respond better to immunotherapy than those whose microbiomes lack them. Several FMT trials in non-responders have shown some evidence that transferring a responder-type microbiome can improve response, though these are early-phase studies.

For other questions, predictive performance is more modest. Predicting obesity, depression, or generic "metabolic health" from microbiome alone tends to produce models with weak accuracy that don't generalize well across cohorts. The reasons are the ones we discussed earlier — the underlying biological associations are weak, the disease definitions are heterogeneous, and the confounders are massive. A model trained on a US cohort might fail completely on a Chinese cohort because the underlying microbiome composition and the demographic-environmental context are so different. **Cross-cohort generalization is the single hardest problem in microbiome machine learning**, and it's one the field is still grappling with.

\[Pause.\]

The most rigorous microbiome ML studies these days do several things right. They use large, multi-cohort datasets so they can train on one cohort and test on another, demonstrating generalization rather than just within-cohort accuracy. They use cross-validation strategies that respect the structure of the data — splitting at the subject level, not the sample level, to avoid leakage from longitudinal samples. They report not just accuracy but the actual clinical utility metrics — sensitivity and specificity at clinically relevant thresholds, calibration of predicted probabilities, comparison to existing diagnostics. They make their code and data public so others can verify and extend. The field has matured significantly in this respect over the past five years, though plenty of weak papers still get through review.

There is also growing interest in **causal machine learning** approaches for microbiome data — methods designed not just to find correlations but to infer which microbial features might be causally involved in producing a phenotype, by combining observational data with assumptions about confounding structure or with interventional data from FMT studies. These methods are still early but are the right direction for translating microbiome ML from biomarker discovery toward mechanistic understanding.

The vision in the long run is that **microbiome features become routine clinical biomarkers**, integrated into standard diagnostic and prognostic workflows alongside the genetic, transcriptomic, proteomic, and metabolomic biomarkers that are increasingly common. We're not there yet for most applications, but for a few — colorectal cancer screening, immunotherapy response prediction, C. diff recurrence risk — we're getting close. The technical pipeline is ready; the validation work for clinical use is the bottleneck.

## SEGMENT 17 — Engineered microbiome therapeutics: the frontier

Now to the most translationally exciting and most commercially uncertain part of the field: **engineered microbiome therapeutics**, the active development of drugs that consist of, or modify, microbial communities for therapeutic effect. This is where the academic microbiome research of the past two decades is now meeting the pharmaceutical industry's drug development apparatus, with mixed results so far and large open questions about what works.

Let me describe the rough taxonomy of microbiome therapeutic strategies. At the simplest end, you have **whole-stool FMT**, which we already covered. Next, **defined bacterial consortia** — preparations of multiple specific bacterial species, isolated and grown in pure culture, then mixed in defined ratios and administered to patients. The hope is that you can replicate the therapeutic effect of FMT with a more reproducible, scalable, safer product. **Seres Therapeutics** has been a major player here; their drug **Vowst** (formerly SER-109), approved by FDA in 2023 for recurrent C. diff, is a defined consortium of purified spore-forming Firmicutes derived from screened donor stool. **Vedanta Biosciences** is developing similar defined consortia for IBD, _C. diff_, and other conditions. **Finch Therapeutics** had a substantial pipeline before scaling back.

Beyond defined consortia, you have **engineered live biotherapeutic products** — bacterial strains that have been genetically modified to perform a specific therapeutic function. **Synlogic** has been the most prominent player here, with engineered _E. coli_ Nissle strains designed to consume specific toxic metabolites in the gut. Their lead program was engineered to consume phenylalanine in patients with phenylketonuria, a metabolic disease in which patients can't metabolize phenylalanine normally and accumulate it to toxic levels. The clinical trial results have been complex — some evidence of biological effect, but ultimately the company has pivoted significantly. Other engineered live biotherapeutic players include **Novome Biotechnologies** and various academic spinouts working on programmable colonization, biosensing, and metabolite delivery.

\[Pause.\]

There are also **microbiome-targeted small molecules** — drugs designed to specifically modify the activity of gut bacteria without replacing them. These include inhibitors of specific microbial enzymes (like inhibitors of beta-glucuronidase, an enzyme that reactivates drug metabolites in the gut and can cause GI toxicity), agonists of host receptors that are normally activated by microbial metabolites, and prebiotics — non-digestible compounds designed to selectively feed specific beneficial bacteria. This is a quieter but in some ways more straightforward area than live biotherapeutics, because it uses traditional drug development paradigms.

And there's the **diet-as-microbiome-modulator** approach: clinical trials testing whether specific dietary interventions, particularly high-fiber and fermented-food diets, can shift gut microbiome composition in ways that improve health. A notable example was a 2021 trial by Erica Sonnenburg, Justin Sonnenburg, and Christopher Gardner at Stanford that compared high-fiber and high-fermented-food diets and found that the fermented-food diet substantially increased microbial diversity and reduced markers of inflammation. Diet-based interventions are cheap, low-risk, and arguably underexploited compared to drug development.

\[Pause.\]

The **gut-brain axis** deserves its own paragraph because it's the most speculatively exciting and most commercially active frontier. The idea that gut bacteria signal to the brain through multiple pathways — vagus nerve, circulating metabolites, immune mediators, HPA axis — is well-established from animal studies. The translation to human therapeutics for psychiatric and neurological conditions is just starting. There are early-phase clinical trials of microbiome interventions for depression, anxiety, autism, Parkinson's disease, and Alzheimer's disease. Some of the results are intriguing — a 2019 trial of FMT for autism by James Adams at Arizona State reported sustained behavioral improvements two years post-treatment in a small cohort — but the field is at the stage where almost everything needs much larger, better-controlled replication. The gut-brain microbiome story is real but premature for the kind of confident claims you sometimes see in popular media.

The commercial landscape has been turbulent. Many of the first wave of microbiome therapeutics companies — Seres, Synlogic, Finch, Second Genome, Evelo — have had significant clinical failures or strategic pivots. Vowst's approval was a genuine milestone, but it took Seres almost a decade and several major setbacks to get there. Investors who piled into the space around 2014-2016 have largely had disappointing returns. The honest assessment is that **building microbiome therapeutics is harder than the early enthusiasm suggested.** The biology is complex, the regulatory path is unfamiliar, manufacturing live bacterial products at scale is nontrivial, and the clinical trials require careful design with appropriate endpoints. The field is now in a more realistic, slightly chastened second phase, where the surviving players have learned from the early failures and are pursuing more focused indications with better mechanistic rationale.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the synthesis. **Microbiome therapeutics is a real and growing area of drug development, with one major success (FMT and its derivatives for C. diff) and many candidates in earlier-stage development for other indications. The clinical readouts over the next five to ten years will determine whether this becomes a major new modality alongside small molecules and biologics, or whether it remains a niche area for a few specific applications. The science is sound; the translation is hard.** That's the honest state of the frontier.

What does the next decade look like? Probably: more defined consortia products for specific GI indications. Probably: real progress on engineered live biotherapeutics, despite the early Synlogic setbacks, because the underlying technology — programmable bacterial chassis, biocontainment, synthetic biology tools — keeps improving. Probably: integration of microbiome biomarkers into routine clinical care for a few specific indications (cancer immunotherapy response, colorectal cancer screening). Possibly: real microbiome-targeted therapies for some neuropsychiatric conditions, if the early gut-brain signals replicate. Almost certainly: a lot of failed trials, a lot of disappointed press releases, and steady underlying progress that compounds over time.

## SEGMENT 18 — Synthesis: what the microbiome era has actually delivered

We've covered a lot of ground in the past two hours, and I want to use this last segment to step back and synthesize what the microbiome era has actually delivered, what it has not delivered, and what to look for in the next decade. Because there's a real risk, if you read too much breathless microbiome journalism, of either thinking the field has revolutionized medicine (it mostly hasn't yet) or that it's all hype (it's not). The truth is more interesting and more nuanced.

What has the microbiome era delivered, unambiguously?

**First, a revised picture of what an animal is.** The realization that humans (and all other animals) are holobionts, half microbial by cell count, with microbial communities that perform essential metabolic and immunological functions for the host, is a permanent change to biology. The pre-microbiome view of ourselves as essentially sterile organisms that occasionally got infections was wrong, and the corrected view has reshaped textbooks, medical education, and basic biological thinking. This is not a small thing, even if it doesn't directly cure any disease.

**Second, a methodological revolution in microbiology.** Culture-free DNA sequencing has opened the unculturable majority. The number of known bacterial and archaeal phyla has expanded enormously. The microbial world we can characterize has gone from a tiny biased slice to a vast catalog that grows every year. The tools — 16S sequencing, shotgun metagenomics, single-cell genomics, viromics, multi-omics — are mature, accessible, and continually improving. Microbiology before metagenomics and microbiology after metagenomics are different fields.

**Third, FMT for C. diff.** A genuine, robust, FDA-approved therapy that demonstrates microbiome composition can causally affect disease and that microbial communities can be transferred between people with therapeutic effect. One disease, one treatment, but a real proof of principle.

**Fourth, microbiome biomarkers for a few specific clinical applications.** Colorectal cancer screening from stool. Cancer immunotherapy response prediction. C. diff recurrence risk. These are early but real translational wins.

**Fifth, a vast discovery engine for new biology.** CRISPR-Cas13. Teixobactin. Novel enzymes from extreme environments. The metagenome is one of the most productive sources of new biological tools and molecules we have ever had access to, and that productivity will continue.

\[Pause.\]

What has the microbiome era _not_ yet delivered, despite confident promises?

It has not delivered transformative treatments for the major chronic diseases that microbiome composition is correlated with — obesity, IBD, depression, autism, metabolic syndrome — beyond the C. diff case. The correlations are real but mostly weak, and the causal interventions have so far underperformed the early hype.

It has not delivered routine, individualized microbiome-based medicine. There is no widely accepted clinical test you take that profiles your microbiome and tells your doctor what to do about it. The diagnostic and prognostic uses of microbiome data are mostly still in research mode.

It has not delivered probiotics that actually do what they're advertised to do. Most over-the-counter probiotic products have weak or absent evidence for the claims on their labels. The probiotics industry, which is enormous, is largely running on aspirational marketing rather than rigorous clinical evidence for most products. Some specific probiotic strains for specific indications (like _Saccharomyces boulardii_ for antibiotic-associated diarrhea, or specific _Bifidobacterium_ strains for infant colic) have decent evidence; the vast majority don't.

It has not yet delivered on the engineered microbiome therapeutics promise at scale. Vowst is a real product. The next ones are still in trials, with uncertain outcomes.

\[Pause.\]

What should you watch for in the next decade?

Larger, better-controlled, multi-cohort studies that distinguish real microbiome-disease associations from artifacts. The field has gone through its catalog phase and its early-correlation phase; the next phase is the rigorous replication and intervention phase. Look for studies that show effects across multiple independent cohorts, that test causality with FMT or humanized-mouse experiments, that integrate multi-omics for mechanism, that use modern causal inference methods.

Maturation of the microbiome therapeutics industry. The early companies have struggled; the next generation, building on lessons learned, may or may not do better. Watch for clinical readouts from Vedanta, Seres's pipeline beyond Vowst, the engineered-bacteria companies, and academic-spinout startups with more focused indications.

Integration of microbiome data into routine clinical decision-making for specific indications, especially cancer immunotherapy, colorectal cancer screening, and C. diff prevention. The technical and validation work is mostly done; the regulatory and reimbursement work is the bottleneck.

Continued expansion of environmental and functional metagenomics. New CRISPR systems, new antibiotics, new enzymes, new metabolic pathways will keep coming out of metagenomic mining. The microbial world is enormous and underexplored, and the discovery engine has decades of productivity ahead.

Better understanding of the gut-brain axis and other host-microbe signaling axes. The mechanistic biology is genuinely interesting, even if the therapeutic translation lags. Expect significant advances in understanding how specific microbial metabolites act on specific host receptors and pathways.

And, frankly, **more humility from the field about what microbiome data can and cannot tell us.** The first wave of microbiome science was exuberant and sometimes overstated its conclusions. The second wave should be more careful, more replicable, and more honest about uncertainty. That's a normal trajectory for any field that goes from a methodological revolution to a mature clinical science.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the picture I want you to leave with. **The microbiome is a vast, ancient, planetary phenomenon that DNA sequencing finally let us see. The human microbiome is a tiny part of it that matters for human health in ways we are only beginning to map. Metagenomics is the foundational tool that unlocked all of this, and it will continue to drive the field forward — through better sequencing, better assembly, better multi-omics integration, better causal inference, and better translation. We are perhaps twenty years into a hundred-year project to understand and harness the microbial world. The first twenty years have given us a methodological revolution, a reframing of what we are, one major therapy, and a discovery pipeline. The next eighty years will give us the rest.**

Go read papers. Be skeptical of headlines. Look at the methods. Look at the effect sizes. Look at the replication. The field needs more rigorous practitioners, and that's where some of you should aim.

Thanks for your attention. I'm happy to take questions.

**Key dates and milestones.**

- 1670s: Antonie van Leeuwenhoek observes "animalcules" — bacteria — under his homemade microscope.
- 1880s: Robert Koch establishes pure-culture microbiology and the postulates that define causal microbial agents.
- 1977: Carl Woese and George Fox publish the discovery of Archaea as a third domain of life, using 16S rRNA comparative sequencing.
- 1985: Norman Pace proposes culture-independent 16S sequencing of environmental samples; foundational papers follow through 1990.
- 1990: SAR11 clade identified by 16S sequencing of Sargasso Sea samples; remains uncultured for over a decade.
- 1998: First bacterial genome (_Haemophilus influenzae_) had been sequenced (1995); microbial genomics enters maturation.
- 2002: First cultured representative of SAR11 (_Pelagibacter ubique_) isolated.
- 2006: Turnbaugh et al. publish microbiome-obesity association in mouse model.
- 2007: Human Microbiome Project (HMP) launched by NIH. MetaHIT launched in Europe.
- 2009-2013: Tara Oceans expedition collects global ocean microbiome samples.
- 2010: Earth Microbiome Project founded.
- 2012: HMP phase one main results published. American Gut Project launched.
- 2013: van Nood et al. publish randomized trial of FMT for recurrent C. diff in NEJM; stopped early for efficacy.
- 2014: crAssphage identified by cross-assembly of gut metagenomes.
- 2015: Tara Oceans flagship papers published in _Science_; teixobactin discovered.
- 2016: DADA2 published; HMP phase two (iHMP) ongoing through 2016.

**A note on the limits of this lecture.**

This script has covered the methodological backbone of the microbiome field and the main areas of application. It has not gone deep on every body site, every disease association, every analytical method, or every commercial player. Build your understanding around the methods and the conceptual frame; treat any specific clinical claim as provisional, especially the ones that get the most press attention.
