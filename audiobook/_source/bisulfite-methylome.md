## SEGMENT 1 — The epigenetic mark, and why a chemical tag on a single base matters

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. Today and for the next two hours, we are going to talk about DNA methylation — specifically, about how you measure it across an entire genome, how that measurement got started in 1992 with a single chemistry paper, how it got scaled to whole human genomes by the late 2000s, and how, in the last few years, the field has finally started to escape from the chemistry that defined it for two decades. We're going to spend the time on bisulfite sequencing and its successors — the family of methods that map the small chemical tag called 5-methylcytosine, and its cousin 5-hydroxymethylcytosine, base by base, across the three billion letters of the human genome.

Let me start with a fact that should reorient how you think about the genome. The DNA in every cell of your body is, with vanishingly small exceptions, identical. A neuron in your cortex, a hepatocyte in your liver, a T cell in your blood, a melanocyte in your skin — same sequence, same three billion letters, same chromosomes. And yet these cells are spectacularly different from one another in shape, in function, in lifespan, in which genes are expressed and which are silent. The genome is the same; the cells are not. The thing that makes them different — the thing that tells a hepatocyte to be a hepatocyte and a neuron to be a neuron, despite having identical instruction manuals — is the **epigenome**. The chemical decorations on the DNA, and on the proteins that package the DNA, that don't change the sequence but do change what gets read out of it.

\[Pause.\]

The most studied, most stable, and historically the most measurable of all those decorations is DNA methylation. And here is the analogy I want you to hold for the next two hours. **DNA methylation is the epigenetic on-off marker — a small chemical tag, a single methyl group, stuck onto certain cytosines in certain positions, that silences nearby genes without changing the underlying DNA letters.** That's the picture. A methyl group is a carbon atom with three hydrogens — CH3 — about the smallest functional decoration you can attach to anything biological. Stuck onto the five-position of a cytosine ring, it converts cytosine into **5-methylcytosine**, often abbreviated 5mC. The DNA still reads as the same sequence by base pairing. But the cell's machinery, particularly the proteins that read chromatin, knows that the methyl group is there, and treats methylated regions differently from unmethylated ones.

In humans and most other vertebrates, methylation is almost entirely deposited at one specific dinucleotide context — **CpG**, meaning a cytosine immediately followed by a guanine on the same strand, with the lowercase "p" just denoting the phosphate that links them. Roughly seventy to eighty percent of all CpG dinucleotides in your genome are methylated at any given moment. The remaining twenty or thirty percent are unmethylated, and that unmethylated minority is concentrated in highly important regulatory regions — particularly the so-called **CpG islands**, which sit at the promoters of about sixty percent of all human genes. Where you have an unmethylated CpG island at a promoter, the gene is generally expressed. Where you have a methylated CpG island at a promoter, the gene is generally silenced. That single rule — methylation at promoters correlates with silencing — is the most reliable rule in all of epigenetics, and it's the one that drove every methylation study from the 1980s through to the modern clinical methylation tests we'll meet at the end of this lecture.

If you walk out of here in two hours and you can do three things — one, sketch the bisulfite conversion reaction and explain why it lets you read methylation as a sequence change; two, describe in plain English the difference between whole-genome bisulfite sequencing, RRBS, the Illumina EPIC array, and the gentler enzymatic and nanopore alternatives, and know which to recommend for which study; three, look at any modern paper that says "we performed bisulfite sequencing and identified differentially methylated regions" and have a real intuition for what's being claimed, what the noise looks like, and what the biological interpretation is — then we've succeeded. Everything else hangs off those three.

\[Pause.\]

One last orienting thought before we dive in. Methylation matters clinically. Not in some distant theoretical way — right now, in 2026, there are commercial blood tests on the market that try to detect dozens of cancers from a single tube of blood, and the signal they read is methylation. There are commercial tests that estimate your biological age from a cheek swab, and the signal they read is methylation. There are prenatal tests that use methylation to deconvolve placental versus maternal DNA in maternal plasma. Methylation has gone from a basic-science curiosity to a clinical workhorse in about fifteen years, and the measurement technology — bisulfite sequencing and its descendants — is what made that transition possible. So this is not just a story about a clever chemistry trick from 1992. It's a story about how a single chemical conversion gave the field a window into the epigenome that we are still expanding through.

So that's the contract. Two hours, one chemical mark, and a tour through the methods — old and new — that let you read it. Let's start with the biology of methylation itself, because to understand the measurement, you have to understand what's being measured.

## SEGMENT 2 — Writers, erasers, readers, and the chemistry of 5mC

Let me build the picture of DNA methylation as a biological system. There are three sets of proteins you need to know about, and the field calls them by a tidy trio of names: **writers**, **erasers**, and **readers**.

The writers are the enzymes that put the methyl group onto cytosine in the first place. They're called **DNA methyltransferases**, abbreviated DNMTs. There are three main ones in humans. **DNMT1** is the maintenance methyltransferase — its job is to copy the methylation pattern from the parent strand to the daughter strand after DNA replication. Every time a cell divides, the genome gets copied, and every methylated cytosine on the old strand corresponds to a freshly unmethylated cytosine on the new strand. DNMT1 follows the replication fork, recognizes the half-methylated CpG sites — methylated on the old strand, unmethylated on the new — and methylates the new strand to match. That's how methylation patterns are inherited across cell divisions. Without DNMT1, every cell division would dilute the methylation pattern by half, and within a few generations the cell would forget its identity.

**DNMT3A** and **DNMT3B** are the de novo methyltransferases — the enzymes that establish methylation patterns in the first place, particularly during embryonic development, when the genome is being patterned from a relatively unmethylated zygote into the highly cell-type-specific patterns of adult tissues. DNMT3A and 3B don't need a half-methylated template; they can methylate fully unmethylated DNA. They're the painters; DNMT1 is the copier. Together, the three DNMTs maintain and establish all the methylation in the genome.

\[Pause.\]

The erasers are the enzymes that remove methyl groups. For decades, the field assumed that methylation was a one-way mark — that the only way to lose a methyl group was passive dilution, by failing to copy it during replication. Then in 2009, Anjana Rao and others discovered the **TET enzymes** — TET1, TET2, and TET3 — which actively oxidize 5-methylcytosine into a chain of intermediates, starting with **5-hydroxymethylcytosine** (5hmC), then 5-formylcytosine, then 5-carboxylcytosine. The downstream intermediates get recognized by DNA repair machinery, which excises them and replaces them with a clean, unmethylated cytosine. So TET enzymes provide an active pathway for demethylation.

The TET story is important for two reasons. First, it overturned the simple picture of methylation as a static mark. Methylation is dynamic; cells write and erase it actively. Second, and this is the part that mattered enormously for the measurement methods we're going to discuss, **5-hydroxymethylcytosine — 5hmC — turned out to be a stable intermediate in its own right.** It accumulates to substantial levels in certain tissues, especially the brain and embryonic stem cells, where it can make up several percent of all cytosines. 5hmC has its own biology, its own readers, its own genomic distribution. It is not just a transient stop on the way to demethylation; it's a distinct epigenetic mark in its own right. And as we'll see in Segment 10, the original bisulfite sequencing chemistry could not tell 5mC and 5hmC apart, which created a problem the field is still working through.

The readers are the proteins that bind methylated DNA and recruit downstream machinery to silence transcription, compact chromatin, or do whatever the methylation is signaling. The classic family is the **methyl-CpG binding domain** proteins, or **MBD proteins** — MBD1, MBD2, MBD3, MBD4, and MeCP2. They have a small protein domain that specifically recognizes a methylated CpG, and they bring along other proteins — histone deacetylases, repressive chromatin complexes — that make the local chromatin closed and silent. The most famous of the MBD proteins is MeCP2, mutations in which cause **Rett syndrome**, a severe neurodevelopmental disorder. That's the clinical reminder that the methylation reading machinery is not optional decoration; cells whose readers don't work end up profoundly impaired.

\[Pause.\]

So you have a complete writing, copying, erasing, and reading system for a single chemical mark on a single base in a four-letter code. The system is genome-wide, cell-type-specific, and tightly regulated. The marks are dynamic across development, stable within a differentiated cell, and increasingly disrupted in disease and aging. And every one of those biological observations — when methylation happens, where it happens, how it changes — depended on having a method to actually measure 5-methylcytosine across the genome. Which brings us to the measurement problem.

Here is the conceptual challenge. Methylation is a single chemical decoration on a single carbon of a single base. The methylated and unmethylated forms are otherwise chemically nearly identical — they base-pair the same way with guanine, they get copied by polymerase the same way, they sit in the chromosome at the same position. Standard DNA sequencing, of any flavor, will report both 5mC and unmethylated C as plain old "C" because that's what the polymerase reads. To detect methylation, you need a chemistry — or, in the case of nanopore, a measurement modality — that distinguishes the two. For two decades, that chemistry was bisulfite. Let's go meet it.

## SEGMENT 3 — Why methylation matters — CpG islands, imprinting, X-inactivation, retrotransposons, cancer

Before we get to the chemistry, I want to make a case for why anyone should care to measure methylation in the first place. Because the technique is real work — bisulfite is harsh, the libraries are tricky, the analysis has its own quirks — and you should know what you're buying with the effort. Let me lay out five biological domains where methylation is central, because once you see those, the motivation for genome-wide methylation maps becomes self-evident.

**Domain one: CpG island promoters and gene regulation.** I mentioned CpG islands briefly in Segment 1; let me unpack them. A CpG island is a short stretch of genome — typically 500 to 2,000 base pairs — that is unusually rich in CpG dinucleotides. Most of the genome is CpG-depleted, because methylated CpGs are mutational hotspots: the methyl group makes the cytosine prone to deamination, converting 5mC into thymine, which permanently mutates the sequence. Over evolutionary time, methylated CpGs in the bulk genome have been gradually replaced by TpGs through this mutational pressure. But about 70 percent of human gene promoters sit inside CpG islands, which have somehow escaped this depletion — because in the germline, they are kept unmethylated, and unmethylated CpGs do not deaminate at high rates. So CpG islands are the protected refugia of CpGs in the genome.

\[Pause.\]

CpG islands at promoters are normally unmethylated, and when they're unmethylated the associated gene is permissive for transcription. When a CpG island gets aberrantly methylated, the associated gene gets shut off. This rule — **CpG island methylation equals gene silencing** — is the most important regulatory consequence of methylation, and it's the one that drives most of the clinical interest in methylation patterns. A tumor suppressor gene with a methylated promoter is a silenced tumor suppressor. A cell-identity gene with a methylated promoter is a locked-off cell identity. The CpG island methylome is, in many ways, the regulatory methylome.

**Domain two: X-inactivation.** Every mammalian female cell carries two X chromosomes, but only one is transcriptionally active in each cell. The other is silenced, in a process called X-inactivation, that happens early in development and is maintained for the rest of the cell's life. The silenced X is heavily methylated across its CpG island promoters, and that methylation is what locks in the silenced state. X-inactivation was one of the first systems where the role of DNA methylation in long-term gene silencing was firmly established, and it remains a textbook example of methylation-mediated inheritance of gene expression states.

**Domain three: genomic imprinting.** Most genes are expressed from both parental alleles — the copy you inherited from your mother and the copy you inherited from your father — at roughly equal levels. But a small set of genes, perhaps a few hundred in the human genome, are **imprinted**: only one parental allele is expressed, while the other is silenced. The silencing is set up in the germline of the parent of origin and inherited through fertilization. The mechanism is methylation. The silenced allele carries a methylated imprinting control region; the expressed allele does not. Imprinting is biologically critical — disruption of imprinting causes diseases like Prader-Willi and Angelman syndromes, and it's a recurring theme in cancers. Methylation is the molecular memory that keeps an imprinted gene silenced on the right parental allele.

**Domain four: retrotransposon silencing.** Almost half of the human genome consists of repetitive sequences derived from ancient retrotransposons — bits of viral and parasitic DNA that copied themselves around the genome over evolutionary time. Most of these are inactivated, but if they were ever to wake up and start copying themselves again, the genome would become unstable, and the consequences would include cancer and developmental disorders. The way the cell keeps them quiet is heavy methylation. Retrotransposon DNA is among the most heavily methylated DNA in the genome, and that methylation is what physically prevents transcription from initiating in these elements. In cells where methylation breaks down — for example, after global demethylating drug treatment, or in certain cancers — retrotransposons can reactivate, with downstream consequences for genomic stability.

\[Pause.\]

**Domain five: cancer.** This is the big one clinically. Cancer cells have profoundly disrupted methylation patterns, and the disruption has a characteristic signature. **Global hypomethylation** — the bulk genome, especially the repetitive regions, loses methylation, which contributes to genomic instability and retrotransposon reactivation. **Focal hypermethylation at CpG island promoters** — specific tumor suppressor genes get their promoters methylated and silenced, which is functionally equivalent to losing those tumor suppressors by mutation, but without any change to the DNA sequence. The combination — losing methylation where you should have it, gaining it where you shouldn't — is so consistent across cancer types that methylation patterns alone can often classify a tumor's tissue of origin. This is the basis for methylation-based liquid biopsies, which we'll cover in detail in Segment 15.

There's also methylation in aging — the so-called epigenetic clock, where methylation at a few hundred specific CpG sites changes systematically with chronological age. There's methylation in environmental exposures — smoking, diet, and stress all leave methylation footprints. There's methylation in cell-type identity — every cell type in the body has its own characteristic methylation pattern, which can be used to identify the cell of origin of a piece of DNA found in blood plasma. All of these biological domains drive demand for methylation measurement, and all of them require methods that work across the whole genome at single-base resolution.

So the question is: how do you actually read a methyl group? The polymerase can't see it. The four-base genetic code doesn't encode it. You need a chemical trick to convert the invisible methylation status into a visible sequence difference. That trick is bisulfite conversion.

## SEGMENT 4 — Frommer 1992 — bisulfite chemistry as detective work

The chemistry that opened up methylation measurement was published in a 1992 paper from a small Australian lab — Marianne Frommer, Susan Clark, and colleagues at the CSIRO and the University of Sydney. The paper, "A genomic sequencing protocol that yields a positive display of 5-methylcytosine residues in individual DNA strands," appeared in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences in March 1992. It is, in retrospect, one of the most consequential method papers in the history of epigenetics, because it provided the chemical handle that the entire field has used for the next thirty-plus years.

Here is the chemistry, and I want you to follow it carefully because it's the conceptual heart of bisulfite sequencing. **Sodium bisulfite**, in solution at moderate acidity and elevated temperature, reacts with cytosines in single-stranded DNA. The reaction proceeds through a series of intermediates, but the end result, after a follow-up alkaline treatment, is that **unmethylated cytosine gets converted into uracil**. The uracil, when you subsequently amplify the DNA by PCR, gets read as thymine, because polymerases read uracil and thymine the same way. So at every unmethylated cytosine position in your starting DNA, after bisulfite treatment and PCR, you read a T.

Here is the crucial second half. **5-methylcytosine — the methylated form — does not react efficiently with bisulfite.** The methyl group on the five-position sterically and electronically protects the cytosine from the bisulfite addition reaction. So methylated cytosines survive the treatment intact, get amplified by PCR as cytosines, and read as C in the sequencing output.

\[Pause.\]

So the logic of bisulfite sequencing is this. You take your DNA, you treat it with bisulfite. Every unmethylated C in the original DNA becomes a U, then a T after PCR. Every methylated C in the original DNA stays as a C. You then sequence the treated, amplified DNA. Wherever you see a C in the output, you know that position was methylated. Wherever you see a T, it was either an unmethylated C or a genuine T in the original sequence — and you can tell those two apart by comparing to the reference genome, where the unconverted reference sequence is known. The reference says C, and the read says T? That position was unmethylated. The reference says C, and the read says C? That position was methylated.

Here is the analogy I want you to hold. **Bisulfite is harsh chemical detective work. You expose the entire sample to a reagent that destroys one class of suspect — every unmethylated cytosine — and leaves the other class — every methylated cytosine — untouched. Then you sequence, and the cytosines that survived are the ones that were methylated. The bodies that are missing are the ones that weren't.** It's a destructive subtraction assay. You don't measure methylation directly; you measure what survives the bisulfite treatment.

This was a beautiful and rigorous chemistry. In the 1992 paper and the years immediately following, it was used to map methylation at individual genes — clone a PCR product from a bisulfite-treated sample, sequence it by Sanger, and read off the methylation status of every CpG in that region of interest. That mode of analysis — locus-specific bisulfite sequencing — was the workhorse of methylation research throughout the 1990s and early 2000s, and it generated almost everything we knew about methylation at specific imprinted regions, tumor suppressor promoters, and developmental loci, for fifteen years.

\[Pause.\]

A few practical points about bisulfite chemistry that matter for everything that follows. First, the reaction is rough on DNA. Bisulfite is acidic, the temperatures involved are high — typically 50 to 70 degrees Celsius — and the reaction takes hours. Under these conditions, the DNA backbone takes a beating. You get strand breaks, depurination, and substantial loss of recoverable material. The typical recovery from a standard bisulfite reaction is somewhere between 10 and 30 percent of the input DNA, and the surviving DNA is fragmented down to a few hundred base pairs in length, regardless of where you started. For a few micrograms of bulk DNA, that's fine. For a precious clinical sample with only nanograms to work with, that loss is brutal. This DNA damage problem is the central limitation of bisulfite chemistry, and it's the limitation that the newer enzymatic and chemical alternatives are designed to solve.

Second, bisulfite conversion has to be efficient. If even a small percentage of unmethylated Cs fail to convert, they look identical to methylated Cs in the sequencing output, and you get a false positive methylation call at every unconverted position. Real-world conversion efficiency is typically 99 percent or better, and quality control of methylation experiments always includes a check on conversion efficiency using spiked-in fully unmethylated controls — most often lambda phage DNA — to verify that essentially all the Cs in the control got converted to Ts. Any deviation from full conversion gets baked into a global correction.

Third, bisulfite treatment turns a four-base sequencing problem into a three-base sequencing problem. After bisulfite, your reads contain essentially no unmethylated Cs at all — they're all Ts now. The information content per base goes down. The reads are harder to align uniquely to the reference genome, because so many positions that were originally C are now T, and the reads look much more similar to each other than untreated reads would. Specialized aligners — Bismark, BSMAP, methylpy — were developed specifically to handle the three-base alignment problem, and they remain standard tools in the methylation pipeline.

These three constraints — DNA damage, conversion efficiency, three-base alignment — define the bisulfite era. Every method we'll discuss after the basic chemistry is, in some sense, an engineering response to one or more of them. Now let's see how the chemistry got scaled up from a single gene to a whole genome.

## SEGMENT 5 — WGBS — whole-genome bisulfite sequencing, the gold standard

The leap from locus-specific bisulfite Sanger sequencing to whole-genome bisulfite sequencing — **WGBS** — happened in 2008, riding on the back of the newly available Illumina short-read sequencers. Two papers, both published in 2008, did the original demonstrations: one from Steve Jacobsen's lab at UCLA, focusing on Arabidopsis thaliana (Cokus et al., Nature, 2008), and the other from Joe Ecker's lab at the Salk Institute, also on Arabidopsis (Lister et al., Cell, 2008). Both papers showed that you could take a whole genome, treat it with bisulfite, sequence the resulting library on Illumina, and produce a base-resolution methylation map covering every cytosine in the genome.

The Arabidopsis result was a proof of principle on a small genome — about 135 megabases, compared to 3,000 for human. The same Salk lab — Lister and Ecker, with Mattick, Edsall, and others — then scaled up to the human genome and published the first human single-base-resolution methylome in 2009. We'll spend a whole segment on that paper in Segment 7. But let me first describe WGBS as a method, because the principles apply across organisms.

\[Pause.\]

The WGBS workflow looks like this. **Step one: fragment your genomic DNA**, by sonication or enzymatic digestion, to a target size of a few hundred base pairs — similar to a standard Illumina library. **Step two: end-repair and adapter-ligate**, using methylated adapters — this is a subtle but critical detail. The adapters themselves contain cytosines, and you don't want those cytosines to get converted by bisulfite, because that would mess up the priming and amplification steps. So the adapters are synthesized with 5-methylcytosine in place of unmethylated C, making them bisulfite-resistant. **Step three: bisulfite-treat the adapter-ligated library**, applying the harsh chemistry to the entire sample. **Step four: PCR-amplify the surviving fragments**, using primers that bind the methylated, bisulfite-resistant adapters. **Step five: sequence on Illumina**, generally with paired-end short reads. **Step six: align the reads with a bisulfite-aware aligner**, like Bismark, that knows how to handle the three-base alignment problem and can map reads to either the original strand or its bisulfite-converted complement. **Step seven: at every cytosine position in the reference genome, count the number of reads showing a C (methylated) versus the number showing a T (unmethylated), and compute a methylation level — typically a fraction between 0 and 1 — for that site.**

Done correctly, the output of WGBS is a value at every single one of the roughly 28 million CpG sites in the human genome — and, if you care, at every CHG and CHH site too, where H is A, T, or C and these contexts are mostly unmethylated in mammals but matter in plants. The dataset is rich. You have base-resolution methylation across the whole genome, with quantitative methylation levels.

Here is the analogy. **WGBS is the gold-standard whole-genome methylation map — comprehensive, base-resolution, every C accounted for. It's expensive: at typical coverage of 30x per strand, you're sequencing 30 times the genome, which is roughly 90 to 100 gigabases of bisulfite-treated reads per sample. It's the methylation equivalent of whole-genome sequencing: nothing missed, nothing assumed, every position observed directly.**

\[Pause.\]

There are costs. The chemistry is harsh, as we discussed, so the libraries are biased toward shorter fragments and have lower complexity than untreated libraries. The three-base sequence space means about 20 to 30 percent of reads don't map uniquely and get discarded. The required coverage is high because you need enough reads at every position to call methylation status with confidence — and at 30x average coverage across the genome, you still have plenty of CpG sites with low or zero coverage by chance. The data volume is enormous: a single human WGBS dataset is a few hundred gigabytes of raw reads, takes hundreds of CPU-hours to align, and requires substantial computational infrastructure to analyze at scale.

The cost per sample, even today in 2026, is several hundred to a couple thousand dollars depending on the platform and the depth — much more expensive than ordinary whole-genome sequencing because you're burning sequencing capacity on reads with reduced information content. For research projects with a handful of samples and serious funding, WGBS is the right answer. For population-scale studies with thousands or tens of thousands of samples, it's economically prohibitive. That economic constraint is what created the demand for the cheaper methods we'll meet next — RRBS, and especially the methylation arrays.

But before we get to those cost-reduction strategies, hold the WGBS picture. **Take your DNA, fragment it, ligate methylated adapters, bisulfite-convert, amplify, sequence at thirty-fold coverage, align with a bisulfite-aware aligner, and compute a methylation fraction at every cytosine in the genome.** That's the canonical methylation experiment. Everything that follows is either a cheaper subset of WGBS, or a gentler chemical alternative to it.

## SEGMENT 6 — RRBS — reduced-representation bisulfite sequencing for the rest of us

If WGBS is the comprehensive, expensive method, **RRBS** — reduced-representation bisulfite sequencing — is the budget-friendly compromise that captures most of the biologically interesting methylation at a fraction of the cost. RRBS was developed by Alex Meissner and colleagues, originally in the lab of Rudolf Jaenisch at the Whitehead Institute, in a 2005 paper, and it became a major workhorse of methylation profiling through the 2010s, particularly for cell-line studies and large epigenetic screens.

The insight behind RRBS is this. WGBS sequences every cytosine in the genome — including the vast majority of cytosines that sit in CpG-poor, methylation-uninformative regions where nothing biologically interesting is happening. The most biologically interesting methylation is concentrated in **CpG-rich regions** — CpG islands at promoters, CpG island shores, and some CpG-rich enhancers — which together make up only a tiny percentage of the genome. If you could enrich your library specifically for CpG-rich regions before sequencing, you'd capture most of the regulatorily meaningful methylation while sequencing far less total DNA. That's RRBS.

\[Pause.\]

The mechanism is beautifully simple. You digest your genomic DNA with the restriction enzyme **MspI**, which recognizes and cuts the sequence CCGG — a sequence that, by definition, contains a CpG. MspI is methylation-insensitive, meaning it cuts the same whether the CpG is methylated or not, so it doesn't bias your coverage by methylation state. After MspI digestion, you get a population of fragments whose ends always abut a CpG site. The crucial property of MspI digestion is that, because CCGG sites are enriched in CpG-rich regions of the genome and depleted in the CpG-poor bulk, the fragments produced by MspI are biased in size and abundance toward CpG-rich regions.

You then size-select — typically picking fragments in the range of 40 to 220 base pairs — and proceed with bisulfite conversion, library prep, and Illumina sequencing. The size selection enriches even further for CpG-rich regions, because MspI produces tightly clustered cuts in CpG-dense areas, generating fragments of the right size from those areas while producing very long, excluded fragments from CpG-poor regions.

The result, after sequencing and analysis, is a methylation map that covers somewhere between 1 and 3 million CpGs — roughly 5 to 10 percent of all CpGs in the genome, concentrated in the regulatorily interesting CpG-rich regions, particularly CpG island promoters. You miss the methylation status of the vast majority of the genome's CpGs, but you capture most of the ones that anyone cared about, at a sequencing cost roughly an order of magnitude lower than WGBS.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy. **RRBS is methylation-on-a-budget. Instead of sequencing every cytosine in the genome, you use a restriction enzyme to pull out just the CpG-rich fragments, which are the regions where the regulatorily interesting methylation lives. You get most of the biologically meaningful signal at maybe a tenth of the cost of WGBS. It's the eighty-twenty rule applied to methylation profiling.**

RRBS dominated cell-line methylation studies through the 2010s, particularly in the ENCODE and Roadmap Epigenomics projects, which generated RRBS profiles of dozens of human cell lines and primary tissues. It's still in active use today, particularly in academic labs that need methylation profiles on many samples and don't have the budget for WGBS. The trade-offs are well-understood: you don't see methylation in CpG-poor regions, you don't see methylation in enhancers that aren't CpG-rich, and you don't see the so-called partially methylated domains — large stretches of intermediate methylation that turn out to be biologically meaningful in many contexts. RRBS sees what it's designed to see, and misses the rest.

For most regulatory questions — is this promoter methylated, is this CpG island differentially methylated between conditions, what's the methylation status of this tumor suppressor — RRBS gives you the answer. For comprehensive epigenomic mapping, including methylation outside CpG islands, you still need WGBS or one of the newer comprehensive methods. The tool fits the question.

There's also a class of intermediate methods — bisulfite-treated capture sequencing, where you use a hybridization capture step (like exome capture) to enrich for specific regions of interest before sequencing. This lets you design custom methylation panels — for example, a panel of all the methylation hotspots known to be relevant to cancer diagnosis — and sequence them deeply at low cost per sample. Capture-based methylation sequencing is common in clinical research, and it's particularly popular for liquid biopsy applications. It's another point on the cost-comprehensiveness trade-off, sitting between RRBS and WGBS in coverage and customizability.

## SEGMENT 7 — Lister 2009 — the first human methylome at single-base resolution

In 2009, Ryan Lister, Mattia Pelizzola, Joe Ecker, and colleagues at the Salk Institute published the paper that opened the human era of methylome research. The paper, titled "Human DNA methylomes at base resolution show widespread epigenomic differences," appeared in Nature in November 2009. It reported the first complete, base-resolution methylation maps of two human cell types — human embryonic stem cells (the H1 line) and a fetal fibroblast cell line (IMR90) — using whole-genome bisulfite sequencing on the Illumina platform.

The numbers from that paper give a sense of the scale. To get adequate coverage, the team sequenced about 87 gigabases of bisulfite-converted DNA per cell line. They quantified methylation at about 94 percent of the cytosines in the human genome, with at least one read of coverage. They reported the first comprehensive view of the human methylome at single-base resolution, with quantitative methylation levels for tens of millions of CpG sites.

\[Pause.\]

The paper made several findings that have shaped the field ever since, but two stand out. **First**, they confirmed at genome-wide scale what locus-specific studies had hinted at for years: that the human methylome is dominated by methylation at CpG dinucleotides, with the bulk of the genome heavily methylated and CpG islands largely unmethylated. About 80 percent of CpG sites in IMR90 were methylated, while CpG islands had average methylation below 20 percent. The expected promoter-methylation rule held up at genome scale.

**Second**, and more surprisingly, they discovered that human embryonic stem cells carry substantial amounts of methylation at non-CpG contexts — particularly **CHG and CHH** contexts, where H is A, C, or T. In differentiated fibroblasts, non-CpG methylation was rare. In embryonic stem cells, it accounted for a non-trivial fraction of all 5mC and was concentrated in gene bodies. This was new biology that simply could not have been seen without comprehensive base-resolution methylome data — locus-specific bisulfite Sanger studies would never have stumbled across genome-wide non-CpG methylation, because they were focused on CpG islands by design. The whole-genome view revealed methylation biology that targeted studies had been blind to.

The Lister 2009 paper became the template for human methylome studies for the next decade. The Roadmap Epigenomics Project, between 2010 and 2015, used WGBS to map the methylomes of dozens of human tissues and cell types. The Lister lab and others extended the technique to additional contexts — neurons, induced pluripotent stem cells, cancer cell lines. The IHEC — International Human Epigenome Consortium — coordinated a global effort to produce reference methylomes for every major human cell type. By 2020, hundreds of human cell types and tissues had publicly available WGBS profiles, all sitting on the foundation laid by the 2009 Lister paper.

\[Pause.\]

The paper also established the analytical conventions that the field still uses. Methylation levels are reported as fractions between 0 and 1 — or equivalently as percentages between 0 and 100 — based on the count of C reads divided by the count of C plus T reads at each cytosine position. Coverage thresholds are set at some minimum number of reads, often 5 to 10, below which methylation calls are considered too noisy to use. Methylation levels are pooled across CpGs in a region of interest, by some weighted average, to give a region-level methylation summary. All of these conventions appear in the 2009 paper and have been carried forward, with minor variations, in every methylation pipeline since.

For the rest of this lecture, when I say "WGBS data" or "genome-wide methylation profile," I mean the kind of dataset that the Lister 2009 paper invented. Base-resolution, quantitative, comprehensive, derived from bisulfite-converted Illumina sequencing. Hundreds of millions of methylation calls per sample, summarized into a few dozen gigabytes of processed data, ready for downstream analyses like DMR calling, which we'll get to in Segment 9.

## SEGMENT 8 — The Illumina EPIC array — the cheap, scalable workhorse

Whole-genome bisulfite sequencing is the gold standard, but it's expensive and computationally heavy. RRBS is cheaper but still requires bisulfite chemistry, library prep, and sequencing. For most clinical and epidemiological studies — where you want methylation profiles on hundreds or thousands of samples, with results that are reproducible across labs and across years — there's a third option that has dominated population epigenetics for over a decade: the **Illumina Infinium methylation array**, particularly the **EPIC** version with about 850,000 probes per sample.

Let me explain how the array works, because it's a different beast from sequencing-based methods, and the design constraints lead to different strengths and weaknesses.

\[Pause.\]

The Illumina methylation arrays are based on the same Infinium bead-array technology that Illumina uses for genotyping SNPs. The array is a physical chip — a piece of glass or silicon — with hundreds of thousands of microscopic silicon beads attached, each bead carrying many copies of a specific 50-base-pair oligonucleotide probe. Each probe is designed to interrogate methylation at one specific CpG site in the human genome. You take your DNA, you bisulfite-convert it — yes, you still need bisulfite for the array — and you hybridize the converted DNA to the array. Each probe binds the DNA fragment that contains its target CpG. Then a single-base extension reaction adds a fluorescently labeled nucleotide at the CpG position. **If the CpG was methylated, the extended base is a G, lighting up the methylated-color channel. If the CpG was unmethylated, the bisulfite converted the C to U, the extended base is an A, lighting up the unmethylated-color channel.** A scanner reads the fluorescence at each bead, and from the ratio of methylated to unmethylated signal, you get a methylation level — typically expressed as a **beta value** between 0 and 1.

The original Infinium HumanMethylation27 array, launched in 2008, covered 27,000 CpGs. The HumanMethylation450 (the "450K") came in 2011 with 450,000 CpGs, mostly concentrated at CpG islands and promoters. The MethylationEPIC, launched in 2016, added another 400,000 probes focused on enhancers and distal regulatory regions, for a total of about 850,000 CpGs per sample. The EPIC version 2.0, released in the early 2020s, expanded again to nearly 940,000 CpGs.

Here is the analogy. **The methylation EPIC array is the cheap mass-market alternative to WGBS — 850,000 hand-picked CpGs measured per sample, covering the most biologically interesting regulatory regions, at a cost of around 100 to 250 dollars per sample including library prep and array processing. It's what most clinical, epidemiological, and population studies use, because it gives you reproducible quantitative methylation data on the regions you care about, with throughput and economics that bisulfite sequencing simply cannot match.**

\[Pause.\]

The EPIC array has been the engine of population epigenetics. The vast majority of epigenome-wide association studies — EWAS, the methylation equivalent of GWAS — use the EPIC or its predecessors. Studies of smoking and methylation, of obesity and methylation, of in utero environmental exposures, of childhood adversity, of psychiatric disease — almost all of them are EPIC studies. The TwinsUK cohort, the ARIES cohort, the Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities study, the Framingham Heart Study — many of the most influential methylation studies in epidemiology have been done on EPIC arrays.

The array also dominates the epigenetic clock space. The most famous methylation age estimators — Horvath's clock, Hannum's clock, the GrimAge clock, the PhenoAge clock — were all developed and trained on data from the Illumina methylation arrays. We'll come back to those in Segment 16. The clinical methylation tests on the market are also largely array-based or targeted-sequencing-based, because of the cost advantage relative to WGBS.

The trade-offs are clear. You only see the CpGs the array designers picked — about 3 percent of all CpGs in the genome, biased toward CpG islands, promoters, and known regulatory regions. You can't discover methylation at sites the array doesn't cover. You can't see cytosines outside CpG context. You can't easily get strand-specific information. And the array technology, while reproducible, has its own quirks — batch effects, probe-specific biases, cross-reactive probes, and so on — that have generated a whole ecosystem of normalization methods (BMIQ, SWAN, noob, funnorm, and many others) that bioinformaticians spend a lot of time tuning.

But for the central use case — quantitative methylation profiling at the regulatorily important CpGs across many samples — the EPIC array is the workhorse, and it will probably remain so for clinical applications even as sequencing-based methods get cheaper. It's the technology that took methylation profiling out of the research lab and into the clinic.

## SEGMENT 9 — DMR calling and the analytics of methylation

Now let's talk about what you actually do with all this methylation data. The headline analysis, in almost every methylation study, is **differentially methylated region** calling — DMR calling. The question is: given two or more groups of samples, which regions of the genome are differentially methylated between groups? Tumor versus normal. Young versus old. Treated versus untreated. Healthy versus diseased. Cell type A versus cell type B. The answer comes out as a list of genomic regions with associated statistics: this CpG island is hypermethylated in cancer with p-value X, this enhancer is hypomethylated in old age with effect size Y.

The analytics of DMR calling are not trivial, and they have their own subfield of bioinformatics. Let me walk through the logic, because it matters for how you read any methylation paper.

\[Pause.\]

At the level of a single CpG site, the question is straightforward in principle. You have, say, ten samples per group, and at each CpG you have a methylation level for each sample — a number between 0 and 1. You can test whether the methylation levels differ between groups using a standard statistical test — a t-test, a Mann-Whitney, a beta regression. You get a p-value per CpG. You correct for multiple testing across all the CpGs you tested — Bonferroni or Benjamini-Hochberg — and you call the CpGs that survive correction as differentially methylated. This is the single-CpG version of the analysis, often called **differentially methylated position** or **DMP** analysis. It's what you do with EPIC array data when you're looking for individual CpG-level associations.

But methylation often varies in a coordinated way across nearby CpGs. A CpG island that gets methylated in cancer doesn't just have one CpG go up; it has dozens of CpGs in the same region go up together. So rather than testing each CpG independently, you can test whether **regions** — contiguous stretches of CpGs — are differentially methylated as a whole. This is **DMR** analysis proper, and it has higher statistical power than per-CpG analysis when the methylation changes are spatially coordinated, which they usually are.

The standard pipeline for DMR calling, in WGBS or RRBS data, looks like this. You partition the genome into windows, or you scan with a sliding window, identifying contiguous stretches of CpGs that show coordinated methylation differences between groups. You apply a statistical model — there are many, including BSmooth, methylKit, DSS, dmrseq, and bsseq — that accounts for the binomial nature of methylation count data (each CpG gives you a count of methylated reads out of total reads, not a continuous value) and that smooths or borrows information across nearby CpGs to improve power. You output a list of DMRs, each with a genomic coordinate, an average methylation difference (often called delta-beta), a statistical significance, and some quality metrics.

\[Pause.\]

The interpretation is then biological. You annotate the DMRs against gene models — does this DMR overlap a promoter, an enhancer, a gene body? You annotate against regulatory element atlases — does it overlap an ENCODE-defined enhancer, a CpG island, a chromHMM state? You ask whether the DMRs are enriched in particular gene sets — are the genes near hypermethylated DMRs enriched for tumor suppressors, for developmental regulators, for immune pathway genes? The biology comes out of the cross-referencing.

There are several recurring analytical pitfalls that you should know about. **First, batch effects.** Methylation data is notoriously sensitive to batch effects — the day the sample was processed, the array chip position, the bisulfite conversion batch, the operator. If your cases and controls happen to have been processed on different days, you can get strong but completely artifactual methylation differences. Standard practice is to randomize cases and controls across batches and to include batch as a covariate in the statistical model. Ignore this and you'll publish nonsense.

**Second, cell-type composition.** Methylation patterns are highly cell-type-specific. If your samples are mixtures of cell types — for example, whole blood, which contains lymphocytes, monocytes, granulocytes, and others in varying proportions — apparent methylation differences between groups can actually be driven by differences in cell-type composition rather than by within-cell-type methylation changes. Standard practice is to estimate cell-type proportions from the methylation data itself, using reference deconvolution methods, and adjust for them in the analysis. Again, ignore this at your peril.

**Third, single CpGs are noisy.** Methylation calls at a single CpG, especially from low-coverage WGBS, can be very noisy. Averaging over regions reduces the noise dramatically and is one of the reasons DMR-based analyses tend to be more reproducible than single-CpG analyses. **Fourth, methylation changes are often small in magnitude.** A 10 percent shift in methylation level can be biologically real and meaningful, especially in mixed cell populations, but it requires careful statistics to distinguish from noise. Don't expect methylation to flip from 0 to 1; expect it to shift from 0.4 to 0.5, and design your analysis accordingly.

The bottom line on analytics: methylation data is rich, but it has its own statistical character — bounded between 0 and 1, derived from binomial counts, spatially correlated across nearby CpGs, sensitive to batch and cell composition. Treat it with the right statistical tools and the biology pops out cleanly. Treat it like generic continuous data and you'll get fooled.

## SEGMENT 10 — The limitations of bisulfite — DNA damage, 5mC versus 5hmC, and the hidden trouble

Welcome back. I hope you stretched your legs and got some coffee. We've spent the first nine segments building up the bisulfite-sequencing universe — the chemistry, the genomic-scale assays, the analytical conventions — and now I want to spend the next several segments dismantling some of the assumptions and meeting the next generation of methods that are slowly displacing the bisulfite era.

Let me lay out, in detail, the three big limitations of bisulfite sequencing. Understanding these limitations is essential, because every modern alternative — oxBS, TAPS, EM-seq, nanopore methylation calling — is designed to solve one or more of them. If you don't see why bisulfite is limited, you won't appreciate why anyone bothered to replace it.

\[Pause.\]

**Limitation one: DNA damage.** I mentioned this in Segment 4, but let me put hard numbers on it. A typical sodium-bisulfite reaction proceeds at pH 5 and 50 to 70 degrees Celsius for several hours. Under these conditions, DNA suffers extensive damage. The phosphodiester backbone hydrolyzes, generating single-strand and eventually double-strand breaks. Purines depurinate at elevated rates, leaving abasic sites that further destabilize the strand. The cumulative effect is that something like 70 to 90 percent of the starting DNA mass is destroyed during bisulfite conversion. The DNA that survives is fragmented down to a few hundred base pairs in length and is enriched for shorter fragments.

For a research project with milligrams of DNA from a tissue culture flask, that loss is annoying but manageable. For a precious clinical sample — say, a 20-microliter plasma draw from a cancer patient, containing maybe a few nanograms of cell-free DNA — that loss is catastrophic. You start with a tiny amount of input; you lose 80 percent of it to bisulfite damage; you end up with a marginal library, low complexity, full of PCR duplicates because the unique molecule count is so low. For applications where input is precious — clinical liquid biopsy, single-cell methylation, formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded tumor samples, ancient DNA — bisulfite damage is the single biggest barrier to getting good data.

**Limitation two: bisulfite cannot distinguish 5mC from 5hmC.** Remember from Segment 2 that 5-hydroxymethylcytosine is a distinct epigenetic mark, with its own biology, accumulating to several percent of total cytosines in some tissues. When you bisulfite-treat DNA, both 5mC and 5hmC fail to convert. They both read as C in the sequencing output. There is no way, from a standard bisulfite experiment, to tell them apart. Every methylation call you make is actually a "5mC plus 5hmC" call, with the contribution of 5hmC silently mixed in.

\[Pause.\]

For most tissues, where 5hmC is rare, this conflation barely matters — the 5mC signal dominates, and treating bisulfite data as a 5mC readout is approximately correct. But for tissues where 5hmC is abundant — particularly the brain, where 5hmC can make up 1 to 2 percent of all cytosines and is concentrated in active gene bodies — the conflation can produce systematically misleading results. A region that looks "methylated" in bisulfite data might actually be hydroxymethylated, with completely different biological implications. The Lister and Ecker groups and others demonstrated this clearly in early-2010s papers comparing bisulfite data to specialized 5hmC assays in brain tissue. The bisulfite map of brain methylation is, in important regions, actually a map of bisulfite-resistant marks of both flavors, and you can't tell which is which.

**Limitation three: three-base alignment, low information content, and PCR bias.** The post-bisulfite sequence has essentially no unmethylated Cs — they've all been converted to Ts. The effective alphabet of the read drops from four letters to three. Reads become harder to align uniquely. About 20 to 30 percent of reads in a typical WGBS experiment fail to map uniquely and are discarded. The library prep also requires extensive PCR amplification after bisulfite conversion, both because the input is low and because the bisulfite-damaged DNA needs to be expanded to a sequenceable mass. PCR amplification introduces sequence-context biases and produces duplicate reads that have to be filtered out, further reducing the effective coverage.

The three-base problem also affects the design of methylation arrays, where probes have to be designed around the bisulfite-converted sequence and where probe specificity is harder to ensure. Some array probes are cross-reactive, binding multiple genomic locations because the three-base converted sequences happen to match in more than one place. Lists of cross-reactive probes are maintained for the Illumina arrays, and standard quality control involves dropping those probes from the analysis.

\[Pause.\]

So bisulfite, the workhorse chemistry of the methylation field for nearly thirty years, has three serious limitations: it damages DNA, it conflates 5mC and 5hmC, and it compresses the sequencing alphabet in ways that hurt alignment and library quality. Each of these limitations has motivated a separate methodological response, and the next several segments are about those responses.

The first response, historically, was a clever chemical addition to bisulfite that resolves the 5mC versus 5hmC ambiguity without abandoning bisulfite itself. Let's meet it.

## SEGMENT 11 — oxBS-seq — adding an oxidation step to distinguish 5mC from 5hmC

The technique called **oxidative bisulfite sequencing**, or **oxBS-seq**, was published in 2012 by Shankar Balasubramanian's lab at Cambridge — yes, the same Balasubramanian who, with Klenerman, invented Illumina sequencing back in the late 1990s. The Balasubramanian lab in the 2010s pivoted hard into the chemistry of modified cytosine bases, and oxBS-seq was one of their most influential contributions to the methylation field.

The chemistry is beautiful in its simplicity. The problem with standard bisulfite is that both 5mC and 5hmC resist conversion to U, so you can't tell them apart. The oxBS solution is to add a chemical pre-treatment, using **potassium perruthenate** (KRuO4), which **selectively oxidizes 5hmC to 5-formylcytosine** (5fC) without touching 5mC. The crucial property of 5fC is that, unlike 5hmC, it _does_ react with bisulfite and gets converted to U. So after the oxidation step, the cytosines look like this: unmethylated C still converts to U; 5hmC has been oxidized to 5fC and now also converts to U; only 5mC survives intact and reads as C in the sequencing output.

\[Pause.\]

So you run two parallel experiments on aliquots of the same sample. **Experiment A: standard bisulfite sequencing.** Both 5mC and 5hmC survive; the readout is "5mC plus 5hmC" at every position. **Experiment B: oxidative bisulfite sequencing.** Only 5mC survives; the readout is "5mC alone" at every position. **Subtract Experiment B from Experiment A** at each cytosine, and you get the 5hmC signal at that position. Two assays, one subtraction, full discrimination between the two marks at base resolution across the entire genome.

The oxBS workflow is otherwise identical to standard WGBS — fragment, ligate methylated adapters, oxidize, bisulfite-convert, amplify, sequence, align. The only addition is the oxidation step before bisulfite, and that step takes about an hour of additional bench work. The downstream pipeline is the same.

oxBS-seq became, for a few years in the mid-2010s, the standard method for studying 5hmC genomically — particularly in brain tissue, where 5hmC is abundant, and in early embryonic development, where 5hmC accumulates as part of the reprogramming process. The Balasubramanian lab and others used oxBS to publish the first base-resolution 5hmC maps of human and mouse tissues, opening up the molecular biology of hydroxymethylation as a measurable mark.

But oxBS inherits the underlying limitations of bisulfite. It still damages DNA — in fact, the additional oxidation step adds to the damage. It still requires high input. It still suffers from three-base alignment difficulties. And because you have to run two parallel experiments, your sequencing cost roughly doubles per sample. For applications where you need to discriminate 5mC and 5hmC and you have plenty of input, oxBS is a clean solution. For low-input or damage-sensitive applications, it doesn't help with the fundamental problem.

\[Pause.\]

There's a related technique called **TAB-seq** — TET-assisted bisulfite sequencing — that takes the opposite approach. Where oxBS oxidizes 5hmC further so it converts with bisulfite, TAB-seq uses the TET enzymes to oxidize 5mC all the way to 5-carboxylcytosine (which converts) while 5hmC is first protected by chemical glucosylation. After bisulfite, only 5hmC survives as a C in the readout — you get a direct 5hmC-specific map without needing a subtraction. TAB-seq, published by Chuan He's lab at the University of Chicago in 2012, is the complementary tool to oxBS-seq: oxBS reads 5mC alone; TAB-seq reads 5hmC alone; combine either with standard bisulfite (which reads both) and you have a complete picture.

These oxidative and enzymatic bisulfite variants opened up the discrimination problem but didn't solve the damage problem. For the damage problem, you needed to escape from bisulfite chemistry entirely. The two techniques that did so — TAPS and EM-seq — emerged in 2019, almost simultaneously, and they are reshaping the methylation field.

---

## SEGMENT 12 — TAPS — TET-assisted pyridine borane sequencing, the gentler successor

The first of the bisulfite-free, base-resolution methylation methods to make a serious commercial impact was **TAPS** — **TET-Assisted Pyridine borane Sequencing**, published in 2019 in Nature Biotechnology by Yibin Liu and Chunxiao Song at the University of Oxford. The paper, titled "Bisulfite-free direct detection of 5-methylcytosine and 5-hydroxymethylcytosine at base resolution," made a clean break with bisulfite while preserving everything that made bisulfite scientifically attractive — base resolution, genome-wide coverage, quantitative methylation levels.

The chemistry of TAPS is conceptually inverse to bisulfite. Bisulfite converts unmethylated C to T and leaves methylated C intact, so methylated Cs are read as Cs. TAPS, by contrast, **converts methylated C to T and leaves unmethylated C intact**, so the methylated Cs are the ones that show up as a sequence change in the reads.

Here's the mechanism in three steps. **Step one: TET enzymatic oxidation.** A TET enzyme — the same TET that does demethylation in the cell — is applied in vitro to oxidize 5mC and 5hmC all the way to 5-carboxylcytosine (5caC). Both 5mC and 5hmC end up as 5caC after this step; unmethylated C is not touched. **Step two: pyridine borane reduction.** A mild chemical reducing agent, pyridine borane, reduces 5caC to dihydrouracil (DHU). The intermediate 5caC, with its carboxyl group, is uniquely susceptible to this reduction; unmodified C is not. **Step three: standard PCR.** The polymerase reads DHU as T. So at every position that started as 5mC or 5hmC, you now read T; at every position that started as unmethylated C, you still read C.

\[Pause.\]

The advantages over bisulfite are substantial. **First, the chemistry is gentle.** Pyridine borane reduction happens at neutral pH and modest temperature. There's no extreme acidity, no extended high-temperature incubation, no extensive depurination. DNA recovery is dramatically better — typically over 90 percent of starting DNA survives, compared to bisulfite's 10 to 30 percent. **Second, longer reads are possible.** Because the DNA is intact, you can do long-fragment libraries — Pacific Biosciences, Oxford Nanopore, or long-fragment Illumina — that you simply cannot do well with bisulfite-damaged DNA. **Third, the alphabet is preserved.** Most Cs in the genome are unmethylated, and TAPS leaves those unmethylated Cs as Cs. The reads still have a four-letter alphabet, alignment is much easier, mapping rates approach those of untreated Illumina sequencing.

There's one trade-off. TAPS reads 5mC plus 5hmC together — the TET step oxidizes both — so to discriminate the two marks you need a variant called **TAPSβ** or **chemical-assisted TAPS** that protects 5hmC by chemical β-glucosylation before the TET step. So discrimination is possible but requires the extra step, much as it does for oxBS or TAB-seq.

Here is the analogy. **TAPS is the gentler replacement for bisulfite. Same single-base resolution, same genome-wide coverage, same quantitative methylation, but the chemistry uses an enzyme and a mild reducing agent instead of harsh acid at high temperature. DNA stays largely intact, libraries are higher-quality, you can do long reads, and the read alphabet is preserved. It's bisulfite's better-engineered successor.**

\[Pause.\]

TAPS has been adopted relatively slowly, partly because the chemistry was patented and the kits were initially expensive, and partly because the bisulfite-based analytical infrastructure was so entrenched that switching cost was non-trivial. But for high-value applications where input is precious or read length matters — particularly liquid biopsy and ancient DNA — TAPS and TAPS-like chemistry have started to displace bisulfite. The company Cambridge Epigenetix, founded around the Balasubramanian lab's earlier work and later acquired by biomodal, commercialized TAPS-related technology, including a method called **duet evoC** that produces methylation, hydroxymethylation, and genetic variant calls simultaneously from a single library prep.

The TAPS story is an example of how, once a foundational technology has been around long enough, the field's accumulated knowledge of its limitations sets up the demand for a better alternative, and someone — often a chemist who specifically thinks about base modifications — figures out the better chemistry. The 5mC discrimination, the input requirement, the read length — all of these were known problems with bisulfite, and TAPS attacks all of them in one chemical move.

## SEGMENT 13 — EM-seq — enzymatic methyl-seq, the commercial gentler alternative

Around the same time TAPS was being published from the Song lab in Oxford, the molecular biology vendor **New England Biolabs (NEB)** released a competing bisulfite-free method called **enzymatic methyl-seq**, or **EM-seq**. The kit launched commercially in 2019 and has since become, in many labs, the de facto replacement for WGBS — particularly for low-input and library-quality-sensitive applications.

The EM-seq chemistry is, like TAPS, enzymatic in its key conversion step. But the enzyme strategy is different. EM-seq uses a sequential two-enzyme reaction. **Step one: TET2 oxidation.** TET2 enzyme is applied to oxidize 5mC and 5hmC into their oxidized forms (5fC and 5caC); these are now chemically modified and protected from the subsequent deamination step. To stop TET2 from over-oxidizing, the reaction also includes an enhancer molecule and is carefully calibrated. **Step two: APOBEC3A deamination.** The cytidine deaminase APOBEC3A is then applied; it deaminates unmodified cytosine to uracil, but it cannot deaminate the TET-oxidized derivatives of 5mC and 5hmC. So at the end, unmethylated Cs read as Ts (because of deamination to U), while 5mC and 5hmC read as Cs (because they were chemically protected by TET2 oxidation).

\[Pause.\]

Note that the readout convention is identical to bisulfite — unmethylated Cs become Ts, methylated Cs stay as Cs — so all the existing bisulfite-style alignment tools (Bismark, BSMAP, methylpy) and DMR-calling pipelines work directly on EM-seq data. This is a deliberate design choice by NEB; they wanted EM-seq to be a drop-in replacement for WGBS at the bioinformatics level, so that labs switching to EM-seq don't have to rebuild their analytical pipelines.

The advantages over bisulfite are similar to TAPS: dramatically less DNA damage, much higher recovery, better library complexity, better representation of GC-rich regions which bisulfite under-samples. NEB's own benchmarking showed that EM-seq libraries from 10 nanograms of input were equivalent in quality to bisulfite libraries from 100 nanograms — a tenfold improvement in input sensitivity, which is exactly what clinical liquid biopsy applications need. EM-seq libraries also tend to have higher complexity, fewer PCR duplicates, and broader genomic coverage uniformity than bisulfite libraries from the same input.

Here is the analogy. **EM-seq is NEB's commercial gentler version of bisulfite — uses TET2 plus APOBEC3A enzymes instead of harsh acidic bisulfite, produces cleaner libraries with much less DNA damage, works well from very low input, and is drop-in compatible with existing bisulfite analysis pipelines. It's become the default WGBS alternative in many labs, especially for low-input or clinical samples.**

\[Pause.\]

EM-seq, like vanilla bisulfite and TAPS, reads 5mC plus 5hmC together by default. NEB sells a separate kit, **EM-seq-Hyd**, that protects 5hmC via glucosylation before the TET2 step, so that only 5hmC is read; subtracting from standard EM-seq gives you the 5mC signal. The same two-experiment design as oxBS, but on the gentler enzymatic chemistry.

There's also a single-cell variant — **scEM-seq** — that, combined with appropriate handling, can profile methylation in individual cells. The gentleness of the chemistry matters enormously here, because single-cell DNA is by definition limited to picograms, and even small losses are devastating. Single-cell EM-seq is now a workable, if expensive, technique for methylation studies of rare cell populations, and we'll return to it in Segment 18.

The competitive dynamic between EM-seq and TAPS is interesting. EM-seq has the advantage of NEB's distribution network, brand recognition, and the drop-in bioinformatics compatibility. TAPS has the advantage of the inverse readout (methylated Cs as Ts), which some argue is more analytically convenient because the signal of interest is the changed base rather than the unchanged one. Both methods deliver on the central promise: base-resolution genome-wide methylation profiling without bisulfite damage. By 2026, the field is gradually shifting away from bisulfite for new work, with EM-seq and TAPS taking over for high-value applications and bisulfite remaining mostly as a legacy method for projects that need backward compatibility with the enormous existing corpus of bisulfite data.

## SEGMENT 14 — Nanopore direct methylation calling — methylation as a bonus output

There is a completely different way to read methylation that doesn't involve chemistry at all. Oxford Nanopore Technologies' platform — which sequences DNA by threading individual molecules through tiny protein pores embedded in a membrane, while measuring the ionic current that flows through the pore as each base passes — turns out to be directly sensitive to base modifications. A methylated cytosine perturbs the ionic current differently from an unmethylated cytosine; a 5hmC perturbs it differently again. The current signal carries the methylation information, embedded in the same data stream that carries the sequence information.

For the first several years of nanopore sequencing, this signal was a curiosity. The basecaller — the software that converts raw current data into sequence reads — was tuned to call A, C, G, and T, and it ignored modifications. But as the basecallers got more sophisticated, particularly with the introduction of neural-network-based basecalling around 2018, it became possible to train the same models to recognize and call modifications directly from the current signal. The modern Oxford Nanopore basecaller — the Dorado software — outputs methylation calls alongside base calls, with no additional sample preparation, no chemistry, no separate experiment.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy. **Nanopore methylation calling is the bonus output. The same long-read nanopore sequencing that gives you sequence also reports methylation status from the raw current signal, no extra chemistry needed. You do one library prep, one sequencing run, and the basecaller gives you sequence plus methylation in the same file. It's the methylation method that bundles with every nanopore experiment for free.**

The implications are substantial. **First, you avoid all the bisulfite/EM-seq/TAPS chemistry entirely.** No conversion, no damage, no library doubling for 5mC versus 5hmC discrimination. **Second, you get long reads.** Standard nanopore reads are tens of thousands of base pairs; ultra-long reads can hit a million base pairs. This means you can phase methylation across long genomic distances — for example, telling you whether two methylated CpGs 50 kilobases apart are on the same DNA molecule or on different molecules, which matters for imprinting analysis, allele-specific methylation, and tumor clonality. **Third, you can call multiple modifications.** Nanopore basecallers can now distinguish 5mC from 5hmC from 6mA (a different modification, important in some bacteria and in some unusual human contexts) — all from the same sequencing run.

The trade-offs are real too. Per-base accuracy of nanopore methylation calls, even in the best modern implementations, is somewhat lower than the accuracy of bisulfite or EM-seq calls at high coverage. To get reliable methylation calls at a given CpG, you typically need higher coverage from nanopore than from short-read converted sequencing — maybe 20x to 30x nanopore versus 5x to 10x bisulfite. The cost per base for nanopore is still higher than for Illumina-based methods, though it's dropping fast. And the basecaller's modification-calling accuracy is sensitive to the sequence context, the basecaller version, and the chemistry of the flow cell, so reproducibility across versions and labs requires careful version control.

\[Pause.\]

PacBio HiFi sequencing, the other long-read platform, also has methylation calling capabilities, derived from polymerase kinetics rather than ionic current. The signal is subtler — PacBio infers methylation from how the polymerase pauses or accelerates at modified bases — and the current implementations primarily call 5mC at CpG sites with somewhat narrower scope than nanopore, but they're improving. By the late 2020s, both long-read platforms will have full methylation-calling capability built into the standard data pipeline.

For applications that need long reads and methylation together — particularly anything involving repetitive regions of the genome, structural variants, imprinted loci, or haplotype-phased methylation — nanopore methylation calling is increasingly the method of choice. For high-throughput, low-cost, large-cohort methylation studies, the EPIC array and EM-seq will probably dominate for some years yet, because the per-sample cost of nanopore is still too high for studies of thousands of samples. But the trajectory is clear: methylation will increasingly be a free side effect of doing long-read genome sequencing, rather than a separate experiment requiring its own chemistry.

The long-term implication is that the entire bisulfite-based methylation industry may eventually shrink as long-read sequencing becomes cheap enough to be the default. That transition is happening now, slowly, and we'll see it play out over the next decade.

## SEGMENT 15 — Methylation in cancer and the liquid biopsy revolution

Now let's bring all the methodology back to one of its biggest real-world applications: cancer. The methylation patterns of cancer cells are profoundly disrupted, and the disruption has a characteristic signature that we touched on in Segment 3. Let me unpack the cancer methylome carefully, because it's the basis for some of the most exciting clinical methylation tests on the market today.

The canonical cancer methylation signature has two parts. **Global hypomethylation.** The bulk of the genome — particularly the repetitive elements, the intergenic regions, the partially methylated domains — loses methylation in cancer. This contributes to genomic instability, retrotransposon reactivation, and the broad chromosomal chaos characteristic of tumor cells. **Focal hypermethylation at CpG island promoters.** A specific set of CpG island promoters, often hundreds to thousands per tumor, become aberrantly methylated, silencing the associated genes. Many of these silenced genes are tumor suppressors, DNA repair genes, or cell-cycle regulators. The combined pattern — global loss plus focal gain — is so consistent across cancer types that methylation alone can often classify a tumor's tissue of origin with high accuracy.

\[Pause.\]

The cell-type-specificity of methylation is the key to the liquid biopsy revolution. Every cell type in the body has its own characteristic methylation pattern — its own combination of methylated and unmethylated CpGs across its genome. When a cell dies, it releases its DNA into the bloodstream as **cell-free DNA** — short fragments, typically 150 to 170 base pairs long, circulating in plasma. A healthy person has cell-free DNA in their blood coming mostly from blood cells dying as part of normal turnover, with smaller contributions from other tissues. A cancer patient has additional cell-free DNA coming from dying tumor cells — sometimes a substantial fraction, particularly in advanced disease, but typically a small minority (1 percent or less) in early-stage cancer.

If you can read the methylation patterns of cell-free DNA in plasma, you can in principle do two things. **First, detect that some fraction of the DNA has tumor-like methylation patterns.** A blood sample that contains DNA fragments with the methylation signature of, say, hepatocellular carcinoma — fragments that look methylated in places where normal liver isn't, and unmethylated where normal liver is — provides evidence of a liver tumor. **Second, infer the tissue of origin of the tumor.** Different cancers — colorectal, lung, breast, pancreatic, ovarian — have characteristic methylation signatures that reflect both the originating cell type and the cancer-specific methylation changes. Methylation-based classifiers trained on hundreds of cancer types can read the cell-free DNA methylation pattern and predict not just "cancer present" but "cancer from tissue X."

\[Pause.\]

The flagship commercial product in this space is **Galleri**, made by the company GRAIL, launched commercially in 2021. Galleri is a blood test that measures methylation patterns at over 100,000 selected genomic regions in cell-free DNA, using a targeted methylation sequencing approach. The test claims to detect a signal consistent with cancer for over 50 different cancer types, many of which currently have no screening tests at all. When the test detects a cancer signal, it also predicts the tissue of origin, narrowing the diagnostic workup that follows.

Here is the analogy. **The Galleri test is methylation-based liquid biopsy — it detects roughly 50 cancer types from cell-free DNA methylation patterns in a single blood draw. The methylation patterns of cancer cells leak into the plasma as fragmented DNA; the test reads those patterns and infers both presence of cancer and likely tissue of origin.** It's a profound application of the same chemistry and analysis we've been building up across this lecture.

There are similar tests in development from multiple companies — Guardant has a methylation-based test, Exact Sciences (the maker of Cologuard) uses methylation as part of its colorectal cancer screen, and several other companies have methylation-based monitoring tests for cancer recurrence after treatment. The clinical validation of these tests is still in progress — large randomized trials are running to assess whether early methylation-based detection actually improves survival outcomes — but the technology has moved from research curiosity to commercial reality in the span of about a decade.

Methylation has also become central to **methylation-based tumor classification** for diagnostic pathology. The DKFZ in Heidelberg developed a methylation classifier for brain tumors that has become the gold standard for diagnosing CNS tumors — it can distinguish over 90 distinct brain tumor subtypes from a single methylation array on a tumor biopsy. Similar classifiers exist for sarcomas, pediatric tumors, and other diagnostic challenges. In many cancer centers today, the methylation profile is a routine part of tumor characterization, alongside histology and mutation testing.

The cancer methylome is also a guide to treatment. Some chemotherapy drugs — particularly the so-called epigenetic drugs like azacitidine and decitabine — work by inhibiting DNA methyltransferases, broadly demethylating the genome and reactivating silenced tumor suppressors. These drugs are approved for treating certain myelodysplastic syndromes and leukemias. Methylation status of specific genes — MGMT in glioblastoma, for example — predicts response to alkylating chemotherapy and is now part of standard clinical practice. The translation from methylation biology to methylation-guided therapy is still early, but the foundation is there.

## SEGMENT 16 — The epigenetic clock and methylation as an aging biomarker

One of the most striking discoveries of the methylation era is that DNA methylation, at a small number of specific CpG sites, changes systematically and predictably with chronological age. This observation — that you can read someone's age from the methylation pattern of a small DNA sample — is the basis for the **epigenetic clock**, one of the most influential biomarkers in modern aging research.

The breakthrough paper was published by **Steve Horvath**, a biostatistician at UCLA, in Genome Biology in 2013. The paper, titled "DNA methylation age of human tissues and cell types," reported a regression-based predictor of chronological age built from the methylation values at **353 specific CpG sites** measured on the Illumina methylation arrays. Trained on 8,000 samples spanning many tissues and ages, the model predicted chronological age from methylation with a median error of around 3 to 4 years. The model worked across nearly every tissue tested — blood, brain, liver, kidney, even teeth — with the same set of CpGs and the same coefficients. It became known as **Horvath's clock**.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy. **Horvath built a regression model on about 350 CpGs whose methylation levels change linearly with age — when you measure those CpGs in a new sample, the model predicts the donor's biological age within a few years. Methylation IS an aging biomarker, and the clock is the formal mathematical machinery for reading age from methylation.**

But the really interesting finding came when researchers started looking at the difference between predicted methylation age and actual chronological age. Some people's predicted age is older than their chronological age — they're aging faster, epigenetically, than the calendar says. Other people's predicted age is younger than chronological — they're aging slower. This difference — often called **methylation age acceleration** — turned out to be correlated with mortality risk, with cardiovascular disease, with cognitive decline, with various age-related conditions. People with accelerated methylation age tend to die younger; people with decelerated methylation age tend to live longer. The clock is not just a chronological-age predictor; it's a biological-age predictor, capturing some genuine signal of physiological aging that goes beyond the calendar.

Several refined clocks have been built since Horvath's original. **Hannum's clock** (Hannum et al., 2013) was built on blood samples specifically and uses about 70 CpGs. **PhenoAge** (Levine et al., 2018) was trained against clinical biomarkers of biological age — albumin, glucose, inflammation markers — rather than against chronological age, producing a clock more directly tied to health. **GrimAge** (Lu et al., 2019) was trained against mortality and smoking-pack-years, producing a clock that is among the most predictive of remaining lifespan. These newer clocks tend to outperform Horvath's original for predicting health outcomes, while Horvath's clock remains the most accurate for predicting chronological age across diverse tissues.

\[Pause.\]

The biology of why methylation changes with age is still incompletely understood. The clock CpGs are not randomly distributed in the genome; they're enriched in particular regions, including polycomb-regulated developmental genes. The methylation drift is partly random — a kind of stochastic accumulation of methylation noise over time — and partly programmed, possibly reflecting changes in stem cell behavior, DNA repair fidelity, or inflammation. Whether the methylation changes are causes of aging, consequences of aging, or just correlates of aging is still actively debated. But the predictive value is unambiguous: a small methylation measurement can read age with surprising precision, and deviations from expected age carry health information.

The clinical and consumer applications have followed. Companies sell direct-to-consumer methylation age tests from saliva or cheek swabs — TruDiagnostic, Elysium Health, and others. Clinical trials of anti-aging interventions increasingly include methylation age as a secondary endpoint, on the hypothesis that interventions that genuinely slow aging will slow the epigenetic clock too. There's even an annual prize — the Methuselah Foundation's prize for reversing epigenetic age — incentivizing interventions that reduce predicted methylation age in human subjects.

Whether the epigenetic clock will live up to its promise as a target for therapeutic intervention remains to be seen. But as a biomarker — a quantitative readout of biological state from a small DNA sample — it's already remarkable, and it's one of the most concrete examples of how methylation measurement has gone from research curiosity to actionable clinical signal.

## SEGMENT 17 — Cell-type methylation atlases and deconvolution of cfDNA

Let me come back to the cell-free DNA story from Segment 15 and add one more layer: the use of methylation as a **cell-type-of-origin barcode**. This is one of the most practically important applications of the comprehensive methylation atlases that the field has built over the last decade, and it has profound implications for non-invasive diagnostics across many disease areas, not just cancer.

Here is the central observation. Each cell type in the body has a characteristic methylation pattern, and that pattern includes a set of CpGs that are uniquely methylated or uniquely unmethylated in that cell type and almost nowhere else. For example, hepatocytes have a small set of CpGs that are unmethylated only in hepatocytes — every other cell type in the body methylates them. Cardiomyocytes have a different set of cardiomyocyte-specific unmethylated CpGs. Pancreatic beta cells have their own. Neurons have theirs. The cell-type-specific CpGs are like ID tags: a fragment of cell-free DNA carrying an unmethylated hepatocyte-specific CpG must have come from a hepatocyte. There's no other source.

\[Pause.\]

If you can build a complete catalog of cell-type-specific methylation markers, by doing WGBS on many sorted cell types and computationally identifying the markers, then you can in principle take any cell-free DNA sample — a tube of plasma — and deconvolve it into its cell types of origin. How much of this DNA came from blood cells? How much from liver? How much from heart? How much from brain? How much from each of dozens of other tissues? **The composition of cell-free DNA reflects the rate of cell death across tissues, and changes in composition signal tissue damage.**

This is the basis for the **liquid-biopsy-of-everything** vision. A heart attack causes cardiomyocyte death; cardiomyocyte-derived cell-free DNA spikes in the plasma. Liver injury causes a spike in hepatocyte cell-free DNA. Pancreatic beta cell death — the central event in type 1 diabetes — causes a spike in beta-cell cell-free DNA. Brain injury, organ rejection after transplant, drug toxicity, sepsis — all of these are reflected in shifts in the cell-type composition of cell-free DNA, and methylation deconvolution can read them.

The pioneering work in this space has come from the labs of Yuval Dor and Ruth Shemer at the Hebrew University in Jerusalem, who in a series of papers in the mid-to-late 2010s established the methylation-based cfDNA deconvolution framework and applied it to detecting beta-cell death in diabetes, hepatocyte death in liver disease, and similar applications. Their work has been complemented by groups at Stanford, Harvard, and several Chinese institutions, building progressively more comprehensive cell-type methylation atlases.

\[Pause.\]

The comprehensive human cell-type methylation atlas project, similar in spirit to the Human Cell Atlas for gene expression, is now well underway. By 2026, methylation profiles are available for several hundred distinct human cell types, with new ones being added continually. The atlases enable not just disease detection but tracking of organ-specific responses to interventions — for example, monitoring myocardial recovery after a heart attack, or assessing graft health after a kidney transplant — non-invasively from blood.

The clinical applications are real. Methylation-based cell-free DNA deconvolution is being used in transplant medicine to detect early organ rejection — donor-derived cell-free DNA in the recipient's blood signals injury to the transplanted organ before it's clinically apparent. It's being used in prenatal diagnostics to deconvolve fetal versus maternal contributions to cell-free DNA. It's being used in oncology to detect tumor-derived cell-free DNA even when the tissue of origin is unknown.

The combination of comprehensive methylation atlases, gentle low-input methylation chemistry (EM-seq, TAPS), and sensitive analytical methods (capture-based methylation sequencing for targeted panels, deep learning for cell-type classification) has put us at the threshold of a much broader liquid biopsy era — not just cancer detection but a real-time, non-invasive readout of cell death across the body, with implications for almost every area of medicine. Methylation is the cell-type barcode that makes this possible, and the methylation methods we've been building up across this lecture are what enable the reading of that barcode at clinical scale.

## SEGMENT 18 — Single-cell, spatial, and in vivo methylation — the frontier

We've spent most of this lecture on bulk methylation analysis — methods that average methylation across the millions of cells in a tissue sample or the billions of cell-free DNA molecules in a plasma draw. The frontier of the methylation field is moving beyond bulk, into spatially resolved, single-cell, and in vivo dynamic methylation measurement. Let me sketch the three main frontier directions before we close.

**Frontier one: single-cell methylation.** The first single-cell bisulfite sequencing was published in 2014 by the Reik lab at the Babraham Institute, demonstrating that you could in principle get a methylation profile from a single cell. The technique was lossy and noisy — bisulfite damage at single-cell scale is brutal, and each single cell only contributes coverage at a small fraction of the genome's CpGs — but it worked. Subsequent improvements (snmC-seq, snmC-seq2, scNMT-seq, scBS-seq) have refined the chemistry, and the introduction of EM-seq-based single-cell protocols has dramatically improved data quality. By 2026, single-cell methylation profiling is a real, if expensive, technique, with datasets of tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of cells being generated for tissue methylation atlas projects.

\[Pause.\]

The biological yield is substantial. Single-cell methylation has revealed methylation heterogeneity within tissues that bulk methods can't see — different cell types within a tumor, different cells in different states within a developmental compartment, rare cell populations with distinctive methylation programs. The brain methylation atlases produced by Joe Ecker's lab and others using single-nucleus methylation sequencing have revealed the methylation diversity of neuronal subtypes at unprecedented resolution. Combined with single-cell ATAC-seq and single-cell RNA-seq, single-cell methylation gives a multi-modal view of cell state that the bulk era couldn't approach.

**Frontier two: spatial methylation.** Just as spatial transcriptomics has revolutionized how we measure gene expression in tissue context, spatial methylation aims to map methylation patterns across the physical landscape of a tissue section. The early techniques here are still relatively primitive — most rely on capturing DNA from spatial barcoded slides similar to the Visium platform, with all the resolution and sensitivity limitations that implies — but progress is rapid. Spatial methylation methods like spatial-CUT&Tag for histone modifications, and the methylation analogs being developed by Rong Fan's lab at Yale and others, are pushing toward a future where you can see methylation patterns across a tissue section at near-cellular resolution. This will matter enormously for tumor biology, where methylation heterogeneity within a tumor maps onto spatial structure, and for development, where methylation gradients across embryonic tissues encode positional information.

**Frontier three: in vivo dynamic methylation.** All the methods we've discussed measure methylation at a single time point — you take a sample, you process it, you read out a snapshot. But methylation is dynamic. It changes during development, during cell-state transitions, in response to environmental signals. Measuring those dynamics in living cells, without disturbing them, is technically very hard, but it's the frontier. Methods like CRISPR-based methylation imaging — fluorescent tagging of methylation readers to localize methylated regions in live cells — are early-stage but exciting. Methods that use metabolic labeling of newly synthesized DNA, combined with methylation profiling, can distinguish "old" methylation patterns from "new" ones and reveal methylation dynamics during cell proliferation. The full vision — watching methylation change in real time at single-locus resolution in living cells — is not yet realized, but the pieces are starting to come together.

\[Pause.\]

There's also a fourth direction worth flagging — **direct in vivo writing of methylation**. Engineered DNA methyltransferases fused to CRISPR Cas9 nickase or dead Cas9 can be targeted to specific genomic sites, where they deposit methylation marks. The same can be done with TET enzymes to deposit demethylation. This is the methylation-editing toolkit, and it's being used both as a research tool to test causal hypotheses about methylation function and as a potential therapeutic modality. If aberrant methylation drives a disease — silencing of a tumor suppressor, for example — targeted demethylation might rescue it. Clinical applications are very early, but the technology is real and improving.

Methylation, then, is no longer a static, hard-to-measure mark. It's a dynamic, increasingly measurable, increasingly manipulable layer of biological information that overlaps with development, with disease, with aging, with environmental response, with cellular identity. The methods we've been building up through this lecture — from Frommer's 1992 bisulfite conversion to nanopore current-based calling and CRISPR methylation editing — are the toolkit that has made all of this possible.

If you walk out of here with one thing, let it be this: methylation is the most measurable, most studied, most clinically actionable layer of the epigenome, and the measurement methods are still evolving rapidly. The bisulfite era is winding down. The enzymatic and nanopore era is just beginning. The single-cell, spatial, and in vivo era is the frontier. And whatever comes next will continue to enrich how we read this small chemical tag that does so much biological work.

Thank you. Take a few minutes to stretch, and then we can take questions.

**Glossary.** **5mC**: 5-methylcytosine, the canonical methylated form of cytosine. **5hmC**: 5-hydroxymethylcytosine, an oxidized derivative of 5mC produced by TET enzymes. **CpG**: a cytosine immediately followed by a guanine on the same DNA strand; the predominant context for methylation in vertebrates. **CpG island**: a short genomic region with unusually high CpG density, typically at gene promoters, usually unmethylated when the gene is active. **DNMT**: DNA methyltransferase; the enzymes that write methylation. **TET**: ten-eleven translocation; the enzymes that oxidize 5mC and start the demethylation process. **MBD**: methyl-CpG binding domain; the readers of methylated DNA. **Bisulfite**: sodium bisulfite, the chemical that converts unmethylated cytosine to uracil while leaving 5mC intact. **WGBS**: whole-genome bisulfite sequencing. **RRBS**: reduced-representation bisulfite sequencing. **EM-seq**: enzymatic methyl-seq, NEB's enzymatic alternative to bisulfite. **TAPS**: TET-assisted pyridine borane sequencing, the Song lab's bisulfite-free chemistry. **oxBS-seq**: oxidative bisulfite sequencing. **TAB-seq**: TET-assisted bisulfite sequencing. **EPIC array**: Illumina Infinium MethylationEPIC array; the dominant commercial methylation array. **DMR**: differentially methylated region. **DMP**: differentially methylated position; single-CpG version of DMR. **EWAS**: epigenome-wide association study; the methylation analog of GWAS. **cfDNA**: cell-free DNA, fragmented DNA circulating in plasma. **Methylation age** or **epigenetic age**: predicted age from methylation patterns, e.g., from Horvath's clock.

**Common pitfalls in methylation analysis.** **Incomplete bisulfite conversion**: always include unmethylated lambda DNA spike-in controls; aim for >99% conversion efficiency. **Batch effects**: randomize cases and controls across batches; include batch as a covariate. **Cell-type composition**: especially in blood and other heterogeneous samples, adjust for estimated cell-type proportions using reference deconvolution. **Cross-reactive array probes**: drop the published list of cross-reactive EPIC probes from analysis. **Low coverage at individual CpGs**: in WGBS or RRBS, set minimum coverage thresholds before computing methylation levels. **Conflation of 5mC and 5hmC**: standard bisulfite, EM-seq, and TAPS all read 5mC + 5hmC together; use specialized assays for discrimination if 5hmC is expected to be substantial (especially in brain). **Confusion with bisulfite-converted reference**: when aligning, always use a bisulfite-aware aligner (Bismark, BSMAP) that handles the three-base mapping problem; standard Illumina aligners give garbage.

**Decision guide: which methylation method should I use?** **You have lots of input, want comprehensive coverage, money is not the issue**: WGBS or EM-seq at 30x coverage. **You have lots of input, want comprehensive coverage, money is an issue, and you mostly care about CpG islands**: RRBS. **You have many samples (hundreds or thousands), want reproducible quantitative data on regulatory CpGs**: Illumina EPIC array. **You have low-input clinical samples, e.g., cell-free DNA, FFPE, single cells**: EM-seq, possibly with targeted capture. **You need to distinguish 5mC from 5hmC**: run paired oxBS-seq + BS-seq, or TAPS + TAPSβ, or use nanopore. **You need long reads, phased methylation, repetitive regions, or structural variants**: nanopore direct methylation calling. **You want a methylation-age estimate from a small DNA sample**: EPIC array followed by Horvath's clock or one of the newer clocks. **You want to detect cancer or tissue injury from plasma**: methylation-based liquid biopsy (commercial: Galleri; research: targeted methylation capture sequencing of cell-type-specific markers).
