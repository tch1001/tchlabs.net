## SEGMENT 1 — The one-sentence definition, and why it matters

Alright, let's get started. Good morning, everyone. For the next two hours, we are going to talk about metabolic engineering — the discipline of rewriting cellular metabolism so that a cell makes, in industrial quantities, a chemical we have chosen. I want to start, the way I always start these things, with a single bumper sticker, because if you internalize the bumper sticker, half the lecture becomes intuitive.

Here is the sentence. **Metabolic engineering is the rewriting of a cell's chemistry so that the cell takes in a feedstock you give it, runs that feedstock through a chosen sequence of enzymes, and excretes the molecule you want.** That is it. That is the field. Every other thing we talk about today — Jay Keasling's artemisinin yeast, the Genentech insulin story, Amyris farnesene, flux balance analysis, CRISPR knockouts of competing pathways, opioids in yeast, milk proteins without cows — every single one is a variation on that single sentence.

So if you walk out of here today and a friend asks you what metabolic engineering is, I want you to be able to say: somebody picks a starting material — sugar, or methanol, or in the frontier case carbon dioxide — and they pick a target chemical — say, a malaria drug, or a fuel, or a protein, or a fragrance. Then they ask, what enzymes, in what order, would convert the starting material into the target? Some of those enzymes the cell already has. Some it does not, and have to be borrowed from another organism, often a plant or a fungus that natively makes the target chemical. The engineer takes the borrowed genes, puts them onto a plasmid or integrates them into the chromosome — and here you can hear the echo from the plasmid lecture and the genetic circuits lecture, because metabolic engineering sits on top of all that earlier machinery — and hands the engineered DNA to a workhorse cell. The cell, dutifully, runs the program. It eats the feedstock, runs it through the new enzymes, and out the other side comes the target chemical. You pump it out of the fermenter, purify it, and sell it. That is the field.

\[Pause.\]

Now I want to spend a minute on why this matters, because the casual undergraduate sometimes hears "metabolic engineering" and pictures a tiny boutique exercise, a niche corner of biotech. It is not niche. It is one of the largest application areas of molecular biology, and it has been since the 1980s. Insulin — the drug that keeps every type 1 diabetic alive in the developed world — is made by metabolic engineering. Human growth hormone, the drug for pituitary dwarfism — metabolic engineering. The hepatitis B vaccine — metabolic engineering. Lactic acid for biodegradable plastics — metabolic engineering. A growing fraction of the world's specialty chemicals, fragrances, flavors, food ingredients, and pharmaceutical precursors are now made by engineered microbes rather than by plant extraction or petrochemical synthesis. The field is doing what chemistry used to do, only with cells, and at increasing scale, and increasingly with a lower environmental footprint.

There is also a longer arc, which I will let frame the whole lecture. Chemistry — the kind we did in the twentieth century, with petroleum feedstocks, hot reactors, heavy metal catalysts, organic solvents — was extraordinarily powerful, and it built modern industry. It is also extraordinarily polluting, energy-intensive, and dependent on fossil inputs. Metabolic engineering is one of the few credible roads to a chemistry that is sustainable: room-temperature, water-based, fed by renewables, catalyzed by enzymes evolved by nature and tuned by us. Whether the road actually gets us to a carbon-neutral chemical industry — whether the cells can be made productive enough, cheap enough, robust enough — is one of the great open questions of applied biology. We will close, in Segment 18, on the frontier where this question is being actively fought out, with engineered _E. coli_ that fix carbon dioxide as their only carbon source. But hold the long arc in mind from the start. This is what is at stake.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you my favorite operational analogy, because we will use it over and over. **Imagine a microbial cell as a tiny chemical factory the size of a single dot of pencil graphite. The factory has a loading dock at the front, where raw material comes in. It has a complex floor plan inside, a network of conveyor belts and stations, each station an enzyme that does one chemical transformation. It has a back door, where waste products are excreted. And it has a goal — survival and reproduction — that shapes how the conveyor belts are arranged. Metabolic engineering is the discipline of walking onto the factory floor, ripping out some of the conveyor belts, installing new ones, redirecting traffic, and instructing the factory: from now on, please send a substantial fraction of your incoming carbon out the back door not as cell mass, but as this particular molecule I want.** The factory grumbles, because growing has been overridden, but it does what you ask, more or less, and out the back comes the product.

Now hold that factory picture in your mind. We will keep adding details — the loading dock is the carbon uptake system, the conveyor belts are enzymes, the floor plan is the metabolic network, the goal of the factory is fitness — and by the end of the lecture you should have a quite rich version of this metaphor in your head.

Here is the contract for the next two hours. By the end of this lecture, you should be able to do three things. First: explain what metabolic engineering is, using the factory analogy, and walk a friend through why anyone thought rewiring a microbe's chemistry was possible at all. Second: tell the founding stories — Genentech insulin in 1978, and Keasling artemisinin in the 2000s — and explain in your own words what each project actually did at the cellular level. Third: look at a news story about an alt-protein company, a biofuel startup, or a new microbial-made drug, and have a real intuition for what is going on inside the cells in those bioreactors. Everything I do today will be in service of those three goals.

One more framing note. Metabolic engineering as a named, self-conscious discipline crystallized in the early 1990s — the name itself is often credited to **Jay Bailey** and **Greg Stephanopoulos**, who wrote textbooks and review papers that gave the field its identity. But the practice goes back to the 1970s, when the first recombinant DNA techniques made it possible at all. We will move roughly chronologically — first the workhorses, then the early hits, then the famous artemisinin story, then the computational and evolutionary methods, then the commercial wave that turned the field into an industry, then the modern frontier. Let's go.

## SEGMENT 2 — The workhorses: who actually does the work inside a fermenter

Before we engineer anything, we need to know which cells we are engineering. Because here is the thing — and I want you to feel the weight of it — you do not get to pick any organism off the tree of life and use it as your metabolic factory. You pick from a very short list. The list of organisms that have been domesticated to the point where they can be reliably engineered and grown at industrial scale is, even today, maybe ten species deep. Four of those species do something like ninety percent of the world's biomanufacturing. Those four are our workhorses, and we need to spend some time on each, because the choice of workhorse shapes every downstream decision.

The first workhorse, and the one you will hear about most often, is **_Escherichia coli_**. _E. coli_ is the lab rat of microbiology. It is a gram-negative bacterium, normally found in the gut of mammals, harmless in its lab-adapted strains. It grows fast — doubling every twenty minutes under good conditions, which means a single cell can produce a fermenter full of biomass in less than a day. It is genetically tractable, which is a polite way of saying we have been engineering it for fifty years and have built up an enormous toolkit — well-characterized promoters, dozens of selectable markers, every kind of plasmid you could want, CRISPR, MAGE, recombineering, all the toys. The first ever recombinant protein drug — insulin — was produced in _E. coli_, in 1978. _E. coli_ is fast, transformable, cheap to grow on simple media, and easy to scale up. Its weaknesses are real but specific. It is a prokaryote, so it cannot do the post-translational modifications — glycosylation, complex disulfide arrangements, proteolytic processing — that some eukaryotic proteins require. It can have trouble with very long proteins. It can dump misfolded protein into insoluble inclusion bodies that you then have to refold. For small molecules and for simpler proteins, though, _E. coli_ is the default choice, and a fermenter full of _E. coli_ is the workhorse setup of the industry.

\[Pause.\]

The second workhorse is **_Saccharomyces cerevisiae_** — baker's yeast, brewer's yeast, the yeast that has fermented bread and beer for ten thousand years. _S. cerevisiae_ is a eukaryote, which is the key fact about it. It has a nucleus, it has an endoplasmic reticulum, it has Golgi, it has the full set of organelles and the full set of eukaryotic post-translational machinery. Proteins that need to be glycosylated, that need complex folding, that need to be secreted — yeast can often do those when _E. coli_ cannot. Yeast is also what is called **GRAS** in the regulatory world, which stands for "generally recognized as safe." Because humans have been eating yeast forever, the regulatory bar for using yeast in food and pharmaceutical applications is lower than for many other organisms. This matters enormously when you are trying to commercialize a product. Yeast grows a little slower than _E. coli_ — doubling time around 90 minutes — and is a little more demanding nutritionally, but it scales up cleanly to enormous fermenters, and the world's brewing industry has built out a hundred years of know-how on how to handle yeast at scale. When you want to engineer a complex eukaryotic biosynthetic pathway — particularly anything involving plant or fungal natural products — yeast is, more often than not, the platform of choice. This will matter when we get to artemisinin.

The third workhorse is **_Bacillus subtilis_** — a gram-positive soil bacterium, also harmless, well-characterized, and remarkably good at one thing in particular: secreting proteins into the growth medium. Where _E. coli_ tends to keep its proteins inside the cell, and you have to lyse the cells to get the product out, _Bacillus_ will happily push proteins out through its envelope into the surrounding liquid, where you can simply spin down the cells and harvest the supernatant. This is a huge advantage for industrial enzyme production. A vast amount of the world's industrial enzymes — proteases for laundry detergent, amylases for high-fructose corn syrup, lipases for cheese — are made by engineered _Bacillus_ strains. The industrial fermenters that crank out these enzymes are some of the largest biotechnology operations in the world, often tens or hundreds of thousands of liters, and they all run on _Bacillus_.

The fourth, and the one you may not have heard of, is **_Yarrowia lipolytica_**. _Yarrowia_ is what is called an **oleaginous** yeast — a yeast that naturally accumulates large amounts of lipid, sometimes up to 40 percent of its dry mass as oil. That makes it the natural platform for engineering anything in the lipid family — biofuels, fatty acids, lipid-soluble vitamins, omega-3s, specialty oils. _Yarrowia_ has been used commercially for things like microbial omega-3 oil for infant formula. As biofuels and oil-based chemicals become a bigger fraction of the metabolic engineering portfolio, _Yarrowia_ has become more and more important. It is the lipid specialist of the workhorse cast.

\[Pause.\]

There are others, with narrower roles. **_Pichia pastoris_**, another yeast, now formally renamed _Komagataella phaffii_, is a methanol-fed yeast that has become a favorite for secreted recombinant proteins. **_Corynebacterium glutamicum_** is the workhorse of the amino acid industry — most of the world's industrial glutamate and lysine are made by engineered _Corynebacterium_. **_Streptomyces_** species, the filamentous bacteria that naturally make a vast number of antibiotics, are engineered to produce novel antibiotic variants and other complex natural products. **_Aspergillus niger_**, a filamentous fungus, makes citric acid at industrial scale — the citric acid in your soda comes from engineered _Aspergillus_. **_Chinese hamster ovary cells_**, the mammalian cell line known as CHO, is the workhorse for therapeutic antibodies, although that is more cell-culture biology than classical metabolic engineering. The list goes on, but the four I listed first — _E. coli_, _S. cerevisiae_, _Bacillus_, _Yarrowia_ — cover most of what we will talk about today.

The analogy I want you to carry: **each workhorse is a different shop floor, with a different layout, different unionized labor, different ventilation. You match the workhorse to the product. Simple bacterial proteins and small molecules go to _E. coli_. Complex eukaryotic natural products and secreted glycoproteins go to yeast. Massive secreted industrial enzymes go to _Bacillus_. Lipids and oils go to _Yarrowia_.** Picking the workhorse is the first major engineering decision, and it constrains everything downstream — the toolkit you can use, the media you can grow on, the regulatory pathway, the scale-up economics. Get it wrong, and no amount of clever genetic engineering will save you.

Now let us look at the first generation of products this discipline made, because the history actually frames a lot of what came later.

## SEGMENT 3 — The early hits: insulin, growth hormone, and the founding of biotech

I want to take you back to 1976, because the company that started all of this was founded in 1976, and the story is worth telling in some detail. The company is **Genentech**, founded by **Bob Swanson**, a venture capitalist, and **Herb Boyer**, a UCSF biochemist who, with Stanley Cohen at Stanford, had co-invented recombinant DNA technology a few years earlier. Swanson cold-called Boyer, the story goes, and convinced him to meet for a beer. The conversation, by all accounts, lasted hours. Swanson asked: can we commercialize this technology? Boyer said: yes. They went and founded the company on what was, at the time, a wildly speculative bet — that you could take a human gene, put it onto a bacterial plasmid, transform _E. coli_, and have the bacteria produce a human protein drug in commercial quantities. Nobody had done that. Nobody knew if it was possible. The whole project was an extended bet that the plasmid and recombinant DNA tools we covered in the plasmids lecture would scale into a manufacturing process.

The first product they picked was a small, well-characterized hormone, partly because they had to pick something the science could plausibly handle. They picked **somatostatin** — a 14-amino-acid peptide — as a proof of concept, and in 1977 they showed they could make it in _E. coli_. That was the demonstration. Then they went after the real prize. **Insulin.**

Insulin, at the time, was extracted from the pancreases of slaughtered pigs and cows. The supply was tied to the meat industry. The product was animal insulin, slightly different from human insulin in amino acid sequence, which caused immune reactions in some patients. There were real, looming concerns about supply — diabetic populations were growing, and the meat industry could not necessarily keep pace. A recombinant source of pure human insulin, made by bacteria, would be a transformative product. So Genentech set out to make it. The team — including a young **David Goeddel** — chemically synthesized the gene for human insulin, since the natural gene was a nightmare of cloning at the time, broke the protein into its two chains, expressed each chain separately in _E. coli_, harvested them, and joined them chemically. In 1978, they announced that they had produced human insulin in _E. coli_. The first recombinant protein drug had been made.

The commercial partner was Eli Lilly, who had decades of expertise in insulin manufacturing. Lilly took the Genentech process, scaled it, and in 1982 the product — **Humulin** — received FDA approval. Recombinant human insulin became the first biotech drug ever sold. Type 1 diabetic patients started transitioning off pig insulin and onto Humulin. The bottleneck of the meat-supply chain was gone. The price came down. The supply scaled. As of today, virtually all insulin used in the world is recombinant, made in either _E. coli_ or yeast, with manufacturing capacity that vastly exceeds what could ever have been extracted from animal pancreases.

\[Pause.\]

I want you to sit with what that means. Recombinant insulin was the first proof that you could replace an entire extractive industry with engineered microbes. The pig-pancreas industry, with all its slaughterhouses and abattoirs and seasonal supply variations, was replaced by a fermenter. The fermenter produced a purer product. It produced more of it. It produced it on demand. It produced human insulin, not pig insulin, so the immune reactions went away. This was not just a new drug. It was a new paradigm. And the paradigm was: take any protein you want, find the gene, put it on a plasmid, put it into a microbe, scale up. That paradigm is the foundation on which the entire biotech industry was built.

The next big product was **human growth hormone**. Before recombinant DNA, human growth hormone was extracted from the pituitary glands of human cadavers — a horrifying logistical process, and one that was eventually shut down in 1985 when several patients developed Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease, the human version of mad cow, traced to prion contamination in the cadaver-derived hormone. The need for a recombinant alternative was, at that point, urgent. Genentech had been working on it in parallel and was ready. In 1985, recombinant human growth hormone, marketed as **Protropin**, received FDA approval. Kids with pituitary dwarfism could now be treated with a safe, abundant supply.

Then came a wave of products — **tissue plasminogen activator** for stroke, **erythropoietin** for anemia, **interferon alpha** for hepatitis and certain cancers, **factor VIII** for hemophilia, dozens of other proteins. Each one was a gene, put on a plasmid or integrated into a chromosome, expressed in either _E. coli_ or yeast or, for the more complex glycosylated proteins, in mammalian CHO cells. By the early 1990s, the recombinant protein industry was a multi-billion-dollar business, and it had created the template for what metabolic engineering would do next.

\[Pause.\]

The thing I want you to notice about all of these early hits is that they were proteins. You picked a gene, you expressed the gene, you harvested the protein. There was, in some sense, only one enzyme that mattered — the one encoded by your gene of interest. The cell's own metabolism was, for the most part, irrelevant. You were using the cell as a translation machine for one specific gene. That is not really metabolic engineering, in the deeper sense. That is heterologous protein production. And the language reflects that — the field at the time was often called "recombinant DNA technology" or "biopharmaceutical manufacturing," not "metabolic engineering."

The next step — the step that turned this into metabolic engineering proper — was to ask: what if the product is not a protein? What if the product is a small molecule, like a drug or a chemical, and to make it you have to install not one enzyme but a whole pathway of enzymes, and you have to redirect the cell's metabolic flux through that pathway? That is a fundamentally different problem. That is metabolic engineering in the strict sense. And the project that, more than any other, established what that looked like was Jay Keasling's artemisinin yeast. We turn to that next.

## SEGMENT 4 — Keasling's artemisinin: the prototype modern metabolic engineering project

Let me tell you about the project that, more than any other single demonstration, made metabolic engineering famous outside its own discipline. The project is **the engineering of yeast to produce artemisinic acid**, a precursor to the antimalarial drug artemisinin. The lead investigator is **Jay Keasling**, then at UC Berkeley. The funder is the **Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation**. The commercial partner is **Sanofi**. The work spans roughly a decade, from the early 2000s to the early 2010s. And it is, by general agreement, the prototype of what modern, ambitious metabolic engineering looks like.

The motivating problem is malaria. Malaria kills hundreds of thousands of people a year, mostly children, mostly in sub-Saharan Africa. The most effective drug class for treating malaria is the artemisinins, derived from a Chinese herb called _Artemisia annua_, sweet wormwood. The active molecule, **artemisinin**, is a sesquiterpene lactone with an unusual peroxide bridge that makes it lethal to the malaria parasite. It is extracted from the plant. The plant is grown in fields, primarily in China and Vietnam, harvested, and the artemisinin is purified. The problem is supply. The plant takes about eight months to grow. Yields are variable, weather-dependent, subject to political and agricultural disruption. Prices spike and crash unpredictably. Sometimes there is too little artemisinin and patients die. Sometimes there is too much and farmers go broke. Neither scenario is acceptable for a life-saving drug. There needed to be a stable, scalable, microbial source.

\[Pause.\]

Keasling's lab proposed to engineer yeast to produce artemisinic acid — the chemical immediate-precursor of artemisinin — and then to use chemical synthesis to finish the conversion from artemisinic acid to artemisinin. This split — biological synthesis of the hard precursor, chemical conversion of the easy final step — is called a **semi-synthetic** process, and it is a common pattern in metabolic engineering. You let the cell do the part the cell is good at, which is the hard, stereochemically complex biosynthesis, and you let chemistry handle the simpler oxidations and rearrangements at the end. The combined process can be cheaper and more reliable than either approach alone.

Now the actual engineering. Artemisinic acid is a terpenoid, which means it belongs to a huge class of natural products built up from five-carbon isoprene units. Yeast naturally has a terpenoid pathway — the mevalonate pathway — that produces a building block called **farnesyl pyrophosphate**, or FPP, a fifteen-carbon terpenoid intermediate that the cell normally uses to make ergosterol, its membrane sterol. Farnesyl pyrophosphate is, conveniently, the right starting point for artemisinic acid. So the strategy was: take yeast, push more carbon flux through its native mevalonate pathway to overproduce FPP, then install the plant enzymes that convert FPP into artemisinic acid.

Pushing flux through mevalonate meant overexpressing the rate-limiting enzymes — particularly **HMG-CoA reductase**, the famous statin target — to lift the bottleneck. It also meant **downregulating** the squalene synthase that pulls FPP off into ergosterol synthesis, so that more FPP would accumulate available for the new pathway rather than being siphoned away to membrane lipids. So you have one set of changes that lifts the upstream flow and another set that closes off the downstream drain, both pushing more FPP into the pool. This kind of paired push-pull strategy is utterly characteristic of metabolic engineering.

Then the new pathway. They found, in _Artemisia annua_, three enzymes that take FPP through the conversion to artemisinic acid. **Amorphadiene synthase** cyclizes FPP into a hydrocarbon called amorphadiene. **CYP71AV1**, a cytochrome P450 enzyme from the plant, oxidizes amorphadiene through several steps to artemisinic acid. There is also a partner reductase needed to keep the P450 supplied with electrons. Each of these enzymes was cloned from _Artemisia annua_, the genes were codon-optimized for yeast, put under strong yeast promoters, integrated into the yeast chromosome, and the engineered yeast was grown in a fermenter on sugar. Out the back came artemisinic acid — at first in tiny amounts, then, through ten years of iterative engineering, in commercially relevant quantities, hitting yields of more than 25 grams per liter in the final published strain.

\[Pause.\]

The downstream story is just as important as the engineering. The Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, which had been investing heavily in malaria treatment, funded the project. The technology was developed at the not-for-profit **Institute for OneWorld Health**, with the explicit social goal that the resulting drug would be affordable in malaria-endemic countries. The completed strain and process were transferred — royalty-free for malaria applications — to **Sanofi**, the French pharmaceutical company, which built out commercial-scale fermentation facilities, took the yeast-produced artemisinic acid through the chemical conversion to artemisinin, and put the resulting drug into the global supply chain in 2014. For several years, Sanofi-manufactured semi-synthetic artemisinin was a substantial source of supply for the World Health Organization's malaria programs. The economics ended up complicated — agricultural artemisinin remained cheaper at times, and Sanofi eventually scaled back the program — but the proof of principle is undisputed. You could take a plant natural product, install its pathway in yeast, hit industrial yields, transfer the technology to a major pharmaceutical company, and feed the resulting drug into a life-saving global supply chain. The whole thing worked.

The analogy I want you to carry: **the Keasling artemisinin yeast is the prototype modern metabolic engineering project — full pathway from yeast precursor to antimalarial precursor, semi-synthetic completion, Gates-funded, technology transferred to Sanofi, drug in the field.** It is the demonstration that this discipline, the way the genetic circuits people demonstrated their field with the toggle switch and the repressilator, can do something at industrial scale that is socially important. After artemisinin, every funding agency and every venture capital firm interested in biology paid attention to metabolic engineering. The field went from a research subspecialty to a recognized arena for serious commercial and humanitarian investment. We will, in later segments, see how the wave that followed broke into biofuels, fragrances, food proteins, and a dozen other product categories.

But before we go to those waves, we need to talk about the central engineering problem you have to solve to do any of this. You cannot install a pathway without knowing what the pathway is. You have to find the enzymes. That is a problem in itself, and it is what we turn to next.

## SEGMENT 5 — Pathway discovery: how you find the enzymes in the first place

So picture yourself at the start of one of these projects. You have a target molecule. Say it is artemisinic acid, or vincristine, or thebaine, or some plant alkaloid nobody has ever made microbially. You know the target's chemical structure. You know — from natural products chemistry — that some plant or fungus or microbe in the world makes this molecule natively. You need to find the enzymes that organism uses to make it, get the genes for those enzymes, and install them in your workhorse. This step — pathway discovery — is, for many projects, the slowest and most expensive part of the whole effort. I want to spend a segment on it because it is invisible in most popular accounts of metabolic engineering and yet it is where a huge fraction of the actual work lives.

The classical approach, the one that worked for artemisinin, is **biochemical purification**. You grow a lot of the source organism — say, tons of _Artemisia annua_ leaves — extract the relevant tissues, look for fractions that contain the enzymes of interest, follow the enzymatic activity through column after column of purification, eventually get a pure protein, sequence it by Edman degradation or mass spectrometry, and use that sequence to clone the gene. This is brutal work. It can take years. And for many enzymes, particularly the membrane-bound cytochrome P450s that show up everywhere in plant natural product pathways, the enzyme is so unstable and so hard to purify that biochemical purification simply does not work.

\[Pause.\]

The modern approach takes advantage of cheap sequencing. You sequence the genome — or more often the transcriptome, the set of expressed mRNAs — of the source organism. For _Artemisia annua_, you sequence the trichomes, the little hair-like structures on the leaf surface where artemisinin biosynthesis happens, and you compare gene expression in those tissues to gene expression in tissues that do not make artemisinin. The genes that are highly expressed in the producing tissue and not in the non-producing tissue are your candidates. You then take each candidate gene, express it in yeast or _E. coli_, see whether it has the chemistry you predict — does it cyclize FPP, does it oxidize amorphadiene — and assemble the pathway one enzyme at a time. This is faster than biochemical purification but it still involves a great deal of guesswork and screening. The first enzymes in a pathway are usually easier — they tend to have characteristic sequence motifs — but the late-stage tailoring enzymes, the P450s and methyltransferases and acyltransferases that add the finishing touches, are notoriously hard to identify, because there are dozens of them per genome and only specific ones are involved in your target pathway.

There is now a sub-discipline called **genome mining** that uses bioinformatics to predict biosynthetic gene clusters from genome sequences. This works particularly well for bacterial and fungal natural products, where the biosynthetic genes are often grouped together in a single chromosomal region — a **biosynthetic gene cluster** — that can be recognized by characteristic patterns. Tools like **antiSMASH** scan a genome and predict where the gene clusters are, what kind of molecule each cluster might make, and what the candidate enzymes are. For plants, where the genes are scattered across chromosomes, this is harder, but co-expression analysis on transcriptomic data plays a similar role.

\[Pause.\]

Now there is a deeper structural problem I want you to appreciate. When you take a plant or fungal enzyme and put it into yeast or _E. coli_, the enzyme is being asked to work in an environment it never evolved for. The cytoplasmic pH might be different. The redox state is different. The set of partner proteins it normally interacts with is missing. The post-translational modifications are different. The substrate concentrations are different. So even if you have the right gene, the enzyme may simply not work, or may work with one percent of its native activity, when expressed in your workhorse. Almost every successful metabolic engineering project has had to either evolve the heterologous enzymes for better activity in their new context — we will spend a segment on directed evolution shortly — or engineer the host to be more hospitable, for example by reconstituting the cytochrome P450 reductase partners or by buffering the cellular redox state.

The factory analogy holds here, too. You can find the right machine — the right conveyor belt — in another factory and ship it to yours. But the machine was designed for the floor layout of the original factory. It expects a particular voltage on the power lines, a particular humidity, a particular set of neighboring machines. Drop it into your floor and it may not run at all. You have to either modify the machine or modify the floor, or both. That iterative co-engineering of the host and the heterologous enzymes is one of the chronic engineering realities of metabolic engineering, and it eats years of effort on most serious projects.

That brings us to the question of how, once you have a pathway installed, you actually figure out how to optimize the flow of carbon through it. Because the cell has its own metabolism, its own pathways, its own priorities, and you are now asking it to share. To do that systematically, the field developed a powerful modeling framework — flux balance analysis — and that is our next topic.

## SEGMENT 6 — Flux balance analysis: doing math on the metabolic network

I want to spend a segment on the computational backbone of modern metabolic engineering, because most popular accounts of the field skip over it entirely, and you cannot really understand how decisions are actually made in industrial strain engineering without it. The tool is called **flux balance analysis**, or FBA, and it is one of the most beautiful applications of mathematical optimization in biology.

Here is the setup. You have a cell. The cell's metabolism is a network of biochemical reactions — a few thousand reactions in _E. coli_, several thousand in yeast — each reaction interconverting a particular set of metabolites with specific stoichiometries. You can draw this as a giant graph: metabolites are nodes, reactions are edges. The whole thing — for a well-studied organism like _E. coli_ — is now reconstructed and curated, and you can download it as a mathematical object. The current _E. coli_ model, called **iML1515**, has roughly 1500 genes, 2700 reactions, and 1900 metabolites. Yeast has comparable models. These are called **genome-scale metabolic models**, and building them is itself a major sub-discipline.

\[Pause.\]

Now, on top of this network, you write down two simple principles. **First, steady state**: assume that the cell, while growing exponentially, is in metabolic steady state — every internal metabolite is being produced at exactly the same rate it is being consumed, so concentrations stay constant. Mathematically, this means that for every internal metabolite, the sum of rates producing it equals the sum of rates consuming it. That gives you one equation per metabolite, which is around two thousand equations for _E. coli_. **Second, constraints**: each reaction has a maximum rate it can run at, set by physics — substrate diffusion limits, enzyme abundance, thermodynamic reversibility. Some reactions are irreversible; their flux must be non-negative. You write down all those bounds as inequalities.

Now you have a linear system. Two thousand steady-state equations. Several thousand inequality constraints. And — because there are more reactions than metabolites — you have many more variables than equations. The system is underdetermined. There are infinitely many flux distributions that satisfy the constraints. You need one more thing — an **objective function** — to pick out a single solution. The classical choice, the one that works astonishingly well for predicting wild-type microbial behavior, is **biomass production**. You assume the cell, evolved by natural selection, is configured to maximize its growth rate — to convert as much of its incoming carbon as possible into more cells. You add an equation that bundles up the necessary precursors — amino acids, nucleotides, lipids, cofactors — in the right proportions to make a new cell, and you ask: what flux distribution maximizes the rate of that bundle production, subject to all the stoichiometric and capacity constraints?

That is a linear programming problem. It is the kind of problem mathematicians can solve very fast for very large systems. You feed it to a solver, and out comes a flux distribution — a predicted rate for every reaction in the network. For wild-type _E. coli_ growing on glucose, the predicted distribution matches measured fluxes remarkably well. The model is genuinely predictive.

\[Pause.\]

Now the engineering trick. You can change the objective. Instead of maximizing biomass, you can ask: what flux distribution maximizes production of my target molecule, subject to the constraint that the cell still has to grow at some minimum rate to be viable? That gives you the theoretical maximum yield of your product on your feedstock, and a flux distribution that achieves it. You can then compare that predicted distribution to the wild-type distribution. The differences tell you what needs to change — which reactions need to be upregulated, which need to be knocked out, which auxiliary pathways need to be installed or removed. There are computational frameworks built on top of FBA — **OptKnock**, **OptGene**, **GDLS** — that automate the search for gene knockouts that would push the cell toward higher product yields. Run the optimization, get a list of candidate knockouts, go test them in the lab.

The analogy I want you to carry: **flux balance analysis is the constraint-based map of the cell's metabolism — given the network, the inputs, the steady-state assumption, what flux distribution maximizes the product? Mathematical optimization picks out a single solution from infinitely many.** It is the computational equivalent of asking a logistics company: given your roads, your trucks, and your warehouses, how do you route shipments to maximize delivery of one particular package? The answer involves trade-offs you would never spot by eye, and it depends critically on the constraints you have specified.

There are caveats, and you should be honest about them. FBA assumes steady state, which is only approximately true in a real fermenter where conditions change over time. It does not capture regulation, only stoichiometric capacity. It assumes you have a complete and correct genome-scale model, which is true for a handful of organisms and approximate for many more. It does not predict kinetics — only flux distributions. So FBA tells you the upper bound of what is possible and roughly which manipulations point in the right direction. It does not, by itself, tell you how to actually achieve those upper bounds. For that you need experiments, evolution, and a lot of strain construction. But FBA frames the problem and prioritizes the experiments. Modern industrial strain engineering, at companies like Genomatica or Amyris or Ginkgo, runs FBA-style analyses every day to guide which manipulations to test next.

That brings us to the question of how, once you have identified the manipulations and the enzymes you want to use, you make those enzymes work well enough to actually hit useful yields. The dominant answer there is directed evolution — and that is the next segment.

---

## SEGMENT 7 — Directed evolution in service of metabolic pathways

So you have a pathway. You have installed it. You have used FBA to predict the manipulations that should push flux into your product. You run the strain. The yield is, charitably, a tenth of what you predicted. Why? Often because one or more of the heterologous enzymes — the plant or fungal enzymes you transplanted into your microbial host — is performing at a fraction of its native activity. The cell's solvent is wrong. The cofactor pool is wrong. The substrate pool is wrong. The enzyme is folding badly. Whatever the reason, the enzyme is the bottleneck, and you cannot fix it by adjusting fermentation conditions. You need to make the enzyme itself better.

The dominant tool for this is **directed evolution**, and we owe its modern form largely to **Frances Arnold**, who shared the 2018 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for it. The idea is to do, in a few weeks at the lab bench, what natural evolution would take millions of years to do — generate variants of an enzyme, screen them for the property you want, keep the winners, repeat. The cycle is mutate, screen, select, repeat.

Mutating is the easy part. You can do **error-prone PCR**, where you deliberately use a low-fidelity polymerase to introduce random errors into your gene at a controlled rate, generating a library of variants. You can do **DNA shuffling**, where you take homologous genes from related organisms, chop them up, and reassemble them, generating chimeric variants that mix and match domains. You can do **site-saturation mutagenesis**, where you target specific residues — for example, residues lining the active site — and generate every possible amino acid substitution at each one. The output is a library: a few thousand to a few million variants of your gene, each carried in a separate cell.

The hard part is screening. You need a way to look at each variant and ask, is this one better than the parent? For some properties — fluorescence, antibiotic resistance, growth on a particular substrate — screening is straightforward. For more exotic chemistry — does this enzyme convert substrate A to product B more efficiently — you often have to invent a custom screen, sometimes by linking the chemistry you care about to a fluorescent or growth readout. There is a whole subculture of clever biosensor design within directed evolution that exists exclusively to make screens feasible. Sometimes you can do **high-throughput screening** with robotics, running thousands of variants through an assay per day. Sometimes you can do **selection**, where the variants that have the property you want simply outgrow the variants that do not — fastest, most powerful, but only works when the property is tied to survival.

\[Pause.\]

The interplay of directed evolution and metabolic engineering is intimate. Almost every successful industrial pathway has had some of its enzymes evolved for the production context. The artemisinin yeast went through rounds of enzyme evolution. The opioid yeast we will discuss in Segment 12 required extensive enzyme engineering of borrowed plant P450s. The biofuels work at Amyris and at LS9 used directed evolution heavily. The pattern is: install the pathway in the host, run it, identify which step is the bottleneck, evolve that step, repeat. You essentially do strain engineering at the level of the whole cell and enzyme engineering at the level of the individual proteins, in parallel, iteratively.

The analogy I want you to carry: **directed evolution is the engineer's gift of accelerated evolution — millions of years of selection compressed into weeks of screening — applied to an enzyme that needs to work in a context evolution never prepared it for.** You take the enzyme, mutate it, find the variants that work better in your cellular context, and the cell now has a tool that fits its new floor plan.

Now, the next layer of complexity. Even if you have all your enzymes working beautifully, the pathway may still fail because the cofactors are wrong. Cofactors are the small molecules that enzymes use as helpers — and the balance of cofactors is one of the chronic battlefields of metabolic engineering.

---

## SEGMENT 8 — Cofactor engineering: NADH, NADPH, ATP, and the redox balance

I want to spend a segment on cofactors, because they are the unglamorous, hidden constraint that breaks more metabolic engineering projects than any other single factor. If you do not understand cofactor balance, you will misdiagnose every yield problem you encounter.

Here is the basic biochemistry, briefly. Most enzymes that catalyze redox reactions — the addition or removal of electrons — do not directly handle the electrons themselves. They use cofactors as electron shuttles. The two big shuttles in cellular metabolism are **NADH** and **NADPH** — two molecules that are chemically very similar, differing only by a phosphate group, but that are kept in two completely separate pools by the cell. NADH is the cofactor that participates in catabolism — the breakdown of sugars to produce energy. The cell typically has lots of NAD-plus available to be reduced to NADH, and the resulting NADH gets reoxidized in the electron transport chain to make ATP. NADPH is the cofactor that participates in biosynthesis — the building up of complex molecules from simple precursors. NADPH is regenerated through the pentose phosphate pathway and other specialized routes, and is used by anabolic enzymes to provide reducing power.

The split is enforced because the cell wants to keep catabolism and anabolism independently regulated. You do not want your sugar breakdown rate to dictate your biosynthesis rate; you want the two to be tunable separately. The two cofactor pools — NADH for catabolism, NADPH for biosynthesis — are how the cell decouples them. The cell maintains these pools at very different ratios. NADH is kept relatively oxidized, with a higher proportion of NAD-plus. NADPH is kept relatively reduced, with a higher proportion of NADPH.

\[Pause.\]

Now here is the problem. When you install a heterologous pathway, you have inherited whatever cofactor preferences the original enzymes had. Many plant biosynthetic enzymes — the ones for terpenoids, alkaloids, polyketides — use NADPH, because in plants those pathways are anabolic and have access to plenty of NADPH from photosynthesis. When you transplant those enzymes into _E. coli_ growing on glucose, the cell's natural inclination is to make NADH, not NADPH. Your pathway demands NADPH at every step. The cell's supply is mismatched. The pathway runs at a fraction of the rate it could.

The cell's response, if you push too hard, is to grow slower while diverting more pentose phosphate flux to generate NADPH. This is metabolically expensive. It is also limited — there is only so much NADPH the cell can generate without sacrificing growth too much. You hit a ceiling.

The engineering response is **cofactor engineering**. There are several flavors. The first is to swap the cofactor preference of the heterologous enzyme — to find or engineer a variant of your enzyme that uses NADH instead of NADPH. There are sometimes natural NADH-using homologs of your target enzyme; if not, you can use directed evolution or structure-guided mutagenesis to switch the preference. There are well-known residues in the cofactor binding pocket that distinguish NADH from NADPH binding, and swapping them often switches the preference, though with some loss of activity that needs to be recovered.

The second flavor is to engineer the host to produce more of the cofactor you need. You can overexpress the enzymes that generate NADPH — particularly the pentose phosphate pathway enzymes. You can introduce a **transhydrogenase**, an enzyme that catalyzes the interconversion of NADH and NADPH, to let the cell shuttle reducing equivalents between the two pools as needed. You can knock out competing reactions that consume NADPH for biosynthesis you no longer need at high rates.

The third flavor is to **redox-balance the pathway itself**. The cleanest engineered pathways are designed so that the total NADH produced and consumed across the pathway is roughly balanced, and likewise for NADPH. If your pathway as drawn requires a net input of three NADPH per molecule of product but produces no NADPH internally, you will pay an enormous cofactor cost. If you can redesign the pathway — perhaps by including an extra step that generates NADPH from another reaction — to be redox-neutral, the cell handles it much better. The art of pathway design includes this kind of cofactor accounting.

\[Pause.\]

ATP is the other big cofactor. ATP is the cell's energy currency — every kinase, every ligase, every active transport step consumes it. Heterologous pathways that require a lot of ATP — for example, pathways that go through phosphorylated intermediates that have to be made by ATP-consuming kinases — drain the cell's energy supply, slow growth, and cap yields. ATP-efficient pathway design is a constant theme in modern strain engineering.

The analogy I want you to carry: **cofactor engineering is the discipline of matching your pathway's cofactor demand to the cell's cofactor supply. You need NADPH, not NADH; the cell makes NADH preferentially. You swap the enzyme to use NADH, or you push the cell to make more NADPH, or you redesign the pathway to be redox-neutral. The pathway only runs as fast as its cofactor supply lets it.** Every successful industrial strain has had its cofactor balance worked out. It is invisible in the papers, mostly, because it is unglamorous work, but it eats months of engineering effort on every serious project.

We have now done a lot of bench-level methods. Let us pull up to a higher level and talk about how the modern field actually edits genomes at scale to make many changes at once. That is the next segment, and it sets up the second half.

## SEGMENT 9 — Genome-scale engineering: MAGE, REXER, and the multi-locus editing era

I want to close the first half of the lecture with the modern tools that have changed how fast metabolic engineering can iterate. Because for a long time — for the first three decades of the field — you would make one or two genome edits per round and then characterize the strain, and a complete project would involve a few dozen edits over several years. The bottleneck was the rate at which you could make and combine edits. In the last fifteen years, that bottleneck has been broken open by a set of tools collectively called **multi-locus** or **genome-scale** editing.

The breakthrough tool, the one that opened the door, was **MAGE — Multiplex Automated Genome Engineering**, developed in **George Church's** lab at Harvard around 2009, with **Harris Wang** as lead author. MAGE built on a technique called **recombineering** — recombination-mediated genetic engineering — which had been developed in the early 2000s. We have a whole separate set of material on recombineering, so I will keep this short, but the gist is that you can use a viral protein called **lambda Beta** to drive the integration of single-stranded DNA oligonucleotides into the bacterial chromosome at any homologous site. Feed a cell a short oligo with the edit you want, expose it to lambda Beta, and the oligo gets incorporated into the chromosome at the corresponding location during DNA replication. One oligo, one edit.

MAGE took this and multiplied it. Instead of feeding the cell one oligo, you feed it a pool of dozens of oligos targeting different sites simultaneously. Some cells take up one oligo, some take up several. After a round, you have a population of cells with various combinations of the edits. You grow them out, repeat the cycle — feed in more oligos, integrate more edits — and after many cycles, some cells will have accumulated many of the edits at once. The Church lab demonstrated this by simultaneously editing dozens of sites involved in lycopene production, an industrially relevant terpenoid pigment, and dramatically boosting lycopene yields in a handful of rounds. The whole thing could be automated on a benchtop robot — hence "Automated" in the name — running thousands of edits per day across millions of cells.

\[Pause.\]

MAGE was a watershed. It demonstrated that you did not have to make edits one at a time. You could make many at once, generate combinatorial libraries of edits, and find the winning combinations by selection or screening. The pace of strain engineering accelerated by an order of magnitude. The cost per edit collapsed.

Building on that, **Jason Chin's lab** at the MRC LMB developed a related but distinct technology called **REXER — Replicon EXcision for Enhanced Recombination** — and its sister technology **GENESIS**, for synthesizing large stretches of recoded genome and inserting them into _E. coli_ chromosomes. REXER lets you replace long stretches of chromosome — tens of kilobases at a time — with synthetic DNA carrying many designed changes. Chin's group used these tools to build _E. coli_ with completely recoded genomes — Syn61, for example, an _E. coli_ in which every instance of one of the redundant codons was swapped out across the entire genome — a genome with thousands of synonymous changes built up over many rounds of REXER. That kind of multi-locus, multi-kilobase editing was, twenty years ago, simply inconceivable. Today it is routine in the leading labs.

There is also, sitting alongside these recombineering-based tools, the CRISPR-based editing era. We will come back to CRISPR for metabolic engineering in Segment 16 specifically. The headline is that CRISPR lets you make precise edits at any specified site with high efficiency, knock out genes, suppress genes with CRISPRi, activate them with CRISPRa, and combine many targeted manipulations more easily than ever before. CRISPR is, for metabolic engineering, less a revolutionary new capability than a substantial cost reduction in capabilities that recombineering had already pioneered, but the cost reduction has been transformative for the throughput of engineering campaigns.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to carry: **MAGE and the multi-locus editing tools are the discipline's equivalent of the move from hand-soldering electronic components onto a breadboard to mass-fabricating silicon wafers with millions of transistors at once. Pre-MAGE, you made one or two edits at a time. Post-MAGE, you can make dozens at once and combine them combinatorially. The throughput of strain engineering campaigns has increased by orders of magnitude.** That capacity is what lets a modern company like Ginkgo Bioworks — which we will meet in Segment 11 — run dozens of strain engineering projects in parallel across many target molecules.

The relationship to recombineering is direct. If you want to dig deeper into how MAGE actually works — how lambda Beta finds its targets, how oligo design affects integration efficiency, how you guard against off-target effects — those mechanistic details belong in the recombineering material. The metabolic engineering perspective here is just: this is the tool that lets you make many genome edits cheaply and in parallel, and the modern field would not exist in its current form without it.

That is the end of the first hour. Let us take a fifteen-minute break. The second half is where the rubber meets the road, where you will see what this discipline has actually produced and where it is going. See you in fifteen.

## SEGMENT 10 — Amyris and biojet fuels: the biofuels-to-cosmetics pivot

Welcome back. I want to start the second half with a story that captures, in miniature, what happens when an ambitious metabolic engineering project meets the real economics of commodity chemicals. The company is **Amyris**, founded in 2003 by several people from Jay Keasling's UC Berkeley lab, including **Jack Newman**, **Kinkead Reiling**, and **Neil Renninger**, along with **John Melo** as CEO. The original mission was, in the broadest possible terms, to use the same yeast engineering platform that had produced artemisinic acid to produce other terpenoid-derived molecules at industrial scale. The early target was a fifteen-carbon terpenoid called **farnesene**.

Farnesene, like artemisinic acid, is built from the mevalonate pathway by way of farnesyl pyrophosphate. By installing a single enzyme — **farnesene synthase**, borrowed from an apple — and pushing flux through the engineered mevalonate pathway, Amyris yeast could be made to secrete farnesene into the fermentation broth. Farnesene is a hydrocarbon, oil-soluble, and it floats on top of the aqueous broth, where it can be skimmed off. That is a beautiful product geometry for downstream processing — no expensive purification, just decant the oil. The ambition was huge. Farnesene is a long-chain hydrocarbon that, after hydrogenation to farnesane, has properties similar to diesel fuel. Amyris went after the **biofuels** market — biojet, bio-diesel — pitched as a renewable alternative to petroleum fuels, with the major airline industry as a target customer. They built fermentation capacity in Brazil, where sugarcane provided a cheap fermentable feedstock, and they raised hundreds of millions of dollars on the biofuels vision.

\[Pause.\]

Then the economics happened. Petroleum prices stayed low. Microbial biofuel production, even at the impressive yields Amyris achieved, remained substantially more expensive per liter than petroleum-derived diesel. The market for renewable jet fuel existed but was small, willing to pay only modest premiums, and dominated by competing approaches like used cooking oil. Amyris could not compete on price in the bulk fuel market. The whole biofuels-from-engineered-microbes movement of the late 2000s — Amyris, LS9, Solazyme, Gevo, several others — collectively bumped up against the same reality. You could make fuel-grade hydrocarbons from sugar in engineered cells; you just could not make them cheaper than petroleum.

So Amyris pivoted. They noticed that farnesene, hydrogenated to farnesane, was chemically very similar to a high-value cosmetic ingredient called **squalane**. Squalane is a saturated hydrocarbon traditionally extracted from shark liver oil — a horrifying supply chain in environmental terms, decimating shark populations — and used as a skin emollient in high-end cosmetics. Demand for squalane was strong, prices were high (orders of magnitude above fuel prices per liter), and there was an obvious appetite for a renewable, animal-free alternative. Amyris took their farnesane and rebranded it as biosqualane, sold it into the cosmetics industry, and found a profitable margin. The same molecule that had been a biofuel commodity became a premium cosmetic ingredient. The pivot worked.

The analogy I want you to carry: **Amyris is the biofuels-to-cosmetics pivot — they started making biojet fuel from engineered yeast, couldn't beat petroleum on price, and discovered that the same farnesene molecule was a great squalane replacement for the cosmetics industry. They pivoted from a commodity market they could not win to a specialty market they could.** It is a pattern that recurs all over metabolic engineering. Microbial production tends to be expensive per kilogram compared to entrenched alternatives. The successful applications are usually high-value specialty chemicals — drugs, fragrances, cosmetic ingredients, food additives — where the per-kilogram premium of microbial production can be justified by the value of the product, the environmental story, or the avoidance of a problematic supply chain.

\[Pause.\]

Amyris kept building out the platform. They expanded into other terpenoid products — flavors, fragrances, vitamins, more cosmetic ingredients — and into specialty food ingredients via spin-off and partnership arrangements. Their financial history has been turbulent, with the company going through restructuring as recently as the mid-2020s, but the strain engineering platform itself produced real products at real scale. The lesson for the field was clear. Pick your markets carefully. Microbial production wins where the alternative is more expensive, more polluting, or based on a finite or ethically problematic source. It loses in commodity markets where it competes directly with cheap petrochemicals. The successful metabolic engineering companies of the next decade, in many cases, learned from the Amyris experience and went straight to specialty markets, skipping the biofuels detour.

We will, in the very last segment, come back to whether the biofuels dream might yet revive if we start counting carbon properly and if cells can be engineered to fix CO2 directly. But for the commercial story up to about 2020, the lesson is: pick specialty, not commodity. With that lesson in mind, let us look at the company that turned strain engineering itself into a horizontal platform — Ginkgo Bioworks.

## SEGMENT 11 — Ginkgo Bioworks and the strain-engineering factory

If Amyris was the bet that you could make a particular product family — terpenoids — at industrial scale, **Ginkgo Bioworks** is the bet that the most valuable thing about metabolic engineering is the **strain engineering platform itself**, not any one product. Ginkgo was founded in 2008 in Boston by **Jason Kelly**, **Reshma Shetty**, **Barry Canton**, **Tom Knight**, and **Austin Che**, several of them out of Tom Knight's MIT lab. Tom Knight — you may remember from the genetic circuits lecture, he was a key figure in the BioBricks and standardized parts era — provided much of the intellectual scaffolding. The pitch was unusual. Ginkgo would not sell a particular molecule. Ginkgo would sell the service of engineering microbes — strains — for whatever customer wanted them, across an enormous breadth of industries: food, fragrance, pharmaceutical, agricultural, materials, cosmetic, anything where an engineered microbe could be the production platform.

The architecture they built — what they call **the Foundry** — is essentially an industrial-scale automated lab. Robotic liquid handlers, DNA synthesis pipelines, microfluidic screening, mass spectrometry stations, fermentation banks, the works, all wired together with software so that a single project manager can run many strain engineering campaigns in parallel. The Foundry runs continuously. Each engineering campaign cycles through the design-build-test-learn loop — design the genetic changes, build the strains, test their performance, learn from the results, repeat — many times per project, far faster than any individual academic lab could manage. The deal with customers is usually structured so that the customer brings the target molecule and the market knowledge; Ginkgo brings the engineering and gets paid in some combination of upfront fees, milestone payments, royalties, and equity. Customer takes the patent on the resulting strain or molecule; Ginkgo takes the upside.

\[Pause.\]

The portfolio is enormous and intentionally horizontal. Ginkgo has worked on engineered yeasts to produce specialty fragrance ingredients for perfume companies. They have worked on engineered microbes for animal-free dairy proteins. They have worked on engineered nitrogen-fixing microbes for sustainable agriculture, in partnership with Bayer through their joint venture Joyn Bio. They have worked on engineered microbes for various pharmaceutical and nutritional products. They have done CRO work for the cannabis industry. They have acquired smaller companies — Bayer's bioscience unit, Zymergen — to consolidate the strain engineering market. The breadth, more than the depth in any one product, is the strategic bet.

The analogy I want you to carry: **Ginkgo Bioworks is the strain-engineering factory — a horizontal pipeline that works for clients across food, fragrance, pharma, agriculture, materials, with the promise: we engineer the strains, you take the patent, we take royalties or fees. The product is engineering capacity itself, not any specific molecule.** That model has been controversial in industry circles. Critics ask whether engineering services without owned IP can sustain the valuations Ginkgo has historically commanded. Defenders argue that the platform — once built at sufficient scale — has economies of scale that no in-house corporate engineering effort can match, and that the cumulative knowledge across many campaigns becomes its own competitive moat. The financial history has been turbulent, with Ginkgo going through significant valuation compression after going public via SPAC in 2021, but the platform itself continues to operate and to take on engineering projects.

\[Pause.\]

The reason I want you to know about Ginkgo, regardless of how the company's financial story ultimately plays out, is that the **platform model** they pioneered is now the dominant structure for ambitious metabolic engineering in industry. The old model — found a company around a single molecule, raise capital, scale it, sell it — turned out to be very risky, because the molecule market often did not cooperate (cf. Amyris and biofuels). The new model — build engineering capacity, find many smaller markets in parallel, hedge across them — spreads the risk and exploits the fact that strain engineering tools developed for one project are largely reusable for the next. Many other companies have adopted variants of this model. Some focus on particular product verticals — Perfect Day for dairy proteins, Geltor for collagen — but rely on contract strain engineering services from companies like Ginkgo for parts of their pipeline. The biomanufacturing industry has restructured itself, in the 2010s and 2020s, around the recognition that the limit on the field is not ideas but throughput, and throughput is built in places like the Foundry.

There is one more cultural point worth making. Ginkgo, more than any other company in the space, has been explicit about treating biology as an engineering discipline rather than a science. They hire industrial engineers, not just biologists. They publish less in academic journals than a typical company of their size and more in trade and industry venues. They treat the strain engineering process as a manufacturing process to be optimized for cost, throughput, and reliability. That stance — biology as engineering, not biology as discovery — is contentious among traditional biologists but has been broadly influential in how the next generation of biotech companies organize themselves. Whether the analogy fully holds up depends on whether biology can ever really be made as predictable as silicon, which we discussed at length in the genetic circuits lecture. The bet, at Ginkgo and at companies that have followed its lead, is that the gap is closing fast enough to support a real industry.

Now let us turn to one of the biggest application areas of the modern metabolic engineering wave: plant-derived natural products, particularly the drugs that come from rare plants and have historically been supply-constrained.

## SEGMENT 12 — Plant-derived natural products: taxol, vincristine, and the opioids in yeast

For most of human history, the most chemically interesting molecules we made and used as medicines came from plants. The morphine in the poppy, the quinine in the cinchona bark, the digitoxin in the foxglove, the taxol in the Pacific yew, the vincristine in the rosy periwinkle — these are some of the most chemically complex molecules in the pharmacopoeia, and they are all evolved by plants for their own purposes, mostly defense against herbivores and pathogens. The active molecules are typically present in tiny quantities in particular tissues, the plants grow slowly, the supplies are unstable, and total chemical synthesis — making the molecules from petrochemical starting materials — is often possible in principle but ruinously expensive in practice. This is an obvious application area for metabolic engineering. If you can find the biosynthetic pathway in the plant and install it in yeast, you can produce the drug in fermenters instead of in fields. That promise has driven a huge fraction of the modern metabolic engineering research portfolio.

Let me give you three case studies.

**Taxol.** Paclitaxel, sold as Taxol, is one of the most successful cancer drugs of the last fifty years, used against breast, ovarian, and lung cancers. It is a diterpenoid extracted, originally, from the bark of the Pacific yew tree, _Taxus brevifolia_. The original extraction process required killing the tree to harvest the bark, which was a problem because the yew is slow-growing and the supply could not possibly meet medical demand. Eventually, a semi-synthetic process was developed that started from a precursor — **10-deacetylbaccatin III** — that could be extracted, non-destructively, from the needles of the more abundant European yew, and converted chemically to paclitaxel. That is the dominant process today. But ongoing metabolic engineering work has aimed to produce paclitaxel — or at least the late-stage precursor — directly in engineered yeast or _E. coli_. The pathway is long and complicated; it involves the cyclization of a diterpenoid called **taxadiene** followed by many oxidative and tailoring steps, mostly catalyzed by P450 enzymes. Several groups have engineered _E. coli_ and yeast to produce taxadiene at high levels. The downstream tailoring steps remain harder, with not all of the P450s yet characterized, but the project is making steady progress, and a fully fermentative taxol pathway is broadly considered a matter of time and engineering, not a fundamental science problem.

**Vincristine and vinblastine.** These are the **vinca alkaloids**, extracted from the rosy periwinkle, _Catharanthus roseus_, and used for various leukemias and lymphomas. The biosynthesis is one of the most complex characterized in plants — over thirty enzymatic steps from a precursor amino acid to the final dimeric alkaloid. The pathway was largely characterized in the 2010s by the work of **Sarah O'Connor**, then at the John Innes Centre, who systematically identified the missing enzymes through transcriptome mining of periwinkle cells. By 2020, a complete pathway from precursor to vincristine had been reconstituted in yeast, in collaboration between O'Connor's group and others. The yields are still very low compared to what would be needed for industrial production, but the demonstration that the pathway can be reconstituted at all is a milestone. It is also a beautiful illustration of how modern transcriptome-based pathway discovery turned a thirty-year mystery into a project that could be completed in about a decade.

**Opioids.** Morphine, codeine, thebaine, and the other opium alkaloids are biosynthesized in the opium poppy, _Papaver somniferum_, through a pathway involving more than twenty enzymes. In **2015**, **Christina Smolke's** lab at Stanford published a landmark paper demonstrating the first complete reconstitution of the opioid pathway in yeast — from glucose to thebaine, a precursor that can be converted chemically to morphine and codeine. The Smolke project required identifying and assembling roughly twenty enzymes from the plant, engineering their activity in the yeast context, and balancing the cofactor and intermediate flows. The yields, again, were initially very low — far below what would be commercially relevant — but the demonstration was startling. **The yeast made thebaine from sugar.** That had never been done before, and the moment the paper came out, it kicked off a public debate about the implications.

\[Pause.\]

I want to spend a minute on that debate, because it captures something important about the social dimension of metabolic engineering. The concern was straightforward: if anyone with a yeast culture and a fermenter can make opioids, then home production of controlled substances might become possible, potentially undermining the regulatory regime that controls medical opioids and their illegal counterparts. Op-eds appeared. Hearings were held. Policy proposals were floated. The reality is that the yields demonstrated in the 2015 paper were far too low for home production to be practical, and even at much improved yields the engineering and equipment requirements would be substantial. But the broader point stands. As metabolic engineering makes more chemicals more easily, more widely, with more accessible tools, the regulatory regime that controls drug production — built around the assumption that synthesis is hard and centralized — will face new challenges. This is one of the genuine dual-use concerns in modern biotechnology, alongside concerns about bioweapons and gene drives, and the field is going to have to engage with it.

The analogy I want you to carry: **opiates in yeast — Smolke's 2015 paper — was the wow moment for plant alkaloid metabolic engineering. The yeast made thebaine from glucose, completing a twenty-enzyme pathway transplanted from poppy. It opened the door to fermentative production of controlled substances and started a still-ongoing public debate about home-brewing.** Whatever you think of the policy implications, the scientific demonstration was a watershed for what could be done.

\[Pause.\]

There are now active programs targeting many other plant natural products — cannabinoids (in yeast and microbes), psilocybin and related psychedelics (in yeast and _E. coli_), shikimate-derived alkaloids of various kinds, terpenoids by the dozens, glycosylated steroids, polyketides for antibiotics and immunosuppressants, and so on. The list is growing every year. The dominant pattern is: identify the plant pathway, reconstitute it in yeast, optimize for industrial yields, and either license to a pharmaceutical partner or build out fermentation capacity in-house. Companies like **Demetrix** (psychedelics and other targets), **Antheia** (opioids and other alkaloids), **Ginkgo's** various partnerships, and many others operate in this space. The plant natural product space is, alongside alt-protein, one of the most active commercial frontiers of metabolic engineering.

Now let us turn to a very different application — the sustainable manufacturing of bulk chemicals that today come from petroleum.

---

## SEGMENT 13 — Sustainable manufacturing: replacing petrochemicals with engineered microbes

Step back and notice the scale of the chemical industry. The petrochemical industry produces, every year, hundreds of millions of tons of bulk chemicals — ethylene, propylene, butadiene, benzene, acrylic acid, ethylene glycol, the building blocks of nearly every plastic, fiber, and synthetic material in modern life. The feedstock is petroleum or natural gas. The processes are energy-intensive. The carbon ends up, eventually, in landfills or in the atmosphere as CO2. The whole pipeline is a major source of greenhouse gas emissions and a primary reason chemistry is sometimes called "the dirty cousin" of the energy industry.

Metabolic engineering offers an alternative, in principle. Engineered microbes, fed on sugar from agricultural waste or — eventually — on CO2 directly, can produce many of the same molecules, or close substitutes for them, at room temperature in water-based fermenters. The challenge is economics. Microbial production has to compete with extremely cheap, extremely optimized petrochemical processes, and for most commodity chemicals it cannot. The successful examples — and there are real ones — tend to be molecules where either microbial production has a unique chemistry advantage, or where there is a regulatory or consumer push toward bio-based alternatives that justifies the price premium.

\[Pause.\]

Let me give you a few of the success stories.

**Lactic acid.** Lactic acid is one of the great commercial success stories of microbial production. It is made by lactic acid bacteria — _Lactobacillus_, _Lactococcus_ — and the engineering involves selecting and optimizing strains for high yield and high product tolerance. The product market is enormous and growing, because lactic acid is the monomer for **polylactic acid**, or PLA, a biodegradable plastic used in packaging, fibers, and increasingly in medical implants. Companies like NatureWorks — a joint venture between Cargill and others — produce hundreds of thousands of tons of PLA per year from corn-derived sugars via lactic acid fermentation. This is one of the largest microbial production processes in the world, and it competes effectively with petrochemical plastics on price for certain market segments.

**1,4-butanediol** is a classic case of using metabolic engineering to access a molecule that does not occur in nature. Butanediol is a precursor to spandex, certain plastics, and many specialty chemicals. **Genomatica**, a San Diego-based company, engineered _E. coli_ to produce butanediol from sugar by stitching together a non-natural pathway — a sequence of enzymes that, in this combination, does not exist in any organism. They identified candidate enzymes through pathway prediction software and biochemical screening, assembled the pathway in _E. coli_, optimized it through many rounds of strain engineering, and licensed the technology to BASF and Novamont for commercial production. Bio-based butanediol is now a real commercial product, and several other Genomatica molecules — butadiene, caprolactam — are in various stages of commercialization.

**Succinic acid** is a four-carbon dicarboxylic acid used as a feedstock for many polymers, plasticizers, and specialty chemicals. Several companies — Reverdia, Succinity, BioAmber — have built engineered microbial processes for succinate at industrial scale, in some cases using yeast (which tolerates the low pH that comes with high carboxylic acid concentrations better than _E. coli_ does). The economics have been mixed; some of these companies have struggled financially, but the technology works.

**Isobutanol** and other higher alcohols are being pursued as next-generation biofuels and as chemical intermediates. **Gevo** and several others have engineered _E. coli_ and yeast to produce isobutanol via the keto acid pathway — a clever bit of metabolic engineering that diverts the cell's amino acid biosynthesis machinery to produce alcohol instead of amino acids. Yields and economics remain challenging, but the engineering itself has been demonstrated to work.

**Adipic acid**, the precursor to nylon, has been a holy grail for sustainable chemistry because the conventional petrochemical process emits enormous quantities of nitrous oxide, a potent greenhouse gas. Several engineering efforts — most notably Verdezyne, before it shut down — pursued microbial adipate. The economics did not, in that case, quite work out, but the engineering progress was significant and the goal remains active.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to carry: **sustainable manufacturing via metabolic engineering is the project of moving the chemical industry off petroleum and onto fermentable feedstocks. Lactic acid for biodegradable plastics. 1,4-butanediol for fibers. Succinate for polymers. Isobutanol for fuel and chemicals. Each one is a fight against the entrenched petrochemical cost structure, and the wins, when they come, depend on a combination of unique chemistry, environmental policy pressure, and consumer demand for bio-based products.** The wins are real but partial. The dream of a fully bio-based chemical industry is, as of the mid-2020s, still a dream, but the foundations under it are getting steadily stronger.

There is a particular sub-thread worth noting: the **carbon source itself** is becoming a frontier. Most of the engineered processes today use sugar — corn-derived glucose in the US, sugarcane-derived sucrose in Brazil, sometimes glycerol from biodiesel byproducts. These sugars compete with food production for arable land, which raises real concerns about land use and food security if microbial chemistry scales to bulk-chemical volumes. The next-generation feedstocks the field is targeting include **lignocellulosic sugars** from agricultural waste (which requires solving the difficult enzymatic breakdown of plant cell walls), **methanol** (which can be produced from natural gas or biomass and supports engineered methylotrophs like _Pichia_ or _Methylobacterium_), and ultimately **CO2** itself, captured directly from the atmosphere or from industrial flue gas. We will close the lecture with the recent CO2-fixing _E. coli_ work, which is the most striking demonstration of what carbon-neutral biomanufacturing might one day look like.

Now let us turn to a wave that has been even more visible to consumers in the last few years — alt-protein and the engineered production of food ingredients that used to come from animals.

---

## SEGMENT 14 — Animal-free dairy and food proteins: the alt-protein wave

The most consumer-visible wave of metabolic engineering in the late 2010s and 2020s has been the **alt-protein movement** — the use of engineered microbes to produce proteins and other ingredients that have historically come from animals. The driver is a combination of environmental concern (livestock contributes roughly 14 percent of global greenhouse gas emissions), animal welfare concern, supply chain considerations, and growing consumer demand for plant-based and microbially produced alternatives to traditional animal products. The economics are starting to work for certain niche products, and the technology is improving rapidly.

The poster child for this wave is **Perfect Day**, founded in 2014 by **Ryan Pandya** and **Perumal Gandhi**. Perfect Day's product is **animal-free whey protein** — the same whey protein you find in cow's milk, but produced not by cows but by engineered _Trichoderma_ filamentous fungus growing in a fermenter. The company engineered the fungus to express bovine beta-lactoglobulin and other whey proteins, optimized the strain for high secretion yields, and scaled up to industrial fermentation. The resulting whey is chemically and functionally identical to dairy-derived whey — it gels, foams, browns, has the same nutritional profile — but contains no lactose, no cholesterol, no animal hormones, and requires no cows. It has been used in ice cream, protein bars, beverages, and increasingly in conventional dairy applications. Perfect Day's technology has been licensed to several consumer brands and the products are on shelves in major retailers.

\[Pause.\]

There is a wave of similar companies. **Clara Foods**, now operating as **The EVERY Company**, engineered the methylotrophic yeast _Pichia pastoris_ to produce animal-free egg white proteins — albumins and other functional proteins — for use in food applications. **Geltor** engineered microbes to produce **collagen** without animals; collagen is a huge market in cosmetics, nutritional supplements, and increasingly in cultivated meat scaffolds. **Motif FoodWorks** — a Ginkgo spinout — engineered microbial production of heme proteins and other meat-flavor molecules for use in plant-based meat substitutes. **Triton Algae Innovations** and others have worked on heme and pigment production in algae. **New Culture** is engineering microbial casein for animal-free cheese, which is a particularly hard problem because cheese functionality depends on subtle protein interactions that have to be reconstituted faithfully.

The technical engineering challenges are significant. Animal proteins often require complex glycosylation patterns that yeast, especially the standard _S. cerevisiae_, does not reproduce naturally. The patterns can be engineered — **glyco-engineered** yeast strains have been built that produce more mammalian-like glycans — but the engineering is delicate. Secretion at high titers, low cost of goods (especially competing with cheap commodity dairy), and consumer perception of GMO-derived foods are all active fronts. The field is also actively engaged with the regulatory frameworks for these products, which differ substantially between jurisdictions; the US FDA has been relatively accommodating through its GRAS (generally recognized as safe) self-affirmation pathway, while the EU has been slower.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to carry: **alt-protein is the next wave — milk proteins from yeast and fungi at Perfect Day, egg white from _Pichia_ at Clara, collagen from yeast at Geltor, meat flavor molecules from microbes at Motif. Animal-free animal products at industrial scale. The driver is a combination of environmental, ethical, and supply-chain pressure, and the technology is becoming economically competitive for an expanding set of high-value applications.**

There is a separate but related wave — **cultivated meat**, the growing of actual animal muscle and fat tissue from cell cultures rather than from animals. That is more cell-culture biology than classical metabolic engineering and we will not cover it in depth here, but it is worth knowing that the cultivated meat industry depends heavily on metabolic engineering for its inputs — the growth media, the scaffolding proteins, the growth factors — many of which are themselves produced by engineered microbes. The two waves are intertwined. A future protein industry that includes both microbial proteins (Perfect Day, Geltor) and cultivated animal tissue (UPSIDE Foods, Aleph Farms, GOOD Meat) will rely on metabolic engineering throughout its supply chain.

Whether alt-protein achieves the kind of mass market displacement of conventional animal products that its proponents project — that is, whether engineered microbial whey and engineered cell-cultured beef collectively eat into a meaningful fraction of the dairy and meat market in the next few decades — is one of the great open questions of the food industry. The technology is improving fast. The economics are still catching up. Consumer acceptance is mixed and varies enormously by region. Public policy, particularly around labeling and regulatory pathways, is unsettled. But the underlying biotechnology is real, the products are on shelves, and the trajectory is clear enough that the major incumbent food companies — Nestlé, Unilever, Danone, JBS — have all made serious investments in the space, hedging their long-term positions. Metabolic engineering, in this wave, has become a consumer-facing technology in a way it never quite was before.

Now let us pull back to a methodological topic that has accelerated all of these waves — the rise of modular cloning standards.

## SEGMENT 15 — Modular cloning and Golden Gate: pathway shuffling at speed

I want to spend a segment on what is, on its face, a deeply unsexy topic — DNA assembly methods. Stay with me, because the rate at which a field moves is set by the cost and speed of its core experimental operations, and for metabolic engineering, the core operation is building large multi-gene pathways and integrating them into the chromosome. The methods that make this fast have been quietly but transformatively important.

If you walked into a molecular biology lab in 2005, the dominant cloning method would be **restriction-ligation cloning** using restriction enzymes — the technology we covered in the plasmids lecture in some depth. You would cut your insert and your vector with the same restriction enzymes, gel-purify the fragments, mix them with ligase, transform into bacteria, screen colonies. To build a multi-gene construct, you would do this many times sequentially. Each round took days. A construct with, say, five genes would take a couple of months. It was hand-soldering, basically.

The first big leap was **Gibson assembly**, developed by **Dan Gibson** at the J. Craig Venter Institute around 2009. Gibson assembly uses a mix of three enzymes — an exonuclease, a polymerase, and a ligase — to seamlessly join multiple DNA fragments with overlapping ends in a single isothermal reaction. You design your fragments to have 20-40 base pairs of overlap at the ends where you want them joined, mix them with the enzyme cocktail, incubate for an hour at 50 degrees, and the fragments find their overlapping partners and get seamlessly stitched together. There are no scar sequences left behind, the assembly can handle many fragments at once, and the whole reaction is done in an afternoon. Gibson assembly transformed how labs build large constructs.

\[Pause.\]

In parallel, a different approach called **Golden Gate cloning** was developed by **Sylvestre Marillonnet** and others. Golden Gate uses a class of restriction enzymes called **Type IIs enzymes** — most famously **BsaI** and **BsmBI** — that cut DNA outside their recognition sequence, leaving custom-defined overhangs. Because the cut sites are separated from the recognition sites, you can design any overhang you want by choosing what sequence sits next to the recognition site. That lets you direct many fragments to assemble in a specific order, with specific junctions, in a single reaction. You can build a multi-gene construct from many parts at once, and the design of the overhangs guarantees that fragments go in the correct order and orientation.

The modular cloning standard that grew out of Golden Gate — **MoClo**, developed by Marillonnet's group — defines a hierarchical part library. Level 0 parts are individual functional pieces — promoters, coding sequences, terminators — flanked by standardized Golden Gate overhangs. Level 1 assemblies combine Level 0 parts into single transcriptional units. Level 2 assemblies combine Level 1 units into multi-gene constructs. The whole hierarchy is designed so that any Level 0 part can be combined with any other Level 0 part to form a Level 1 unit, and any Level 1 unit can be combined with any other Level 1 unit to form a Level 2 construct. The architecture is genuinely modular, in a way that the BioBrick standard we discussed in the genetic circuits lecture aspired to but did not quite achieve.

For metabolic engineering, MoClo and related modular standards have been transformative. Building and testing variants of a multi-gene pathway, with different promoters and different RBSes for each gene, used to take months. With MoClo, you build a library of part variants — say, five different promoters and three different RBSes for each of your pathway genes — and you can combinatorially assemble dozens or hundreds of pathway variants in a single Golden Gate reaction. Drop the assemblies into the host, screen for product titers, and the winners point you to the best part combinations. The design-build-test-learn loop, which is the iterative cycle that drives metabolic engineering forward, runs an order of magnitude faster than it did fifteen years ago.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to carry: **modular cloning — Golden Gate, MoClo — is what made rapid pathway shuffling possible. You build a library of standardized parts with defined junctions, and you can combinatorially assemble many pathway variants in a single reaction. The design-build-test-learn loop runs much faster. The genetic circuits people aspired to this with BioBricks; the metabolic engineers achieved it with Golden Gate.** This is the engineering infrastructure that sits under projects like Keasling artemisinin, like the Smolke opioid yeast, like the alt-protein companies. Without modern assembly methods, those projects would have taken decades. With them, they took years.

There is an emerging follow-up to all of this — **DNA synthesis at scale**. Companies like Twist Bioscience, IDT, and Ansa Biotechnologies can now synthesize DNA to spec at scales of kilobases to megabases, at prices that have been collapsing for two decades. For a metabolic engineering project, you increasingly just design what you want — promoters, codon-optimized genes, regulatory elements — and order it from a synthesis house. The synthesis houses use Gibson or Golden Gate-style assembly internally to build the pieces. The engineering workflow is increasingly "design on a computer, order the DNA, transform into the host, screen for performance." The wet-lab molecular biology of building constructs is, for many projects, almost vestigial. That is a profound shift in how the field operates.

Now let us look at the editing technology that has been the other big throughput driver — CRISPR.

## SEGMENT 16 — CRISPR for metabolic engineering: knock out, knock down, redirect

CRISPR — the gene-editing technology developed from bacterial immunity systems and adapted for use in eukaryotic cells by **Jennifer Doudna**, **Emmanuelle Charpentier**, **Feng Zhang**, **George Church**, and others around 2012-2013 — has been transformative for many areas of biology, and metabolic engineering is one of them. We are not going to do a full primer on CRISPR mechanism here; that lives in a separate set of material. The metabolic engineering perspective is what specifically the CRISPR tools have changed.

The first thing CRISPR changed was the ease of **gene knockout** in workhorse strains. Before CRISPR, knocking out a gene in _E. coli_ involved building a knockout cassette with antibiotic markers and homology arms, transforming it in, selecting recombinants, often curing the marker afterward — a multi-week process per gene. With CRISPR-Cas9 plus a homologous repair template, the same operation can be done in days, often without leaving any selection marker behind. You design the guide RNA to target the gene you want to knock out, you co-deliver a repair template with the desired modification, and Cas9 makes the cut, the cell repairs using your template, and you have your edit. The cost per knockout has dropped dramatically. The number of genes you can routinely manipulate per project has gone up.

In yeast, CRISPR has been even more transformative. Yeast has very efficient homologous recombination — it was actually the easier-to-edit organism for many years — but CRISPR has made the cuts more targeted, the editing more efficient at hard-to-reach loci, and has enabled multiplexed editing of many sites at once by simply expressing multiple guide RNAs from a single construct.

\[Pause.\]

The second thing CRISPR enabled is **gene knockdown without knockout** — the use of catalytically inactive Cas9 (**dCas9**) to block transcription at a target locus without actually cutting the DNA. This technique, called **CRISPRi** (CRISPR interference), lets you reduce the expression of a gene to a tunable level rather than eliminating it entirely. For metabolic engineering, this is enormously useful. Many essential genes that you would want to downregulate to push flux through a heterologous pathway — for example, squalene synthase in yeast, which competes with the artemisinin pathway for FPP — cannot be knocked out entirely because the cell needs them to live. With CRISPRi, you can dial them down to a tunable level, finding the sweet spot between cell viability and product titer. The flip side, **CRISPRa** (CRISPR activation), uses dCas9 fused to a transcriptional activator to boost expression of target genes. Together, CRISPRi and CRISPRa give you a tunable dial for any gene in the genome.

The third thing CRISPR enabled is **library-scale screens** for metabolic phenotypes. You can build a CRISPR guide library targeting every gene in the genome — or a focused library targeting a particular set of pathways — and apply it to your engineered strain, then screen for cells with improved product titers. The cells with knockouts or knockdowns that boost product accumulate, and you sequence them to identify which gene perturbations were beneficial. This is a way of doing rational FBA-style strain design and exhaustive genome-wide screening in parallel, which often turns up improvements that the model did not predict.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to carry: **CRISPR for metabolic engineering is the high-throughput multi-locus editing scalpel. Knock out competing pathways. Knock down essential bottlenecks to a tunable level. Activate underexpressed enzymes. Run genome-wide library screens for productivity. The cost and difficulty of every kind of genome manipulation has dropped, and the scale at which you can push flux into a target has gone up.**

CRISPR sits alongside MAGE and the other recombineering tools we discussed in Segment 9. They are complementary, not redundant. MAGE is particularly good for simultaneously introducing many small modifications, often single base pair changes, across many loci. CRISPR is better for precise, single-locus editing and for tunable transcriptional control. Modern strain engineering campaigns use both, choosing the tool to fit the manipulation.

The combined effect of all these throughput-increasing tools — MAGE, REXER, CRISPR, MoClo, DNA synthesis — is that the cost of running a strain engineering campaign has dropped roughly an order of magnitude per decade for the last two decades. What used to take a postdoc five years now takes a small team a year. What used to require a dedicated lab now happens routinely at platform companies like Ginkgo. The discipline is, in throughput terms, where electronics was somewhere in the 1980s — past the era of one-off custom designs, into the era of design tools and platform fabrication. That is what enables the alt-protein wave, the plant natural product wave, the sustainable chemicals wave — all of them depend on engineering throughput that simply did not exist twenty years ago.

Now let us look at the layer that is starting to sit on top of all of this — the AI-driven design of metabolic pathways and enzymes.

## SEGMENT 17 — AI-driven design: machine learning enters the loop

Let me tell you about the most recent layer to land on top of the field, because if the trajectory of the last few years continues, this will become the dominant story of the next decade. The layer is **machine learning** — specifically, the application of large neural network models to problems in pathway design, enzyme engineering, retrosynthesis, and high-throughput screening.

There are several distinct applications. The first is **retrosynthesis prediction**. Retrosynthesis is the chemistry game of starting from a target molecule and working backward through possible reactions to reach available starting materials. Chemists have done this in their heads for a century, guided by experience and pattern recognition. In the 2010s, software started to automate it. The most prominent system is **IBM RXN for Chemistry**, which uses a transformer-based language model trained on millions of published chemical reactions to suggest synthetic routes from target to starting material. Similar systems have been built at academic labs — the **Synthia** software from Bartosz Grzybowski, the **AiZynthFinder** project from AstraZeneca, **Chemputer**-style systems from Lee Cronin. For metabolic engineering, the equivalent question is: given a target small molecule, what enzymatic pathway, from what set of starting metabolites, could plausibly produce it? Several groups — most notably **Linda Broadbelt's** lab at Northwestern with their **BNICE** framework, and **Vassily Hatzimanikatis** at EPFL — have built tools that algorithmically enumerate candidate enzymatic pathways from arbitrary starting materials to arbitrary targets, using reaction rules learned from biochemistry databases. The output is a ranked list of candidate pathways, complete with predicted enzymes for each step. The engineer then picks the most promising pathway and tries to build it. This kind of computational pathway design, which would have been science fiction in 2000, is now a routine first step in many engineering campaigns.

\[Pause.\]

The second application is **enzyme design and engineering**. We talked about directed evolution in Segment 7 as the dominant tool for improving heterologous enzyme activity. Machine learning is now starting to augment, and in some cases replace, evolution-based approaches. Tools like **AlphaFold** and **ESMFold** can predict the three-dimensional structure of an enzyme from its sequence, opening up structure-guided rational design even when no crystal structure exists. Models trained on enzyme sequence-function relationships can predict which mutations are likely to improve activity, substrate specificity, or thermostability, narrowing the search space directed evolution has to explore. **De novo protein design** tools — particularly the **RFdiffusion** and related models from David Baker's lab — can now design enzymes from scratch for specific reactions, although the success rate for catalytically efficient designs is still modest. The trajectory, though, is unmistakably upward. Within a decade, "design an enzyme that does X reaction" may be as routine a request as "synthesize this DNA sequence" is today.

The third application is **strain phenotype prediction**. Machine learning models trained on large datasets of strain genotypes and corresponding product titers can predict which combinations of genetic manipulations are likely to produce the best strains, augmenting or replacing FBA-style predictions. The most ambitious efforts integrate genome-scale models, kinetic data, omics measurements, and historical strain engineering results into unified predictive models. Companies like **Zymergen** (before acquisition by Ginkgo) pursued this aggressively. The data requirements are enormous, and to date no model has been a clear replacement for empirical screening, but the augmentation effect — making each experiment more informative for the next — is real.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to carry: **AI-driven metabolic design is the layer that is starting to sit on top of the whole stack — retrosynthesis tools predict pathways, structure prediction tools guide enzyme engineering, phenotype prediction models prioritize manipulations. The field is moving from purely empirical strain engineering, in which you try things and see what works, toward model-guided engineering, in which the models suggest what to try and the experiments validate the suggestions. The combination is much faster than either approach alone.**

I want to be careful about the hype here. Many of these models, particularly the ones trained on specific industrial datasets, are still narrower in their predictive power than their marketing suggests. The field has been burned before by overconfident computational predictions — early FBA-based strain design promised more than it delivered until experimental validation became routine. Machine learning is now going through the same cycle. The hype is ahead of the steady, real progress. But the underlying progress is real, and the trajectory does not appear to be slowing. If you are a student entering the field today, the safe bet is that your career will be deeply shaped by the integration of machine learning into every part of the metabolic engineering workflow.

The final segment takes us to the frontier — to the engineered cells that are starting to fix carbon directly from CO2, and what that means for the long-term arc of the field.

## SEGMENT 18 — The frontier: CO2-fixing _E. coli_ and the path to carbon-neutral biomanufacturing

I want to close with the frontier, because the long arc of metabolic engineering — the arc that motivates the whole project — points toward a chemistry that does not depend on extracting carbon from the ground. Today, almost every microbial production process is fed by sugar, which was made by plants from CO2 and sunlight. The plants and the sugar processing are between us and the CO2. The metabolic engineering question becomes: can we cut out the middleman? Can we engineer microbes that, themselves, take CO2 from the air, fix it into organic molecules, and run the rest of the chemistry on that internally generated carbon?

That sounds like science fiction, but in 2019 a remarkable paper came out of **Ron Milo's** lab at the Weizmann Institute in Israel that demonstrated it. The paper, with **Shmuel Gleizer** as first author, described an engineered strain of _E. coli_ — an organism that is naturally a heterotroph, eating sugars and other organic carbon sources — that had been converted, by metabolic engineering and laboratory evolution, into an **autotroph**. The strain used CO2 as its only carbon source, fixing it via the engineered installation of the **Calvin-Benson-Bassham cycle** — the same pathway plants use for carbon fixation — plus an external energy source from formate, an organic molecule but one that does not contribute carbon to biomass.

\[Pause.\]

Let me unpack that, because it is a stunning piece of work. The Calvin cycle requires a key enzyme called **RuBisCO** — ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase — which catalyzes the addition of CO2 onto a five-carbon sugar acceptor. RuBisCO is famously the most abundant protein on Earth, because plants and algae need so much of it to fix carbon, and it is also famously inefficient and slow. _E. coli_ does not have RuBisCO. The Milo team engineered into _E. coli_ a working Calvin cycle by introducing RuBisCO and phosphoribulokinase from cyanobacteria, then knocking out the _E. coli_ enzymes that would have prevented the engineered pathway from running productively. They also rewired the central carbon metabolism so that the cell would route its precursor demand through the new CO2-fixation entry point rather than through the sugar uptake systems.

Just having all the enzymes in place was not enough. The engineered strain, on first construction, did not grow on CO2. The team then subjected the strain to **laboratory evolution** — a directed evolution at the level of the whole cell — by growing the strain for many generations under conditions of steadily decreasing organic carbon availability and steadily increasing reliance on CO2 fixation. After about 350 days of evolution, with periodic genome sequencing to track the accumulating mutations, they had a strain that grew autotrophically — using CO2 as its only carbon source — with no organic carbon input beyond formate for energy. The strain had picked up a handful of beneficial mutations that the rational engineering had missed. The combination of design and evolution had worked.

\[Pause.\]

That paper was a landmark. It demonstrated that you could, by metabolic engineering plus directed evolution, take a heterotrophic workhorse and convert it into a CO2-fixing autotroph. If this can be made efficient enough, and if the energy source can eventually come from renewable electricity rather than from formate-from-natural-gas, you have the foundation for a kind of microbial chemistry that genuinely closes the carbon loop. CO2 goes in. Products go out. No net new fossil carbon is released. Combined with renewable energy, this is the kind of biomanufacturing that could be part of a credible path to a decarbonized chemical industry.

There are major caveats. The engineered _E. coli_ in the Milo paper grows slowly compared to wild-type _E. coli_ on glucose. The energy input from formate is expensive. The yields of any product on CO2 would be lower than yields on sugar. The infrastructure for an industrial-scale CO2 fermentation industry does not exist. None of these problems are fundamental, but all of them are real, and the time horizon for converting this proof of concept into industrial reality is at minimum a couple of decades.

There are parallel approaches worth mentioning. **One-carbon metabolism** — engineering organisms to grow on methanol, formate, methane, or syngas instead of sugar — is a closely related research program. **Methanol-utilizing _E. coli_** strains have been engineered by several groups, notably the labs of Greg Stephanopoulos at MIT and Julia Vorholt at ETH Zürich. **Cupriavidus necator** (formerly **Ralstonia eutropha**) is a natural hydrogen-and-CO2-fixing bacterium that has been the subject of substantial engineering effort, including for the production of bioplastics like PHB. **Synechocystis** and other cyanobacteria are being engineered as photoautotrophic production platforms that use sunlight directly to drive CO2 fixation. The collection of platforms for one-carbon and CO2-based biomanufacturing is growing, and the technology is steadily maturing.

\[Pause.\]

The analogy I want you to carry: **the frontier of metabolic engineering is the rewiring of one-carbon metabolism — engineered _E. coli_ that fix CO2 (Milo lab, 2019), methylotrophic platforms that grow on methanol, photoautotrophic cyanobacteria that use sunlight. The destination is a truly carbon-neutral biomanufacturing in which the chemical industry is fed by CO2 and renewable energy, not petroleum.** Whether we get there — whether the engineering matures fast enough and the economics shift far enough — is one of the great applied biology questions of the next half century.

Let me close on the long arc. We started this lecture with a single sentence: metabolic engineering is the rewriting of cellular chemistry so the cell makes what you want. We have now walked through the workhorses, the founding hits, the Keasling artemisinin story, the methods of pathway discovery, flux balance analysis, directed evolution, cofactor engineering, the multi-locus editing tools, the commercial waves of Amyris and Ginkgo, the plant natural products, sustainable manufacturing, alt-protein, modular cloning, CRISPR, AI-driven design, and now the frontier. The arc — from one recombinant gene in _E. coli_ producing insulin in 1978 to engineered _E. coli_ fixing CO2 in 2019, with everything in between — is the arc of a discipline maturing from a research curiosity into a foundational industrial technology.

If I had to bet on what the field looks like in 2050, I would say something like this. By 2050, a substantial fraction of pharmaceutical small molecules — possibly the majority — will be produced by engineered microbes in fermenters rather than by chemical synthesis or plant extraction. A substantial fraction of specialty chemicals — flavors, fragrances, cosmetic ingredients, food additives — will be microbially produced. A meaningful fraction of bulk chemicals will be microbially produced, increasing year by year as the technology matures and carbon pricing makes petrochemical production more expensive. Alt-protein and microbial food ingredients will be a normal part of the food supply rather than a niche category. The first CO2-fed industrial fermentation processes will be operational, initially for high-value products, with steady expansion toward bulk applications. The chemical industry of the late twenty-first century, if we are lucky and if we engineer well, will look much less like the chemical industry of the twentieth century and much more like an extension of biology — water-based, room-temperature, fed by sunlight and CO2 and waste streams, catalyzed by enzymes evolved by life and tuned by us.

That is the long arc. Metabolic engineering is one of the most important tools we have for making it real. Whether it succeeds depends in part on the engineering — which is going well — and in part on the policy and market context in which the engineering operates, which is more uncertain. But the science is on a trajectory that, ten years from now, will make today's capabilities look quaint, the same way today's capabilities make 1995's look quaint. If you are in this field, you are in it at a moment when the foundational tools are finally good enough to attempt at scale what the founders of the discipline imagined in the 1990s. That is a remarkable position to be in. Make the most of it.

Thank you. Questions.

**A note on names and dates.** The history of metabolic engineering is rich and the attribution of foundational contributions is sometimes contested. I have tried to credit the most prominent figures fairly, but anyone with a deeper interest in the field's history should consult primary sources. **Jay Bailey** and **Gregory Stephanopoulos** are widely credited with formally defining metabolic engineering as a named discipline in the early 1990s, through textbooks and review papers. **Jay Keasling** and the artemisinin project at UC Berkeley are the most-cited demonstration of modern pathway engineering. **Christina Smolke's** opioid yeast (2015) and **Ron Milo's** CO2-fixing _E. coli_ (2019) are the canonical "wow" demonstrations of recent years. **Frances Arnold** shared the 2018 Nobel in Chemistry for directed evolution, which is foundational across many enzyme engineering applications.

**A final note on the cell as factory.** The factory analogy I have used throughout — cell as chemical factory, enzymes as conveyor belts, feedstock as raw material, product as output — is a useful first approximation but, like all analogies, it eventually breaks. Cells are not factories in any straightforward sense. They evolved for fitness, not for product titer. They are noisy in ways factories are not. They reorganize themselves in response to environmental and internal cues, in ways factories cannot. The whole engineering challenge of metabolic engineering is, in some sense, about negotiating with the cell — coaxing, pressuring, evolving, redesigning, until the cell's behavior aligns with the engineer's goals. That negotiation is rarely complete and never permanent. A strain engineered for high yield today will, if allowed to evolve under selective pressure, often slowly lose its engineered behavior over generations, because the engineered behavior is a fitness burden the wild-type configuration does not carry. Maintaining engineered metabolism is an ongoing project, not a one-time accomplishment. That is one of the deepest lessons the field has learned: cells push back. The engineer's job is to push back well enough, with tools good enough, to keep the cell on the path the engineer chose. The discipline is, at root, a particular kind of dialogue with life. Doing it well requires both technical sophistication and a kind of humility about what you can and cannot make a cell do. I will leave you with that thought.
