## SEGMENT 1 — The shortage, the stakes, and the one sentence I want you to leave with

Alright. Good morning, everyone. For the next two hours we're going to talk about xenotransplantation — the science of putting an organ from another species, in practice almost always a pig, into a human being and keeping it alive. I'm warning you up front: this is one of those topics where the science is so fresh that some of what I'm telling you happened in the last calendar year. We're going to talk about human beings, named human beings, who are alive right now with a pig's kidney filtering their blood. That's the world we live in. So buckle in.

Here is the one sentence I want you to walk out of here with, and then I'll spend two hours unpacking it. **Xenotransplantation is borrowing an organ from a pig — and CRISPR is what finally let us do it.** That's the whole thing. Pig organ, human body, with the immune problem solved by genome editing. Every other thing I'm going to tell you today — the alpha-gal sugar, the triple knockout, the porcine retroviruses, David Bennett's heart, Towana Looney's kidney, the FDA pathway, the gnotobiotic herds, the religious considerations, the 3D-printing rivals — every one of those is a variation on or an extension of that one core idea. A pig grew an organ, we made the pig less foreign with editing, we transplanted the organ, and a human lived.

I want you to feel why this matters, and the way to feel it is with the numbers. In the United States, right now, there are roughly **one hundred thousand people on the organ transplant waiting list**. The list is not abstract. It is a literal database run by an organization called UNOS — United Network for Organ Sharing — and each row of the database is a person who needs a heart, a liver, a kidney, a lung, or some combination, and who will die without one. About **seventeen of them die every day** waiting. Do the math. That's more than six thousand people a year, in the United States alone, who are essentially executed by an organ shortage. Most of them are on the kidney list. The kidney list is the longest, and the wait for a kidney can stretch to five, six, seven years, during which the patient is tethered to a dialysis machine three days a week. Dialysis keeps you alive, but it's not living. Five years on dialysis is a death sentence in slow motion.

And this is the supply side. There just are not enough human donors. Brain-dead trauma victims, the traditional source, are a finite resource — and modern medicine, thankfully, has gotten very good at keeping head-injury patients alive. Living donor kidneys help with the kidney list, but most people don't have a sibling who's a match and willing. The math does not work. The waiting list grows faster than the donor pool. You cannot fix this with more organ donor checkboxes on driver's licenses.

\[Pause.\]

So here's the dream, and the dream is at least a century old. What if we could just **manufacture** organs? What if every patient on the list could walk into a hospital and, within a week, get a custom-sized, immunologically compatible heart, or kidney, or lung? What if the supply was, in principle, infinite?

For about a hundred years, doctors looked across the species barrier — at chimpanzees, at baboons, at sheep, at pigs — and asked: could we use their organs? The very first kidney transplant in human medical history, in 1906, was a xenotransplant. Not human-to-human. A French surgeon named Mathieu Jaboulay sewed a pig kidney into a woman's elbow. It lasted three days before it turned black and died. Through the twentieth century, dozens more attempts followed, each one a disaster. The most famous is **Baby Fae**, in 1984, who received a baboon heart and lived about three weeks. The field was repeatedly humiliated. By the 1990s, the consensus in transplantation was that xenotransplantation was a beautiful idea that the immune system would never permit.

And then, between roughly 2010 and 2022, the field came back from the dead. The reason it came back is exactly what we talked about in the CRISPR lecture. Once you can edit the genome of a pig — once you can knock out the genes that make a pig's organs look foreign to a human, and knock in the human genes that calm the human immune system down — the wall starts to crack. By 2022, we had a man living with a 10-gene-edited pig heart. By 2024, we had three patients in a row, each one a different person, receiving 69-edit pig kidneys. The field went from impossible to clinical in about a decade. That's why we're spending two hours on it.

\[Pause.\]

So here's what I want from you in two hours. **If you walk out of here** and can do three things — first, explain, to a confused friend, why the alpha-gal sugar is the central villain of the last fifty years of xeno research; second, walk through what the modern multi-gene-edited pig actually has done to it, gene by gene; and third, tell me the story of David Bennett and the 2024 kidney wave — Slayman, Pisano, Looney — and what the field learned from each — then we've succeeded. Everything else is decoration on those three.

One last framing before we dive in. Xenotransplantation is really two stories braided together. One story is **immunology** — fifty years of fighting the human immune system's instant, scorched-earth response to anything non-human. The other story is **technology** — three decades of cloning, transgenics, and finally CRISPR, which gave us the toolkit to remake a pig genome at will. The first story explains why we needed the second. You can't really understand why we edit pigs the way we do without understanding what the immune system does to an unedited pig organ. So that's where we'll go. But first, a stop on the supply side — why pigs, of all animals.

## SEGMENT 2 — Why pigs: anatomy, breeding speed, and the ethics line

So why a pig? Why not, say, a chimpanzee, which is much more closely related to us — only one percent different in DNA? Why not a sheep, which we already farm? Why a pig?

There are three answers, and they all matter. **Anatomy. Reproduction. Ethics.**

Let's take anatomy first. A grown adult pig — a particular breed, a smaller breed I'll come back to — has organs that are remarkably similar in size and shape to adult human organs. A pig heart is roughly the same size as a human heart. A pig kidney is roughly the same size as a human kidney. The vessels come off in roughly the right configuration. The pressures the heart pumps at are roughly the right range. If you look at a pig heart and a human heart side by side on a table — and surgeons have done this many, many times — you will see something startling. The two are within, call it, ten or fifteen percent of each other on most dimensions. A pig's heart will fit in the cavity left behind when you remove a human heart. The pulmonary artery and the aorta line up. The surgical anatomy is, broadly, compatible.

Compare this to a baboon. A baboon weighs maybe thirty kilos. Its heart is fine for a thirty-kilo animal, but if you transplant it into an eighty-kilo human, it cannot keep up with the metabolic demand. The Baby Fae case in 1984 used a baboon heart in an infant precisely because an infant was small enough that the baboon heart could plausibly cope. For an adult, you need an adult-sized organ from an animal of human-comparable mass. Pigs fit. Most other livestock — sheep, goats — are too small or their organs are the wrong shape. Cattle are the right mass but their organs are quite different in geometry. Pigs are, anatomically, the closest match we have outside of the great apes.

Now, reproduction. A pig reaches sexual maturity in about six months. A sow's gestation is three months, three weeks, three days — pig farmers will quote that to you. A litter is typically eight to twelve piglets. A pig will give birth twice a year. So if you start with one breeding pair of genetically engineered pigs, in three years you can have a herd of thousands. Compare this to chimpanzees, which take eight to ten years to reach reproductive maturity and have one offspring at a time, every five years or so. There is no plausible commercial path to a chimpanzee organ supply. The numbers just don't work. With pigs, you can run a herd of the size you'd need to supply the United States. You can run it on a single piece of farmland. You can run it within a single business cycle.

\[Pause.\]

And then, the ethics question. This is the one most people don't think about until I bring it up, but it is, I think, the most important of the three. We already kill, in the United States alone, about a hundred and thirty million pigs a year. For bacon, for pork chops, for ham, for hot dogs, for the gelatin in your gummy bears. As a society, we have already made a moral decision that the death of a pig is acceptable when the benefit is human nourishment — or, frankly, just human pleasure at lunchtime. If we accept that for a sandwich, it is hard to argue that we shouldn't accept it to save a human life. The moral cost of taking a pig's heart, when you're already taking ten thousand pig hearts a year for sausage, looks much lower than the moral cost of taking a chimpanzee's heart.

Chimpanzees and other great apes occupy a different ethical category. They're our nearest cousins. They show evidence of culture and tool use and mourning and self-recognition. The entire community of bioethicists and primatologists agrees that taking organs from great apes is not on the table. There were experiments with chimpanzee kidneys in the 1960s — they're done. They're not coming back. The line society has drawn is: not great apes, not for transplant. Pigs sit on the other side of that line.

There's also the disease angle, which I'll spend a whole segment on later. We share many viruses with chimpanzees, including HIV. Cross-species transplant from a chimp carries a real risk of moving novel pathogens into humans — and into humans whose immune systems are pharmacologically suppressed, no less. Pigs have their own viruses, but the genetic distance means the overlap with human pathogens is smaller. That's also a vote for the pig.

Now, having said all of that, I want to be honest about a complication. The pigs we use for xenotransplantation are not the pigs on a farm. They are specifically bred and engineered — usually a breed called a **Yucatan miniature pig** or one of the Göttingen minipig lines, which max out at around the size of an adult human rather than continuing to grow to four hundred kilos like a commercial Yorkshire. They're raised in extreme isolation, in facilities that look more like semiconductor fabs than barns, to keep them free of every infectious agent we can possibly exclude. They live their entire short lives in a clean room. Whether that's a better or worse life than a factory-farm pig is a question for a different lecture, but it's worth flagging that the xenotransplant pig is its own particular thing.

So: pigs, because the anatomy fits, the reproduction is fast, and the ethics line has been drawn between us and primates rather than us and livestock. Cleared up. Let's go talk about the wall that nearly killed the field.

## SEGMENT 3 — Hyperacute rejection: the immune system's instant-kill response

I want you to picture, for a minute, what happens if you do the naive experiment. Take an unmodified pig — a regular pig, the kind your bacon came from — and surgically transplant its kidney into a human. You sew the artery, you sew the vein, you sew the ureter. You unclamp the artery. Blood from the human rushes into the pig kidney.

And the kidney turns black. Within minutes. Not hours, not days. **Minutes.** The kidney swells, the vessels burst, the tissue dies, the urine that should be coming out the ureter is replaced by blood. By the time the surgeon has finished closing the abdomen, the kidney is already a dead, hemorrhagic lump.

That phenomenon has a name. It is called **hyperacute rejection**. And it is the central villain of the last fifty years of xenotransplantation. Every story I tell you from here on is going to come back to it. So I want to make sure you understand mechanistically what's happening.

\[Pause.\]

Here is the analogy. **Hyperacute rejection is the immune system's instant-kill — your antibodies see a sugar on the pig organ, complement activates, the organ dies in minutes.** That's the elevator pitch. Now let me unpack it.

Your blood is not just red cells in salt water. Your blood is a soup of millions of different antibodies, manufactured by your B cells over the course of your life. Most of them target specific pathogens you've encountered — flu, rhinovirus, chickenpox. But some of them target things you've never been deliberately vaccinated against. They're called **natural antibodies**, or sometimes pre-formed antibodies. They're floating in your blood right now, and they bind specifically to sugar structures that human cells don't make but that bacteria, fungi, and many animals do make.

The single most important of these — the chief antigen for hyperacute rejection — is called **alpha-gal**. Its full name is **galactose-alpha-1,3-galactose**. It is a sugar — specifically, a disaccharide, two sugar molecules linked together in a specific configuration. Pig cells decorate their surface proteins with alpha-gal all over the place. So do most mammals — cows, dogs, mice, baboons. There is one major exception. The great apes — chimpanzees, gorillas, orangutans — and Old World monkeys, and **humans**, do not make alpha-gal. We lost the gene for the enzyme that makes it, roughly twenty-five million years ago, in a common ancestor.

So picture the immune situation. You, a human, grew up in a world full of bacteria and protozoa that paint themselves with alpha-gal. Your immune system, very early in life, looks at alpha-gal and says: that's not us. That's foreign. Let's make antibodies. By the time you're a few months old, you have an enormous population of B cells producing anti-alpha-gal antibodies. They circulate in your blood for your entire life. Roughly one percent of your total antibody pool is anti-alpha-gal. One percent. That's an enormous fraction.

Now you transplant a pig kidney into that person. The pig kidney is **covered** in alpha-gal — every glycoprotein on every endothelial cell of every blood vessel inside that kidney has alpha-gal sticking out. The moment the human's blood enters those vessels, the anti-alpha-gal antibodies in the blood see alpha-gal on the kidney's endothelium and bind. Tens of thousands of antibodies per cell, instantly.

What happens next is called **complement activation**. Complement is a cascade of proteins, also floating around in your blood, that gets switched on whenever antibodies cluster on a surface. Picture a row of dominoes: an antibody binds, the first complement protein binds the antibody, that protein activates the next, and the next, and the next, until at the end of the cascade you have a structure called the **membrane attack complex** — a literal molecular drill that punches a hole through the cell membrane. The endothelial cells lining the pig's blood vessels start dying. The vessel walls leak. Platelets activate, clots form, more vessels block, the tissue downstream dies of starvation. In a matter of minutes, the entire organ is a coagulated, dead lump.

This is hyperacute rejection. It is not the slower rejection you've heard about with kidney transplants from one human to another — that's a T cell process that takes weeks or months, and we have drugs for it. Hyperacute rejection is the antibody and complement system, working in seconds to minutes, on a target it has been primed to attack since infancy. There is no drug that prevents it once it starts. You cannot dial down preformed antibodies. You cannot stop the complement cascade fast enough.

\[Pause. Let it land.\]

So for fifty years, this was the wall. You could try to remove the antibodies from the patient's blood by filtering — a procedure called plasmapheresis — but you'd remove them for a few hours and then the body would make more, and the new ones would attack the graft. You could try to inhibit complement with drugs — and several were tried — but you couldn't shut it off completely. The fundamental problem was that the **organ itself was the wrong organ**. It was painted with a sugar that the human body was permanently primed to hate.

The solution, when it finally came, was conceptually simple. **Don't try to suppress the human's response. Change the pig.** Take the alpha-gal off the pig. If the pig doesn't make alpha-gal, the antibodies have nothing to bind. The complement cascade never fires. Hyperacute rejection doesn't happen.

Saying that and doing it were two different things, of course. The enzyme that makes alpha-gal is called **alpha-1,3-galactosyltransferase**, and the gene for it is called **GGTA1** — though in xeno papers you usually see it abbreviated as **GalT**. To take alpha-gal off a pig, you have to knock out the GalT gene in every cell of the pig's body. Which, in 2002, when this was finally accomplished, was an extraordinary achievement of genetic engineering. That's the next segment.

## SEGMENT 4 — The alpha-gal knockout pig: the first crack in the wall

The year is 2002. CRISPR doesn't exist yet — it won't be invented as a tool for another decade. But people had developed an older, slower, much more expensive technique for making genetically engineered pigs. The technique combined two earlier breakthroughs. The first was somatic cell nuclear transfer — the cloning technology that produced Dolly the sheep in 1996. The second was a method for knocking out genes in cells in culture using something called homologous recombination — a slow, low-efficiency, but functional way of disabling a specific gene.

The recipe went like this. Take pig fibroblasts — skin cells — and grow them in a dish. Disable the GalT gene in those cells using homologous recombination. Screen for the rare cells where the knockout worked, which might be one in a million. Once you find such a cell, take its nucleus, inject it into a pig egg whose own nucleus has been removed. Implant the resulting reconstructed egg into a surrogate sow. Wait three months and three weeks and three days. Out comes a piglet whose every cell carries the GalT knockout — because every cell traces back to that one engineered fibroblast.

This was, at the time, an extraordinarily difficult procedure. Each step was inefficient. Most attempts failed. The animals that did get born often had developmental defects from the cloning. But in 2002, a company called **Immerge BioTherapeutics**, and shortly afterward a company called **PPL Therapeutics** (the same outfit that had cloned Dolly), published papers announcing successful GalT knockout pigs. The first **GalT-KO pig** — sometimes called the alpha-gal-free pig, or the **GTKO pig** — was born.

These pigs are normal pigs. They walk around, they eat, they grow, they have piglets. The GalT enzyme isn't essential for pig life — they get by without alpha-gal on their cells just fine. But their organs, when looked at under a microscope and stained for alpha-gal, are clean. The sugar is gone.

And when you transplant a heart or a kidney from a GalT-KO pig into a baboon — which is the standard preclinical xenotransplant model, because baboons, like humans, lack alpha-gal and make anti-alpha-gal antibodies — the result is dramatically different from the unmodified pig. **Hyperacute rejection doesn't happen.** The minutes-to-hours catastrophic rejection is gone. The organ pinks up after the clamps come off and stays pink. The animal can urinate, or the heart can beat, for days or weeks.

\[Pause.\]

Now, here's where I have to be careful. The GalT knockout solved hyperacute rejection. It did not solve rejection. What it did was kick the can down the road. Once hyperacute is out of the picture, you get a slower process — called **acute humoral xenograft rejection**, sometimes abbreviated AHXR — that kicks in over days. It looks different at the microscope, but functionally it's the same idea: the human's immune system, now given a little more time, still recognizes the pig organ as foreign through other antigens, and still mounts a destructive response.

Why? Because alpha-gal is the loudest signal, but it isn't the only one. Pigs have other sugars on their cells, also absent in humans, also recognized by human antibodies. The two big ones are a sugar called **Neu5Gc** — N-glycolylneuraminic acid — made by an enzyme called **CMAH**; and a glycan called **Sda**, made by an enzyme called **β4GalNT2**. Humans lack both. Both elicit antibodies. Both contribute to graft attack.

So after the 2002 GalT knockout, the field had a clear roadmap. The single knockout wasn't enough. You needed to knock out alpha-gal, Neu5Gc, and Sda — all three — to strip the major xenoantigens off the pig surface. That goal was called the **triple knockout**, or **TKO**. And in the era of homologous recombination, achieving a TKO was nearly impossible. Each knockout took years. Stacking three of them — by breeding and re-cloning — was a project of monumental scale. A few groups tried. Some got two out of three. Some got close.

What changed the game, around 2013, was the arrival of CRISPR. The bacterial immune system, repurposed as a genome editing tool, made it possible to knock out multiple genes in the same fibroblast in a single experiment. Suddenly, the triple knockout was easy. Suddenly, you didn't have to stop at three. You could pile on more edits. You could knock out a dozen things at once. You could **insert** human genes in addition to **deleting** pig genes. The field's ambition expanded as soon as the tooling stopped being a bottleneck. And once you piled on enough edits, the survival numbers in baboon preclinical experiments started to climb — from days, to weeks, to months, to, eventually, a record of over two years. By the late 2010s, the field believed, for the first time, that a clinical attempt in humans was plausible. That belief is what made David Bennett's surgery thinkable.

But before we get to Bennett, I have to tell you what's actually inside the modern xeno pig — gene by gene. That's the next segment.

## SEGMENT 5 — The multi-gene-edit pig: subtractions and additions

Let me describe the modern xeno pig in detail. I want you to see it not as a generic "GMO pig" but as a very specifically designed biological device. There are roughly a dozen edits in the Revivicor 10-gene pig that David Bennett received, and there are nearly seventy in the eGenesis pigs that the 2024 kidney patients received. Each edit has a reason. The edits fall into three families: **subtractions** (knocking out pig genes that make foreign antigens), **subtractions** (knocking out porcine retroviruses, which I'll save for the next segment), and **additions** (inserting human genes that calm down the human immune system once the organ is in place).

\[Pause.\]

Let's go through them.

First family of edits, the antigen knockouts. We've already talked about **GalT** — alpha-gal, gone. Then **CMAH** — Neu5Gc, gone. Then **β4GalNT2** — Sda, gone. That's the **triple knockout**, the GMO patient I want you to remember: GalT, CMAH, β4GalNT2 — three sugar-modifying enzymes deleted, three xenoantigens stripped off the pig's cell surface. With just these three knockouts, when you take a sample of human blood and pour it over pig cells in a dish, the human antibodies barely stick at all. The chemical foreignness of the cell surface has been radically reduced.

Second family, the human gene insertions. Even with the antigens reduced, the human immune system has other layers of attack. There's the **complement cascade** I told you about — the molecular drill that punches holes in foreign cells. To suppress that, you insert genes encoding **human complement regulatory proteins**. The three big ones are **CD46** — which has a clinical name, membrane cofactor protein; **CD55** — also called decay-accelerating factor; and **CD59** — the membrane attack complex inhibitor. In a normal human, these three proteins sit on the surface of all your cells, telling complement: "this is one of us, don't drill us." Pigs make their own versions of these regulators, but the pig versions are tuned for pig complement, and they don't recognize human complement well. So we insert the human versions. Now when human complement starts to activate on the pig endothelium, the human regulators sitting there shut the cascade off. The complement attack stops before it can really get going.

There's also a clotting problem. When pig endothelium meets human blood, even without the antibody attack, you tend to get inappropriate clotting — because human clotting proteins and pig anticoagulant proteins don't mesh well. To fix this, you insert human anticoagulant genes like **thrombomodulin** and **endothelial protein C receptor**, which keep the pig endothelium from triggering human clots.

And finally there's a slower immune layer — the T cells. T cells take days to ramp up, but they're powerful. Once they decide an organ is foreign, they attack. To slow T cells down, you can insert genes that make the pig endothelium less stimulating to human T cells — genes like **CD47**, the "don't eat me" signal, in a human form; or genes that downregulate the major histocompatibility complex on the pig surface.

\[Pause.\]

Add it all up, and you have the modern multi-edit pig. The triple knockout strips off the sugars. The complement regulator insertions stop the membrane attack complex. The anticoagulant insertions stop the clotting. The T cell modulators slow the cellular response. Each layer of the immune system has been individually disarmed, by a specific edit, for a specific reason. **It's the GMO patient** — and I want you to hold that phrase, because it's the right framing. The pig is not just a donor. The pig is a custom-engineered biological device, designed from the genome up, to look as little like a foreign object as we can make it.

The Revivicor 10-edit pig has the triple knockout, two complement regulator transgenes (CD46 and CD55), two anticoagulant transgenes, and a couple of others including a growth hormone receptor knockout to keep the heart from growing too big after transplant. The eGenesis 69-edit pig adds more antigen knockouts, more transgenes, and a sweep of edits across the porcine endogenous retroviruses, which I'll cover in the next segment. The trajectory of the field is: more edits, more layers of immune evasion, more compatibility with the human environment.

If you want one mental image to take away from this segment, here it is. **The unedited pig is a foreigner who walks into your country wearing a different uniform, speaking a different language, with a different passport.** The edited pig has been given local clothes, taught the local language, and issued a human passport. The immune system still notices small differences, sometimes — but it doesn't immediately call the police.

That analogy is useful but incomplete, because there is one more class of problem, and it isn't about appearance. It's about hitchhikers. That's the next segment, and it's its own kind of horror story.

## SEGMENT 6 — Porcine endogenous retroviruses: the hitchhiker problem

I want to take a detour now into a problem that is, in some ways, scarier than rejection. Rejection kills the graft. The problem I'm about to describe could, in principle, start an epidemic.

Pigs, like every other vertebrate on Earth, carry **endogenous retroviruses** — retroviruses that, sometime in the deep evolutionary past, infected an ancestor of the species, integrated their DNA into the host germ line, and have been passed down through every generation since. Roughly **eight percent** of the human genome is endogenous retroviruses. We carry them, you and I, in every cell. Most are fragmentary and inert. But some retain functional viral genes. They're a permanent installation, written into the chromosomes, copied at every cell division.

Pigs carry their own set, called **porcine endogenous retroviruses**, or **PERVs**. Every pig genome has them. They sit at multiple locations on the chromosomes — anywhere from twenty-five to fifty copies, depending on the breed. Most of them are dormant, but some retain the ability to produce viral particles. In the right environment — pig cells in a dish, next to human cells in a dish — PERVs have been shown to **jump species**. Pig cell makes a viral particle. Particle drifts over. Particle infects a human cell. Human cell starts to express pig viral genes. Sometimes the new infection integrates into the human cell's DNA.

\[Pause.\]

You can see the nightmare scenario. You take a pig organ — covered in pig endothelial cells, full of pig genome, and therefore full of PERVs — and you implant it inside a human being whose immune system is being deliberately suppressed by transplant drugs. The pig cells inside the graft start producing PERV particles. The particles infect the patient's own cells. A new retrovirus is now resident in a human host. The patient — and anyone the patient has contact with — could become the index case of a novel zoonotic retrovirus epidemic. Like HIV, but starting from a hospital instead of a jungle.

Now, in fairness, the actual evidence for cross-species PERV transmission in living patients is thin. Some early xeno experiments — including some perfusion procedures where patients' blood was passed through pig livers for therapeutic purposes — were followed up for years, and no PERV transmission was ever detected. The risk may be lower than the worst-case scenarios. But the **possibility** alone was enough to keep regulators awake at night. For decades, the FDA's position was, essentially: until you can prove PERV won't jump, we won't approve a clinical xeno trial.

The breakthrough on this front came in 2017, and it's a story I want to spend a few minutes on, because it's also a CRISPR story. The lab was **George Church's** at Harvard — yes, the same George Church we mentioned in the CRISPR lecture, who co-discovered mammalian CRISPR editing in 2013 — and the first author was a postdoc named **Luhan Yang**. Yang would go on to co-found a company called **eGenesis**, which we'll come back to.

The Church-Yang team did something audacious. They took pig fibroblasts. They identified, in the pig genome, every functional copy of PERV — 25 copies in the cell line they were working with. They designed CRISPR guide RNAs to a conserved sequence in the viral genome — the **pol gene**, which every functional PERV needs to be infectious — and they hit all 25 copies at once, simultaneously, with Cas9. The result was a fibroblast cell line in which every PERV had been disabled.

Then they did the cloning step. They took those PERV-free fibroblasts, transferred their nuclei into pig eggs, and made the first PERV-free piglets. The 2017 paper in _Science_ was titled, with refreshing directness, "Inactivation of porcine endogenous retrovirus in pigs using CRISPR-Cas9." The piglets were born. They grew up. PERV was, in their cells, undetectable. The hitchhiker problem had a solution.

\[Pause.\]

I want to pause and appreciate the technical achievement here. Knocking out 25 copies of a gene at once — at 25 different chromosomal locations — was, in 2017, a record. Most CRISPR experiments target one gene at a time. The Church-Yang work showed that you could push the tooling to dozens of simultaneous edits in one cell, all integrated through one cloning step, all transmitted through the germ line. That capability — multiplexed editing at scale — is what eventually made the 69-edit eGenesis pig possible.

But I also want to be honest about the limits of the PERV solution. The Church 2017 work showed you could knock out PERV in fibroblast lines and produce viable piglets. It did not yet show that those PERV-free piglets, when bred, kept the PERV out across generations — though follow-up work has shown that they do. And it did not show that PERV-free organs are clinically safer than PERV-positive organs — we still don't have human data on that question. What it did show is that the regulatory concern about PERV is, technically, addressable. We can build a pig without functional PERV.

Now, an important footnote. The pigs used in the 2022 Bennett heart transplant — the Revivicor 10-edit pigs — did **not** have the PERV knockout. The FDA had concluded that with appropriate screening and monitoring of the donor herd, the residual risk of PERV transmission was acceptable. The 2024 kidney patients, who received eGenesis pigs, did receive PERV-inactivated donors. Both companies have taken different paths on this question, and we'll talk about the regulatory side of it in segment 14.

For now, what I want you to remember is this. **PERVs are the endogenous retroviruses every pig genome carries. They could, in principle, jump into humans during transplant. Church's 25-knockout pig eliminated them.** That's the headline. It's a tour de force of multiplex CRISPR, it solved a fifty-year regulatory headache, and it's one of the foundations on which the modern clinical xenotransplant program is built.

Alright. We've now covered the three big immune barriers — alpha-gal, the broader xenoantigen suite, and the PERV question — and the technological responses to each. We have, in principle, a pig that can survive in a human body for at least a while. Now I want to introduce the two companies actually building these pigs. Because xenotransplantation, like a lot of modern biology, has moved from academic basic science into the corporate world, and the two companies are not interchangeable. They have different histories, different pigs, and different bets.

## SEGMENT 7 — Revivicor: the company that made Bennett's heart

So let's talk about **Revivicor**. If there is a hero company in this story — or at least a protagonist company — it is this one. Revivicor's history is, in itself, a tour of the last quarter century of mammalian biotechnology.

The lineage begins, as I hinted earlier, with **PPL Therapeutics**, the Edinburgh-based biotech that, in 1996, cloned Dolly the sheep with the Roslin Institute. After Dolly, PPL pivoted into making genetically engineered farm animals as biological factories — pigs that could produce human pharmaceuticals in their milk, that sort of thing. In 2003, PPL split off its xenotransplantation program into a new company called **Revivicor**. The name is a portmanteau, roughly "revive" plus a Latin root for "heart." It tells you what they cared about. Revivicor's bet, from the start, was that genetically engineered pig organs were going to save human lives.

For the first decade, the bet was lonely. The 2008 financial crisis nearly killed the company. They were acquired in 2011 by a larger group called **United Therapeutics**, which is a publicly traded pharmaceutical company best known for cardiopulmonary drugs. The deal was, in the parlance of biotech, a strategic acquisition — United Therapeutics' founder, **Martine Rothblatt**, had a personal stake in the field, because her own daughter has a serious lung disease, and Rothblatt had become convinced that xenotransplantation was the eventual answer to the donor lung shortage. So Revivicor went from being a struggling spinout to being the well-funded subsidiary of a Fortune-list pharma, with a leader who genuinely believed in the mission.

\[Pause.\]

The Revivicor pigs are, broadly, the lineal descendants of the 2002 GalT knockout work. Over the 2010s, Revivicor stacked additional edits — initially through breeding and cloning, then increasingly through CRISPR — onto the original GalT-KO line. By the late 2010s, they had pigs with the triple knockout, multiple human complement regulators, anticoagulant transgenes, and a growth hormone receptor knockout. They called the final clinical product the **10-gene-edited pig**. The word "edited" is doing some work here — some of the 10 edits are knockouts, some are knockins, some are combinations. But ten distinct genetic interventions.

Revivicor's pigs, importantly, do **not** have PERV knockouts. The company's position is that with their gnotobiotic herd management — meaning the pigs are raised in barrier facilities, screened intensively for pathogens, and isolated from each other — the PERV risk is acceptable without inactivating the retroviruses in the genome. It's a defensible position, and the FDA agreed for Bennett's surgery. But it is a meaningful difference from the eGenesis approach.

Revivicor is based in Blacksburg, Virginia, and its pig facility — a high-containment, climate-controlled barrier facility — is in the Virginia hill country. The pigs live in clean rooms. Their feed is sterilized. Their water is filtered. The humans who work with them shower in and shower out, wear sterile garments, and are screened for human pathogens that might cross over to the pigs. It is, again, more semiconductor fab than barn. The Revivicor herd is, in a sense, the most expensively maintained set of pigs in human history.

In 2020, the FDA granted approval — under a peculiar regulatory pathway called the **Intentional Genomic Alteration** designation — for Revivicor's GalT-KO pigs to be used as food, marketed under the name **GalSafe**. This was for a different application: people with **alpha-gal syndrome**, an allergy to mammalian meat caused by tick bites, can eat GalSafe pork without the allergic reaction. It was the first FDA approval of any genetically engineered animal for both food and biomedical use. It doesn't really affect the transplant program, but it was a regulatory milestone — proof that the agency would, in principle, approve products from a genetically engineered pig herd.

The 10-edit Revivicor heart is the heart that went into David Bennett in January 2022. We'll get to him in two segments. Before that, the other company.

## SEGMENT 8 — eGenesis: the 69-edit competitor

The other major player is **eGenesis**, founded in 2015 by George Church and Luhan Yang — the principal investigators on the 2017 PERV-knockout paper we discussed in segment 6. eGenesis is based in Cambridge, Massachusetts, near the Harvard and MIT campuses where Church has his lab. The company's founding bet was different from Revivicor's. Revivicor came up through the cloning and homologous recombination era and added CRISPR later. eGenesis was born in the CRISPR era and built its entire pipeline around multiplexed gene editing. Their advantage was always going to be the ability to make many edits at once.

The eGenesis pig is sometimes called the **69-edit pig**, though the count depends on how you tally — some descriptions are of 59 edits, some are of 69, depending on which subset of the porcine retroviruses are counted and which transgenes are bundled together. The order of magnitude is right: roughly seventy genetic interventions, in one animal.

Let me walk through what's in there. Three antigen knockouts — the triple knockout, alpha-gal, Neu5Gc, Sda — the same as Revivicor. **Sixty-plus PERV inactivations** — every functional copy of porcine endogenous retrovirus knocked out, descending from the 2017 Church work. **Seven human transgenes** — complement regulators including CD46 and CD55, anticoagulants including thrombomodulin and CD39, and immune modulators including HLA-E, a human MHC molecule that signals "self" to certain immune cells.

The arithmetic of building this pig is staggering. To make a fibroblast cell line with seventy edits, you cannot do them one at a time and clone in between — you would run out of time and the cells would senesce. You have to use multiplexed CRISPR, dropping in many guide RNAs simultaneously, and accept that you will get a fraction of cells with every edit you wanted plus some unintended consequences, and then screen by deep sequencing for the rare clone that's clean. Once you have such a clone, you do the nuclear transfer step, implant in a surrogate sow, and get piglets that carry all seventy edits.

The eGenesis pigs that went into the 2024 kidney patients were named, in some early presentations, the **EGEN-2784** line. The number isn't important — what matters is that this is the most heavily engineered animal that has ever existed and survived to clinical use.

\[Pause.\]

The two companies have a friendly rivalry. They use slightly different breeds — Revivicor uses Yucatan, eGenesis uses Yucatan as well but with some line differences. They make different bets on the right edit set. Revivicor leans on barrier herd management to handle PERV; eGenesis leans on edit-out. Revivicor was first to clinical, with Bennett's heart. eGenesis came in shortly after with the 2024 kidney wave. Both are now running clinical trials in coordination with the FDA. Both have institutional partners — Revivicor with the University of Maryland and the University of Alabama at Birmingham, eGenesis with Massachusetts General Hospital and NYU Langone.

The competition is healthy. It's the kind of two-company race that, in other fields, has driven the technology forward — think of the way Genentech and Eli Lilly competed in early recombinant insulin, or the way Illumina and the original 454 competed in next-generation sequencing. You want two contenders. The field is better for it.

Okay. Now we get to the human patients. Real people. Real surgeries. Real outcomes. This is where xenotransplantation stopped being a slide deck and started being history.

## SEGMENT 9 — David Bennett: the first pig heart, January 2022

I want to tell you a story. The story is set in a hospital room at the University of Maryland Medical Center, in Baltimore, in January 2022. The patient is a 57-year-old man named **David Bennett**. He's been in the hospital for six weeks. He has end-stage heart failure. He's on a machine called ECMO — extracorporeal membrane oxygenation — which is essentially a portable heart-lung bypass that keeps him alive while his own heart is no longer pumping effectively. He cannot get up. He cannot eat. He is, in the most literal sense, sustained by tubes.

He is not eligible for a human heart transplant. There are several reasons, but the principal one is a record of poor compliance with previous medical treatments — earlier in his life, he had not consistently taken his medications. Transplant programs use compliance as a criterion because a transplanted human heart requires daily, lifelong immunosuppression, and a patient who won't take their pills will reject the graft. So the human transplant list is closed to him. Without a transplant, he will die in days, possibly hours.

The team at Maryland, led by **Dr. Bartley Griffith**, the surgeon, and **Dr. Muhammad Mohiuddin**, the immunologist who had spent decades on xenotransplantation, present David Bennett with an option. Under an FDA pathway called **expanded access** — sometimes called compassionate use — the agency can authorize, on a one-patient basis, the use of an experimental therapy in a patient with no other options and a high likelihood of death. Maryland had filed paperwork. Revivicor had a 10-edit pig ready. The FDA, after weeks of back-and-forth, had given a single-patient authorization.

Bennett, after long conversations with his family and the medical team, agreed.

\[Pause.\]

On January 7, 2022, in an eight-hour operation, Bartley Griffith and his surgical team removed David Bennett's native heart and replaced it with the heart of a Revivicor pig — a 10-gene-edited animal whose three xenoantigens were knocked out and which carried six human transgenes. The pig had been sacrificed earlier that morning, in an adjacent building. Its heart had been kept on a perfusion device — a machine called the **OCS Heart**, made by a company called TransMedics, which pumps oxygenated blood through an organ outside the body to keep it viable during transport. The pig heart was transported across the medical campus, brought into the operating room, examined, and sewn into David Bennett's chest.

The clamps came off. The new heart, the pig heart, started beating. It pinked up. It contracted. It did not turn black. There was no hyperacute rejection. The fifty-year-old wall, the alpha-gal cliff, had not been hit. The team closed the chest. Bennett was wheeled to the ICU. By the next morning, he was awake, able to talk, able to recognize his family. He could move his hands. He had a pig's heart inside him and he was alive.

The world found out the next day. The story exploded. Front page of every newspaper. Every news network. It was, depending on your perspective, either the dawn of a new medical era or a Frankenstein nightmare. The medical community was, in the main, cautiously excited. Surgical xenotransplantation had moved from preclinical primate experiments into a human patient. The proof was alive.

\[Pause.\]

For the next several weeks, Bennett continued to improve. He could sit up. He spoke with the press. He watched the Super Bowl. He told the team he was grateful, that he was happy to be a pioneer. He understood the risk. He knew his survival was not guaranteed. The team was carefully monitoring his immune function, his renal function, his cardiac output. Everything looked good.

Then, around day 40, things began to deteriorate. The pig heart started to show signs of damage — thickening of the muscle, signs of cellular distress. Bennett's overall condition worsened. The team escalated treatment. On day 60, on March 8, 2022, David Bennett died.

\[Pause. Let it land.\]

The autopsy and detailed post-mortem analysis took months, and the findings were published the following year. The story they tell is humbling.

The principal finding was not, in fact, classical immune rejection. The pig heart's tissue did not show the hallmarks of T-cell attack or antibody-mediated rejection in the way the team had feared. What the autopsy showed was something more subtle: evidence that the pig heart had been infected with **porcine cytomegalovirus**, or **pCMV**, a herpesvirus that lives in pigs, and that the infection had compromised the heart muscle from within.

This was a surprise, because the donor pig had been screened for pCMV before transplant. The screening test that had been used was a blood-based PCR, which detects circulating virus — but pCMV, like other cytomegaloviruses, can sit latent in tissues without showing up in the blood. The Revivicor herd's screening protocol, as it existed in early 2022, had a blind spot. The team learned this after the fact. The screening protocol has since been updated to use tissue-based detection.

This was the most important lesson of the Bennett case, and it's worth pausing on. **Bennett's heart was destroyed not by immune rejection but by a pig virus that the screening missed.** The immune problem — the problem the field had spent fifty years on — had been substantially solved by the genetic engineering. The new problem was infectious. The Bennett experience told the field: it's not enough to engineer the pig. You also have to surveil it. We'll come back to the gnotobiotic herd question in segment 15.

David Bennett's family, after his death, said publicly that they had no regrets. The team at Maryland received both criticism and credit. The trial was, by the metric the team set in advance — survival to discharge, evidence that the genetic engineering had cleared the hyperacute hurdle — a partial success. The man lived sixty days with a pig's heart. He lived two months he otherwise would not have had. The field had its first proof. **Tragic, but proof.**

The Maryland team performed a second pig heart transplant in September 2023, into a different patient named **Lawrence Faucette**. He lived six weeks before succumbing to rejection. The second case taught additional lessons about the immune protocol but did not produce the same paradigm shift as the first. After Faucette, the heart program paused while the kidney program — which had been preparing in parallel — moved into clinical use.

That was 2024. And 2024 was a remarkable year.

## SEGMENT 10 — Rick Slayman: the first pig kidney transplant, March 2024

Welcome back. I hope you took your break.

The story of 2024 begins on March 16, in Boston, at **Massachusetts General Hospital**. The patient is **Richard Slayman** — Rick to everyone who knew him — a 62-year-old man from Weymouth, Massachusetts, with end-stage kidney disease. He had been on dialysis for seven years. He had received a human kidney transplant in 2018, but that transplanted kidney had failed in 2023, and he had returned to dialysis. He was, in the clinical phrase, **highly sensitized** — meaning his immune system had developed broad antibody reactivity from the prior transplant, making it very hard to find a compatible second human kidney. His dialysis access was failing. He was running out of options.

The eGenesis pig, with the full 69-edit package, had been advancing toward clinical use, and Mass General — under the leadership of transplant surgeon **Dr. Tatsuo Kawai** and nephrologist **Dr. Leonardo Riella** — had been preparing for this for years. They had FDA authorization, under the same compassionate use pathway that had been used for Bennett. They had an eGenesis donor ready. And they had a patient.

\[Pause.\]

The kidney came out of an eGenesis pig in their Massachusetts barrier facility, was transported under controlled conditions, and was implanted in Rick Slayman in a four-hour operation. The implant site was the standard one for a kidney transplant — the right iliac fossa, in the lower abdomen. The pig kidney was sewn into Slayman's iliac artery and iliac vein. The ureter was sewn into the bladder. The clamps came off. The kidney pinked up. It started producing urine.

He went to the ICU. He recovered. His creatinine — the standard blood marker of kidney function — came down from the dialysis-dependent range to nearly normal. He was discharged from the hospital two weeks after surgery. He went home. He gave interviews. He said he felt good, that he wanted to encourage other patients to consider the option. He played with his grandchildren.

He died seven weeks after the surgery, on May 11, 2024. The cause, according to the family and Mass General's statement, was not the pig kidney. The kidney itself, on autopsy, was functioning, with no signs of acute rejection. Slayman died of an unrelated cardiac event — likely a consequence of his underlying long-standing cardiovascular disease, which had been deteriorating for years before the transplant. From the team's perspective, the kidney had **worked**. The first xenotransplanted kidney from a multi-edit pig had functioned in a human patient for seven weeks without rejection. The proof of concept for the kidney program was in.

I want to emphasize what that meant. The Bennett heart had survived 60 days, but had been damaged by a pig virus and ultimately failed. The Slayman kidney had survived 49 days with no signs of rejection at all, until the patient died of something else. The kidney program, on its first try, had cleared a different bar — the immunology was holding. The genetic engineering was doing what it was supposed to do.

The Mass General team published the case in the _New England Journal of Medicine_ later that year. The paper is, by the standards of clinical case reports, dramatic — but it's also restrained, with the appropriate caveats. One patient. Seven weeks. We need more.

They had more, almost immediately.

## SEGMENT 11 — Lisa Pisano: pig kidney plus pig thymus, April 2024

The next patient was **Lisa Pisano**, a 54-year-old woman from New Jersey, transplanted on April 12, 2024 — just under a month after Slayman — at **NYU Langone Health** in New York City, by a team led by **Dr. Robert Montgomery**.

Pisano had a particular combination of problems. She had end-stage kidney disease, like Slayman, and she had been on dialysis. But she also had a failing heart — she had received a mechanical pump called a **left ventricular assist device** to keep her circulation going. Most patients with that severity of cardiac disease are not candidates for a kidney transplant, because the surgery itself is risky and a kidney transplant doesn't solve their cardiac problem. She was, in transplant terms, a no-options patient.

\[Pause.\]

Montgomery's team did something different with Pisano. They didn't just transplant a pig kidney. They transplanted a pig kidney **with attached pig thymus tissue**. I want to spend a minute on why.

The thymus is the organ in your chest where your T cells finish their development. When you're young, your T cells are made in the bone marrow and then migrate to the thymus, where they undergo a kind of finishing school — a process of selection in which T cells that react too strongly to "self" antigens are killed, and T cells that don't react at all are also killed. The ones that survive — the ones that react just enough — are released into the bloodstream as mature T cells. The thymus is, in effect, an education facility for T cells, where they learn to recognize self versus non-self.

Here is the idea behind the **thymokidney** approach. If you transplant a pig kidney along with a piece of the donor pig's thymus, you are placing inside the human a small fragment of a foreign immune education facility. Some of the human's developing T cells will pass through that foreign thymus and be educated by it. They will learn to recognize the pig's tissue as "self." When they emerge as mature T cells, they will tolerate the pig kidney instead of attacking it.

This was a concept that had been worked out in earlier preclinical baboon experiments by a researcher named **David Sachs**, who spent decades on the idea of inducing donor-specific tolerance through thymic tissue. The Pisano case was, in a sense, the first time the concept was tried in a human alongside a clinical kidney transplant.

\[Pause.\]

Pisano's surgery itself went well. The kidney took. Her cardiac status was monitored closely. She came off dialysis. She had a functioning pig kidney and a functioning pig thymus inside her.

The clinical course, unfortunately, was difficult. Pisano's underlying cardiovascular disease worsened in the weeks after the surgery. The kidney itself was functioning — but her LVAD developed problems, her cardiac output declined, and she was returned to dialysis when blood pressure could not be maintained at adequate levels to perfuse the kidney. The kidney was eventually removed. Pisano herself died in July 2024, about three months after the original transplant, of cardiac causes that the team has reported were largely independent of the xenograft.

So Pisano's outcome was, from the immunological standpoint, also a partial success — the kidney functioned for weeks without rejection, the thymokidney approach was tolerated. But from the clinical standpoint, the patient died, and the team learned that patient selection — choosing patients robust enough to survive the broader surgical and medical course — was going to be its own learning curve.

What Pisano's case added to the field's knowledge was: the kidney plus thymus combination is feasible. The thymus tissue can be transplanted alongside the kidney without overwhelming complications. Whether the thymus is actually inducing tolerance, in the long-term immunological sense, remains an open question — but the surgical concept has been demonstrated.

Two patients so far. Two kidneys. Both functioned. Both patients eventually died, neither from rejection. The third patient is, in some ways, the most interesting case of the three.

## SEGMENT 12 — Towana Looney: the longest survival so far

**Towana Looney** is a woman from Alabama who, as of the most recent updates I have, is alive with a pig kidney inside her — and has been alive with it longer than any other patient in the history of clinical xenotransplantation. Her story is the one that, more than the others, is telling the field that this might actually work.

Looney is 53. She donated a kidney to her mother, who was in renal failure, back in 1999 — twenty-five years before her own transplant. Some years after that donation, Looney herself developed pregnancy-related complications that destroyed her remaining kidney. She had been on dialysis for eight years. Like Slayman, she was highly sensitized — by virtue of pregnancies and dialysis-related transfusions, her body had developed broad antibodies against human tissues, making her a very poor candidate for a human kidney transplant. She was on the list, but the chance of finding a compatible donor was small. She was running out of time.

\[Pause.\]

In November 2024, at **NYU Langone** — the same team led by Robert Montgomery that had transplanted Pisano — Looney received an eGenesis pig kidney. Same 69-edit pig design as Slayman and Pisano. Same surgical approach. The transplant went smoothly. She produced urine immediately. She recovered in a way that, by the team's account, was the smoothest of any xeno patient to date.

She went home. She gave interviews. She said she felt better than she had in years. She came off dialysis. Her creatinine stabilized in the near-normal range. She walked around Times Square as a tourist a few weeks after surgery, just for the experience of feeling well enough to do it.

As of the time I'm preparing these notes — and I want to be careful because this is the fastest-moving part of the lecture, and by the time you hear it the situation may have evolved — Towana Looney has lived longer with a pig kidney than any other patient. The most recent public updates have her past four months, into 2025, with continuing kidney function. The team is reporting periodic episodes of rejection that have been managed with adjustments to her immunosuppression. The kidney is, so far, holding.

\[Pause.\]

If you want one mental image for the 2024 kidney wave, here it is. **Slayman, Pisano, Looney — three patients in a row, each living longer with their pig kidney than the last.** The trajectory is the right direction. The first patient functioned for seven weeks, the second for several weeks, the third for months and counting. The team is learning with each case how to manage the immune protocol, the supportive care, the patient selection, the surveillance. The bar is moving.

That said, I want to be honest. Three patients is not a clinical trial. The fact that Looney is doing well is enormously encouraging, but extrapolating from one patient to a treatment for tens of thousands of dialysis patients is not warranted. The patients we are seeing now are no-options patients, transplanted under compassionate use authorizations, with the most experienced teams in the world watching them around the clock. The transition to a formal clinical trial — and from a trial to a routine standard of care — is a long process. We are at the beginning of it.

The field's leadership knows this. There is a planned formal clinical trial for pig kidneys, expected to begin in 2025, that will enroll a defined cohort of patients under a more conventional protocol. The compassionate use cases — Bennett, Faucette, Slayman, Pisano, Looney — are the bridge from preclinical baboon work to that formal trial. They tell us, broadly: it can work, the immunology is manageable, the surveillance must be careful, the patients matter. The formal trial is where we will learn whether xeno can become medicine.

## SEGMENT 13 — What they're taking: the immunosuppression cocktail

Let's talk about drugs. Because the patient isn't just walking around with an edited pig kidney and an unmodified immune system. The patient is on a pharmacological regimen that is, by transplant standards, unusual and aggressive. Understanding the regimen tells you something important about which immune problems are still live, even after all the genetic engineering.

Standard human-to-human kidney transplant is, in most centers, managed with a three-drug cocktail. The cornerstone is **tacrolimus**, a calcineurin inhibitor — a drug that blocks T-cell activation. The second is **mycophenolate**, an antiproliferative drug that blocks B and T cell division. The third is a low-dose **corticosteroid** like prednisone, which suppresses inflammation broadly. That triple combination has been the standard since the 1990s, and it works well enough for human-to-human transplant.

For xenotransplant, that standard triple is not enough. The pig organ, even with the triple knockout and human transgenes, still presents some antigens the recipient's T cells can recognize. Without additional suppression, you would get T-cell-mediated rejection within weeks.

\[Pause.\]

The xeno protocol that has been used in the 2024 kidney cases adds two more drugs to the conventional cocktail. The first is **belatacept**, a fusion protein that blocks something called CD28 — a key costimulatory receptor that T cells need to fully activate. Belatacept is already approved for human-to-human kidney transplant in patients who can't tolerate tacrolimus; in xeno, it's used in addition to tacrolimus, layered on top.

The second, and more interesting, addition is an experimental class of antibody called **anti-CD40L** — sometimes shorthanded as anti-CD154 or as a monoclonal called **tegoprubart** or similar agents. CD40L is a molecule on T cells that, when it binds CD40 on antigen-presenting cells, drives a major arm of the immune response — the so-called costimulatory pathway. Blocking CD40L is a way of cutting the second signal that T cells need to commit to attack. The drug class has been studied for human autoimmune disease for decades but ran into safety problems — earlier generations caused thrombosis. Newer-generation anti-CD40L antibodies have been engineered to avoid that problem and are now in human trials specifically for xenotransplant.

So the regimen is, roughly: **tacrolimus, plus belatacept, plus anti-CD40L, plus low-dose steroids**. Four drugs. Each one targeting a different part of the T-cell activation pathway. The reasoning is: layered, redundant blockade of T-cell function, on top of an already heavily-engineered organ. The hope is that this combination — once the antibody and complement problems are out of the way thanks to the gene editing — is enough to keep the cellular immune response in check.

It is, as you can imagine, a heavy regimen. Each of these drugs has side effects. Patients are at significant risk of opportunistic infection. They have to be carefully monitored. But this is the price of admission, currently, for a xenotransplant. Over time, as the field gains experience, the regimen will be refined. Some drugs may be dropped. Doses may be lowered. The goal, eventually, is a regimen no more intense than standard human-to-human transplant. We are not there yet.

## SEGMENT 14 — The FDA pathway: IND, compassionate use, and the road to approval

How does any of this actually get authorized? Because you don't just decide to put a pig kidney in a person. You go through the FDA.

The xenotransplantation pathway at the FDA is run by a part of the agency called the **Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research**, or **CBER**. CBER is the part of the FDA that regulates products that derive from living systems — vaccines, gene therapies, cell therapies, and yes, organs from genetically engineered animals. Each pig organ is, in CBER's framework, a kind of biological product, subject to the same broad regulatory architecture as a gene therapy or a vaccine.

The principal regulatory document is called an **Investigational New Drug** application, or **IND**. To run a clinical trial with a xenograft, the sponsor — usually the company, sometimes the academic medical center — submits an IND to CBER describing the donor pig, the genetic edits, the manufacturing process, the surgical protocol, the immunosuppression, the surveillance plan, the patient selection criteria, and the proposed clinical endpoints. CBER reviews the IND, asks questions, sometimes requires additional preclinical data, and, if satisfied, allows the trial to proceed.

\[Pause.\]

The cases we've talked about so far — Bennett, Faucette, Slayman, Pisano, Looney — were not done under formal INDs in the trial sense. They were done under a special pathway called **expanded access**, sometimes called **compassionate use**, which allows the FDA to authorize the use of an experimental therapy in a single patient who has no other options. Each case required its own separate FDA authorization. The team had to make the case, in writing, that this particular patient had no alternative, that the risks were understood, that the patient had consented. The agency had to agree, case by case.

The next step — the one the field is actively working on — is moving from individual expanded-access cases to a formal **Phase 1 clinical trial**. A Phase 1 xeno trial would enroll a defined cohort of patients, probably starting with six to ten, with pre-specified inclusion criteria, a uniform surgical protocol, a defined immunosuppression regimen, and predefined endpoints. The first such trials are expected to begin in 2025, with eGenesis and United Therapeutics — the parent of Revivicor — each pursuing their own programs.

If those trials are successful, the next phase would be **Phase 2** and **Phase 3** trials, with larger patient cohorts and randomization to compare xeno to standard of care. The full pathway to FDA approval — to the point where a doctor can prescribe a pig kidney without a special authorization — is generally estimated, by people in the field, to require **first approvals around 2027 to 2028**. That timeline could slip — it almost certainly will, in some direction — but the order of magnitude is right. Within five years, we may have an approved xenograft. Within ten, it could be a standard of care for the kidney waiting list.

One more wrinkle worth mentioning. The FDA, in 2003, published a major **guidance document** for xenotransplantation that laid out, in detail, the source-animal requirements, the screening requirements, the patient surveillance requirements, and the reporting requirements. That document has been updated periodically since, most recently in light of the Bennett experience. Among other things, the guidance requires that xenotransplant patients agree to **lifelong surveillance** — periodic blood testing for pig pathogens — and that they accept restrictions on certain activities like blood donation. Some critics have argued that the surveillance requirements amount to a permanent restriction on personal autonomy. The counterargument is that the public health stakes — preventing a novel zoonotic disease — justify the constraint. The debate continues.

## SEGMENT 15 — Pig virus screening: gnotobiotic herds and the Bennett lesson

I want to come back to the infectious disease question, because it is, in my opinion, the most underappreciated piece of the xenotransplantation puzzle. The genetic engineering gets the headlines. The herd management is what makes it safe.

A xenotransplant donor pig is not a regular pig. It is raised in a facility called a **designated pathogen-free** or **gnotobiotic** facility. The word "gnotobiotic" comes from Greek roots meaning "known life" — it refers to an environment in which every microbial organism present is known and characterized. The pigs in a gnotobiotic xeno facility are born by **C-section** in a sterile chamber, immediately transferred into a sterile isolator, never touch the outside world, and live their entire lives within a controlled microbial environment.

\[Pause.\]

The reason for this is simple. Pigs naturally carry a long list of viruses, bacteria, and parasites. Most are harmless to humans. Some are not. **Porcine cytomegalovirus**, which I told you about in the Bennett story, is one of the dangerous ones. **Hepatitis E virus**, in its pig-adapted form, is another. **Influenza A**, which moves between pigs and humans, is a third. **Streptococcus suis**, a bacterium that lives in pig tonsils, can cause meningitis in humans. The list is long.

The screening protocol for a xenotransplant donor pig involves periodic blood testing across the herd — by PCR, by serology, by viral culture where appropriate — for every known pathogen of concern. Sentinel animals — pigs deliberately housed with the donor herd but reserved for testing rather than transplant — are sacrificed periodically and their tissues examined. The screening is not just at the time of transplant; it's continuous, across the herd's life.

The Bennett case taught the field that screening for circulating virus is not the same as screening for tissue-resident virus. Porcine cytomegalovirus, like all herpesviruses, can sit latent in tissues — in spleen, in kidney, in cardiac muscle — without circulating in the blood. Blood-based PCR misses these animals. After Bennett, the protocols were updated to include **tissue biopsy** from the donor pig, as close to the time of transplant as possible, with PCR testing of the biopsied tissue. The Slayman, Pisano, and Looney cases used the updated protocol. No detectable pCMV in any of them. The protocol improvement worked.

This is the kind of lesson that you only learn from clinical experience. The 2002-2022 preclinical baboon era did not teach the field about pCMV because the baboon work didn't have enough survival time to make pCMV reactivation the limiting factor. It took a human case, and a tragic outcome, to put the issue on the radar. This is a general lesson of translational medicine — the first patients teach you things the animal models cannot. We owe a real debt to David Bennett for that lesson.

The screening regime is, of course, expensive. The cost of maintaining a gnotobiotic xeno herd is enormous — by some estimates, in the hundreds of thousands of dollars per donor pig that actually gets used. Whether that cost can come down enough to make xeno organs broadly affordable is, in itself, an open question. Right now, the cost is justified by the experimental nature of the work. At scale, it will have to drop.

## SEGMENT 16 — Ethics: religion, animal welfare, and informed consent

Every time I give this lecture, somebody raises their hand and asks about ethics. So let me address the question head-on. There are three layers of ethical concern, and they are not the same. **Religious considerations. Animal welfare. Informed consent.** Let me take them in turn.

\[Pause.\]

First, religion. Several major religious traditions — particularly **Islam** and **Judaism** — have dietary prohibitions on pork. The question that people often ask is: does a Muslim or Jewish patient face a religious problem in receiving a pig organ?

The short answer, which I want to be careful with because I am not a religious scholar and the views vary, is that the major religious authorities in both traditions have generally ruled that **life-saving medical use of pig tissue is permitted**. The principle invoked is, broadly, that the prohibition on pork is dietary — it relates to consuming pig flesh as food — and that medical use to save a life falls under a separate framework. In Islamic jurisprudence, the principle is sometimes called **darura**, necessity, which permits otherwise-prohibited acts when life is at stake. In Jewish law, the principle is **pikuach nefesh**, the supreme value of preserving life. Most rabbinical and Islamic medical-ethics opinions have, in fact, ruled that pig organ transplants are permissible. Individual patients of course consult with their own religious advisors and make their own decisions, and not every authority agrees, but the major-tradition position is permissive.

Hindu and Buddhist traditions, which take more general views on animal welfare and non-violence, also tend to permit pig transplants on similar life-saving grounds, though again with individual variation.

Second, animal welfare. The pigs in question are bred specifically to be donors. They live in sterile facilities, are anesthetized at the time of organ procurement, and are euthanized in the process. There is a meaningful animal-welfare cost to xeno — one pig dies for each transplanted organ. Compared to the hundred and thirty million pigs killed annually for food in the United States, the marginal animal-welfare cost is small. But it is not zero. Some ethicists have argued that we should be raising xeno pigs in higher-welfare conditions than commercial farm pigs, even if the lifespan is short. Some have argued the opposite, that the use of an animal to save a human life is straightforwardly justified. The debate is active. My own view is that the welfare standards applied to xeno pigs are, in general, higher than those applied to commercial swine, and that this is appropriate.

Third, and most subtle, informed consent. The xeno patients we've talked about — Bennett, Slayman, Pisano, Looney — were all patients with no good alternative. The compassionate use pathway, by design, restricts xenotransplantation to patients who would otherwise die. The question is whether such patients can genuinely give **informed consent** when the alternative is death. Critics argue that the desperate cannot freely consent. Defenders argue that the alternative — refusing to offer experimental therapies to the dying — is itself a moral wrong, depriving them of agency. The field's response, in practice, has been to invest heavily in the consent process — extended conversations, family involvement, independent ethics review boards, the option to decline at any point — to make consent as informed as it can be. Whether that is enough is, I think, a judgment each of you will have to make.

\[Pause.\]

There is also the **public health consent** question. Xenotransplant patients are agreeing, on behalf of the rest of us, to be possible vectors for novel zoonotic disease. The surveillance and contact restrictions are partial mitigations. But the patients are accepting risk on behalf of a society that is, in effect, conducting a long-term experiment through them. This is an ethical structure we have used for clinical trials for a long time, but it is more vivid in xenotransplant than in most other settings. I don't have a tidy answer here. I think it's a real consideration. I think it has to be balanced against the very real loss of life on the waiting list.

## SEGMENT 17 — The competition: 3D bioprinting and stem-cell organs

Xeno is not the only contender for the title of "future source of transplantable organs." There are at least two other approaches that are sometimes presented as alternatives. Let me cover them briefly, because the comparison matters.

The first is **3D bioprinting**. The idea, which has been around since roughly the early 2000s, is that you can use a modified 3D printer to deposit layers of living cells, suspended in a hydrogel scaffold, in the shape of an organ. The cells could come from the patient themselves — autologous, eliminating the immune problem entirely — or from a universal donor cell line. Over time, the printed scaffold would mature into a functional organ.

The progress has been real but slow. Bioprinted skin, bioprinted cartilage, bioprinted bladder — these have been demonstrated, and in a few cases used clinically. The challenge for solid organs is **vascularization**. A kidney has roughly a million functional units called nephrons, each with its own intricate capillary network, all hooked up to a central vascular tree. Printing the macro-anatomy is the easy part. Recreating the dense, hierarchical microvasculature that delivers blood to every cell is the hard part. Without functional vasculature, the inner cells of the print starve and die.

There are companies — including United Therapeutics' own subsidiary **3D Bio-Therapeutics**, and others like Prellis Biologics and Aspect Biosystems — working on this problem. Recent advances in printing speed and scaffold chemistry have closed some of the gap. But no bioprinted solid organ has yet been transplanted into a human. The field is, by most estimates, ten to twenty years behind xeno.

\[Pause.\]

The second alternative is **stem-cell-derived organs**, sometimes called **organoids** scaled up, or **iPSC-derived tissues**. Induced pluripotent stem cells — iPSCs — can be coaxed in culture to develop into miniature versions of organs: kidney organoids, liver organoids, cardiac organoids. These mini-organs are useful for research and drug screening. The question is whether they can be scaled up to a transplantable size and complexity.

So far, mostly no. Organoids are millimeter-scale. A functional kidney is the size of a fist. The developmental cues needed to take a millimeter-scale organoid up to organ scale are not yet well understood, and we don't know how to deliver them in a culture dish. Some progress has been made — there are papers on growing scaled-up bits of intestine, on producing pancreatic islets that can be implanted to treat diabetes. But a transplantable solid organ grown from iPSCs has not been achieved.

There is, however, a hybrid idea that bridges xeno and stem-cell approaches. The idea is **interspecies chimerism**: implant human iPSCs into a pig embryo, with the pig embryo modified so that it cannot develop its own pancreas (for example), and let the human cells fill in the missing organ. The result is a pig that grows a human pancreas. The pancreas is then harvested for transplant.

This sounds like science fiction, but it has been demonstrated in proof of principle. Several labs — including teams at Stanford, the Salk Institute, and in Japan — have produced pig embryos with partial human contribution. The technique is called **blastocyst complementation**. It has not yet produced a transplantable human organ inside a pig. But it is a real direction, and it would solve some of xeno's immune problems by making the donor organ literally human in cellular composition.

So the field's competition is real but distant. For the next decade, the most plausible source of large-scale transplantable organs is **edited pigs**. Bioprinting and stem-cell-derived organs may eventually surpass xeno, but xeno is the one that's in clinical use today. That's why we've spent two hours on it.

## SEGMENT 18 — The frontier: universal donors, chimeras, and the road to routine

Let me close by sketching where the field is going. The trajectory, if you extrapolate from where we are now, points in several directions at once.

The first direction is **further multi-edit pigs**. Both eGenesis and Revivicor are working on pigs with even more edits — knocking out additional pig-specific MHC genes, adding additional human tolerance-inducing transgenes. The goal is what is sometimes called a **universal donor pig** — an animal whose organs can be transplanted into any human recipient with minimal immune suppression, regardless of the recipient's antibody profile. We are not there yet, but the trajectory is toward fewer drugs and less rejection per generation of pig.

The second direction is **in-pig humanization** — the blastocyst complementation work I mentioned in the last segment. If you can grow a fully human pancreas inside a pig, you have eliminated the antigen problem entirely. Several groups are pursuing this. The current bottleneck is the developmental biology — how do you get enough human cells to contribute to the relevant organ, in the relevant proportion, in the relevant developmental window. Some labs have demonstrated pig embryos with substantial human cellular contribution. None have yet produced a transplantable humanized organ. The path is long but the destination is real.

The third direction is **routine clinical xeno**. The Phase 1 trials that begin in 2025, if successful, will be followed by Phase 2 trials, then Phase 3, then first approvals around 2027 to 2028, then expanded clinical access. By the early 2030s, if all goes well, a patient on the kidney waiting list might be offered, as a standard of care, either a human kidney or an edited pig kidney, depending on availability and compatibility. By the late 2030s, the human waiting list may begin to shrink, not because more people are donating, but because the supply of pig kidneys is keeping up with demand.

\[Pause.\]

Will it actually go that way? I want to be honest. The field has a habit of overpromising and underdelivering. Every five years from 1990 to 2015, somebody announced that clinical xeno was just around the corner, and it wasn't. The current optimism could turn out to be premature again. There could be infectious disease setbacks. There could be long-term immune issues that don't manifest in the first year of patient follow-up. There could be regulatory delays. There could be public opinion shifts that constrain the field.

But the underlying technology is real in a way it wasn't before. **CRISPR was the enabling technology.** Without programmable, multiplexed gene editing, you cannot make a 69-edit pig, you cannot inactivate the PERVs, you cannot stack the human transgenes. The 2020s are the first decade in which the genetic toolkit was, finally, sufficient to address the immune barriers that had blocked the field for fifty years. From **impossible in 2000 to clinical reality in 2024** — that trajectory is unlikely to reverse.

If I had to bet, I would say: by the end of this decade, there will be at least a few hundred patients living with xenografted organs, mostly kidneys. By the end of next decade, the technology will have moved from experimental to standard of care, at least for the highest-need patients. The seventeen-people-a-day on the waiting list will begin to be served by a supply chain that runs through Virginia and Massachusetts barrier facilities, not just through brain-death-trauma donors. That is the world that xenotransplantation is, finally, beginning to build.

\[Pause.\]

We started two hours ago with one sentence. **Xenotransplantation is borrowing an organ from a pig — and CRISPR is what finally let us do it.** I hope, having now spent two hours on it, that you can do the three things I asked you for. Explain why alpha-gal was the central villain for fifty years. Walk through the modern multi-edit pig, gene by gene. Tell the story of Bennett, Slayman, Pisano, and Looney, and what each one taught the field.

If you can do those three things, you understand xenotransplantation as well as anyone outside of the field. The rest is updates — new patients, new trials, new edits. But the framework is what we've built today. The field is yours to follow.

Thank you. We'll take questions.

## APPENDIX — Quick-reference summary (not for reading aloud)

**Core idea**: Xenotransplantation is **borrowing an organ from a pig — and CRISPR is what finally let us do it**. A genetically edited pig grows an organ. Surgeons transplant it into a human. The edits make the organ look enough like "self" that the immune system tolerates it.

**The unmet need**: ~100,000 people on the US transplant waiting list. ~17 die per day waiting. Mostly kidney patients, with multi-year dialysis-tethered waits.

**Why pigs**: Anatomy (organ size and shape close to human), reproduction (six-month maturity, two litters per year of 8-12), ethics (not great apes, on the right side of the species-line that society has drawn).

**Hyperacute rejection**: **"The immune system's instant-kill — your antibodies see alpha-gal sugar on the pig organ, complement activates, the organ dies in minutes."** The fifty-year wall. Caused by pre-formed anti-alpha-gal antibodies binding pig endothelium, triggering complement, leading to membrane attack complex, endothelial death, and vessel-wide thrombosis.

**Alpha-gal**: galactose-alpha-1,3-galactose. The sugar pigs make and humans don't. Lost in great apes ~25 million years ago. The chief antigen for hyperacute rejection. Made by an enzyme called GalT, encoded by the GGTA1 gene.

**Discovery/engineering timeline**: 1906 (Jaboulay's pig kidney) → 1984 (Baby Fae's baboon heart, 21 days) → 2002 (GalT-KO pig, Immerge/PPL) → 2013 (CRISPR enters the field) → 2017 (Church/Yang PERV-free pigs, 25 simultaneous knockouts) → January 2022 (Bennett, first pig heart, 60 days) → March 2024 (Slayman, first pig kidney, 49 days) → April 2024 (Pisano, kidney + thymus) → November 2024 (Looney, longest survival so far).

**Triple knockout**: GalT (alpha-gal) + CMAH (Neu5Gc) + β4GalNT2 (Sda). "The GMO patient — three sugar-modifying enzymes deleted, three xenoantigens stripped off."

**Human transgenes inserted**: complement regulators (CD46, CD55, CD59), anticoagulants (thrombomodulin, EPCR, CD39), T-cell modulators (CD47, HLA-E). "Make the pig look more like self."

**PERVs**: porcine endogenous retroviruses, ~25-50 copies per pig genome. **"Endogenous retroviruses every pig genome carries — could theoretically jump into humans during transplant; Church's 25-KO eliminated them."** 2017 Niu/Yang/Church paper inactivated all functional PERVs in pig fibroblasts and produced PERV-free piglets.

**Companies**:
- **Revivicor** (United Therapeutics, Blacksburg VA): descends from PPL/Dolly. 10-gene-edited pig. No PERV knockout — relies on barrier herd. Made Bennett's heart.
- **eGenesis** (Cambridge MA, founded by Church and Yang): 69-edit pig including PERV knockouts. Made the 2024 kidney pigs for Slayman, Pisano, Looney.

**Patients**:
- **David Bennett** (January 7, 2022, University of Maryland, Bartley Griffith): first pig heart, 10-edit Revivicor donor, 60-day survival, died of porcine CMV reactivation that screening missed.
- **Lawrence Faucette** (September 2023, Maryland): second pig heart, ~6 weeks, rejection.
- **Rick Slayman** (March 16, 2024, Mass General, Tatsuo Kawai): first pig kidney, eGenesis 69-edit, 49-day survival, died of unrelated cardiac event with functioning kidney.
- **Lisa Pisano** (April 12, 2024, NYU Langone, Robert Montgomery): pig kidney + pig thymus. ~7-week kidney function, died of cardiac causes.
- **Towana Looney** (November 2024, NYU Langone): longest survival; eGenesis kidney; still alive at last update, 4+ months.

**Immunosuppression**: **Tacrolimus + belatacept (CD28 blockade) + anti-CD40L (costimulation blockade) + low-dose steroids**. Heavier than standard human-to-human protocol. Layered T-cell blockade on top of edited organ.

**FDA pathway**: CBER regulates. Current cases under **expanded access / compassionate use**, single-patient authorizations. Formal **Phase 1 IND** trials expected to begin 2025. **First approvals expected ~2027-2028**. Lifelong patient surveillance for pig pathogens required.

**Pig virus screening**: **Gnotobiotic herds** — born by C-section into sterile isolators, never touch the outside world. Sentinel testing. Tissue-biopsy PCR for latent herpesviruses (the Bennett lesson — blood-based PCR missed pCMV). Hep E, influenza A, _S. suis_ also on the screening list.

**Ethics**:
- **Religion**: Islamic and Jewish authorities generally permit pig organs for life-saving use (darura / pikuach nefesh). Hindu and Buddhist traditions similar.
- **Animal welfare**: ~1 pig per organ. High-welfare facilities. Set against ~130 million pigs/year for US food.
- **Consent**: Patients are no-options; debate over whether the desperate can truly consent. Mitigated by extended consent process and ethics review.
- **Public health**: Surveillance and contact restrictions to prevent zoonosis.

**Competition**:
- **3D bioprinting**: vascularization is the unsolved problem; ~10-20 years behind xeno.
- **Stem-cell-derived organs (iPSC organoids)**: still millimeter-scale, scaling to organ-size unsolved.
- **Blastocyst complementation**: grow human organs inside pigs by injecting human iPSCs into pig embryos with developmental knockouts. Proof-of-concept demonstrated; not yet transplantable.

**Frontier**: more edits per pig, universal donor pigs, in-pig humanization (chimeras), formal Phase 1 trials in 2025, first approvals 2027-2028, routine clinical xeno by 2030s.

**One-sentence summary**: Genetic editing of pig genomes — knocking out alpha-gal and other xenoantigens, inserting human regulatory transgenes, inactivating endogenous retroviruses — has, after fifty years of failure, made pig organs survivable in humans, with Bennett in 2022 and Slayman/Pisano/Looney in 2024 as the proof of concept and routine clinical use plausible within a decade.
